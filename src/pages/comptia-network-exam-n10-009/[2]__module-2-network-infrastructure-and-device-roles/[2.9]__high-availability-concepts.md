## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**High availability overview**

High availability (HA) is about keeping services reachable when something fails. In practice, you design for continuity by adding redundancy so a single failure does not take down the whole network.

The main goal is to reduce downtime without overengineering the environment. A good HA design matches your workload, risk tolerance, and uptime requirements.

- **Redundancy** adds backup components or paths.
- **Failover** moves traffic to a working path when the primary one fails.
- **Single points of failure** are components that can stop the entire service if they break.

> A second link or device only helps if it can take over automatically or with minimal disruption.

**Why redundancy matters**

A network can fail because of a cut fiber, an ISP outage, or hardware failure. If you rely on one circuit, one provider, or one router, that single dependency can interrupt service.

Redundant links provide an alternate path so traffic can reroute in the background. The user experience should stay as seamless as possible, with applications continuing to work during the switchover.

- A single circuit creates a risk if that circuit goes down.
- A single provider creates a risk if the provider has an outage.
- A single router creates a risk if the device fails.

**Common high availability patterns**

High availability often uses more than one device or connection so the network can keep operating during a fault. In firewall environments, HA may use active-passive or active-active configurations so one device can take over if another fails.

Link aggregation is another common approach. With Link Aggregation Control Protocol (LACP), multiple physical links are bonded into one logical interface. This can improve fault tolerance and may also balance traffic across links.

| Concept | Purpose | Practical effect |
|---|---|---|
| Redundant link | Backup path | Traffic can reroute if one link fails |
| HA pair | Backup device | Another device can continue service |
| LACP bundle | Logical link from multiple physical links | Adds resilience and can distribute traffic |

**Design considerations**

You should balance resilience with cost. More redundancy can improve uptime, but unnecessary complexity can waste budget and make the design harder to manage.

A practical HA design focuses on the most likely failure points first. That usually means protecting the links, devices, and providers that would otherwise create a single point of failure.

- Match redundancy to the importance of the service.
- Protect critical paths before less important ones.
- Keep the design simple enough to operate and troubleshoot.

**What to remember for the exam**

For Network+ questions, look for wording that points to continuity, backup paths, or automatic switchover. Those clues usually indicate HA or redundancy.

- **Redundancy** prevents one failure from stopping service.
- **Failover** is the move to a backup resource.
- **LACP** combines links for resilience and traffic distribution.
- **Single points of failure** are the first thing to eliminate in an HA design.

**Quick review**

If a question describes a network that must keep working during hardware or link failure, think about redundant links, redundant devices, or failover clustering. If the question mentions multiple links acting as one, think about LACP.
