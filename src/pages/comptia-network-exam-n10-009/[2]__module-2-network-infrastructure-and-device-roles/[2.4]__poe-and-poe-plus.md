## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Power over Ethernet basics**

Power over Ethernet (**PoE**) delivers DC power and network data through the same copper Ethernet cable. This reduces the need for separate power supplies, outlets, and extra cabling, which simplifies installation and lowers cable clutter.

PoE is useful when devices are hard to power with a nearby outlet. Common examples include:

- **IP cameras**
- **Wireless access points**
- **IP phones**
- **Sensors and other powered endpoints**

PoE does not add Ethernet data capability. It changes how power is delivered to the device while the network link still carries data normally.

**PoE standards and power levels**

The main PoE standards you need to recognize are:

- **IEEE 802.3af**: original PoE, up to **15.4 W** per port
- **IEEE 802.3at**: **PoE+**, up to **30 W** per port
- **IEEE 802.3bt**: **PoE++**, up to **100 W** per port

A practical exam takeaway is that PoE and PoE+ differ mainly in available power. Higher-power devices need the higher standard, while lower-power endpoints can run on standard PoE.

| Standard | Common name | Max power per port |
|---|---|---:|
| IEEE 802.3af | PoE | 15.4 W |
| IEEE 802.3at | PoE+ | 30 W |
| IEEE 802.3bt | PoE++ | 100 W |

**How PoE is delivered**

PoE uses **Power Sourcing Equipment (PSE)** to send power to a **Powered Device (PD)** over twisted-pair Ethernet cabling. In practice, the switch often acts as the PSE and the endpoint acts as the PD.

This design helps you place devices where power outlets are limited or inconvenient. It is especially helpful in deployments that need cleaner cabling and faster installation.

**PoE vs. PoE+**

PoE+ is the higher-power version of standard PoE. Use it when the endpoint needs more power than IEEE 802.3af can provide.

- **PoE** supports lower-power devices and is often enough for basic endpoints.
- **PoE+** supports devices with greater power demand, up to 30 W per port.
- Both standards still carry data over the same Ethernet link.

A simple way to remember the difference is that PoE+ extends the usable power budget, not the network speed.

**Common use cases**

PoE is widely used in environments that benefit from centralized power delivery:

- Surveillance systems
- Wireless networking
- Smart building devices
- Industrial and office endpoints

These use cases share a common need: reliable network connectivity with fewer separate power runs.

**Exam focus points**

- **PoE** means power and data share one Ethernet cable.
- **PoE+** is IEEE 802.3at and provides more power than standard PoE.
- **PSE** supplies power; **PD** receives it.
- PoE simplifies installation and reduces cable clutter.
- The standard you choose depends on the device’s power requirement.

> If a device needs more power than standard PoE can provide, choose PoE+ rather than assuming the link speed changes.

**Quick comparison**

| Feature | PoE | PoE+ |
|---|---|---|
| IEEE standard | 802.3af | 802.3at |
| Power budget | Up to 15.4 W | Up to 30 W |
| Typical use | Lower-power endpoints | Higher-power endpoints |
| Cabling | Single Ethernet cable for data and power | Single Ethernet cable for data and power |
