## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Network type overview**

Network types are often grouped by the area they cover, the devices they connect, and the way they share resources. The main idea is to match the network design to the need: small personal connections, local building networks, or large-scale links across cities and countries.

- **Scope** is the most common way to classify a network.
- **Access model** also matters, especially when comparing peer-to-peer and client-server designs.
- **Use case** determines whether you prioritize speed, reach, simplicity, or centralized control.

**Peer-to-peer and client-server**

A peer-to-peer network has no dedicated server role. Each device can act as both a client and a server, which makes it simple and inexpensive to deploy for small environments. The tradeoff is administration: authentication, data, and application logic are distributed, so security and management are harder to control.

Client-server networks centralize services on one or more servers. This model is easier to manage because users and devices rely on dedicated systems for access, authentication, and shared resources. In practice, this is the more common choice when you need stronger control and easier administration.

- **Peer-to-peer** is low cost and simple to set up.
- **Client-server** is easier to administer at scale.
- Distributed control in peer-to-peer can make security harder to enforce.

**Common network scope types**

These terms describe how far the network reaches and what it connects. The names are useful shorthand, but the real distinction is the size and purpose of the environment.

| Type | Meaning | Typical use case |
|---|---|---|
| **PAN** | Personal Area Network | Connects devices around one person, such as nearby personal devices |
| **LAN** | Local Area Network | Connects devices in a building or nearby group of buildings |
| **MAN** | Metropolitan Area Network | Connects networks across a city or metropolitan region |
| **WAN** | Wide Area Network | Connects locations over long distances, such as across countries or continents |
| **CAN** | Campus Area Network | Connects multiple buildings in a campus or enterprise site |

A LAN usually provides high bandwidth because the devices are local. Ethernet and 802.11 wireless are common LAN technologies. A WAN spans much larger distances, so it usually has lower speeds than a LAN because of the physical distance and transport complexity involved.

- **PAN** is the smallest scope and centers on one user.
- **LAN** is local and typically fast.
- **WAN** is long-distance and usually slower than a LAN.
- **CAN** fits a campus or enterprise site with multiple nearby buildings.

**LAN and WAN in practice**

A LAN is the default choice when devices are in the same building or in nearby buildings under local control. It supports fast communication and resource sharing inside an organization.

A WAN is used when you need to connect distant sites. Because the connection crosses large geographic areas, the design usually focuses more on reach and reliability than on the raw speed you expect from a LAN.

> A slower connection does not automatically mean a problem; if the link spans a long distance, it may simply be a WAN rather than a LAN.

**Enterprise network context**

An enterprise network is the connectivity infrastructure that helps employees and machines communicate, share files, access systems, and support business operations. It is usually designed to connect authorized systems and provide secure, efficient communication for specific organizational needs.

Enterprise networks often combine multiple network types. For example, a company may use LANs inside buildings, a CAN across a campus, and a WAN to connect remote offices.

- **Enterprise network** is a business-focused network environment.
- It supports communication, file sharing, service access, and performance analysis.
- It often combines several network scopes rather than relying on just one.

**How to choose the right network type**

Use the smallest network that meets the requirement, then expand only when the use case demands it. Start with the location of the devices, the number of users, and the level of administrative control you need.

1. Identify where the devices are located.
2. Decide whether the connection is local, campus-wide, metropolitan, or long-distance.
3. Choose the access model that matches your management needs.
4. Confirm whether the environment needs centralized control or distributed sharing.
5. Select the network type that best fits the scope and operational goals.

**Exam focus**

For Network+ purposes, focus on matching the network type to the scenario.

- **PAN**: personal devices near one user.
- **LAN**: local building or nearby buildings.
- **CAN**: multiple buildings in a campus.
- **MAN**: city-scale connectivity.
- **WAN**: long-distance connectivity.
- **Peer-to-peer**: simple and low cost, but harder to manage securely.
- **Client-server**: centralized and easier to administer.
