## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Network topology overview**

Network topology is the arrangement of devices and links in a network. It describes how nodes such as computers, servers, and other devices connect and how data flows between them. The topology you choose affects performance, cost, reliability, security, and how easy the network is to troubleshoot.

There are two broad ways to describe topology:

- **Physical topology** describes the actual layout of cables and devices.
- **Logical topology** describes how data moves across the network, regardless of the physical layout.

A network can have one physical layout but a different logical flow. That distinction matters when you document a network or investigate traffic behavior.

**Why topology matters**

Topology is not just a diagram style. It helps you plan networks, understand how traffic reaches its destination, and identify where failures may occur. In enterprise environments, topology also influences scalability and how well the design handles growth or downtime.

- A good design supports efficient traffic flow.
- A poor design can create slow connections and costly outages.
- Topology choices can affect security gaps and fault isolation.

**Common topology types**

The following topologies are commonly discussed as physical layouts. Each one has different tradeoffs in cost, resilience, and complexity.

- **Point-to-point** connects two nodes directly.
  - It is the simplest communication model.
  - It can provide high bandwidth between the two endpoints.
- **Bus** uses a shared backbone for communication.
  - Devices share the same main transmission path.
- **Star** connects devices to a central device.
  - It is also called a hub-and-spoke layout.
  - It is common in large networks because the central device simplifies connectivity.
- **Mesh** connects nodes with multiple paths.
  - It improves redundancy because more than one route may exist.
- **Ring** connects devices in a loop.
  - Data follows a circular path through the network.
- **Tree** arranges networks in a hierarchical structure.
  - It combines branching relationships into layers.
- **Hybrid** combines two or more topology types.
  - It is used when one design alone does not meet the network’s needs.

**Physical vs. logical topology**

Physical and logical topology are related, but they are not the same thing. Physical topology is what you can map from cables, ports, and devices. Logical topology is how traffic behaves on the network.

- **Physical maps** show device placement and cable connections.
- **Logical maps** show how networks connect at a higher level.
- Virtualized environments may make logical documentation more useful than a purely physical view.

When you document a network, choose the map that matches the question you are trying to answer. If you need to trace a cable or identify a rack device, use a physical map. If you need to understand traffic flow or network relationships, use a logical map.

**Topology in planning and troubleshooting**

Topology is useful during both design and troubleshooting. During planning, it helps you decide how to connect systems and where to place central devices. During troubleshooting, it helps you see how one network segment connects to another and where a failure might interrupt communication.

- Use topology diagrams to visualize paths between networks.
- Check central devices first in star-based designs.
- Look for alternate paths in mesh-based designs.
- Use documentation to match the observed traffic flow with the intended design.

**Quick comparison**

| Topology | Basic idea | Practical note |
|---|---|---|
| Point-to-point | Two nodes communicate directly | Simple and high bandwidth |
| Bus | Shared backbone | Multiple devices use the same path |
| Star | Devices connect to a central device | Common and easy to manage |
| Mesh | Multiple interconnections | Strong redundancy |
| Ring | Devices form a loop | Traffic follows a circular path |
| Tree | Hierarchical branching | Useful for layered designs |
| Hybrid | Mixed design | Flexible for varied requirements |

**Study focus**

For Network+, focus on recognizing the topology name, understanding the physical and logical distinction, and explaining why a design choice matters. You should be able to connect the topology to real-world concerns such as performance, reliability, security, and troubleshooting.

> A topology diagram is only useful if it helps you understand how traffic moves and where a failure would matter.
