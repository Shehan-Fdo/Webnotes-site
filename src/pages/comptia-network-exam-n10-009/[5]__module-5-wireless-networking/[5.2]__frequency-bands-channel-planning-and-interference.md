## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Wireless channel planning**

Wi-Fi channel planning determines how devices share available radio frequencies. Good planning reduces overlap, contention, and retransmissions so access points (APs) and clients can communicate more efficiently.

Channel planning matters because only so many frequencies are available in a given area. If multiple APs compete on the same or overlapping channels, performance drops and interference increases.

- **Overlap** happens when nearby APs use channels that bleed into each other.
- **Contention** increases when too many devices compete for the same channel.
- **Retransmissions** rise when collisions or interference corrupt frames.

**2.4 GHz planning**

The 2.4 GHz band has a small number of non-overlapping channels, so careful placement is important. In the United States, channels 1, 6, and 11 are commonly used to reduce overlap.

This band is useful for compatibility and range, but it is more prone to congestion because many devices share it. Keep channel assignments simple and avoid placing adjacent APs on overlapping channels.

- Use the three non-overlapping channels where applicable.
- Avoid stacking nearby APs on channels that overlap.
- Expect more interference in dense environments.

**5 GHz planning**

The 5 GHz band offers more channels than 2.4 GHz, which gives you more flexibility for channel planning. However, wider channels reduce the number of available channel options and can increase contention when APs are close together.

Mixing channel widths in 5 GHz can also hurt performance. Different widths can increase collisions and retransmissions, especially in dense deployments.

- Prefer channel widths that match the environment.
- Use wider channels only when spectrum is available.
- Avoid mixing widths without a clear design reason.

> Wider 5 GHz channels can reduce the number of usable channels and degrade performance in high-density areas.

**6 GHz planning**

The 6 GHz band expands the available spectrum and increases the number of non-overlapping channels. That makes it useful for cleaner channel planning and lower congestion.

Channel-width recommendations vary by region. In the United States, 80 MHz channels are supported by the larger available spectrum, while regions with less spectrum, such as the EU, are better suited to 20 MHz or 40 MHz channels.

- Use the available spectrum conservatively in smaller regulatory domains.
- Match channel width to regional availability.
- Plan for density, not just peak speed.

**Channel width tradeoffs**

Channel width is a tradeoff between throughput and channel availability. Wider channels can increase speed, but they also consume more spectrum and reduce the number of channels you can assign.

Narrower channels usually provide better reuse in busy environments. That makes them a practical choice when many APs operate near each other.

| Channel width | Typical effect | Planning impact |
|---|---|---|
| 20 MHz | Narrowest option | More channel reuse, less contention |
| 40 MHz | Moderate width | Balanced choice in some environments |
| 80 MHz | Wider option | Higher throughput potential, fewer available channels |

**Dynamic Frequency Selection**

<abbr title="Dynamic Frequency Selection">DFS</abbr> channels can be useful, but radar detection may force an AP to change channels. That can interrupt connectivity, especially for devices that do not support DFS channels.

Use DFS-aware planning when you need the extra spectrum, but account for possible channel changes during operation.

- Expect channel moves when radar is detected.
- Verify client support before relying on DFS channels.
- Treat DFS as a planning constraint, not a guaranteed stable choice.

**Practical planning approach**

1. Survey the environment to identify nearby APs and existing channel use.
2. Choose a band strategy based on density, client support, and available spectrum.
3. Assign channels to minimize overlap and collisions.
4. Select channel widths that fit the region and deployment size.
5. Recheck performance and adjust if interference or retransmissions increase.

A good wireless design balances coverage, capacity, and interference control. In practice, that means using the smallest channel width that still meets performance needs, especially in dense areas.

**Common interference outcomes**

Interference is often visible as poor throughput, unstable connections, or frequent retransmissions. In crowded deployments, the problem is usually not a lack of signal strength but too many devices competing for the same spectrum.

- Nearby APs on overlapping channels create avoidable interference.
- Excessive channel width can reduce the number of usable channels.
- Automatic channel selection can help, but it still needs a sensible design baseline.
