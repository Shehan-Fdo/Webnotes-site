## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Wireless planning overview**

A wireless deployment starts with understanding the environment before you install hardware. A **site survey** helps you identify where coverage is strong, where it drops off, and where interference or physical barriers may affect performance. The goal is to place access points (APs) so users get reliable connectivity without unnecessary overlap or channel contention.

Wireless networks are also affected by nearby APs, existing frequencies in use, and changes over time. Because of that, you may need to revisit the survey later to confirm the network still performs as expected.

- Site surveys help you plan AP location and tune radio settings.
- Coverage problems can come from interference, channel noise, or walls that block signal.
- Re-surveying later helps you catch environmental changes that affect performance.

**Signal strength and coverage**

Signal strength is the wireless signal power level received by the client. It is commonly represented in dBm, with values ranging from `0` to `-100`. Stronger signal strength generally means more reliable connections and higher speeds.

Noise and interference reduce usable wireless performance even when an AP is technically reachable. In practice, you want enough signal for the client to connect cleanly and enough separation between APs to avoid unnecessary overlap.

> Strong signal alone is not enough; you also need a clean RF environment with minimal interference.

- **Signal strength** affects reliability and speed.
- **Noise levels** can reduce throughput and stability.
- **Interference** becomes more likely when multiple wireless networks operate nearby.

**Site survey types and purpose**

A wireless site survey is an assessment of a location used to plan or improve a WLAN (wireless local area network). It helps you understand the current or planned network environment and identify issues before or after deployment.

Common survey approaches include:
- **Predictive surveys** that estimate coverage from a floor plan or planned layout.
- **Active surveys** that measure performance while connected to the network.
- **Passive surveys** that observe wireless activity without joining the network.

These surveys help you decide where to install APs, whether to move an antenna, and whether a radio needs different tuning for better coverage.

**Access point placement**

AP placement should support strong coverage where users actually work. In environments such as classrooms, offices, and campus spaces, poor placement can create dead zones, buffering, or unstable roaming behavior.

When you plan placement, consider:
- User density and where devices are used most often.
- Physical obstacles such as walls that may block or weaken signal.
- Nearby APs that may create overlapping coverage or interference.
- Whether the AP location supports the intended coverage area.

**Channel planning**

Channel planning reduces interference by limiting how often nearby APs compete for the same frequencies. Wireless standards provide ways to automatically select frequencies, and many APs can also be configured manually.

For practical planning:
- Use channels that avoid existing wireless activity when possible.
- Adjust channel assignments when nearby APs or interference sources change.
- Review both 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz planning as part of the deployment.

**Survey-driven deployment workflow**

1. Assess the environment with a site survey before installation.
2. Identify coverage gaps, interference sources, and physical barriers.
3. Choose AP locations that improve coverage and reduce dead zones.
4. Assign channels and power levels to support stable throughput.
5. Recheck the wireless environment after deployment or when conditions change.

**Practical outcomes**

A good survey leads to better AP placement, cleaner channel use, and more reliable wireless service. It also helps you avoid common problems such as weak coverage at the edge of a building, unnecessary overlap between APs, and performance issues caused by interference.
