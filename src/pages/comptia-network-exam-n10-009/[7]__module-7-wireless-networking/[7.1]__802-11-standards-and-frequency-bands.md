## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**802.11 overview**

IEEE 802.11 is the family of standards that defines Wi-Fi networking. It specifies the medium access control (MAC) and physical layer (PHY) protocols used for wireless local area network (WLAN) communication.

Wi-Fi devices use these standards to connect laptops, printers, smartphones, and other endpoints without wires. In practice, the standard and its amendments form the basis for most home and office wireless networks.

- **IEEE 802.11** is the core WLAN standard family.
- **MAC** and **PHY** define how wireless devices access the medium and transmit data.
- Wi-Fi products are built on 802.11 amendments and are widely used for internet access and local device communication.

**Common Wi-Fi standards**

The 802.11 family has evolved over time to improve speed, range, and reliability. The evidence highlights several widely recognized generations and their typical bands and rates.

| Standard | Typical band | Notable rate or trait |
|---|---:|---|
| `802.11` | `2.4 GHz` | Up to `2 Mbps` |
| `802.11a` | `5 GHz` | Up to `54 Mbps` |
| `802.11b` | `2.4 GHz` | Up to `11 Mbps` |
| `802.11g` | `2.4 GHz` | Up to `54 Mbps` |
| `802.11n` | Not specified in the evidence | Commonly discussed as Wi-Fi 4 |
| `802.11ac` | Not specified in the evidence | Commonly discussed as Wi-Fi 5 |
| `802.11ax` | Not specified in the evidence | Commonly discussed as Wi-Fi 6 |
| `802.11be` | Not specified in the evidence | Commonly discussed as Wi-Fi 7 |

The key exam takeaway is that newer standards generally improve wireless performance, but the exact experience still depends on the client device, router support, and the band in use.

- `802.11a` and `802.11g` both support up to `54 Mbps`, but they use different bands.
- `802.11b` stays on `2.4 GHz`, which is also used by `802.11g`.
- Modern Wi-Fi discussions often reference Wi-Fi 6, Wi-Fi 6E, and Wi-Fi 7 as later generations of the 802.11 family.

**Frequency bands**

A Wi-Fi frequency band is the radio range used to send data between a device and a router. The most common bands are `2.4 GHz` and `5 GHz`, and some newer routers also support `6 GHz`.

Each band has tradeoffs:

- **2.4 GHz**
  - Common in older and many current devices
  - Often associated with broader coverage
  - Used by standards such as `802.11b` and `802.11g`
- **5 GHz**
  - Common in faster Wi-Fi deployments
  - Used by `802.11a`
  - Often chosen when you want higher throughput and lower interference than `2.4 GHz`
- **6 GHz**
  - Found on some modern Wi-Fi 6E and Wi-Fi 7 routers
  - Expands available spectrum for newer devices

Band choice affects both performance and compatibility. If a device does not support a band, it cannot use that band even if the router offers it.

> Band support matters as much as standard support: a fast router does not help if the client device cannot connect on the available frequency.

**How standards and bands relate**

The standard tells you what wireless technology a device uses, while the band tells you where it operates in the radio spectrum. These are related, but they are not the same thing.

- A standard can be associated with one or more bands.
- A band can carry multiple standards.
- Real-world performance depends on both the standard and the band, plus environmental factors and device capability.

For example, `802.11g` operates in the `2.4 GHz` ISM band and can reach signaling rates up to `54 Mbit/s`. That makes it a useful reference point for understanding how a standard maps to a specific frequency range.

**Practical exam focus**

When you study 802.11 standards and frequency bands, focus on recognition and comparison.

- Match common standards to their bands.
- Remember the original standard and its early throughput:
  - `802.11` → `2.4 GHz`, up to `2 Mbps`
- Remember the classic faster variants:
  - `802.11a` → `5 GHz`, up to `54 Mbps`
  - `802.11b` → `2.4 GHz`, up to `11 Mbps`
  - `802.11g` → `2.4 GHz`, up to `54 Mbps`
- Understand that newer Wi-Fi generations continue improving speed, range, and reliability.
- Keep in mind that wireless performance depends on the client, the router, and the available band.

**Quick study checklist**

1. Identify the 802.11 family as the basis for Wi-Fi.
2. Associate `2.4 GHz` with `802.11b` and `802.11g`.
3. Associate `5 GHz` with `802.11a`.
4. Remember that `6 GHz` appears in newer Wi-Fi 6E and Wi-Fi 7 equipment.
5. Compare standards by speed, band, and compatibility.
