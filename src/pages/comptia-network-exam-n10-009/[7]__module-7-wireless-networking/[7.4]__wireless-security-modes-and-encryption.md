## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Wireless security overview**

Wireless networks broadcast through the air, so nearby devices can potentially intercept traffic. To protect confidentiality, you use wireless security protocols that encrypt data between a client device and the access point or router. These protections apply on the wireless link itself; once traffic leaves the router and travels across the Internet, other protections such as HTTPS or a VPN may also be involved.

- **Encryption** helps keep wireless traffic private.
- **Authentication** limits who can join the network.
- **Integrity** checks help verify that received data has not been altered.

**Common wireless security modes**

The main Wi-Fi security generations are WEP, WPA, WPA2, and WPA3. They were developed to replace earlier, weaker protection and improve wireless security over time.

- **WEP** stands for Wired Equivalent Privacy.
- **WPA** stands for Wi-Fi Protected Access and appeared as an intermediate step.
- **WPA2** became the common implementation of the IEEE 802.11i standard.
- **WPA3** adds newer security improvements over WPA2.

**WEP limitations**

WEP is an early wireless encryption protocol. It was designed to secure WLANs, but it is considered weak compared with later standards. For exam purposes, treat WEP as legacy protection that should not be your preferred choice.

- **WEP** is the oldest option in this set.
- It uses 64-bit and 128-bit key lengths.
- It exists as a historical baseline, not a modern best practice.

> WEP is included mainly for comparison. In practical deployments, you should favor newer wireless security modes.

**WPA, WPA2, and WPA3**

WPA was introduced in 2003 as a transitional solution while the industry moved toward stronger protection. WPA2 followed in 2004 and is widely associated with the full IEEE 802.11i standard. WPA3 arrived in 2018 and improves security further.

- **WPA** is often associated with TKIP.
- **WPA2** is the common baseline for modern wireless security.
- **WPA3** is the newest of the three and provides security enhancements.

**Authentication and access control**

Wireless security is not only about encryption. You also need a way to control who can connect. Common access methods include a shared password for a home or small office network, or user-specific credentials in more advanced environments.

- **Shared credentials** are common in simple deployments.
- **User-based access** supports stronger control in managed environments.
- **Multi-factor authentication** may be used where supported.

**How encryption fits into wireless communication**

Wireless devices act as both transmitters and receivers, so anyone nearby may be able to listen to the radio signal. Encryption protects the content even if the signal is captured. A message integrity check helps confirm that the data received matches what was sent.

1. You authenticate to the wireless network.
2. The network encrypts traffic over the air.
3. Integrity checks help validate the received data.

**Practical exam takeaways**

When you compare wireless security modes, focus on the progression from weak to stronger protection. The key idea is that newer standards improve security and are preferred for modern networks.

- **WEP** is legacy and weak.
- **WPA** is transitional.
- **WPA2** is widely used and tied to 802.11i.
- **WPA3** is the most secure option in this set.

**Scope reminder**

Wireless encryption protects the link between the device and the router or access point. It does not replace end-to-end security on the Internet. If you use secure web traffic or a VPN, those protections layer on top of wireless encryption.
