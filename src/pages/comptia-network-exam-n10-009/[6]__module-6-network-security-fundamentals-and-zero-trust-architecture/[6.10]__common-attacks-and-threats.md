## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Overview**

Common attacks and threats target availability, integrity, and confidentiality. In practice, you look for patterns such as traffic floods, spoofed control messages, rogue infrastructure, and user manipulation. The goal is to recognize the attack type quickly and apply the right mitigation.

- **Availability** attacks try to make services unreachable or unusable.
- **Integrity** attacks alter trust relationships, such as gateway or address mapping.
- **Confidentiality** attacks expose traffic for sniffing or interception.

**Denial of Service and Distributed Denial of Service**

A <abbr title="Denial of Service">DoS</abbr> attack floods a system or network with excessive traffic to cause downtime. A <abbr title="Distributed Denial of Service">DDoS</abbr> attack uses multiple devices, often a botnet, to amplify the flood and make blocking harder.

DDoS attacks commonly fall into three broad types:

- **Volumetric** attacks overwhelm available bandwidth.
- **Protocol** attacks target network infrastructure, such as a SYN flood.
- **Application-layer** attacks disrupt services like HTTP and DNS.

> A DDoS attack is often easier to recognize than to stop because the traffic can come from many sources at once.

**Layer 2 Attacks**

Layer 2 attacks exploit switching and address-resolution behavior on the local network.

- **MAC flooding** overloads a switch’s MAC address table with fake entries.
- When the table fills, the switch may enter flooding mode and send unicast traffic broadly.
- **Port security** limits the number of MAC addresses allowed per port and helps reduce this risk.

**ARP Spoofing and ARP Poisoning**

<abbr title="Address Resolution Protocol">ARP</abbr> attacks manipulate the mapping between IP addresses and MAC addresses. An attacker sends forged ARP messages so devices send traffic to the attacker’s MAC address instead of the real destination.

This enables:

- **Man-in-the-Middle** interception.
- Packet sniffing on the local network.
- Redirection of traffic intended for a legitimate gateway.

Use these defenses:

- **Dynamic ARP Inspection** verifies ARP responses against trusted information.
- **Static ARP entries** can be used where practical to reduce spoofing opportunities.

**VLAN Hopping**

VLAN hopping exploits misconfigured virtual LANs to reach traffic on another VLAN without authorization. The attack often relies on switch spoofing or double-tagging behavior.

To reduce exposure:

- Keep VLANs properly segmented.
- Avoid unnecessary trunking.
- Review switch port configuration for misconfigurations that allow cross-VLAN access.

**Rogue Infrastructure and Gateway Spoofing**

Some attacks provide a malicious network service that looks legitimate to clients. On a wired network, a malicious default gateway IP can be introduced through ARP poisoning, causing devices to send outbound traffic to the attacker instead of the real router.

This is especially dangerous because it can:

- Redirect traffic silently.
- Support credential capture or traffic inspection.
- Create a stable interception point for further attacks.

**Social Engineering and Phishing**

Not all threats are purely technical. Social engineering uses deception to persuade users to reveal information or take unsafe actions. Phishing is a common form that targets users through fraudulent messages or prompts.

Mitigation depends on people and process as much as technology:

- Train users to verify requests before acting.
- Use policies and procedures that reduce risky behavior.
- Reinforce awareness of suspicious links, attachments, and login prompts.

**Practical Defense Themes**

The same defensive ideas appear across many attack types. You reduce risk by limiting trust, validating control-plane information, and constraining what each device or user can do.

- **Least privilege** limits access to only what is needed for the job.
- **Segmentation** reduces the blast radius of a compromise.
- **Validation** tools such as DAI help verify network control messages.
- **Patching and training** reduce exposure to known weaknesses and user-driven attacks.

**Quick Recognition Guide**

| Attack type | What it does | Common impact |
|---|---|---|
| DoS | Floods a target from one source | Service downtime |
| DDoS | Floods a target from many sources | Wider disruption and harder filtering |
| MAC flooding | Overloads a switch MAC table | Traffic flooding and sniffing opportunity |
| ARP poisoning | Forges IP-to-MAC mapping | MITM and packet capture |
| VLAN hopping | Crosses into another VLAN | Unauthorized access to segmented traffic |
| Phishing | Tricks users into revealing data | Credential theft and compromise |

**Study Focus**

When you review these threats, connect the attack to the layer it affects and the control that stops it. That makes exam questions easier to separate:

- If the issue is bandwidth exhaustion, think **DoS/DDoS**.
- If the issue is forged local address mapping, think **ARP poisoning**.
- If the issue is switch table abuse, think **MAC flooding**.
- If the issue is crossing VLAN boundaries, think **VLAN hopping**.
- If the issue is user deception, think **social engineering** or **phishing**.
