## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Security control strategy**

Network security works best as a layered model. You combine **preventive**, **detective**, and **corrective** controls instead of depending on a single safeguard. This approach supports the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of data and resources across the network.

- **Preventive** controls reduce the chance of compromise before it happens.
- **Detective** controls help you identify suspicious activity and validate what occurred.
- **Corrective** controls help you recover and restore normal operations after an incident.

Segmentation and zero trust strengthen this model by limiting lateral movement. When you break a network into controlled segments and verify each access request, you reduce the spread of intrusions and make containment easier.

**Logging and monitoring**

Security logging and monitoring are core detective capabilities. Logging gives you evidence, while monitoring helps you notice patterns, anomalies, and active threats. Together, they support faster investigation and better decision-making during an event.

Effective logging is not just about collecting data. You need logs you can query and analyze so you can confirm what happened, understand scope, and support remediation. Centralized analysis is especially useful when multiple systems contribute evidence.

- Collect logs from relevant systems and services.
- Keep logs available for analysis during an incident.
- Use monitoring to detect changes that may indicate compromise.
- Treat logging as part of an ongoing security process, not a one-time setup.

**Incident response awareness**

Incident response (IR) is a planned and tested process for handling security events. Cyberattacks can range from minor issues to major incidents, so preparation matters for business continuity. A response plan helps you limit damage, coordinate actions, and restore operations more quickly.

A practical IR approach depends on people, processes, and technology working together. Front-line staff and IT personnel need to know how to recognize an incident and what to do next. In larger environments, a Computer Security Incident Response Team (CSIRT) may coordinate the response.

1. Detect or receive notice of a security event.
2. Use available logs and evidence to validate what happened.
3. Determine the scope and affected systems.
4. Take remediation actions to contain and recover.
5. Review the event and improve the response process.

> A tested incident response plan matters more than a written plan that has never been practiced.

**Resilience and recovery**

Security is an ongoing cycle. You improve resilience by combining continuous monitoring, tested incident response plans, backups, and forensic readiness. These measures help you respond more rapidly and preserve the information needed for analysis.

Forensic readiness means you prepare to collect and preserve useful evidence before an incident occurs. That preparation makes it easier to investigate events and support remediation without losing critical details.

**Key takeaways**

- Use layered controls rather than a single defense.
- Apply segmentation and zero trust to reduce lateral movement.
- Log and monitor continuously so you can detect and investigate events.
- Test your incident response process so recovery is faster and more reliable.
- Prepare for evidence collection before an incident happens.
