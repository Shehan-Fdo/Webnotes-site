## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Wireless standards overview**

IEEE 802.11 is the family of standards that defines wireless local area network (WLAN) communication. It covers the medium access control (MAC) and physical layer (PHY) behavior used by Wi‑Fi devices in homes, offices, and other environments.

Wi‑Fi is the common brand name built on 802.11 standards and amendments. In practice, you use these standards to connect laptops, phones, printers, and other devices without wires.

- **802.11** is the IEEE standard family for WLANs.
- **Wi‑Fi** is the market name associated with 802.11-based products.
- **IEEE** stands for Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers.

**Major Wi‑Fi generations**

The naming scheme often uses both the IEEE amendment and the Wi‑Fi generation name. This makes it easier to identify the technology in use and compare capabilities across devices.

| IEEE standard | Wi‑Fi name | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| `802.11ac` | Wi‑Fi 5 | Commonly referenced as a major prior generation |
| `802.11ax` | Wi‑Fi 6 | Backward-compatible with earlier Wi‑Fi standards |
| `802.11ax` extension | Wi‑Fi 6E | Extends Wi‑Fi 6 into the 6 GHz band |
| `802.11be` | Wi‑Fi 7 | Newer evolution focused on improved performance, capacity, and reliability |

Wi‑Fi 6E is not just a rename of Wi‑Fi 6. It extends the Wi‑Fi 6 standard into the 6 GHz radio-frequency band, which helps reduce congestion and interference for compatible devices.

> Wi‑Fi 6E supports only Wi‑Fi 6E devices, so it is not backward-compatible in the same way Wi‑Fi 6 is.

**Frequency bands and practical tradeoffs**

Wireless networks commonly use 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, and 6 GHz bands. The band you choose affects range, congestion, and performance.

- **2.4 GHz** is widely used and appears in older and many current devices.
- **5 GHz** supports higher throughput than 2.4 GHz in many deployments.
- **6 GHz** offers a newer, less congested space for compatible Wi‑Fi 6E and Wi‑Fi 7 devices.

Lower frequencies generally travel farther and can better handle obstacles, while higher frequencies often provide more capacity and less interference in crowded environments. In practice, you balance coverage and speed based on the environment.

**Interference and channel planning**

Wireless performance depends on how well you manage available frequencies and channels. Nearby access points, client devices, and other wireless systems can compete for the same spectrum and create interference.

A site survey helps you identify what frequencies are already in use before you deploy or adjust wireless equipment. Access points may also select channels automatically to avoid busy frequencies, or you may configure them manually when you need tighter control.

- **Interference** reduces wireless performance when multiple devices compete for the same spectrum.
- **Channel usage** matters because only so many frequencies are available in a given band.
- **Automatic selection** can help an access point move away from crowded channels.

**Wireless topology terms**

Wireless networking is not always access-point based. You may also see direct device-to-device communication in an ad hoc setup.

- **IBSS** means Independent Basic Service Set.
- **Ad hoc** describes a direct wireless connection between devices without an access point.
- **BSS** and **ESS** are common wireless network naming concepts used when identifying network structure and coverage.

Ad hoc connections are useful when you need a temporary or direct link between devices, such as initial setup for some Internet of Things devices.

**How to think about band selection**

Choose the band based on the environment and the devices you need to support. If you need broader compatibility, 2.4 GHz is often the safest baseline. If you need better performance and can tolerate less range, 5 GHz is often a strong choice. If you have compatible hardware and want a cleaner spectrum, 6 GHz can provide a better experience.

1. Identify the devices that must connect.
2. Check which Wi‑Fi generations they support.
3. Review the local wireless environment for congestion and interference.
4. Select the band and channel plan that best matches coverage and performance needs.
5. Revisit the configuration if users report slow speeds or unstable connections.

**Key exam takeaways**

- IEEE 802.11 defines the WLAN standards behind Wi‑Fi.
- Wi‑Fi 6E extends Wi‑Fi 6 into the 6 GHz band.
- Wi‑Fi 7 is the newer evolution of the standard.
- Band choice affects range, interference, and throughput.
- Site surveys and channel planning help reduce wireless issues.
