## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**WAN termination and the handoff point**

A wide area network (WAN) connection needs a place to terminate when it reaches your site. That termination is the point where the provider’s network hands off service to your internal network. This boundary is often called the **demarcation point** or **demarc**.

In practice, the demarc can be outside a building, inside a building, or at a central location in a business network. On the customer side of that boundary, you connect customer-premises equipment (CPE) and other internal devices.

- The demarc marks the boundary between the provider and your network.
- The handoff may be on the outside of a house or at a business network interface.
- Your equipment connects on the customer side of the demarc.

**Modems and what they do**

A **modem** converts digital data so it can travel over a phone line. In exam terms, it is associated with regular plain old telephone service (POTS) lines rather than leased digital circuits. Use a modem when the connection must traverse an analog phone line.

- A modem supports communication over a phone line.
- It converts digital signals for transmission across that line.
- It is not the same device role as a CSU/DSU on a leased digital circuit.

**CSU/DSU function**

A **CSU/DSU** stands for Channel Service Unit/Data Service Unit. It is a hardware device about the size of a modem, and it provides the interface between data terminal equipment (DTE) and a synchronous WAN link. It converts digital data frames from the local network into a format appropriate for the WAN, and it handles the clocking and digital signaling required by synchronous circuits.

CSU/DSUs are commonly used with leased digital lines such as T1 or fractional T1 connections. They can also support 56 Kbps or 64 Kbps channels and connect to an internet service provider’s gateway or a phone company circuit.

- A CSU/DSU connects to a leased digital line.
- It handles clocking for synchronous networks.
- It bridges DTE to the WAN circuit.

**Modem versus CSU/DSU**

The key difference is the type of circuit each device supports. A modem is used for a phone line, while a CSU/DSU is used for a network circuit such as a T1.

| Device | Typical circuit | Main role |
|---|---|---|
| Modem | Phone line / POTS | Converts signals for analog line transport |
| CSU/DSU | Leased digital line / T1 | Interfaces DTE to synchronous WAN service |

> Use the circuit type to choose the device: phone line points to a modem, while a leased digital WAN circuit points to a CSU/DSU.

**How the ISP handoff fits together**

When a third-party provider delivers WAN service, the handoff occurs at the demarc. From there, the customer side equipment takes over. In a home, this may be a box on the side of the house with RJ-11 or RJ-45 connectivity. In a business, the handoff may land in a data center or another central network location.

This is the point where you separate provider responsibility from your own network responsibility.

- Provider equipment ends at the handoff.
- Customer equipment begins on your side of the demarc.
- The physical interface can vary by site and service type.

**Practical exam cues**

If a question mentions a leased line, T1, fractional T1, or synchronous WAN service, think **CSU/DSU**. If it mentions a regular phone line, think **modem**. If the question asks where the provider connection ends and your network begins, think **demarcation point**.

- Leased digital circuit: CSU/DSU
- Phone line: modem
- Provider-to-customer boundary: demarc

**Quick recall**

- **Demarcation point**: provider handoff boundary
- **Modem**: phone line signal conversion
- **CSU/DSU**: leased digital WAN termination and clocking
