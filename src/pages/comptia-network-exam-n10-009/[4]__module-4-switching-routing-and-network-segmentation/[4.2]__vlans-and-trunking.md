## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**VLAN fundamentals**

A <abbr title="Virtual Local Area Network">VLAN</abbr> lets you create separate broadcast domains on the same physical switching infrastructure. This helps you segment a network for organization, security, and traffic control without needing a separate switch for every group.

- A VLAN keeps devices in the same logical network even when they share physical hardware.
- Broadcast traffic stays within the VLAN unless routing moves it elsewhere.
- VLANs are commonly used to separate user groups or services on the same switch.

When you place devices on different VLANs, they do not communicate directly at Layer 2 without a routing function. That separation is the main reason VLANs are useful for network design.

**Why trunking is needed**

A trunk link carries traffic for multiple VLANs over one physical connection. This becomes important when you need to extend VLANs between switches or connect devices that must see more than one VLAN.

- A trunk reduces the number of separate physical links you need.
- It helps preserve VLAN separation while sharing the same cable.
- It is commonly used between switches and in other multi-VLAN connections.

Without trunking, each VLAN would need its own dedicated link between devices that must transport that VLAN’s traffic. Trunking makes multi-switch VLAN designs practical and efficient.

**Trunk ports and access ports**

A **trunk port** carries traffic from multiple VLANs and uses tagging so the receiving device can identify which VLAN each frame belongs to. An **access port** belongs to a single VLAN and does not use VLAN tags for normal user traffic.

| Port type | VLAN handling | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| Trunk port | Multiple VLANs with tags | Switch-to-switch links, multi-VLAN uplinks |
| Access port | One VLAN, untagged traffic | End devices such as PCs and printers |

> Trunking depends on both ends agreeing on how VLAN information is carried. If the link is not configured consistently, traffic may not reach the intended VLAN.

**802.1Q tagging**

<abbr title="Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers">IEEE</abbr> 802.1Q is the common VLAN tagging method referenced in the evidence. It adds VLAN identification to frames on a trunk so the receiving switch can place the traffic into the correct VLAN.

- Tagging is what allows multiple VLANs to share one link.
- Untagged traffic is typically associated with a single access VLAN.
- The tag is used only on the trunked portion of the network, not for ordinary access-port traffic.

**Broadcast domains and switch scaling**

VLANs help you control broadcast domains across one or more switches. When switches are interconnected, a broadcast domain can span multiple switches if the VLAN is extended across them.

- A broadcast frame is forwarded within its broadcast domain.
- Interconnected switches can carry the same VLAN across multiple devices.
- Trunk links make that extension possible without flattening the network into one large segment.

This is why VLANs and trunking are often discussed together: VLANs define the logical separation, and trunks transport that separation across the physical network.

**Practical design benefits**

Trunking improves network efficiency by consolidating multiple VLANs onto a single physical link. That reduces cabling needs and helps you manage larger networks more cleanly.

- You can support multiple logical networks without adding many extra cables.
- You can keep traffic separated while still using shared infrastructure.
- You can scale switch-based networks more effectively.

**Common exam focus**

For Network+ purposes, focus on the relationship between VLANs, broadcast domains, and trunk links. You should be able to distinguish when a port carries one VLAN versus many VLANs and understand why tagging is required on trunks.

- VLANs create logical separation.
- Trunks carry multiple VLANs.
- Access ports carry one VLAN.
- 802.1Q is the standard tagging method referenced for trunking.
