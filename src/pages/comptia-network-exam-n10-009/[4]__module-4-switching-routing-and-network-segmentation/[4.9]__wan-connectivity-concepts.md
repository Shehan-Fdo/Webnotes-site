## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**WAN connectivity overview**

A <abbr title="Wide Area Network">WAN</abbr> connects geographically distant networks, such as branch offices, data centers, and remote users. The main goal is to move traffic reliably across long distances while balancing performance, cost, and flexibility.

WAN design choices often depend on the application mix and the organization’s cloud strategy. Traditional private circuits can provide predictable paths, while internet-based options can improve agility and reduce cost.

- **Leased lines** provide dedicated point-to-point connectivity.
- **MPLS** and similar provider-managed services support private WAN transport.
- **VPNs** create secure, encrypted tunnels across the internet.
- **Transmission media** can include copper, fiber, wireless, and satellite.

**MPLS and traditional WAN transport**

<abbr title="Multiprotocol Label Switching">MPLS</abbr> is a transport technology used in traditional networking to move packets efficiently along predetermined paths. It has been used for decades and is commonly associated with private circuits between sites.

MPLS is known for reliability and performance because traffic follows provider-controlled paths. It is often deployed in hub-and-spoke designs where branch traffic reaches a central data center for processing and redistribution.

- MPLS uses physical circuits installed at sites that need connectivity.
- It is commonly leased from a service provider.
- It fits environments that value predictable routing and stable performance.
- It is less flexible than internet-based approaches when organizations need rapid change.

> MPLS is a practical choice when you want consistent paths and private connectivity, but it may be less adaptable for cloud-heavy environments.

**SD-WAN compared with MPLS**

<abbr title="Software-Defined Wide Area Network">SD-WAN</abbr> is a more flexible WAN approach that helps organizations support multicloud use, improve user experience, and reduce bandwidth costs. It is often adopted as organizations move away from strict dependence on MPLS.

SD-WAN builds on existing WAN ideas but adds centralized control and more agility. It is commonly positioned as a step toward future <abbr title="Secure Access Service Edge">SASE</abbr> architecture.

| Option | Typical strength | Typical tradeoff |
|---|---|---|
| MPLS | Predictable paths and reliable performance | Less flexible and often more costly |
| SD-WAN | Agility, scalability, and cloud alignment | Depends on design and underlying links |
| Internet | Low cost and broad availability | Less predictable than private transport |

- **SD-WAN** supports flexible routing across multiple links.
- **Centralized management** simplifies policy control.
- **Failback capabilities** can improve resilience after a link issue.
- **Built-in security functions** may be managed from a central platform.

**Internet-based WAN connectivity**

Broadband internet is often used as a WAN transport option when cost and availability matter more than private-circuit predictability. It can be combined with SD-WAN or VPN technologies to improve security and traffic handling.

Internet-based connectivity is useful for cloud access and distributed work, but it usually does not provide the same deterministic behavior as MPLS. For that reason, organizations often use it as part of a hybrid WAN design rather than as the only transport.

- Internet links are widely available.
- They are usually less expensive than private circuits.
- They are well suited to cloud and SaaS access.
- They may require overlay security and traffic optimization.

**VPNs and secure remote connectivity**

A <abbr title="Virtual Private Network">VPN</abbr> creates an encrypted connection across the internet. It is a common way to protect traffic between sites or for remote access when users connect from untrusted networks.

VPNs are especially useful when you need secure communication without deploying dedicated private circuits everywhere. They do not replace all WAN transport options, but they often complement them.

- **Encryption** protects data in transit.
- **Tunneling** carries traffic over public infrastructure.
- **Remote access** and site-to-site designs are both common use cases.
- **Security** depends on correct configuration and strong authentication.

**WAN transmission media**

WAN links can use different physical or wireless media depending on location and service availability. The medium affects speed, latency, cost, and deployment options.

Satellite is useful in locations that are difficult to reach with terrestrial services, but it introduces higher latency because traffic travels to space and back. Fiber and copper are common terrestrial choices, while wireless can help where cabling is impractical.

- **Fiber** supports high throughput and is common for modern WAN links.
- **Copper** may still be used where available and cost-effective.
- **Wireless** can provide flexibility for remote or temporary sites.
- **Satellite** helps reach hard-to-wire locations but increases delay.

**Common WAN design considerations**

When you choose a WAN solution, you balance performance, cost, security, and operational complexity. No single option fits every site or workload.

A practical design often mixes multiple transports. For example, you might keep MPLS for predictable branch connectivity while adding internet links for cloud access and SD-WAN for path selection and policy control.

- Match the transport to the application requirement.
- Consider latency-sensitive traffic separately from bulk traffic.
- Use secure overlays when traffic crosses public networks.
- Plan for resilience with alternate paths or failover.

**Key takeaways**

WAN connectivity is about linking distant networks in a way that supports business needs. MPLS emphasizes predictable private transport, SD-WAN emphasizes flexibility and centralized control, and internet-based options emphasize cost and availability.

For exam purposes, focus on how each option affects reliability, latency, security, and scalability.
