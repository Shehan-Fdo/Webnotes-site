## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Service-level terms and how they fit together**

Service-level language helps you connect business expectations to measurable technical performance. An <abbr title="Service Level Agreement">SLA</abbr> is the commitment made to a customer, while an <abbr title="Service Level Objective">SLO</abbr> is the internal target used to help meet that commitment. An <abbr title="Service Level Indicator">SLI</abbr> is the measurement that shows whether the service is performing as expected.

In practical terms, you use SLIs to observe the system, SLOs to set goals, and SLAs to define the external promise. This structure helps you focus on user experience instead of only infrastructure health.

- **SLA**: the formal agreement with the customer.
- **SLO**: the internal performance target.
- **SLI**: the metric you measure to track service behavior.
- **Reliability**: the overall ability to meet expected service levels over time.

> SLOs are useful because they create a safety buffer between normal operations and an SLA breach.

**Monitoring and performance targets**

Modern monitoring ties service goals to measurable data. Common service measurements include availability, latency, errors, and successful transactions. These metrics help you see whether the service is meeting the target from the user’s perspective.

A practical SLO example is a web application target such as videos starting in less than 2 seconds. The exact metric matters less than the idea: you define a measurable outcome, track it consistently, and use it to guide operational decisions.

- Track metrics that reflect user experience.
- Use dashboards and alerts to show progress toward targets.
- Watch trends so you can correct issues before they affect customers.
- Keep the target realistic enough to prevent constant false alarms.

**Capacity planning basics**

Capacity planning helps you make sure the network can handle current and future demand. It uses performance metrics such as bandwidth, traffic, and latency to estimate how much data the network can support and where limits may appear.

Good capacity planning is both reactive and predictive. You assess current capacity, forecast future traffic, and prepare for growth before performance degrades. This approach supports reliability and helps avoid service problems caused by underprovisioning.

- **Bandwidth**: how much data the network can carry.
- **Traffic**: the amount of data moving across the network.
- **Latency**: the delay experienced by traffic.
- **Forecasting**: estimating future demand from current patterns.

**Best practices for capacity and performance**

Capacity planning works best when you treat it as an ongoing process, not a one-time upgrade project. You start by measuring current conditions, then compare them with expected demand and business priorities.

1. Assess current network capacity.
2. Forecast future traffic and requirements.
3. Design for scalability so growth does not create immediate bottlenecks.
4. Optimize performance and reliability as demand changes.
5. Keep documentation current so planning decisions remain traceable.

These steps help you align technical resources with business needs. They also support better resource allocation by showing where performance issues are likely to affect service quality first.

**Why these topics matter together**

SLA, SLO, and capacity planning are connected by the same goal: delivering service that meets expectations. SLAs define the promise, SLOs define the internal target, and capacity planning helps you build and maintain the network so those targets remain achievable.

When you monitor service metrics and plan capacity together, you reduce the chance of missed targets, improve customer satisfaction, and catch problems before they become visible outages.
