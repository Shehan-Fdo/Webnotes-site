## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Availability and resilience overview**

Availability focuses on keeping services accessible, while resilience focuses on recovering quickly when something goes wrong. In practice, both support business continuity by reducing downtime, limiting disruption, and helping critical systems stay usable during failures or maintenance.

- **Fault tolerance** keeps a system functioning even when part of it fails.
- **High availability** reduces service interruption by using failover to alternate resources.
- **Redundancy** adds extra components so a single failure does not stop service.
- **Disaster recovery** defines how you restore services after a major outage or disaster.

Fault tolerance is the stronger design goal when you want the system to continue operating through component failures such as hardware faults, software bugs, or network outages. It often relies on mirrored storage, backup power, or other duplicate resources. High availability is more about minimizing interruption than eliminating it entirely, and it commonly addresses planned maintenance and upgrades by shifting workloads automatically.

**Fault tolerance vs. high availability**

These terms are related, but they are not identical. Fault tolerance aims for continuous operation through failures, while high availability aims for fast recovery and minimal downtime. Fault-tolerant designs usually add more complexity and cost because they require extra components and procedures.

| Concept | Primary goal | Typical approach |
|---|---|---|
| Fault tolerance | Keep operating through a failure | Mirroring, backup power, duplicate components |
| High availability | Minimize interruption | Failover to alternate resources, workload switching |

> Fault tolerance does not mean “no failures”; it means the service keeps running despite them.

**Common resilience technologies**

Resilience often depends on multiple layers of protection. You may use one technology to keep power stable, another to keep storage available, and another to keep workloads reachable if a device fails.

- **RAID** can provide storage redundancy for individual devices.
- **Load balancing** spreads traffic across multiple systems to support availability.
- **NIC teaming** combines network interfaces for redundancy and continuity.
- **Multipathing** provides alternate paths to storage or network resources.
- **Backup power** helps systems stay online during electrical issues.

These controls work best when they support a broader continuity plan rather than standing alone. The goal is not only to survive a failure, but also to keep the business operating while the issue is being fixed.

**Facilities and infrastructure support**

Availability is not only a server or network design issue. Physical infrastructure also affects whether services stay online during disruptions. Power, cooling, and fire protection all contribute to resilience.

- **Uninterruptible power supply (UPS)** provides short-term power during outages.
- **Power distribution units (PDUs)** help distribute electrical power to equipment.
- **Generators** extend runtime during longer power failures.
- **HVAC** (heating, ventilation, and air conditioning) helps maintain safe operating temperatures.
- **Fire suppression** reduces damage from fire-related incidents.

If these systems fail, even well-designed network redundancy may not be enough to preserve service. You should treat facility support as part of the availability strategy, not as an afterthought.

**Recovery sites and backup states**

Disaster recovery planning uses site options and restore states to match recovery needs and budget. The more quickly you need to resume service, the more prepared and costly the solution usually becomes.

- **Cold site**: basic space and utilities, but little or no ready-to-run equipment.
- **Warm site**: partially equipped and faster to bring online.
- **Hot site**: highly ready environment designed for rapid recovery.

You also need backup and restore procedures for network devices and configurations so you can return systems to a known good state after an outage. A disaster recovery plan (DRP) is the organization-wide document that defines how you respond to and recover from a significant disruption.

**How to think about availability design**

1. Identify the services that must stay online.
2. Determine which failures are most likely to interrupt them.
3. Add redundancy where a single point of failure would cause downtime.
4. Choose failover or recovery methods that match the service’s importance.
5. Test the plan so you know recovery steps work before an outage occurs.

Availability and resilience are practical design goals, not just theory. The best solution depends on how much downtime the organization can tolerate, how quickly it must recover, and how much complexity it can support.
