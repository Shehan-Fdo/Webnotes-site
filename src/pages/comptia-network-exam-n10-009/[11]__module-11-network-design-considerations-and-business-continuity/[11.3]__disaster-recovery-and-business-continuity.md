## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Disaster recovery and business continuity overview**

<abbr title="Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery">BCDR</abbr> refers to the set of processes that help an organization return to normal operations after a disaster. Business continuity and disaster recovery are closely related, but they focus on slightly different goals: continuity keeps critical services running, while recovery restores systems after disruption.

A **disaster recovery plan** (<abbr title="Disaster Recovery Plan">DRP</abbr>) is the documented contingency plan for responding to unexpected events. It should cover the procedures, technologies, and responsibilities needed to recover from outages, natural disasters, human-caused incidents, and system failures.

- **Business continuity** focuses on keeping essential operations available during disruption.
- **Disaster recovery** focuses on restoring IT services and data after an incident.
- **BCDR** combines both approaches into a coordinated response strategy.
- A DRP should be practical, repeatable, and aligned with business priorities.

**Core planning concepts**

Effective recovery planning starts with identifying what must be protected and how quickly it must return. Organizations often use recovery metrics to measure outage impact and guide planning decisions. These metrics help you compare recovery options and set realistic expectations for downtime and data loss.

- **RTO** (<abbr title="Recovery Time Objective">RTO</abbr>) is the target time to restore a service after disruption.
- **RPO** (<abbr title="Recovery Point Objective">RPO</abbr>) is the maximum acceptable amount of data loss measured in time.
- **MTTR** (<abbr title="Mean Time to Repair">MTTR</abbr>) helps describe how long repair and restoration typically take.
- **MTBF** (<abbr title="Mean Time Between Failures">MTBF</abbr>) and **MTTF** (<abbr title="Mean Time To Failure">MTTF</abbr>) help evaluate reliability and failure patterns.

> Use these measurements to match recovery design to business needs instead of relying on a one-size-fits-all plan.

**Recovery technologies and strategies**

A DRP can include multiple recovery methods depending on the service being protected. Common approaches include backups, offsite data replication, cloud-based alternatives, and remote recovery sites. These options reduce the impact of downtime and help preserve access to critical systems and data.

- **Backups** provide restorable copies of data after loss or corruption.
- **Offsite replication** keeps data available outside the primary location.
- **Cloud recovery** can recreate services in a hosted environment.
- **Remote sites** provide an alternate location for continuing operations.

**High availability and redundancy**

Disaster recovery planning often overlaps with high availability. High availability reduces service interruption by using redundant components and alternate paths so a failure does not stop operations. Network and infrastructure resilience depend on both design and maintenance.

- **Load balancing** distributes traffic across multiple resources.
- **Multipathing** provides more than one path for data or network traffic.
- **NIC teaming** (<abbr title="Network Interface Card">NIC</abbr> teaming) combines multiple network adapters for redundancy or performance.
- **Backup and restore states** should be documented for network devices and configurations.

**Facilities and infrastructure support**

Recovery is not only about data and servers. Physical infrastructure also affects whether services stay online during an emergency. Power, cooling, and fire protection are part of the continuity plan because they support the systems that keep the network running.

- **Uninterruptible power supply** (<abbr title="Uninterruptible Power Supply">UPS</abbr>) helps bridge short power interruptions.
- **Power distribution units** (<abbr title="Power Distribution Unit">PDU</abbr>) distribute electrical power to equipment.
- **Generators** provide longer-term backup power.
- **HVAC** (<abbr title="Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning">HVAC</abbr>) supports safe operating temperatures.
- **Fire suppression** helps protect equipment and facilities from damage.

**Recovery site options**

Recovery sites differ in readiness, cost, and how quickly they can support operations. You choose the site type based on how much downtime the organization can tolerate and how much preparation it can afford.

| Site type | Readiness | Typical use |
|---|---:|---|
| Cold site | Low | Space and basic utilities are available, but equipment and data must be brought in |
| Warm site | Medium | Some systems and data are already in place, so recovery is faster |
| Hot site | High | A fully prepared alternate site supports rapid failover |

**Testing and maintenance**

A recovery plan is only useful if it works when needed. Testing validates procedures, exposes gaps, and confirms that staff can execute the plan under pressure. Ongoing maintenance keeps the plan aligned with current systems, dependencies, and business requirements.

1. Review the DRP for current systems, contacts, and dependencies.
2. Test recovery procedures to confirm that backups, failover, and restoration work as expected.
3. Update the plan after changes to infrastructure, applications, or business priorities.
4. Recheck recovery metrics so the plan still matches acceptable downtime and data loss targets.

**Practical exam focus**

For Network+ scenarios, expect questions that connect recovery goals to the right technology or site choice. If the goal is minimal interruption, think about redundancy and high availability. If the goal is restoring service after a major outage, think about backups, replication, alternate sites, and documented procedures.

- Match the solution to the required recovery speed.
- Distinguish between keeping services available and restoring them after failure.
- Remember that physical infrastructure can be part of the recovery design.
- Treat testing as a required part of continuity planning, not an optional extra.
