## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Performance metrics overview**

Network performance is the measure of service quality as perceived by the user. In practice, you evaluate both the *quality* of the connection and the *quantity* of traffic it can carry. These metrics are related, so a change in one often affects the others.

- **Bandwidth** is the amount of data a network can transmit in a fixed time.
- **Throughput** is the actual amount of data delivered over the network.
- **Latency** is the delay in data transmission.
- **Jitter** is the variation in latency over time.
- **Packet loss** occurs when data packets fail to reach their destination.

A useful exam habit is to separate the metric you measure from the symptom you observe. For example, a user may report slow application response, but the underlying issue could be limited bandwidth, high latency, jitter, or packet loss.

**Bandwidth, throughput, and latency**

These three terms are often confused because they all describe network performance, but they are not interchangeable. Bandwidth describes capacity, throughput describes what you actually get, and latency describes delay.

| Metric | What it describes | Practical meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Bandwidth | Capacity of the link | How much data can move in a given time |
| Throughput | Actual delivered data | How much data is successfully transferred |
| Latency | Transmission delay | How long data takes to travel |

Bandwidth can be a limiting factor when a network must carry more traffic than it can handle efficiently. Throughput is usually lower than theoretical capacity because of protocol overhead, congestion, and other real-world conditions. Latency matters when users need fast response times, especially for interactive services.

**Jitter and packet loss**

Jitter and packet loss are especially important when traffic is sensitive to timing or delivery consistency. Jitter is the change in delay from packet to packet, while packet loss means some packets never arrive.

- **Jitter** can disrupt voice and video because timing consistency matters.
- **Packet loss** can cause retransmissions, gaps in audio or video, and slower application behavior.
- Both metrics can indicate congestion or unstable network conditions.
- Monitoring them together gives a clearer picture than looking at one metric alone.

> If latency is high but stable, the experience may still be usable for some applications. If latency varies widely or packets are lost, real-time traffic usually suffers more quickly.

**Availability and service continuity**

Availability describes whether a service is reachable and usable when needed. In troubleshooting, availability is not just about whether a device is powered on; it also includes whether the network path and dependent services are functioning well enough to support access.

- A service can be technically online but still feel unavailable if performance is too poor.
- Intermittent issues are harder to diagnose than complete outages because they may only appear under specific conditions.
- Good documentation, monitoring data, and knowledge of the network design help you isolate availability problems faster.

**Troubleshooting approach**

Performance troubleshooting works best when you use a systematic process. Because networks are distributed and complex, you need to inspect the right systems, collect diagnostic data, and understand how the network is designed and operated.

1. Identify the symptom and determine whether it affects LAN or WAN traffic.
2. Check the relevant metric or metrics, such as latency, throughput, jitter, or packet loss.
3. Compare the observed behavior with expected service quality.
4. Use monitoring and diagnostic tools to gather evidence before changing the network.
5. Verify whether the issue is intermittent, location-specific, or tied to a particular application.

A practical approach is to start with the user impact and then narrow the scope. That helps you avoid guessing and keeps your troubleshooting aligned with measurable performance data.

**Key exam takeaways**

- **Bandwidth** is capacity; **throughput** is delivered traffic.
- **Latency** is delay; **jitter** is variation in delay.
- **Packet loss** means packets do not arrive.
- Performance metrics are interdependent, so one problem can affect several symptoms.
- **Availability** includes both reachability and usable service quality.
- Systematic troubleshooting depends on monitoring, documentation, and understanding the network design.
