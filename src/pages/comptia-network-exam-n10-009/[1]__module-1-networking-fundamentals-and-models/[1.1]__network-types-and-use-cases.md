## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Network type basics**

A network is a set of connected devices that share information or resources. For exam purposes, the key idea is that network types are often grouped by the area they cover and the way devices communicate.

- **PAN**: Personal Area Network; short-range connections between personal devices.
- **LAN**: Local Area Network; devices connected within a limited area such as a home, office, or campus segment.
- **MAN**: Metropolitan Area Network; spans a city or other larger local region.
- **WAN**: Wide Area Network; connects networks across large geographic distances.
- **CAN**: Campus Area Network; links multiple LANs across a campus or similar organization.

A useful way to remember these is to move from smallest to largest coverage area. The smaller the scope, the more likely the network is to support nearby devices and local resource sharing. The larger the scope, the more likely it is to connect separate sites or broader organizational locations.

**Personal Area Networks**

A <abbr title="Personal Area Network">PAN</abbr> is a short-range network for a few nearby devices. The evidence highlights smartphones, tablets, and computers as typical examples, with Bluetooth as a common wireless technology.

- Best for device-to-device sharing in close proximity.
- Usually covers less than 10 meters.
- Often uses wireless links to avoid cables.
- Fits personal accessories and mobile device pairing.

PANs are useful when you want simple, low-distance communication between devices you carry or keep nearby. They are not designed for broad coverage or large user groups.

**Local Area Networks**

A <abbr title="Local Area Network">LAN</abbr> is a network within a defined area. The evidence describes homes, businesses, and even college campuses as examples of LAN coverage.

- Supports resource sharing inside a limited location.
- Common in homes, offices, and campus buildings.
- Can be wired, wireless, or a mix of both.
- Serves as the foundation for many everyday network deployments.

LANs are the most familiar network type in many environments because they connect devices that are physically close and usually under the same administrative control.

**Metropolitan and wide area networks**

A <abbr title="Metropolitan Area Network">MAN</abbr> covers a city-scale area, while a <abbr title="Wide Area Network">WAN</abbr> spans much larger distances. These types are useful when you need to connect separate LANs across broader regions.

- **MAN**: Useful for city-wide connectivity or linking sites within a metropolitan region.
- **WAN**: Useful for connecting branch offices, remote sites, or geographically separated networks.
- Both are designed for larger coverage than a LAN.
- Both support communication across distances that exceed a single local site.

When you compare them, think of MAN as regional and WAN as long-distance. That distinction helps you choose the right term on exam questions that describe the physical reach of the network.

**Campus Area Networks**

A <abbr title="Campus Area Network">CAN</abbr> connects multiple LANs across a campus or similar organization. This is a practical fit for schools, corporate campuses, or other environments with several buildings under one administration.

- Links multiple LANs together.
- Works well when one organization controls several nearby sites.
- Sits between a LAN and a larger regional network in scope.
- Helps organize connectivity across a shared physical campus.

CAN is especially useful when the network must stay local to one organization but extend beyond a single building.

**Wireless network types**

Network types also include wireless connection styles. The evidence specifically mentions mesh, ad hoc, point-to-point, and infrastructure mode.

- **Mesh**: Multiple access points communicate with each other, extending coverage across a relatively close area.
- **Ad hoc**: Devices connect directly to each other without a central access point.
- **Point-to-point**: Two devices or sites communicate directly over a dedicated link.
- **Infrastructure mode**: Devices connect through an access point that acts as the central wireless connection point.

These terms describe how wireless devices are arranged and how traffic moves. They are not the same as PAN, LAN, MAN, or WAN, which describe coverage and scope.

**How to choose the right network type**

Use the network’s size, distance, and purpose to identify the best fit.

1. Determine the physical scope of the connection.
2. Decide whether the network is for personal devices, a local site, a campus, a city, or a wide geographic area.
3. Match the description to the appropriate network type.
4. For wireless scenarios, identify whether the design is mesh, ad hoc, point-to-point, or infrastructure.

A practical exam approach is to look for clues in the scenario. Device proximity suggests PAN. A single building or local site suggests LAN. Multiple buildings on one campus suggest CAN. City-scale coverage suggests MAN. Long-distance interconnection suggests WAN.

**Quick comparison**

| Type | Typical scope | Common use case |
|---|---:|---|
| PAN | Very short range | Personal device pairing and nearby sharing |
| LAN | Local site | Home, office, or campus building networking |
| CAN | Multiple LANs on one campus | Organization-wide campus connectivity |
| MAN | City-wide | Regional connectivity across a metropolitan area |
| WAN | Large geographic area | Connecting distant sites or networks |

**Exam focus**

The exam is likely to test your ability to match a scenario to the correct network type. Focus on the relationship between distance, administrative scope, and the devices involved.

- **Scope** matters more than brand or product.
- **Wireless topology** terms describe connection style, not geographic size.
- **Resource sharing** is a common reason networks exist at any scale.
- **Peer-to-peer** and **client-server** are communication models, so do not confuse them with area-based network types.

> If a question describes how far devices are apart, use that clue first. If it describes how devices connect wirelessly, look for mesh, ad hoc, point-to-point, or infrastructure mode.
