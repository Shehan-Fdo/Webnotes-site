## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Network media overview**

Network cabling and wireless links are the physical or wireless channels that carry data between devices. The media you choose affects distance, speed, cost, installation effort, and susceptibility to interference. In practice, you select media based on the network’s size, topology, protocol needs, and performance goals.

- **Copper**, fiber, and wireless are the three basic media categories.
- Media choice depends on required data rate, distance, and cost.
- Some environments use one medium throughout; others mix several types.

**Copper media**

Copper cabling uses electrical signals to send and receive data. It is widely used because it is economical and easy to install, but it has lower bandwidth than fiber and is more affected by signal degradation over distance.

- **Twisted pair** is the most common copper media in LANs.
- Copper is limited by distance and signal interference.
- Common interference sources include electromagnetic interference (EMI), radio frequency interference (RFI), and crosstalk.
- Shielding can reduce noise and improve signal quality.

<dl>
<dt>UTP</dt>
<dd>Unshielded twisted pair; common and basic, with no shielding.</dd>
<dt>STP</dt>
<dd>Shielded twisted pair; adds shielding to help reduce interference.</dd>
</dl>

> Copper is practical for many local connections, but it is not the best choice when you need long distance, high bandwidth, or strong resistance to interference.

**Fiber optic media**

Fiber optic cable sends data as light pulses through thin strands of glass or plastic. It supports very high bandwidth and long-distance transmission, and it is less vulnerable to electrical interference than copper.

- **Fiber** is often described as faster and more reliable than copper.
- It carries large amounts of information over long distances.
- It is a strong choice when future growth and performance matter.
- Fiber deployment costs have narrowed over time, making it more competitive.

**Wireless media**

Wireless media carries data without a physical cable. It is useful when mobility, convenience, or reduced cabling is important, but performance depends heavily on the environment and design.

- **Wireless** avoids cable runs and supports flexible placement.
- It is often chosen when physical installation is difficult.
- It can be more exposed to environmental and signal-related limitations than wired media.

**Key comparison factors**

When you compare media types, focus on the tradeoffs that matter most to the network design. No single medium is best in every situation.

| Factor | Copper | Fiber | Wireless |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cost | Usually lower | Historically higher, now more competitive | Varies by deployment |
| Bandwidth | Lower than fiber | Very high | Depends on conditions |
| Distance | Limited | Strong for long distances | Depends on coverage |
| Interference | More susceptible | Resistant to electrical interference | Can be affected by environment |
| Installation | Familiar and simple | More specialized | Flexible, no cable required |

**Practical selection guidance**

Use the network’s requirements to narrow the choice. If you need a low-cost, easy-to-install medium for short runs, copper is often suitable. If you need high throughput, long distance, or better resistance to interference, fiber is usually the stronger option. If mobility or minimal cabling is the priority, wireless may fit best.

1. Identify the required distance.
2. Determine the needed bandwidth.
3. Check whether EMI, RFI, or crosstalk is a concern.
4. Compare installation cost and complexity.
5. Choose the medium that best matches the environment and performance target.

**Exam focus points**

- **Transmission medium** is the channel that carries data signals between devices.
- Copper uses electrical impulses.
- Fiber uses light pulses.
- Wireless uses no physical cable.
- Media selection is a design decision, not just a hardware preference.

**Common troubleshooting mindset**

If a link performs poorly, check whether the medium matches the use case. Short copper runs can be fine for many office connections, but they may struggle in noisy environments. Fiber is a better fit when you need speed and distance. Wireless is convenient, but you should expect more variability than with a wired link.
