## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**WAN technology overview**

A wide area network (WAN) connects devices and sites beyond the geographic scope of a local area network (LAN). In enterprise environments, WAN design focuses on linking branch offices, data centers, cloud services, and remote users across long distances.

WAN choices are usually driven by a balance of performance, cost, flexibility, and security. Modern organizations often compare private carrier services such as MPLS with software-driven approaches like SD-WAN and with public broadband internet access.

- **WAN** links locations across broad geographic areas.
- **Branch connectivity** often depends on the business need for reliability and reach.
- **Cloud adoption** increases the need for flexible WAN design.

**MPLS fundamentals**

Multiprotocol Label Switching (MPLS) is a transport method used in traditional networking to move packets efficiently along predetermined paths. It typically requires physical circuits at the sites that need to communicate and is delivered as a contracted carrier service.

MPLS is commonly associated with reliability and predictable performance. It is also described as expensive, slower to deploy, and less flexible than newer WAN approaches. Some sources note that it does not directly support encryption, so security requirements may need to be addressed separately.

- **MPLS** uses predetermined paths for traffic.
- It relies on carrier-provided circuits.
- It is often chosen for predictable performance.
- It can be costly and time-consuming to set up.

> MPLS remains useful where organizations value stable, private connectivity, but it can be less practical for highly cloud-centric or rapidly changing environments.

**SD-WAN characteristics**

Software-defined wide area network (SD-WAN) is a virtual overlay that is decoupled from the underlying physical transport. It can use multiple connection types, including broadband internet, cellular, and satellite links, and it uses routing and overlay tunnels to connect sites securely.

SD-WAN improves application performance by dynamically selecting the best available path and by prioritizing traffic according to business policy. It commonly uses encrypted VPN tunnels and quality of service (QoS) controls to support secure, policy-based traffic handling.

- **SD-WAN** is a virtual overlay.
- It can use multiple transport types.
- It supports dynamic path selection.
- It can improve flexibility and cost efficiency.
- It is often used to support cloud and multicloud access.

**Broadband internet and cellular WAN options**

Broadband internet is a common WAN transport option because it is widely available and cost-effective. It is often used as part of an SD-WAN design or as a direct connectivity option where private circuits are not required.

Cellular WAN connectivity, including 5G, can help reach sites that are difficult to serve with wired connections such as cable, DSL, or fiber. This makes cellular links useful for remote locations, temporary sites, and environments where physical cabling is impractical.

- **Broadband** offers broad availability.
- **Cellular WAN** extends connectivity where wired service is limited.
- **5G** can provide a practical alternative for distributed sites.
- Public internet-based options usually trade some predictability for flexibility.

**Comparing MPLS, SD-WAN, and internet connectivity**

Each WAN approach has different strengths, so the best choice depends on the organization’s priorities. MPLS emphasizes private, predictable transport. SD-WAN emphasizes flexibility, policy control, and efficient use of multiple links. Internet-based connectivity emphasizes accessibility and lower cost.

| Technology | Primary strength | Common tradeoff |
|---|---|---|
| MPLS | Predictable performance and private transport | Higher cost and slower deployment |
| SD-WAN | Flexibility, dynamic routing, and cloud-friendly design | Depends on underlying transport quality |
| Broadband internet | Low cost and wide availability | Less inherent predictability than private circuits |
| Cellular WAN | Reach for hard-to-wire locations | Variable performance compared with wired links |

A hybrid approach is common in practice. For example, an organization may keep MPLS for critical traffic while adding SD-WAN over broadband or cellular links to improve agility and support cloud access.

**How to think about WAN selection**

When you evaluate WAN technologies, start with the business requirement rather than the product name. A branch office that needs stable access to a corporate data center may benefit from MPLS, while a distributed organization with many cloud applications may prefer SD-WAN over multiple internet links.

1. Identify the sites and applications that need connectivity.
2. Decide whether predictability, flexibility, or cost matters most.
3. Match the transport to the requirement.
4. Consider a hybrid design if one option does not meet all needs.

**Exam focus points**

For Network+ study, focus on the practical differences between private and public WAN transport, and on how SD-WAN changes the way traffic is routed and managed.

- **MPLS** is carrier-managed and path-predictable.
- **SD-WAN** is policy-driven and transport-agnostic.
- **Broadband and cellular** expand reach and reduce dependence on dedicated circuits.
- Cloud adoption often pushes organizations toward more flexible WAN designs.
