## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Cisco SD-WAN overview**

Cisco <abbr title="Software-Defined Wide Area Network">SD-WAN</abbr> is a software-defined approach to WAN design that helps you manage traffic more intelligently across the overlay fabric. In the Network+ context, the key idea is that SD-WAN can optimize cloud-based application traffic and move traditional networking functions into a more virtualized environment.

Cisco describes its Catalyst SD-WAN training as focused on foundational concepts, business drivers, and an architectural vision for agile, secure, and cloud-ready WAN solutions. That framing is useful for exam prep because it connects the technology to practical enterprise goals rather than isolated features.

- SD-WAN separates network behavior into software-managed functions.
- It supports cloud-ready WAN designs.
- It is used to improve application traffic handling across the WAN.

**Core planes and virtualized networking**

A useful way to think about SD-WAN is through the traditional networking planes. The control plane handles decision-making, the data plane forwards traffic, and the management plane provides administration and visibility. SD-WAN takes those functions and implements them in software so they can be deployed more flexibly.

This model matters because it explains why SD-WAN is more adaptable than a purely hardware-centric WAN. You are not just moving packets; you are also centralizing policy and control logic in a way that can be applied across many sites.

- **Data plane**: forwards user traffic.
- **Control plane**: makes routing and path decisions.
- **Management plane**: supports configuration and oversight.

**Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN architecture**

Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN uses controllers and edge devices to build the overlay network. Cisco’s documentation highlights policy overview, centralized policy, localized policy, and traffic-flow features as part of the platform’s policy-driven design.

The practical takeaway is that Cisco SD-WAN is not only about connectivity. It is also about how routes, tunnels, and service behavior are shaped by policy across the fabric.

- The overlay fabric uses controller-driven coordination.
- Policies influence how traffic and routes are handled.
- The platform supports both centralized and localized policy approaches.

**Centralized control policy**

A centralized control policy manipulates route and <abbr title="Transport Locator">TLOC</abbr> information exchanged between controllers and edge devices in the Cisco SD-WAN overlay fabric. It can influence the overlay topology of <abbr title="Internet Protocol Security">IPsec</abbr> tunnels and the routing paths through the fabric.

Two common centralized control policy types are described in the evidence:

- **Topology policy**
  - Controls route information such as <abbr title="Overlay Management Protocol">OMP</abbr>, TLOC, and service routes.
  - Is typically used to limit the number of overlay tunnels between sites.
  - Helps shape the overlay topology.
- **VPN membership policy**
  - Controls distribution of routing information for specific VPNs to selected sites.
  - Can support guest-network designs where Internet access is allowed but site-to-site communication is restricted.

> If no centralized control policy is configured, the default behavior is to let sites exchange route, TLOC, and service information through the control plane without that extra policy filtering layer.

**Localized policy and default behavior**

Localized policy is part of the broader Cisco SD-WAN policy model, but the evidence emphasizes the default behavior when no centralized control policy is present. In that case, edge routers send learned prefixes, TLOC routes, and service routes to the controllers, which then build the topology map and redistribute routes within the appropriate VPN.

This is important for troubleshooting and exam questions because it shows what the system does before you add policy. You should expect the control plane to exchange information broadly unless policy narrows that behavior.

- Edge routers advertise local prefixes to the controller.
- Controllers learn TLOC and service routes.
- The controller builds a topology map from the received information.
- Routes are redistributed to devices in the same VPN.

**Operational goals and benefits**

Cisco positions SD-WAN as a way to meet modern business demands with lower cost, higher diversity, and better support for business-critical and real-time applications. The training overview also points to transport-independent WAN design, which is a common SD-WAN benefit.

For exam prep, focus on the outcome rather than memorizing vendor marketing language. SD-WAN is used to improve agility, security, and cloud readiness while giving you more control over how traffic uses available paths.

- Helps establish a transport-independent WAN.
- Supports service-level agreement <abbr title="Service Level Agreement">SLA</abbr> goals for important applications.
- Improves path selection for cloud and real-time traffic.
- Adds policy control to WAN operations.

**What to remember for the exam**

Cisco SD-WAN is best understood as a policy-driven WAN architecture. It uses software control to shape routing, tunnels, and service distribution across an overlay network.

- SD-WAN virtualizes WAN control and management functions.
- Centralized control policies can shape topology and VPN membership.
- Default behavior without centralized policy is broad route exchange through the control plane.
- Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN focuses on agile, secure, cloud-ready WAN operation.
