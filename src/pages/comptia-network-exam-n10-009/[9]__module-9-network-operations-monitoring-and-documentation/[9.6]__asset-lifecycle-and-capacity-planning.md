## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Asset lifecycle management**

Asset lifecycle management (ALM) is the process of managing an asset from purchase through storage, use, maintenance, and retirement. In network environments, this includes both physical and non-physical assets such as equipment, infrastructure, and related resources.

ALM helps you keep assets running smoothly across their useful life. It supports better efficiency, lower maintenance cost, and fewer unplanned outages by making maintenance and replacement decisions based on asset condition and usage.

- **Asset**: something useful or valuable to an organization.
- **Lifecycle**: the end-to-end path from acquisition to decommissioning or replacement.
- **Maintenance**: regular upkeep that extends service life and reduces downtime.

Organizations often use monitoring data, including data from sensors connected to the Internet of Things (IoT), to track asset health and performance. This supports preventive maintenance before a failure disrupts service.

**Lifecycle stages and documentation**

A practical lifecycle approach treats assets as part of a connected workflow rather than isolated projects. The asset moves through planning, design, construction, operation, maintenance, and eventual retirement, reconstruction, replacement, or decommissioning.

Good documentation makes lifecycle decisions easier because it keeps asset-related information accessible across teams. Useful records include as-designed data, as-built data, condition data, and work history.

- **Planning and design**: define the need and expected use.
- **Construction and deployment**: build and place the asset into service.
- **Operations and maintenance**: keep the asset available and efficient.
- **Retirement or replacement**: remove, rebuild, or swap the asset when it no longer meets requirements.

> Keep lifecycle records current. Incomplete documentation makes troubleshooting, maintenance planning, and replacement decisions harder.

**Capacity planning fundamentals**

Capacity planning is the process of matching supply of network resources to demand. You aim for enough capacity to support users and services without overspending on unused infrastructure.

If capacity is too low, you risk slowdowns and outages. If capacity is too high, you waste money on people, technology, and infrastructure you do not need.

- **Demand**: the workload or service need you must support.
- **Supply**: the people, technology, and infrastructure available to meet that need.
- **Balance**: the target state where resources fit demand closely enough to stay efficient and reliable.

Capacity planning is ongoing because demand changes over time. You review current usage, anticipate growth, and adjust resources before performance drops.

**Planning considerations**

You should evaluate more than just hardware when planning capacity. People, technology, and infrastructure all affect whether a service can meet demand.

- **People**: staffing must support operations and maintenance.
- **Technology**: tools and platforms must handle the workload.
- **Infrastructure**: network and supporting systems must scale with demand.

In asset-heavy environments, lifecycle management and capacity planning work together. Lifecycle data shows what you own and how it is performing, while capacity planning shows whether those assets can still meet current and future needs.

**Practical workflow**

1. Identify the asset or service you need to support.
2. Review current usage, condition, and work history.
3. Compare available supply with expected demand.
4. Decide whether to maintain, expand, replace, or retire the asset.
5. Update documentation so the next decision uses current information.

**Key takeaways**

Asset lifecycle management focuses on the full life of an asset, from acquisition to retirement. Capacity planning focuses on whether your resources can meet demand efficiently. Together, they help you reduce cost, avoid downtime, and make better infrastructure decisions.
