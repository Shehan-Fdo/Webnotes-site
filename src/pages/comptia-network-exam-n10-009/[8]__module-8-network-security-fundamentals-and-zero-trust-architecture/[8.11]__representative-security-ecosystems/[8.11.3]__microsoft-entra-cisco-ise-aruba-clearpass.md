## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Network access control context**

Network access control (NAC) helps you secure corporate data when devices try to reach on-premises resources. In modern environments, NAC fits into a broader zero trust approach where users, devices, and applications are not automatically trusted.

Microsoft Intune integrates with NAC partners through a compliance retrieval service. This service replaced the previous Intune NAC service in July 2021, and Microsoft continued support for the legacy service through March 31, 2024. For exam prep, focus on the idea that Microsoft Entra-related access workflows can be part of a larger identity and compliance story, while the NAC platform enforces access decisions at the network edge.

- **NAC** is the access control layer that checks device or user posture before granting network access.
- **Zero trust** means you verify every user, device, and application instead of trusting the internal network by default.
- **Compliance retrieval service** is the newer Intune integration model for NAC partners.

**Cisco ISE in NAC integrations**

Cisco Identity Services Engine, or **ISE** (Identity Services Engine), appears in Microsoft’s supported partner list for Intune NAC integration. The evidence specifically lists Cisco ISE 3.1 and later as supported with the compliance retrieval service.

Cisco ISE is also described in vendor-neutral comparisons as a platform for identifying users and devices and applying access control policies on wired and wireless networks. That makes it a useful reference point when you need to connect identity, device posture, and policy enforcement in one NAC design.

- Cisco ISE 3.1 and later is supported in the Intune NAC partner ecosystem.
- Cisco ISE applies access control policies based on user and device identity.
- Cisco ISE is relevant for both wired and wireless policy enforcement.

**Aruba ClearPass positioning**

Aruba ClearPass is another NAC platform commonly compared with Cisco ISE. The evidence does not provide a direct Microsoft integration listing for ClearPass, so keep the note conservative: ClearPass is best treated as a competing NAC option rather than a confirmed Intune partner in this material.

In practical network planning, ClearPass is often evaluated alongside Cisco ISE when you need centralized policy enforcement across enterprise access points. If your environment already uses Aruba switching or Aruba management tools, ClearPass may be part of the vendor comparison process, but the evidence here does not support stronger claims.

- **ClearPass** is a NAC platform used for access control comparisons.
- Use it as a comparison point when evaluating architecture, deployment complexity, and ecosystem fit.
- Do not assume Intune support unless your source explicitly confirms it.

**How to think about the comparison**

The main exam-relevant idea is not vendor marketing; it is how NAC integrates with identity and compliance systems. Microsoft’s integration model shows that NAC can consume compliance data, while Cisco ISE and Aruba ClearPass represent the policy enforcement platforms that make access decisions.

A simple way to organize the topic is:

1. Identify the device or user.
2. Check compliance or posture data.
3. Apply the network access policy.
4. Allow, restrict, or deny access based on the result.

> Use conservative wording on the exam: if a question asks which platform is confirmed in Microsoft’s NAC partner list, Cisco ISE is supported by the evidence; ClearPass is not confirmed here.

**Key takeaways**

- Microsoft Intune uses NAC integrations to protect access to on-premises resources.
- Cisco ISE 3.1 and later is explicitly listed as a supported partner.
- Aruba ClearPass is a NAC competitor used in platform comparisons, but this evidence does not confirm Microsoft integration.
- Zero trust and policy-based authentication are the broader concepts that connect these tools.
