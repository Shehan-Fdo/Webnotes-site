## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Network Access Control overview**

<abbr title="Network Access Control">NAC</abbr> is a policy-based approach that controls which users and devices can access a corporate or private network. It focuses on visibility, access management, and compliance enforcement so you can keep unauthorized or risky endpoints out.

NAC matters because modern networks include more mobile devices, <abbr title="Bring Your Own Device">BYOD</abbr> endpoints, and <abbr title="Internet of Things">IoT</abbr> devices. Manual device-by-device control does not scale well, so NAC automates authentication and policy enforcement.

- NAC restricts unauthorized users and devices from entering the network.
- It supports authenticated and authorized access based on policy.
- It helps reduce risk from noncompliant or insecure endpoints.

**How NAC responds to noncompliant devices**

A NAC system does not have to choose only between full access and total denial. It can deny access, place a device in a quarantined area, or allow only restricted access to resources. This gives you options for containment while you investigate or remediate the endpoint.

NAC also supports policy lifecycle management, which means policies can be enforced across operating scenarios without requiring separate products or extra modules. In practice, this helps you keep access rules consistent as conditions change.

> A noncompliant device should not be treated as trusted just because it is connected. NAC can limit its reach before it affects the rest of the network.

- **Deny access** for devices that fail compliance checks.
- **Quarantine** devices that need remediation.
- **Restrict access** to limit exposure while preserving limited connectivity.

**Device posture checks**

A device posture check evaluates whether a device is secure and compliant before it gets access to the network or specific resources. The check looks at the device’s operating system, installed software, security configurations, and patch levels.

The goal is to confirm that the device is in a healthy state before it connects. Common checks include antivirus status, patch currency, and other security settings that align with organizational policy.

- Check the operating system state.
- Verify installed software and security configuration.
- Confirm patch levels and other compliance requirements.
- Block or limit devices that do not meet standards.

**Authentication and authorization flow**

NAC typically starts with authentication, then applies authorization rules. Authentication verifies identity, while authorization determines what the user or device can access based on policy.

Device posture checks fit into this flow as a compliance gate. Even if a user authenticates successfully, the device can still be denied or restricted if it does not meet security standards.

1. Authenticate the user or device.
2. Evaluate device posture against policy.
3. Grant, restrict, quarantine, or deny access based on the result.

**Why NAC supports stronger security**

NAC helps you enforce access control at the edge of the network, where devices first connect. This reduces the chance that an insecure endpoint can spread threats or reach sensitive resources.

It also supports broader security goals by working with visibility and compliance requirements. In practical terms, NAC helps you know what is connecting, decide whether it should connect, and control what it can do after it connects.

- Improves visibility into connecting devices and users.
- Enforces compliance before access is granted.
- Limits the spread of insecure or compromised endpoints.

**Exam focus**

For Network+ purposes, remember the relationship between NAC and device posture checks:

- NAC is the access control framework.
- Device posture checks verify endpoint health and compliance.
- Noncompliant devices can be denied, quarantined, or restricted.
- Authentication and authorization are separate steps, and posture checks can affect the final access decision.
