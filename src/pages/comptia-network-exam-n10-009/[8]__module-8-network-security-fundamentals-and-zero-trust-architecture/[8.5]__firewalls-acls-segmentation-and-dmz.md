## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Firewalls and access control**

Firewalls help you control traffic entering and leaving a network. In practice, they are part of the broader effort to protect the edge of the network and reduce exposure to untrusted traffic. Access control lists (ACLs) support that goal by allowing or denying traffic based on defined rules.

- **Firewall** rules filter traffic between networks or security zones.
- **ACLs** enforce traffic decisions based on criteria such as source, destination, or service.
- You use these controls to limit access to only what is needed.
- Strong edge controls are useful, but they do not replace internal segmentation.

A practical way to think about these tools is that they reduce the number of paths an attacker or unauthorized user can take. They also help you separate trusted and untrusted traffic so that security policy is applied consistently.

**Segmentation as a defense strategy**

Network segmentation breaks a flat network into smaller zones or subnets. This improves security, management, and troubleshooting because traffic is no longer shared across one large broadcast domain or one overly open internal network. It also gives you more visibility into traffic moving between zones.

- Segmentation divides a network into smaller, more manageable areas.
- It improves troubleshooting by narrowing where problems can occur.
- It can improve performance by reducing local traffic.
- It lets you apply different security controls to different zones.

Common segmentation methods include virtual LANs (VLANs), security zones on firewalls, and virtual routing and forwarding (VRFs) on routers. The key idea is to keep systems with different trust levels separated so you can control how they communicate.

**DMZ design**

A demilitarized zone (DMZ) is a perimeter network that sits between the internal local area network (LAN) and untrusted networks such as the internet. It gives external users access to public services while keeping the internal LAN protected. The DMZ acts as a buffer and adds an extra layer of security.

- Place internet-facing services in the DMZ.
- Keep the internal LAN isolated from direct untrusted access.
- Allow only limited access from the DMZ to the LAN when required.
- Use the DMZ for services such as web, mail, DNS, FTP, proxy, and VoIP servers.

The purpose of the DMZ is not to make public services fully trusted. Instead, it contains them in a separate area so compromise of a public-facing system does not automatically expose the internal network.

> A DMZ should be treated as a controlled exposure zone, not as part of the trusted internal LAN.

**How the pieces work together**

Firewalls, ACLs, segmentation, and DMZs work best as a layered design. Segmentation creates boundaries, firewalls enforce policy between those boundaries, and ACLs provide rule-based filtering where needed. The DMZ gives you a place to host public services without placing them directly on the internal network.

- Use segmentation to separate trust zones.
- Use firewalls to inspect and control inter-zone traffic.
- Use ACLs to permit only required flows.
- Use a DMZ for services that must be reachable from outside.

This layered approach supports least privilege network access by limiting each user, device, or service to only the traffic it needs. It also aligns with a zero trust mindset, where internal traffic is not assumed to be safe simply because it is inside the network.

**Design priorities for exam scenarios**

When you evaluate a scenario, focus on the business need first and then place controls around it. If a company needs a public website, the website belongs in the DMZ, not on the internal LAN. If a department only needs access to its own resources, segmentation and ACLs should prevent unnecessary lateral access.

- Public-facing services belong outside the trusted LAN.
- Sensitive internal resources should remain isolated.
- Flat networks increase risk because everything shares the same trust boundary.
- More zones usually mean more control, but also more design planning.

**Quick comparison**

| Concept | Main purpose | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| Firewall | Control traffic between networks | Enforce security policy at boundaries |
| ACL | Allow or deny specific traffic | Filter based on defined rule criteria |
| Segmentation | Split the network into zones | Reduce exposure and improve control |
| DMZ | Isolate public-facing services | Protect the internal LAN from untrusted traffic |

**Exam focus**

For Network+ questions, look for clues about:
- separating public services from internal systems
- limiting access between departments or zones
- protecting the LAN from internet traffic
- reducing risk in a flat network

If the scenario mentions a web server, mail server, DNS server, or similar service that must be reachable from the internet, the best answer often involves a DMZ plus filtering controls. If the scenario emphasizes restricting internal access, think segmentation and least privilege.
