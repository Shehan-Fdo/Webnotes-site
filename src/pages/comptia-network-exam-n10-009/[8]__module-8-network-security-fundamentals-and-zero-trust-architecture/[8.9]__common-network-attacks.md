## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ (Exam N10-009)

**Attack goals and impact**

Common network attacks usually target one or more of the core security goals: availability, confidentiality, and integrity. In practice, that means an attacker may try to knock a service offline, intercept traffic, or alter data in transit.

Attackers often look for weaknesses at different layers of the network and exploit misconfigurations, exposed services, or user trust. Recognizing the attack pattern helps you separate a real outage from malicious activity.

- **Availability** attacks try to disrupt access to a website, service, or network.
- **Confidentiality** attacks focus on eavesdropping or capturing data.
- **Integrity** attacks attempt to tamper with traffic or redirect it.

**Denial of Service and Distributed Denial of Service**

A <abbr title="Denial of Service">DoS</abbr> attack overwhelms a target with excessive traffic or malicious requests from a single source. A <abbr title="Distributed Denial of Service">DDoS</abbr> attack uses multiple compromised systems, often a botnet, to make the flood harder to stop.

These attacks can be organized by how they consume resources:

- **Volumetric** attacks saturate bandwidth.
- **Protocol** attacks target network infrastructure, such as a SYN flood.
- **Application-layer** attacks disrupt services such as HTTP or DNS.

> A sudden traffic spike is not always a DDoS attack, but repeated saturation from many sources is a strong indicator.

**Spoofing and man-in-the-middle attacks**

Spoofing involves pretending to be a trusted system or identity. One common result is a <abbr title="Man-in-the-Middle">MitM</abbr> attack, where the attacker positions themselves between two parties to observe or alter traffic.

ARP spoofing is a common example. The attacker sends forged Address Resolution Protocol (ARP) messages so devices send traffic to the attacker’s Media Access Control (MAC) address instead of the real destination.

- **ARP spoofing** can support packet sniffing and traffic interception.
- **MitM** attacks can affect both confidentiality and integrity.
- **Static ARP entries** and Dynamic ARP Inspection help verify valid ARP responses.

**MAC flooding**

MAC flooding targets a switch’s MAC address table by sending many fake MAC addresses. If the table is overloaded, the switch may enter flooding behavior and send unicast traffic more broadly than intended.

This creates an opportunity for an attacker to capture traffic that would normally stay on a specific port.

- **Port security** limits the number of MAC addresses allowed per port.
- **Switch flooding** behavior increases exposure to traffic capture.
- **Layer 2** protections matter because the attack abuses switching logic, not just endpoints.

**VLAN hopping**

VLAN hopping exploits misconfigured Virtual Local Area Networks (VLANs) to gain access to traffic on another VLAN. The attack often depends on switch spoofing or double-tagging techniques.

The practical lesson is simple: segmentation only helps when the switch configuration is correct and trunks are tightly controlled.

- **Misconfiguration** is the enabling condition.
- **Unauthorized access** to another VLAN is the goal.
- **Segmentation** reduces risk when implemented correctly.

**Rogue access points and evil twin attacks**

A rogue access point is an unauthorized wireless access point on a network. An evil twin attack goes further by creating a fake Wi-Fi access point that looks legitimate so users connect to it instead of the real one.

Once connected, the user’s traffic passes through attacker-controlled infrastructure. These attacks are especially common on public Wi-Fi, where users may connect quickly without verifying the network.

- **Public Wi-Fi** is a common target because users expect open access.
- **Fake access point** behavior is the key indicator of an evil twin.
- **Traffic interception** becomes possible after the victim connects.

**Enumeration and fingerprinting**

Enumeration and fingerprinting are information-gathering techniques used to learn about hosts, services, and network characteristics. A ping sweep checks a range of IPv4 or IPv6 addresses to find active hosts, while port scanning identifies open or filtered TCP and UDP ports.

These techniques are often used before a more targeted attack because they reveal what is present and what may be vulnerable.

- **Ping sweep** helps identify live hosts.
- **Port scanning** helps identify services in use.
- **Fingerprinting** can reveal device or operating system details.

**Password cracking and social engineering**

Some attacks focus on the user rather than the network itself. Password cracking attempts to recover credentials, while social engineering manipulates people into revealing information or granting access.

These attacks matter because a valid login can bypass many technical controls. Even strong network defenses can be weakened if users are tricked into giving away access.

- **Password cracking** targets authentication secrets.
- **Social engineering** targets human trust.
- **Credential theft** often leads to broader network compromise.

**Wireless and infrastructure weaknesses**

Wireless attacks often begin with weak access control or poor segmentation. Public Wi-Fi, rogue access points, and evil twins all show how wireless trust can be abused.

Infrastructure weaknesses also matter. Misconfigured switches, weak VLAN boundaries, and insufficient port controls can expose traffic or allow unauthorized movement across the network.

- **Wireless trust** should be verified, not assumed.
- **Switch controls** help limit traffic exposure.
- **Configuration errors** often create the opening for attack.

**How to think about attack recognition**

When you troubleshoot, focus on the pattern of failure. If a service is unreachable, ask whether the issue is a legitimate outage or a deliberate attempt to exhaust resources. If traffic appears redirected or exposed, consider spoofing, MAC flooding, or a MitM scenario.

A practical response starts with identifying the likely attack type, then matching it to the control that limits it.

1. Identify the symptom: outage, interception, unauthorized access, or suspicious discovery activity.
2. Match the symptom to the likely attack class.
3. Check the relevant control point, such as port security, ARP validation, or wireless authentication.
4. Confirm whether the issue is a misconfiguration or an active attack.

**Quick comparison**

| Attack type | Primary effect | Common clue |
|---|---|---|
| DoS / DDoS | Availability loss | Excessive traffic or requests |
| Spoofing / MitM | Confidentiality and integrity loss | Traffic appears redirected or intercepted |
| MAC flooding | Traffic exposure on a switch | Unexpected flooding behavior |
| VLAN hopping | Unauthorized VLAN access | Segmentation failure or misconfiguration |
| Evil twin | Wireless traffic interception | Fake access point that mimics a real one |
| Enumeration / fingerprinting | Reconnaissance | Ping sweeps or port scans |

**Key takeaways**

Common network attacks usually exploit trust, misconfiguration, or resource exhaustion. If you can identify what the attacker is trying to affect, you can usually narrow the attack type quickly.

- **Availability** problems often point to DoS or DDoS.
- **Traffic redirection** often points to spoofing or MitM.
- **Layer 2** anomalies often point to MAC flooding or VLAN hopping.
- **Wireless impersonation** often points to rogue AP or evil twin activity.
