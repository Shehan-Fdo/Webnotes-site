## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Connector overview**

Display connectors move video from a computer or source device to a monitor or television. Some also carry audio, and some are more common on older hardware while others are used on modern systems. For exam prep, focus on how each connector looks, what it carries, and where you are most likely to see it.

- **HDMI** stands for High Definition Multimedia Interface.
- **DisplayPort** is a modern digital display interface that can carry audio and video.
- **USB-C** can be used for video over a USB Type-C connection.
- **VGA** is an older analog connector.
- **DVI** is a display interface with multiple variants.

**HDMI**

HDMI is one of the most common connectors on monitors and televisions. It uses a digital connection that carries both audio and video through the same cable. The connector is a 19-pin Type A design, and its shape helps distinguish it from other ports.

- HDMI commonly appears on consumer electronics and display devices.
- The connector is almost rectangular with indented bottom corners.
- A practical range reference is about 20 meters before signal quality can degrade.

> HDMI is a strong exam keyword because it combines audio and video in one digital connection.

**DisplayPort**

DisplayPort is another digital display interface used to connect a computer to a monitor. It also carries audio and video, and it is commonly found on PCs and displays. DisplayPort connectors feature 20 pins and an L-shaped design, and many include a locking mechanism for a secure fit.

- DisplayPort is designed for audio and video over one cable.
- The connector shape is L-shaped.
- A locking mechanism may help prevent accidental disconnection.

**USB-C video**

Video can also travel over USB-C, which is useful when a device uses a USB Type-C port for display output. The evidence only supports the fact that USB-C can carry video, so keep the note conservative and avoid assuming a specific alternate mode or feature set.

- USB-C may be used for video output.
- It is often relevant on modern laptops and compact devices.
- Check the port labeling and device documentation when you need to confirm display support.

**VGA**

VGA stands for Video Graphics Array and is an older display connector. It is often described as DB-15, though the connector is technically DE-15. VGA uses 15 pins and is associated with a blue connector color on many systems.

- VGA is an analog video connection.
- The connector is commonly identified by its blue color.
- You may see it labeled as DB-15 or DE-15.

**DVI**

DVI stands for Digital Visual Interface. It has multiple versions, including single-link and dual-link, and it also appears in several connector variants. The evidence identifies DVI-A for analog signals, DVI-D for digital signals, and DVI-I as another variant you should recognize.

- DVI can be single-link or dual-link.
- Single-link supports lower bandwidth than dual-link.
- DVI-A carries analog signals.
- DVI-D carries digital signals.
- DVI-I is another DVI variant to recognize on sight.

**Quick comparison**

| Connector | Signal type | Common use | Notable clue |
|---|---|---|---|
| HDMI | Digital audio/video | Monitors, TVs, consumer electronics | 19-pin Type A, indented corners |
| DisplayPort | Digital audio/video | PCs and displays | 20 pins, L-shaped, often locking |
| USB-C | Video over USB Type-C | Modern devices | Uses a USB-C port for display output |
| VGA | Analog video | Older displays and systems | Blue connector, DB-15/DE-15 |
| DVI | Analog or digital, depending on variant | Display connections | DVI-A, DVI-D, DVI-I |

**Exam tips**

When you see a connector question, identify the signal type first, then match the physical shape. That approach helps you separate older analog interfaces from modern digital ones.

- Use **VGA** for older analog display connections.
- Use **HDMI** when you need a common digital connector that carries audio and video.
- Use **DisplayPort** when you see a digital display connector with an L-shaped plug.
- Use **DVI** when the question mentions single-link, dual-link, or DVI-A/DVI-D/DVI-I.
- Use **USB-C** when video is delivered through a USB Type-C port.
