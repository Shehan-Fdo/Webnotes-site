## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Display technology overview**

Display devices are a primary computer output. For A+ purposes, you should recognize the basic idea behind each common panel type and the practical differences that affect size, power use, and image quality.

- **LCD** stands for Liquid Crystal Display.
- LCD panels use a backlight shining through liquid crystals and color filters.
- The liquid crystals twist to control how much light passes through the panel.
- LCD displays are relatively light, low power, and inexpensive.
- These traits make LCD a common choice for portable systems and wall-mounted displays.

**LED as an LCD backlight type**

In this context, LED usually refers to an LCD display that uses Light-Emitting Diode backlighting instead of older backlight types. The panel is still LCD; the backlight method changes.

- **LED** backlighting can make panels thinner.
- LED backlighting is associated with better energy efficiency.
- LED backlighting can improve brightness.
- When you see “LED monitor,” think of an LCD panel with LED backlighting.

**OLED fundamentals**

OLED stands for Organic Light-Emitting Diode. Unlike LCD-based displays, OLED pixels produce their own light, so the display does not need a separate backlight.

- **OLED** removes the need for backlighting.
- Self-lit pixels support true blacks.
- OLED can provide very high contrast.
- OLED panels can be extremely thin.

> <mark>Key distinction:</mark> LCD and LED rely on a backlight, while OLED pixels emit their own light.

**Common LCD panel behaviors**

A+ questions may reference LCD panel behavior or panel families. The evidence highlights TN and IPS as common examples.

- **TN** means Twisted Nematic.
- TN panels are known for fast response times.
- TN panels have limited viewing angles.
- **IPS** means In-Plane Switching.
- IPS panels provide better color accuracy.
- IPS panels provide wider viewing angles.

**How to compare the main display types**

| Type | Light source | Practical strengths | Common tradeoff |
|---|---|---|---|
| LCD | Backlight through liquid crystals | Light, low power, inexpensive | Depends on backlight and panel behavior |
| LED | LCD with LED backlighting | Thinner, brighter, more efficient | Still an LCD-based design |
| OLED | Self-emitting pixels | True blacks, high contrast, very thin | Not described in the evidence beyond these traits |

**Troubleshooting awareness**

Display troubleshooting often starts with simple physical checks. If a screen is black or shows `No Signal`, verify the basics before assuming a deeper hardware fault.

1. Check that the video cable is connected securely at both ends.
2. Check that the monitor has power.
3. Confirm that the display is receiving the correct input source.

Common display issues mentioned in the evidence include fuzzy images, burn-in, dead pixels, and flashing screens. For exam prep, connect each symptom to the display technology or connection path before choosing a fix.

**Exam focus reminders**

- Know the acronym expansions for `LCD`, `LED`, `OLED`, `TN`, and `IPS`.
- Remember that LED is commonly an LCD with LED backlighting.
- Remember that OLED does not need a backlight.
- Use the simplest troubleshooting checks first when a display is blank or reports `No Signal`.
