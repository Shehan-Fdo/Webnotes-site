## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**USB overview**

<abbr title="Universal Serial Bus">USB</abbr> is the common standard for connecting peripherals, and it has been used since 1996 for connectors, signals, and power delivery. In practice, you use USB for devices such as cameras, mice, keyboards, and many other accessories because it is widely supported and usually works with minimal setup.

USB has changed over time to improve performance and power capabilities. For exam purposes, focus on the idea that USB is not just a data connector anymore; it also plays a major role in charging and powering devices.

- USB is a long-standing standard for data, signaling, and power.
- It is widely used because it is convenient and broadly compatible.
- Newer USB developments emphasize stronger power delivery support.

**USB-C and connector basics**

USB Type-C, often written as USB-C, is a newer connector style that supports modern power delivery features. It is part of the latest USB developments that make USB a stronger option for powering devices than older USB implementations.

USB-C is important because it is designed for more flexible use than older connector styles. A single USB-C power supply can support several simultaneous applications, which makes it useful for modern laptops and mobile devices.

- **USB-C** is the connector style associated with newer power delivery capabilities.
- It supports broader use cases than older USB connectors.
- It is commonly tied to charging and power-focused designs.

> USB-C is not the same thing as USB Power Delivery. USB-C is the connector; power delivery is the charging and negotiation behavior.

**USB Power Delivery**

<abbr title="USB Power Delivery">USB PD</abbr> is a protocol that automatically determines voltage configuration and improves how devices receive power. This makes charging more flexible than older fixed-power USB behavior.

USB Power Delivery is designed to support higher power needs, including devices that require more than standard USB charging can provide. The evidence also notes that USB-C PD can deliver up to 100 watts, which is enough for laptops and other higher-demand devices.

- **USB PD** negotiates voltage automatically.
- It supports higher power output than standard USB charging.
- It is especially relevant for laptops and other power-hungry devices.

**Programmable Power Supply**

The evidence also references <abbr title="USB Programmable Power Supply">USB PPS</abbr>, which is part of the newer USB power ecosystem. In exam terms, treat it as an enhancement related to modern USB power delivery designs.

You do not need to overcomplicate the relationship: USB-C is the connector, USB PD is the power negotiation standard, and USB PPS is another related power feature in the same modern ecosystem.

- **USB PPS** is mentioned as part of newer USB power supply designs.
- It belongs to the broader USB power delivery family.
- It supports the move from simple charging to smarter power management.

**Voltage and power behavior**

USB-C power supplies always support 5 V, and some support higher voltages. Common voltage steps mentioned in the evidence include 5 V, 9 V, 15 V, and 20 V, with 12 V described as optional. The evidence also notes newer extended power ranges that add 28 V, 36 V, and 48 V for much higher wattage.

For A+ study purposes, the key takeaway is that USB power delivery can scale beyond basic charging. That scaling is what makes USB-C useful for devices that need more than a simple low-power charge.

| Power concept | Practical meaning |
|---|---|
| `5 V` | Baseline support on USB-C power supplies |
| Higher voltage steps | Used when devices request more power |
| `100 W` | Enough for many laptops and similar devices |
| Extended power ranges | Support even higher power needs with newer cables |

**Exam-focused comparisons**

Use the following distinctions to avoid confusion on test questions:

- **USB** refers to the overall standard family.
- **USB-C** refers to the connector.
- **USB Power Delivery** refers to the power negotiation and charging behavior.
- **USB PPS** refers to a related programmable power feature.

These terms often appear together, but they do not mean the same thing. If a question asks about charging behavior, think about USB PD. If it asks about the physical connector, think about USB-C.

**Quick study checklist**

1. Identify USB as a standard for data, signals, and power.
2. Remember that USB-C is the connector, not the power protocol.
3. Associate USB PD with automatic voltage negotiation.
4. Recall that USB-C PD can reach up to `100 W`.
5. Recognize USB PPS as part of the modern USB power ecosystem.

**Common exam takeaway**

The most important concept is that USB has evolved from a mostly data-focused interface into a flexible power-delivery system. Modern USB-C systems can charge more demanding devices, and USB Power Delivery is the mechanism that makes that possible.
