## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**What vishing is**

Vishing is a social engineering attack that uses voice communication to manipulate people instead of technology. Attackers try to get you to reveal information by sounding convincing, urgent, or authoritative over the phone or through other voice-based contact.

Social engineering attacks are difficult to stop because they change often and can involve one person or multiple people across one or more organizations. Vishing fits this pattern because it targets human behavior directly rather than relying on malware or technical exploits.

- **Voice-based** attacks often happen through phone calls.
- Attackers may pose as a disgruntled customer or another believable caller.
- The goal is to pressure you into giving up information.

**How vishing works**

Attackers use manipulation tactics to bypass security controls by influencing the person who answers the call. They may use details from current events or personal context to make the story seem real and increase trust.

These attacks can be hard to identify because they do not always look suspicious at first. A caller may be aggressive, urgent, or persuasive, which can make the target act before verifying the request.

- **Manipulation** is the core technique.
- Attackers may bully or pressure the target.
- Context from news or personal topics can make the call more believable.

**Why vishing is effective**

Vishing works because people often trust a live conversation more than a message. The attacker can respond in real time, adjust the story, and keep the target engaged until the request succeeds.

This makes vishing especially challenging for traditional electronic defenses. Training users to recognize suspicious calls is an important part of reducing risk.

- **Real-time** conversation helps the attacker adapt.
- Human trust can override caution.
- User awareness is a key defense.

**Recognition and response**

You should treat unexpected requests for information with caution, especially when the caller creates pressure or urgency. Verify the caller through an independent method before sharing anything sensitive.

1. Pause before responding to a suspicious call.
2. Verify the caller using a trusted contact method.
3. Do not share information until the request is confirmed.
4. Report the incident through your organization’s security process.

> If a caller tries to rush you, that pressure is a warning sign rather than proof of legitimacy.

**Exam focus**

For A+ exam prep, remember that vishing is the voice-based form of social engineering. It belongs with other human-targeted attacks such as impersonation and phishing, but it specifically uses calls or voice contact to trick the victim.

- **Vishing** = voice + phishing-style deception.
- It targets people, not systems.
- Verification and awareness reduce the chance of success.
