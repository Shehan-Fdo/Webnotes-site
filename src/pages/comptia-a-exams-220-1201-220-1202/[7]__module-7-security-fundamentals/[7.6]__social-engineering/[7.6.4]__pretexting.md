## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Pretexting overview**

Pretexting is a **social engineering** tactic where an attacker creates a believable story, or *pretext*, to manipulate a target into sharing sensitive information. The story is designed to sound legitimate enough that the target lowers their guard and complies.

Attackers often impersonate someone the target might trust, such as:

- a coworker
- a customer support representative
- an authority figure
- a delivery person or other familiar role

The goal is not to break technical controls first. Instead, the attacker uses persuasion, trust, and context to make the request seem normal.

**How the attack works**

A pretexting attempt usually starts with a fabricated scenario that gives the attacker a reason to ask for information. The attacker may reference personal or professional details, use flattery, or act urgent to build credibility.

Common patterns include:

- claiming to need account verification
- pretending to follow up on a support issue
- posing as a business contact or executive
- using a story that sounds time-sensitive or routine

This approach can happen in person, by phone, or through email. The format matters less than the story and the trust it tries to create.

**Why pretexting is effective**

Pretexting works because people tend to respond to requests that appear familiar, helpful, or authoritative. Attackers may use knowledge of the target’s role, workplace, or recent events to make the request feel real.

Key factors that increase success include:

- apparent legitimacy
- social pressure
- confidence and persuasion
- familiarity with the target’s environment

> <mark>Critical point:</mark> the attacker’s story is the tool. If the story feels credible, the target may reveal information without checking the request carefully.

**Defensive habits**

You reduce risk by validating requests before sharing anything sensitive. If a request seems unusual, verify it through a trusted channel rather than replying directly to the original message or caller.

Practical defenses include:

1. Confirm the requester’s identity using a known contact method.
2. Check whether the request matches normal business process.
3. Escalate suspicious requests to the appropriate team or supervisor.
4. Train users to recognize manipulation tactics and to pause before responding.

A strong response process matters because pretexting often succeeds when people feel rushed or assume the request is routine.

**What to remember for exam prep**

- Pretexting is a form of **social engineering**.
- It uses a believable story to gain trust and extract information.
- The attacker may impersonate a trusted role or authority.
- Verification through secure channels is the safest response.
- User awareness training helps reduce the chance of success.
