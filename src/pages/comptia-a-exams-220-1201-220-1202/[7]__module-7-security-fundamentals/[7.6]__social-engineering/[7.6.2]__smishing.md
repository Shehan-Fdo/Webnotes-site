## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Smishing overview**

Smishing is **SMS phishing**: a social engineering attack delivered through text messages. Attackers use the same basic idea as other phishing attacks, but they move the lure to a channel people often trust and check quickly.

The goal is usually to get you to click a link, reveal information, or take an action before you verify the message. Texts that look urgent, personal, or official are especially effective because they create a fast emotional response.

- **SMS** means Short Message Service.
- **Phishing** means tricking someone into giving up information or taking a harmful action.
- Smishing often uses messages that appear to come from banks, delivery services, or other familiar organizations.
- The attack works because people tend to trust their phones and respond quickly to texts.

**Why smishing works**

Smishing is effective because it combines social engineering with the immediacy of mobile messaging. A text feels direct and personal, so the message can seem more believable than a random email.

Attackers often rely on urgency and fear. A message may claim that an account is suspended, a package is delayed, or action is needed right away. That pressure can make you click before you think.

> Treat unexpected text messages with the same caution you use for suspicious email links.

**Common smishing traits**

Smishing messages often share a few recognizable patterns. You should look for signs that the message is trying to rush you or move you to a link.

- **Urgency**: the message says you must act now.
- **Impersonation**: the sender pretends to be a trusted brand or service.
- **Suspicious links**: the text pushes you to tap a link or open a page.
- **Request for details**: the message asks for account or identity information.
- **Unexpected contact**: the text arrives without a reason you can verify.

**How to respond**

When you receive a suspicious text, slow down and verify it through a trusted channel. Do not rely on the message itself to prove it is real.

1. Stop before tapping any link.
2. Check whether the message makes sense in context.
3. Verify the claim using a known phone number, official app, or official website.
4. Delete the message if it is suspicious.
5. Report it if your organization has a reporting process.

**Practical exam takeaway**

For exam purposes, connect smishing to the broader category of social engineering. The key idea is that attackers manipulate people instead of technology, and they may use multiple channels or coordinated messages to increase trust.

If you see a scenario involving a text message that pressures you to click a link or provide information, smishing is the likely answer.

**Quick memory cue**

- Email phishing uses email.
- **Smishing** uses SMS text messages.
- The attack depends on trust, urgency, and a fast response.
