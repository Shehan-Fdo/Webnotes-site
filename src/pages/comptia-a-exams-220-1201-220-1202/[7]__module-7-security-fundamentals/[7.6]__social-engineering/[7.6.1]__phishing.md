## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Phishing overview**

Phishing is a type of social engineering attack that targets people instead of technology. Attackers use deceptive messages to trick a target into revealing sensitive information or installing malicious software.

These attacks often look legitimate at first glance. The sender may pretend to be a trusted company, a bank, or an internal department. The message may ask you to verify an account, update credentials, or click a link to a fake website.

- **Deception** is the core tactic.
- Messages can arrive by email, text message, social media, or phone call.
- The goal is usually to steal information or deliver malware.
- Attackers rely on trust, urgency, or curiosity to get a response.

**How phishing fits social engineering**

Phishing is one example of social engineering, where attackers manipulate people rather than systems. This makes the attack harder to stop with technical controls alone.

Social engineering attacks can be unpredictable and may involve one person or multiple people across multiple organizations. They can also happen in person or through electronic communication.

- **Social engineering** uses human manipulation to bypass security.
- Attackers may pose as a disgruntled customer or a trusted contact.
- Real-world events or personal details can make a message more convincing.
- Training users is important because the attack method changes constantly.

**Common phishing patterns**

Phishing messages often try to create a quick reaction. They may pressure you to act before you verify the request.

Typical patterns include:
- A message that claims there is a problem with your account.
- A link that leads to a fake login page.
- A request to open an attachment or provide credentials.
- A message that uses a news event or personal context to seem believable.

> Be cautious with any message that asks you to click, sign in, or share information unexpectedly.

**Why phishing is effective**

Phishing works because it targets human behavior. Even when security tools are in place, a convincing message can still persuade someone to act.

The evidence emphasizes that attackers keep changing their methods, which makes awareness and training essential. Users are often the weakest link when they are untrained, but they become a stronger defense when they know what to look for.

- **Awareness training** helps users recognize suspicious messages.
- Simulated phishing can help measure user readiness.
- Click rates alone do not show whether behavior has improved.
- Better metrics include reporting habits and overall resilience.

**How to respond to suspicious messages**

When you receive a suspicious message, slow down and verify it through a trusted channel. Do not rely on the message itself to confirm legitimacy.

1. Check the sender and the request for signs of urgency or inconsistency.
2. Verify the request using a known phone number, website, or internal process.
3. Avoid clicking links or opening attachments until you confirm the message.
4. Report the message according to your organization’s security process.

**Exam focus points**

- Phishing is a **social engineering** attack.
- It commonly uses email, but it can also use text, social media, or phone calls.
- The attacker often impersonates a trusted source.
- The goal is to steal information or install malware.
- User training is a key defense because the attack targets people.
