## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Tailgating overview**

Tailgating is a **physical security** issue where an unauthorized person follows an authorized person into a restricted area. It is also commonly called *piggybacking*. The key risk is that the attacker bypasses normal access controls by relying on human behavior instead of defeating the lock, scanner, or guard directly.

Tailgating is a form of **social engineering** because it exploits trust, distraction, or courtesy. In practice, the attacker may slip through a door, gate, or checkpoint right behind someone who has legitimate access.

- Unauthorized entry happens by following closely behind an authorized person.
- The attack can bypass electronic or human access controls.
- It targets people and habits, not just technology.
- It can expose equipment, data, and personnel to risk.

**Why it works**

Tailgating succeeds when the person with access is careless, rushed, or unwilling to challenge someone behind them. The attacker may appear confident or act as if they belong, which makes the situation feel normal enough to avoid confrontation.

This matters in places with sensitive hardware or restricted rooms, such as offices, labs, and data centers. Even strong technical controls can fail if someone simply holds the door open for the wrong person.

> Tailgating is a security gap created by behavior, so prevention depends on both access control and user awareness.

**Prevention methods**

You reduce tailgating by combining physical controls, monitoring, and staff training. No single control is enough in every situation, so layered defenses work best.

- Use **turnstiles** or **mantraps** to allow one person at a time.
- Add door locks, deadbolts, or keyless entry systems where appropriate.
- Place security personnel near entrances to watch for unauthorized entry.
- Train staff to challenge unknown people and avoid holding doors open blindly.
- Build awareness so employees understand that courtesy can create risk.

**Practical response**

If you notice someone trying to follow you into a secure area, do not assume they belong there. You should verify their access or direct them to the proper entry process. In many environments, a polite challenge is better than allowing an unknown person to enter.

1. Confirm whether the person has legitimate access.
2. Direct them to badge in or use the approved entrance.
3. Report repeated attempts or suspicious behavior to security.

**Exam focus**

For A+ purposes, remember the core definition: tailgating is unauthorized entry by following an authorized person into a secure area. If a question asks how to stop it, look for answers involving physical barriers, security staff, and employee awareness.

**Key terms**

- **Tailgating**: unauthorized entry by following an authorized person.
- **Piggybacking**: another name for tailgating.
- **Mantrap**: a controlled entry area that limits access to one person at a time.
- **Turnstile**: a physical barrier that helps enforce single-person entry.
