## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Overview**

Data handling and privacy basics focus on how you protect information, preserve evidence, and follow organizational rules when working with sensitive data. In A+ contexts, this includes incident response steps, chain of custody, software licensing, digital rights management (DRM), non-disclosure agreements (NDAs), regulated data handling, acceptable use policies (AUPs), and privacy incident handling.

- **Chain of custody** documents who handled evidence and helps prevent tampering.
- **Hashing** can help confirm that digital evidence has not changed after collection.
- **Labeling** and cataloging evidence support traceability and later review.
- **Policy enforcement** matters because licensing misuse and policy lapses can trigger penalties.

**Evidence handling and integrity**

When you collect evidence, you need to preserve its integrity from the start. Chain of custody records every person who comes in contact with the evidence, which makes it easier to verify that the item was not altered. For digital evidence, hashing provides a practical way to compare the collected item with the original and confirm that it matches.

> If evidence is physical, seal it in an evidence container. If it is digital, protect it so the data remains unchanged during storage and review.

A careful collection process also includes clear identification. You should label and catalog everything you take so the evidence can be tracked and referenced later.

- Record each transfer of evidence.
- Use hashing for digital items when integrity verification is needed.
- Keep physical evidence sealed and controlled.
- Maintain a clear catalog of what was collected.

**Privacy, licensing, and policy concepts**

Privacy and policy work together to reduce risk and keep users and organizations compliant. The source material highlights software licensing types, DRM, NDAs, regulated data handling, internal policy audits, AUPs, user compliance monitoring, and privacy incident handling. These topics are administrative, but they directly affect how you store, share, and use data.

Licensing misuse can create serious consequences, so you need to follow the rules for the software and content you use. DRM is part of controlling how digital content is accessed and used. NDAs add confidentiality obligations, while regulated data handling requires you to treat certain data according to specific rules.

- **AUPs** define acceptable user behavior and help set expectations.
- **Internal audits** check whether users and teams follow policy.
- **Privacy incidents** require attention and response, not informal handling.
- **Regulated data** needs stricter handling than ordinary internal data.

**Encryption and data protection**

Encryption is a core way to protect data. It limits access so only authorized people can view the information. Full-disk encryption (FDE) protects an entire drive and is often described as data at rest because it secures stored data. On Windows, BitLocker is the common example of full-disk encryption.

You may also encounter encryption for individual files or folders, depending on the operating system and file system features. Removable media such as USB drives are another important target for encryption because they are easy to lose.

- **FDE** protects the whole storage device.
- **BitLocker** provides full-disk encryption on Windows.
- File- or folder-level encryption may be available in some systems.
- USB encryption helps reduce risk if a drive is lost.

**Incident response and compliance**

Incident response includes documenting what happened and preserving evidence correctly. The evidence notes mention incident response steps and the order of volatility, which means you should think carefully about what data is most likely to disappear or change first. That helps you decide what to capture before it is lost.

Compliance is not just about reacting to incidents. It also includes ongoing oversight, monitoring user behavior, and conducting audits to confirm that policies are being followed. This is especially important when privacy, licensing, or regulated data is involved.

1. Identify the incident or policy issue.
2. Preserve evidence and document chain of custody.
3. Apply the relevant policy, licensing, or privacy controls.
4. Review compliance through monitoring or internal audit.
5. Close the issue only after the required records are complete.

**Practical exam focus**

For A+ exam prep, connect each concept to its purpose:

- Evidence handling protects integrity.
- Privacy controls protect sensitive information.
- Licensing rules prevent misuse and penalties.
- Encryption protects data from unauthorized access.
- Policies and audits help enforce consistent behavior.

A good way to study this topic is to group terms by function: collection, protection, compliance, and response. That makes it easier to distinguish between evidence integrity tasks and privacy or licensing tasks on exam questions.
