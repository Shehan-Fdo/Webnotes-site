## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Overview**

The CIA triad is a foundational information security model built around three goals: **confidentiality**, **integrity**, and **availability**. It gives you a simple way to think about protecting data and systems, and it helps you evaluate security controls based on what they protect.

You may also see it called the AIC triad. That wording is used to avoid confusion with the U.S. Central Intelligence Agency, but the meaning is the same.

- **Confidentiality** focuses on preventing unauthorized access to private information.
- **Integrity** focuses on making sure data stays accurate and unchanged unless authorized.
- **Availability** focuses on keeping systems and data accessible when needed.

**Confidentiality**

Confidentiality means only approved users can view sensitive information. In practice, this is about privacy and access control. If data is confidential, you limit who can read it and reduce the chance that outsiders or unauthorized insiders can see it.

Common security thinking around confidentiality includes:
- Restricting access to private data
- Protecting information from unauthorized disclosure
- Considering both external threats and insider misuse

**Integrity**

Integrity means data remains correct, complete, and trustworthy from source to destination. If you send information to someone, integrity ensures the recipient gets exactly what was intended, without unauthorized changes.

Integrity matters when you need confidence that:
- A message has not been altered in transit
- Stored data still matches the original record
- Security controls preserve accuracy over time

**Availability**

Availability means systems, services, and data stay up and usable when needed. Security is not only about blocking access; it also has to keep legitimate users from being shut out by outages or disruptions.

Availability is important because:
- Users need reliable access to systems
- Business operations depend on uptime
- Security controls should not make resources unusable

**Balancing the three goals**

The three parts of the CIA triad can sometimes conflict. Strong confidentiality controls may make access more cumbersome, which can reduce availability. In real environments, you balance the three based on the organization’s needs and risk tolerance.

A practical approach is to ask:
- What data needs the most protection?
- How much access do users need to do their jobs?
- What level of downtime is acceptable?

**How to remember the triad**

A simple memory aid is to connect each letter to its purpose:
- **C** = Confidentiality
- **I** = Integrity
- **A** = Availability

This helps you quickly map security requirements to the right goal when you review controls, policies, or threats.

**Key takeaway**

The CIA triad is a high-level model, but it is useful because it organizes security thinking into three clear priorities. When you evaluate a system, ask whether it protects privacy, preserves accuracy, and stays accessible.
