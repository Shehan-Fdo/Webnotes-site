## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Incident response overview**

Incident response (IR) is the planned way an organization handles a security event so it can limit damage and keep business running. A cyberattack can range from a moderate threat to a major incident involving malware or a data breach, so preparation matters before the event happens.

A practical IR process is not just a document. It combines people, processes, and training so front-line staff and IT personnel know what to do when an attack occurs. In larger environments, a Computer Security Incident Response Team (CSIRT) may handle the response.

- **Business continuity** is a core goal of IR.
- A tested process helps prevent further damage.
- Staff should know their role before an incident occurs.
- A CSIRT may be needed for larger or more complex incidents.

**Why awareness matters**

Security incidents are a normal part of the business day, so you should treat them as expected events rather than rare exceptions. The goal is to respond quickly enough to stop the problem from spreading while preserving the organization’s ability to operate.

An incident response analyst focuses on minimizing the overall effect of a breach. That includes preventing escalation, reducing damage, guiding responders, and supporting post-resolution review. Even if you are not the analyst, you still need to recognize when an event is beyond routine troubleshooting and needs escalation.

- **Escalation** helps move the issue to the right people quickly.
- Responders work to stop the bleeding, eradicate the threat, and remediate damage.
- Post-resolution analysis helps improve future readiness.
- Policies, protocols, and training support better response over time.

**Evidence handling and documentation**

When an incident involves physical or digital evidence, you protect integrity by documenting what you collect and who handles it. Chain of custody records every person who contacts the evidence and any changes made to it. This documentation helps show whether evidence remained intact.

Digital evidence often uses hashes as an integrity check during collection and handling. Physical evidence can be sealed and labeled so its condition is easier to verify later.

> Preserve evidence first. Do not alter or discard items that may be needed for investigation or recovery.

- **Chain of custody** tracks evidence handling from collection onward.
- **Hashes** help verify digital evidence integrity.
- Sealed packaging supports physical evidence preservation.
- Documentation should be complete and consistent.

**What to do during an incident**

1. Recognize the event and determine whether it is a security incident or a routine issue.
2. Escalate promptly when the event may affect systems, networks, or digital assets beyond your authority.
3. Document what you observed, what you collected, and who handled the evidence.
4. Preserve evidence using the appropriate method for the item type.
5. Follow the organization’s incident response process and support remediation.

This sequence keeps the response organized and reduces the chance that important details are lost. It also helps responders make decisions based on reliable information rather than incomplete observations.

**Key response priorities**

- **Containment** limits further spread or damage.
- **Eradication** removes the threat from the environment.
- **Remediation** restores affected systems and addresses the impact.
- **Reporting** gives the security team the information needed to act.
- **Improvement** uses lessons learned to strengthen future response.

**Practical takeaway**

You do not need to solve every incident yourself. Your job is to notice the warning signs, escalate to the right team, and preserve the evidence and documentation needed for a reliable response. That approach supports continuity, reduces risk, and improves the organization’s ability to handle the next event.
