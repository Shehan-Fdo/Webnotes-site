## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Encryption fundamentals**

Encryption protects data by making it unreadable without the proper key or authorization. In A+ contexts, the main goal is to reduce the impact of lost, stolen, or otherwise exposed devices and storage media.

A practical way to think about encryption is that it protects data at rest. If someone gains physical access to a device, encryption helps prevent immediate access to the contents.

- **Full-disk encryption** protects everything on the drive, including documents, files, and the operating system.
- **Mobile devices** benefit strongly from full-disk encryption because they are easier to lose or steal.
- **Removable media** and individual files can also be encrypted when you need narrower protection.
- **Databases** may be encrypted when sensitive records need additional protection.

**Full-disk and volume protection**

Full-disk encryption encrypts the entire storage device, not just selected folders. That means the operating system and all user data remain protected if the drive is removed and connected elsewhere.

This approach is especially useful when a device leaves your control. If the hardware is stolen, the encrypted contents remain difficult to access without the correct credentials or recovery information.

- The entire drive is protected, not just selected files.
- It is well suited to laptops and other portable systems.
- It helps reduce exposure when devices are lost, stolen, or decommissioned.

**BitLocker and device encryption**

BitLocker is a Windows security feature that provides encryption for entire volumes. Microsoft describes it as a way to address threats from lost, stolen, or inappropriately decommissioned devices.

Device encryption is a related Windows feature that protects data so only authorized users can access it. On some systems, standard BitLocker encryption may be available if device encryption is not.

> Recovery information matters. Some Windows device-encryption setups automatically back up the BitLocker key to a Microsoft account, so account access and recovery planning are important.

- **BitLocker** protects entire volumes in Windows.
- **Device encryption** is available on a wide range of Windows devices.
- **Recovery keys** should be backed up or otherwise protected for future access.

**Where encryption is commonly applied**

Encryption is not limited to one storage type. In exam scenarios, you should recognize the common places where it is used to protect sensitive data.

- **Full disks** for whole-device protection
- **Databases** for structured sensitive data
- **Individual files** when only specific content needs protection
- **Removable media** such as external drives
- **Mobile devices** that are frequently carried or lost

**Why encryption matters in endpoint security**

Endpoint protection uses layered defenses to keep devices safe. Encryption is one layer in that defense-in-depth approach because it protects data even when other controls fail or a device is physically compromised.

This makes encryption a practical safeguard for desktops, laptops, tablets, and phones that store sensitive information locally.

**Quick exam reminders**

- Encryption protects data from unauthorized access.
- Full-disk encryption covers the operating system and all files on the drive.
- BitLocker is the Windows volume-encryption feature to remember.
- Device encryption may be available on supported Windows devices.
- Recovery keys are part of the operational planning for encrypted systems.
