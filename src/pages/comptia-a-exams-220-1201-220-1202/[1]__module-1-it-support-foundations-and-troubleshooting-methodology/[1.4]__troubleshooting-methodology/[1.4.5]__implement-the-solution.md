## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Overview**

The **implement the solution** step is where you carry out the chosen fix from the troubleshooting process. CompTIA’s methodology places this after you identify the problem, establish and test a theory, and create a plan of action. At this stage, you either apply the fix or escalate the issue if the solution is outside your scope.

This step matters because a good theory is not enough on its own. You need to make the change carefully, with awareness of any side effects the action may create.

- Apply the agreed fix from your plan of action.
- Escalate the issue when the solution is not appropriate for you to complete.
- Consider possible effects before you make the change.

**Where this step fits in the troubleshooting model**

CompTIA’s troubleshooting methodology follows a structured sequence:

1. Identify the problem.
2. Establish a theory of probable cause.
3. Test the theory to determine the cause.
4. Establish a plan of action to resolve the problem and identify potential effects.
5. Implement the solution or escalate as necessary.
6. Verify full system functionality and, if applicable, implement preventive measures.

This sequence keeps you from jumping straight to a fix before you understand the issue. The implementation step is the point where analysis becomes action.

- **Plan of action** comes first, so you know what you are changing.
- **Escalate** when the issue needs a higher level of support.
- **Verify** after implementation to confirm the fix worked.

**Implementing the fix**

When you implement the solution, you carry out the selected corrective action in a controlled way. The evidence emphasizes that troubleshooting is a structured process built from experience, so the goal is not just speed. The goal is to solve the issue efficiently without creating new problems.

Use practical judgment when applying the fix:

- Make the change that matches the confirmed cause.
- Avoid unnecessary changes that were not part of the plan.
- Keep track of what you changed so you can explain the result later.

If the issue cannot be resolved at your level, escalation is part of the process rather than a failure. It is the correct action when the solution requires different permissions, expertise, or responsibility.

> Do not treat implementation as the final step. You still need to verify the system works and check for any unintended effects.

**Escalation and risk awareness**

The troubleshooting model explicitly includes escalation as an option. That means you should not force a solution when it is not appropriate or when it may exceed your authority. The plan of action should already account for possible effects, so you can decide whether to proceed or hand off the issue.

- Escalate when the fix is outside your role or access level.
- Escalate when the risk of change is too high for the current situation.
- Escalate when the cause is not fully confirmed and more investigation is needed.

This keeps the process safe and professional. It also helps preserve system stability while the issue moves to the right support level.

**Practical takeaway**

The implementation step is about disciplined execution. You use the evidence from earlier troubleshooting steps, apply the chosen solution, and escalate when necessary. Then you move on to verification so you can confirm the system is fully functional.

**Quick review**

- Identify the problem before you act.
- Confirm the cause with a tested theory.
- Build a plan that includes possible effects.
- Implement the solution or escalate as necessary.
- Verify the result after the change.
