## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Troubleshooting methodology overview**

The first step in the CompTIA troubleshooting methodology is to **identify the problem**. This step sets the direction for every later action: you gather facts, clarify symptoms, and define what is actually failing before you form a theory or make changes.

CompTIA describes troubleshooting as a structured process that begins with identifying the problem and then moves through probable cause, testing, planning, implementation, and verification. In practice, this means you should slow down early, collect accurate information, and avoid jumping to a fix too soon.

- Focus on the observed symptom, not your first guess.
- Gather enough detail to describe the issue clearly.
- Keep the problem statement specific and practical.

**What to identify**

At this stage, you are trying to understand the scope and behavior of the issue. The goal is to separate the real problem from assumptions, unrelated complaints, or incomplete reports.

- What is not working as expected
- When the issue started
- Which device, user, or system is affected
- Whether the issue is isolated or widespread
- Any recent changes that may relate to the problem

A strong problem definition helps you avoid wasted effort later. If you cannot describe the issue clearly, it is harder to choose a valid theory of probable cause or test the right thing.

**Information gathering**

You identify the problem by asking targeted questions and observing the environment. The evidence emphasizes that troubleshooting depends on both experience and a repeatable framework, so your early questions should support that framework.

- Ask the user to describe the issue in their own words.
- Note exact error messages, symptoms, or unusual behavior.
- Check whether the issue can be reproduced.
- Compare the affected system with a known-good system when appropriate.

Use active listening and careful observation. Small details often matter, especially when the issue involves intermittent behavior or a partial failure.

**Practical approach**

1. Confirm the reported symptom.
2. Determine the scope of the issue.
3. Collect relevant details about recent changes, errors, and affected systems.
4. Restate the problem in a concise, technical way.

```text
Reported issue: "The printer is broken."
Refined problem: "The office printer on the third floor does not print from any workstation, but local test pages still work."
```

This refinement matters because it turns a vague complaint into a usable troubleshooting statement. Once the problem is clearly identified, you can move on to establishing a theory of probable cause.

**Why this step matters**

The identify-the-problem step reduces guesswork. It gives you a reliable starting point and helps you stay organized under pressure. For A+ exam prep, remember that this step is about understanding the issue before attempting to solve it.

- It improves troubleshooting accuracy.
- It prevents unnecessary changes.
- It supports faster diagnosis in later steps.
- It creates a clear baseline for verification.

**Exam focus**

For CompTIA A+, expect this step to emphasize structured problem solving and careful fact gathering. The key idea is simple: define the issue first, then investigate causes.

> Do not skip directly to a fix. A poorly defined problem often leads to the wrong solution.
