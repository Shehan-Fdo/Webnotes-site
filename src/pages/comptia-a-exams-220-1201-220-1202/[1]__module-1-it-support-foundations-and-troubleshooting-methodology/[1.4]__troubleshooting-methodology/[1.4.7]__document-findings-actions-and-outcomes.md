## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Why documentation matters**

Documenting findings, actions, and outcomes closes the troubleshooting loop. It helps you explain what happened, what you tried, and what resolved the issue so the customer does not have to repeat the same story to multiple agents. Good notes also support accountability, reduce repeated work, and preserve knowledge for future support.

In practice, ticket notes and work-order records turn a one-time fix into a reusable reference. They help teams avoid guesswork, improve consistency, and keep escalations organized.

- **Findings** capture the problem you observed.
- **Actions** record the steps you took to diagnose or repair the issue.
- **Outcomes** show whether the fix worked and what the customer confirmed.

**What to document**

After the repair or troubleshooting session, record the details in the work order and your journal if your process uses both. Keep the record clear enough that another technician can understand the case without asking follow-up questions.

Include the core items that were identified in the troubleshooting process:

- Description of the problem
- Steps taken to resolve the problem
- Components used in the repair

You can also note who handled the ticket and any important context about the interaction. That level of detail helps when the issue returns later or needs escalation.

**Verify the solution**

Before you close the ticket, confirm that the problem is actually resolved. If the customer is available, show them the fix and let them test it. This verification step matters because a repair is only complete when the customer can confirm the issue no longer occurs.

1. Explain the problem and the solution to the customer verbally or in writing.
2. Demonstrate the fix if the customer is present.
3. Have the customer test the system and try to reproduce the issue.
4. Close the documentation only after the customer verifies the outcome.

> If the customer cannot verify the fix immediately, document the limitation clearly so the next technician understands the current status.

**Good ticket notes support the team**

Detailed notes create accountability and make escalations easier to manage. They also reduce the chance that another technician repeats the same troubleshooting steps or misses an important repair detail. Over time, this improves service quality and helps the support team work faster with less rework.

A useful note set is concise, factual, and complete. It should tell the next person:

- What was wrong
- What you tried
- What changed
- Whether the customer confirmed success

**Practical note-taking habits**

Use a consistent format so your records stay readable across tickets. Write in active voice, keep entries specific, and avoid vague statements that do not help the next technician.

- Use clear language instead of shorthand that others may not understand.
- Record the exact steps you performed when they matter to the fix.
- Capture the final state of the system and the customer’s confirmation.
- Keep the note focused on support value, not on unnecessary detail.
