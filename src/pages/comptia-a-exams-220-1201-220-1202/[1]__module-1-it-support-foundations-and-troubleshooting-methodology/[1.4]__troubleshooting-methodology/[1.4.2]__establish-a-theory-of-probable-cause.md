## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**What this step does**

Establishing a theory of probable cause is the troubleshooting step where you use the information you have gathered to identify the most likely reason a problem is happening. It sits after you identify the problem and before you test the theory.

This step is not about guessing. You use symptoms, user reports, and your own experience to narrow the field of possible causes. If new evidence appears, you reassess the theory and keep an open mind.

- Focus on the **most likely** cause first.
- Use observed symptoms and user input to guide your thinking.
- Revisit your theory when new information changes the picture.
- Treat this as the foundation for the next troubleshooting step.

**How you build the theory**

You start by reviewing what the user reported and what you observed during the initial investigation. Then you compare those details with your knowledge of common failures and likely patterns. In practice, this is a structured reasoning step, not a final diagnosis.

A good theory is practical and testable. It should point you toward a specific cause that you can confirm or rule out in the next step of the troubleshooting process.

- Gather information from the user.
- Analyze the symptoms you can verify.
- Consider likely causes based on experience and documentation.
- Refine the theory as evidence changes.

**What helps you avoid weak theories**

Weak theories usually come from incomplete information or from locking onto one idea too early. The evidence emphasizes that good troubleshooting depends on the quality of the information you collect, so this step works best when you have enough detail to compare symptoms against possible causes.

Research, documentation, and consultation with colleagues can help when the issue is unfamiliar. These resources support better judgment and reduce the chance that you miss a more likely explanation.

> Do not treat the first idea as the answer. Keep testing your assumptions against the facts you collect.

**How this fits the troubleshooting model**

CompTIA’s troubleshooting methodology places this step in a larger sequence:

1. Identify the problem.
2. Establish a theory of probable cause.
3. Test the theory to determine the cause.
4. Establish a plan of action to resolve the problem and identify potential effects.
5. Implement the solution or escalate as necessary.
6. Verify full system functionality and, if applicable, implement preventive measures.

This means your theory should be strong enough to support testing, but flexible enough to change if the test results do not match your expectation.

**Practical exam focus**

For exam purposes, remember the purpose of this step: you are forming a likely explanation from the evidence you have, not proving it yet. The next step is where you validate or reject that explanation.

- Use the evidence to narrow the cause.
- Keep the theory specific enough to test.
- Avoid skipping ahead to the solution.
- Expect to revise the theory if the facts do not fit.
