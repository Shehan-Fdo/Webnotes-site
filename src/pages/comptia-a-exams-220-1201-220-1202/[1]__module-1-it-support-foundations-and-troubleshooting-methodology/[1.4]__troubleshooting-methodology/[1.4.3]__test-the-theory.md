## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Overview**

The **test the theory** step is where you verify a likely cause instead of guessing. In the CompTIA A+ troubleshooting flow, you use the information you gathered earlier to check the most probable causes first and narrow the issue to an exact cause.

This step sits after you establish a theory of probable cause and before you decide on a fix. The goal is practical: confirm or reject each theory with evidence, then move forward with the most likely answer.

- Test one theory at a time.
- Start with the quickest and easiest checks.
- Keep an open mind if new evidence changes your direction.
- If a theory fails, form a new one and test again.

**What you do in this step**

You compare symptoms against your theory and use targeted checks to prove whether the theory explains the problem. This is a disciplined troubleshooting habit, not a broad search. The evidence emphasizes that you determine the exact cause by testing theories one at a time.

A useful approach is to keep the process narrow and repeatable:

1. Review the symptoms and the theory you want to test.
2. Perform the simplest check that can confirm or reject the theory.
3. Observe the result and decide whether the theory still fits.
4. If needed, revise the theory and test the next most likely cause.

- Use the fastest test first when multiple causes are possible.
- Avoid jumping to a repair before you have evidence.
- Document each test you try, especially if the issue remains unresolved.

**Why this step matters**

Testing the theory prevents wasted effort. It helps you isolate the exact cause before you commit to a plan of action. That makes the rest of the troubleshooting process more efficient and reduces the chance of fixing the wrong thing.

If you cannot determine the exact cause after testing all reasonable theories, you should escalate the issue to a more experienced technician. Before escalation, record what you tested and what happened so the next person can continue without repeating work.

> <mark>Important:</mark> If a theory does not hold up, do not force it. Reassess and test a new probable cause.

**Troubleshooting flow context**

This step is part of a larger sequence:

1. Identify the problem.
2. Establish a theory of probable cause.
3. Test the theory to determine cause.
4. Establish a plan of action to resolve the problem.
5. Verify full system functionality.
6. Document findings.

- The sequence moves from observation to verification.
- Testing comes before planning the fix.
- Documentation supports escalation and future reference.

**Practical exam focus**

For exam prep, remember the purpose of this step: you are proving the cause, not solving the issue yet. If a question asks what comes after a theory of probable cause, the answer is to test the theory to determine the cause.
