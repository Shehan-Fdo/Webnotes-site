## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Why objective mapping matters**

CompTIA A+ uses two exams, Core 1 (`220-1201`) and Core 2 (`220-1202`), and the objectives span a wide range of entry-level IT skills. Mapping those objectives to your course content gives you a clear study roadmap instead of a vague reading plan. It helps you see what you already cover, what still needs work, and which topics belong to each exam.

The objective mapping approach is practical because the exams cover distinct but related domains. Core 1 emphasizes hardware, networking, mobile devices, virtualization, cloud concepts, and troubleshooting. Core 2 emphasizes operating systems, security, software troubleshooting, backup and recovery, and operational procedures. A good map keeps those boundaries visible while still showing how the skills connect in real support work.

- Use the exam objectives as your checklist, not just your textbook chapters.
- Track coverage by topic so you can spot gaps early.
- Separate Core 1 and Core 2 content to avoid mixing study priorities.
- Revisit objectives that appear in troubleshooting scenarios, since they often require applied understanding.

**Building an objective map**

A useful map links each objective to a lesson, lab, note set, or practice question source. The goal is not to create more paperwork; it is to make your study plan measurable. If an objective has no matching resource, it becomes an immediate gap to fill.

A simple mapping workflow keeps the process manageable:

1. List the official objectives for the exam version you are taking.
2. Match each objective to the course lesson or resource that covers it.
3. Mark objectives as covered, partially covered, or not covered.
4. Prioritize the weak or missing items before moving to broad review.
5. Recheck the map after practice tests to confirm whether the gap is real or just a wording issue.

**Weak-area remediation strategy**

Weak-area remediation means you stop treating all topics equally and focus on the ones that lower your score or confidence. CompTIA recommends planning ahead and using practice exams to identify areas that need attention. That makes remediation a feedback loop: test, identify, review, and retest.

The most effective remediation is specific. If you miss questions on networking basics, do not just reread the entire networking chapter. Narrow the fix to the exact concept, then verify it with practice.

- Review the objective statement to confirm what the exam expects.
- Relearn the concept in a focused pass using notes, labs, or short videos.
- Practice the skill or scenario until you can explain it without prompts.
- Retest with questions that target the same objective.
- Keep a short log of repeated misses so you can see patterns.

> If you keep missing the same objective, treat it as a skill gap, not a memory problem. Rebuild the concept from the objective wording upward.

**Core 1 and Core 2 focus areas**

The two exams emphasize different skill sets, so remediation should stay version-aware and exam-specific. Core 1 centers on foundational hardware and connectivity work, while Core 2 shifts toward software, systems, and support procedures. Both exams still expect troubleshooting, so weak-area work should include scenario practice, not just definitions.

| Exam | Main emphasis | Remediation focus |
|---|---|---|
| `220-1201` Core 1 | Hardware, networking, mobile devices, virtualization, cloud concepts, troubleshooting tools | Component identification, connectivity issues, SOHO network basics, and applied troubleshooting |
| `220-1202` Core 2 | Operating systems, security, software troubleshooting, backup and recovery, operational procedures | OS support tasks, security concepts, recovery steps, and procedure-based questions |

When you study across both exams, keep the same version in mind. CompTIA notes that Core 1 and Core 2 must be taken from the same version, so your objective map should align to the same release rather than mixing older and newer material.

**Using practice exams effectively**

Practice exams are most useful when you use them to diagnose, not just to score. A low score is only helpful if it tells you which objective to revisit. After each practice set, sort misses into content gaps, careless errors, and wording misunderstandings.

- Content gaps mean you need more study on the objective itself.
- Careless errors mean you know the topic but need better reading discipline.
- Wording misunderstandings mean you need more exposure to exam-style phrasing.

A short review cycle works well:

1. Take a timed practice set.
2. Record every missed objective.
3. Group misses by exam domain.
4. Review the weakest domain first.
5. Retake questions after remediation to confirm improvement.

**Keeping remediation efficient**

Efficient remediation stays narrow, repeatable, and measurable. You do not need to restart the whole course when one area is weak. Instead, return to the objective map, isolate the problem, and use the smallest study loop that fixes it.

Good remediation habits include:

- Updating your map after each practice session.
- Spending more time on repeated misses than on already-mastered topics.
- Using hands-on labs when the objective involves configuration or troubleshooting.
- Balancing review across hardware, software, security, and procedures so one weak area does not hide another.

A well-maintained objective map turns exam prep into a controlled process. It shows where your study time goes, which objectives still need attention, and when you are ready to move from remediation to final review.
