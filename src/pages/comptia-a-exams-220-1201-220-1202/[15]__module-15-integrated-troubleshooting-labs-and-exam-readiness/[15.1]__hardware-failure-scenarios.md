## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Hardware failure symptoms**

Hardware problems often show up as startup failures, sudden power loss, or unstable behavior during use. A practical troubleshooting approach starts by identifying whether the system fails during startup, while running, or only under load.

- **POST** (power-on self-test) checks key components as the PC starts.
- Beeps or on-screen messages can point to missing or faulty hardware.
- If video fails, you may hear beeps without seeing the error on screen.
- A sudden black screen with no warning can indicate a power or thermal issue.
- Systems may shut down automatically to protect components from overheating.

When you see a failure, focus on the timing and the symptoms. That helps you separate a boot-time problem from a runtime problem and reduces guesswork.

**Startup and POST behavior**

The POST process runs in seconds and verifies that core components are available. It checks the CPU, video, keyboard, and installed memory. If one of these items is missing or not working properly, the system may stop early in the boot process.

- Check for beep codes when the system does not display video.
- Look for messages that identify the failing component.
- Treat missing memory, inactive CPU behavior, and video problems as likely startup blockers.
- Use the presence or absence of display output to judge whether the error can be read on screen.

> If the video subsystem fails, the system may still report the problem through beeps because the screen cannot be trusted.

**Power and shutdown failures**

Unexpected shutdowns are especially disruptive because they often happen without warning. In the evidence, a system can turn off suddenly and leave only a black screen. One practical next step is to power the system back on and check Windows Event Viewer for clues.

- Sudden shutdowns may leave no visible error message.
- Event Viewer can contain useful post-failure details in Windows.
- Heat buildup is a common concern when the system powers off unexpectedly.
- High-CPU or graphics-heavy workloads can raise temperatures faster.

**Overheating checks**

Overheating can trigger protective shutdowns. This is more likely when the system is under heavy CPU or graphics load. Fan operation and airflow matter because blocked cooling can cause temperatures to rise quickly.

1. Check that all fans are spinning.
2. Make sure nothing blocks airflow through the case.
3. Consider whether the failure happens during demanding applications.
4. Compare the shutdown timing with system load to see whether heat is the trigger.

**Troubleshooting mindset**

A structured troubleshooting method helps you avoid random part swapping. Start with the symptom, then narrow the failure to startup, power, or thermal behavior. If the system fails before video initializes, rely on beep codes and POST behavior. If it fails after running for a while, focus on power delivery and cooling.

- Use the symptom timing to guide your next step.
- Separate boot failures from runtime failures.
- Check for environmental causes such as blocked airflow.
- Use Windows logs when the system still boots far enough to record them.

**Quick scenario cues**

| Scenario | Likely direction |
|---|---|
| Beeps at startup, no display | POST or video-related failure |
| Black screen with sudden power loss | Power or overheating issue |
| Shutdown during heavy workloads | Thermal protection or cooling problem |
| Error shown during boot | Component identified by POST |
