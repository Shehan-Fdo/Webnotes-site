## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Storage failure symptoms**

Storage devices are critical for operating system availability and data access. When a drive starts failing, the symptoms often show up as read/write errors, boot problems, or severe slowdowns. In A+ troubleshooting scenarios, you should treat these signs as indicators of possible data loss and act quickly.

- A message such as `Cannot read from the source disk` points to a read/write problem on the storage device.
- Very slow response during file access can mean the drive is retrying reads or writes.
- Constant LED activity on the drive can indicate repeated access attempts.
- A loud clicking noise from a hard disk drive is often called the **click of death**.

> If a storage device is making unusual noises or repeatedly failing to read data, assume the drive may be failing until proven otherwise.

**Performance clues and likely causes**

Slow storage performance can look like general system slowness, but the drive itself may be the bottleneck. The evidence highlights slow reads and writes, repeated access attempts, and LED activity as common signs. Broader system performance issues can also come from limited random access memory (RAM), because the system may rely more heavily on virtual memory stored on the hard drive.

- **RAM** shortages can push more activity to the hard drive and reduce responsiveness.
- Outdated software or incompatible drivers can also contribute to slow performance.
- Startup programs can increase boot time and make the system feel sluggish.

**Troubleshooting approach**

Use a structured troubleshooting mindset. Start with the most visible symptoms, confirm whether the issue is isolated to the storage device, and then decide whether the problem is performance-related, failure-related, or both. If the drive is failing, prioritize data protection before deeper testing.

1. Check for error messages during boot or file access.
2. Listen for clicking or other abnormal drive sounds.
3. Observe whether the drive activity light stays on continuously.
4. Determine whether the system is slow only during storage operations or across the whole machine.
5. Protect data and plan recovery if the drive appears to be failing.

**Data protection and recovery**

The evidence emphasizes that storage failures can lead to loss of access to important data. That means your first practical goal is to preserve what you can before the device becomes unreadable. In exam scenarios, the safest response is to avoid unnecessary writes and focus on recovery options when the drive is unstable.

- Treat repeated read failures as a warning sign for possible drive failure.
- Consider data recovery steps when the device still responds intermittently.
- Use RAID recovery options when the storage issue affects an array rather than a single drive.

**RAID and broader storage issues**

Storage troubleshooting is not limited to standalone drives. RAID arrays can also fail or become degraded, and those problems can affect availability and performance. A degraded array may still function, but it needs attention to restore redundancy and reduce risk.

- A **RAID** issue can involve a controller problem or a failed member drive.
- A degraded array may continue operating with reduced fault tolerance.
- Recovery steps depend on whether the issue is with the drive, the controller, or the array state.

**Exam focus**

For CompTIA A+, focus on recognizing symptoms and matching them to the likely storage problem. The exam expects you to connect the clue to the cause, then choose the safest next step.

- `Cannot read from the source disk` suggests a storage read/write problem.
- Slow access plus constant drive activity suggests repeated retries.
- Clicking from a hard drive suggests mechanical failure.
- Storage problems can affect booting, application loading, and data access.
