## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Ethernet cable basics**

Ethernet networks commonly use **twisted-pair copper cabling** for wired communication. The cable contains four wire pairs, and the pairs are color-coded to keep the conductors organized during termination and troubleshooting.

- One pair uses solid blue and blue/white conductors.
- One pair uses solid green and green/white conductors.
- One pair uses solid orange and orange/white conductors.
- One pair uses solid brown and brown/white conductors.

The cable choice matters because wired network cabling is the communication path that carries traffic between devices. In practical deployments, you want the correct cable type before installation, especially in new builds or data center work where rework is costly.

**Category cable focus**

Ethernet cable categories describe the cable’s construction and performance characteristics. For exam prep, focus on the category name, the intended use, and the fact that higher categories are generally associated with better support for modern network demands.

- **Cat5e** is widely used in modern networks.
- **Cat6** and **Cat6a** are common higher-category options.
- Category selection affects how well the cable supports network speed and throughput requirements.

A useful exam mindset is to match the cable to the job rather than assuming any Ethernet cable is interchangeable. The evidence emphasizes that cabling must keep up with the network gear and the intended data transfer rate.

**What to remember about Cat5e, Cat6, and Cat6a**

These three categories are the main ones highlighted in the evidence. They are often compared when planning or upgrading a network.

| Category | Practical takeaway |
|---|---|
| Cat5e | Common choice for modern Ethernet; supports gigabit use cases. |
| Cat6 | Higher-category option for stronger performance needs. |
| Cat6a | Another higher-category option used when more headroom is needed. |

The exact speed and distance limits are not fully specified in the evidence here, so keep your study conservative: know that these categories differ in capability, and that the network design must align with the cable’s supported performance.

**Why cable selection matters**

Cable selection affects more than connectivity. It influences whether the network can deliver the expected speed and reliability.

- Use the right cable category for the planned network performance.
- Consider the cable during initial installation, not after the network is already built.
- Treat cabling as part of the overall network design, alongside switches, routers, and other devices.

> When the evidence does not provide a specific maximum speed or distance, do not guess on the exam. Use the category comparison to eliminate clearly unsuitable choices.

**Study approach**

To prepare for questions on Ethernet categories, focus on recognition and practical matching.

1. Identify that Ethernet commonly uses twisted-pair copper cabling.
2. Recall that the cable contains four color-coded pairs.
3. Associate Cat5e, Cat6, and Cat6a with category-based performance differences.
4. Choose the cable that fits the network’s intended speed and deployment needs.

**Quick recall**

- **Twisted pair** is the common Ethernet copper medium.
- **Four pairs** are present in the cable.
- **Cat5e**, **Cat6**, and **Cat6a** are the key category names to know.
- Cable choice should match the network’s performance requirements.
