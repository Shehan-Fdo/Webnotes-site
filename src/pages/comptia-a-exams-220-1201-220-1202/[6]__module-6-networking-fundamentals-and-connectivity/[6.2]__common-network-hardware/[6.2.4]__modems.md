## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Modem fundamentals**

A modem is a common internet access device, especially in cable-based home and small office setups. In the A+ context, you should recognize that cable modems reuse existing coaxial infrastructure that originally carried video services and now carries data, voice, and video traffic.

- **Cable modem** connections use coaxial lines.
- **DOCSIS** stands for Data over Cable Service Interface Specification.
- DOCSIS supports data rates from about 4 Mbps up to 250 Mbps and higher with newer standards.
- Cable networks can carry data, video, and voice on the same infrastructure.

**DSL and related access types**

DSL, or digital subscriber line, also uses existing wiring already present in homes and businesses. The evidence specifically calls out ADSL, or asymmetric digital subscriber line, as a common form. The key exam idea is that both cable and DSL leverage pre-existing physical plant rather than requiring entirely new cabling.

- **DSL** uses existing telephone lines.
- **ADSL** is an asymmetric form of DSL.
- These services are examples of internet connection types that reuse installed wiring.

**What to check when a cable modem fails**

When a cable modem cannot connect, start with the basics before assuming the device is defective. Practical troubleshooting begins with power, cable seating, and visible indicators such as modem lights.

1. Check that the modem is plugged in securely.
2. Verify that the power and coaxial cables are fully inserted.
3. Inspect cables for fraying, bends, or damage.
4. Observe the modem lights.
5. Compare the light pattern with the modem manual or manufacturer guidance.

> No lights at all usually point to a power problem, while flashing lights can indicate the modem is trying to sync with the internet service provider (ISP).

**Troubleshooting mindset for network connectivity**

A structured approach helps you separate a modem issue from a broader network problem. For wired troubleshooting, a link light confirms that the Ethernet connection is at least signaling to the device on the other end. If there is no link light, suspect a bad cable or a poor connection.

- **Link light** on an Ethernet port suggests physical connectivity.
- No link light can indicate a bad cable or no valid connection.
- Pinging `127.0.0.1` checks the local loopback stack on the device itself.
- This local test helps you determine whether the issue is inside the device or farther out on the network.

**Exam focus summary**

For A+ exam prep, keep the modem topic practical:

- Cable modems use coax and DOCSIS.
- DSL uses existing telephone wiring.
- Modem troubleshooting starts with power, cabling, and status lights.
- Network checks often begin with link lights and loopback testing.
