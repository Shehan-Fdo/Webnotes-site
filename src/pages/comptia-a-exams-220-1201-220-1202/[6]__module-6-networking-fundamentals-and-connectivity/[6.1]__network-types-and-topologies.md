## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Network types: what the labels mean**

Network types are usually classified by size and location. That makes the category useful when you are planning connectivity, comparing performance, or troubleshooting where traffic is going.

- **LAN** stands for *Local Area Network* and covers a close area such as a floor, building, or group of nearby buildings.
- **WAN** stands for *Wide Area Network* and connects locations over long distances, such as across cities, states, or countries.
- **PAN** stands for *Personal Area Network* and links personal devices over short range, often with Bluetooth.
- **WLAN** stands for *Wireless Local Area Network* and is a local network that uses wireless connectivity.
- **SAN** stands for *Storage Area Network* and is a specialized network used for storage access.

A practical way to remember the distinction is that local networks usually deliver faster throughput than distant networks, while wide-area links trade speed for geographic reach.

**Why network types matter in practice**

You use network type to set expectations. A local Ethernet or wireless connection should behave differently from a remote connection to another city or country. That difference affects design choices, performance expectations, and troubleshooting steps.

- Local connections usually provide the fastest speeds.
- Remote connections usually have more latency and lower throughput.
- Wireless local networks still count as local networks when they serve a nearby area.
- Specialized networks, such as storage networks, serve a specific purpose rather than general user access.

**Common topologies**

A topology describes how devices and links are arranged. It helps you understand the path data takes between endpoints, which is useful for both design and troubleshooting.

- **Star** topology places a central device in the middle, with endpoints connecting outward like spokes.
- **Mesh** topology uses multiple links between devices or to the same destination.
- **Bus** topology shares a common backbone.
- **Ring** topology connects devices in a loop.
- **Hybrid** topology combines more than one topology type.
- **Wireless** topology uses radio-based connections rather than physical cabling.

**Star topology**

Star is one of the most common layouts. In a switched Ethernet network, the switch sits at the center and devices connect back to it.

- The central device is easy to identify.
- A single endpoint failure usually affects only that endpoint.
- The central device becomes an important point of dependency.

**Mesh topology**

Mesh provides multiple paths between devices or to the same destination. That gives it strong resilience.

- **Redundancy** improves availability if one link fails.
- **Fault tolerance** lets traffic continue over alternate paths.
- **Load balancing** can spread traffic across multiple links.
- Fully connected networks are a common way to describe mesh.

> A mesh design is useful when continuity matters more than simplicity, but it usually requires more links and more planning.

**Other topology types**

Some topologies are less common in modern networks, but they still appear in exam material and in design discussions.

- **Bus** uses a shared communication path.
- **Ring** sends traffic around a closed loop.
- **Hybrid** mixes two or more topology styles.
- **Wireless** focuses on the logical arrangement of radio-connected devices.

**How to study topology questions**

When you see a topology question, first identify the path traffic takes. Then match the layout to the likely strengths and weaknesses.

1. Identify the network goal, such as resilience, simplicity, or coverage.
2. Look for the physical or logical arrangement of devices.
3. Match the arrangement to the topology name.
4. Check whether the question is asking about design or troubleshooting.

**Quick comparison**

| Type | Main idea | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| LAN | Local connectivity | Nearby devices and shared resources |
| WAN | Long-distance connectivity | Remote sites and broad geographic links |
| PAN | Personal device connectivity | Phones, tablets, and nearby peripherals |
| WLAN | Wireless local connectivity | Local access without cables |
| SAN | Dedicated storage connectivity | Storage traffic and storage devices |

**Exam focus**

For A+ questions, focus on the relationship between distance, speed, and purpose.

- Local networks are close and usually faster.
- Wide-area networks span larger distances and usually run slower than local links.
- Topologies describe how devices connect, not just what devices are present.
- Mesh emphasizes alternate paths and resilience.
- Star emphasizes a central connection point.
