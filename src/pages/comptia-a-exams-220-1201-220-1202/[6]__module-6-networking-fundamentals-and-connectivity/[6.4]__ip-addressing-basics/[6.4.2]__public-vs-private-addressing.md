## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Addressing overview**

An <abbr title="Internet Protocol">IP</abbr> address identifies a host or network interface and helps establish where traffic should go. For A+ exam purposes, you need to distinguish between addresses used inside a local network and addresses used on the internet.

- **Public IP address**: Routable on the internet and typically assigned by an <abbr title="Internet Service Provider">ISP</abbr> to the network edge.
- **Private IP address**: Used inside local networks such as home, office, and enterprise <abbr title="Local Area Network">LAN</abbr>s.
- IPv4 is a 32-bit address format commonly written as four decimal numbers, such as `192.168.1.131`.
- IPv6 is also part of the exam scope, but the core public/private distinction is most often discussed with IPv4.

**Private address ranges**

Private IPv4 ranges are reserved for internal use and are not meant to be routed directly on the public internet. These ranges help conserve public IPv4 space and let many organizations reuse the same internal addressing scheme without coordination with an Internet registry.

- `10.0.0.0` to `10.255.255.255`
- `172.16.0.0` to `172.31.255.255`
- `192.168.0.0` to `192.168.255.255`

> Private addresses are not allocated to a specific organization, so anyone can use them inside a private network without approval from regional or local Internet registries.

**How public and private addressing work together**

In a typical home network, your router has the public address that the internet can reach, while your devices use private addresses behind it. This setup allows multiple internal devices to share one public IPv4 address.

- The router is the internet-facing point of contact.
- Internal devices keep private addresses for local communication.
- <abbr title="Network Address Translation">NAT</abbr> or <abbr title="Port Address Translation">PAT</abbr> is commonly used to provide internet connectivity to multiple hosts through a single public address.
- Private addressing also adds a practical layer of isolation because internal devices are not directly reachable from the internet by default.

**Exam-ready comparison**

| Aspect | Public addressing | Private addressing |
|---|---|---|
| Reachability | Routable on the internet | Used inside local networks |
| Assignment | Usually provided by an ISP | Chosen from reserved private ranges |
| Scope | External communication | Internal communication |
| Typical use | Internet-facing router or service | PCs, phones, printers, and other LAN devices |

**What to remember for troubleshooting**

When you check connectivity, first determine whether the device should be using a private address on the LAN or a public address at the edge. If internal devices can talk to each other but cannot reach the internet, the issue may involve the router, NAT/PAT, or upstream ISP connectivity rather than the private address itself.

- Verify the device has an address in the correct private range when it is on an internal network.
- Confirm the router has a valid public address if the whole network cannot reach external sites.
- Use the address type to narrow the fault domain before changing settings.
