## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**5 GHz in 802.11 wireless networks**

The 5 GHz band is one of the frequency ranges used by <abbr title="Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers">IEEE</abbr> 802.11 wireless networks. Some standards use 2.4 GHz, some use 5 GHz, and some use both bands to communicate. In A+ terms, you should recognize 5 GHz as a common Wi‑Fi frequency choice, especially when comparing wireless standards and channel selection.

- **Frequency band**: 5 GHz is a wireless operating range used by some 802.11 standards.
- **Dual-band support**: Some wireless standards can communicate on both 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz.
- **Standard example**: 802.11a operates in the 5 GHz range.
- **Naming context**: 802.11ac is also called Wi‑Fi 5.

**Channels and access point planning**

Within the 5 GHz range, there are separate channels. A channel is a group of frequencies, and the IEEE assigns channel numbers so you can refer to them more easily. When you deploy multiple access points in the same area, you want them on different wireless channels to reduce overlap and improve coexistence.

- **Channel**: A defined group of frequencies inside the band.
- **Channel numbering**: IEEE channel numbers make configuration and troubleshooting easier.
- **Multi-AP design**: Use different channels for nearby access points when possible.
- **Practical focus**: Channel planning matters as much as the band itself.

**Why 5 GHz matters for exam prep**

For CompTIA A+, the key idea is not just that 5 GHz exists, but that it is part of wireless frequency planning. You should be able to connect the band to 802.11 standards, recognize that channels are assigned within the band, and understand that access points should avoid using the same channel in the same area.

> Keep the distinction clear: the band is the frequency range, while the channel is the specific slice used inside that range.

**Quick recall**

- 5 GHz is used by some 802.11 wireless standards.
- 802.11a uses 5 GHz.
- 802.11ac is Wi‑Fi 5.
- Multiple access points should use different channels when possible.
