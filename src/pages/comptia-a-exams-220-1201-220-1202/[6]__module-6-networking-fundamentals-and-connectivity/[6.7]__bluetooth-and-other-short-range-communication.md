## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Short-range wireless overview**

Short-range communication covers technologies that connect devices over small distances without a cable. For A+ purposes, focus on what each technology is best suited for, how far it reaches, and whether it needs direct alignment or physical contact.

- **Infrared** uses light-based signaling and needs line-of-sight.
- **Bluetooth** supports short-range device-to-device communication for common peripherals and nearby connections.
- **NFC** stands for Near Field Communication and works at very close range, often when devices are intentionally brought together.
- **RFID** appears alongside NFC in wireless technology discussions, but the evidence here only confirms it as a related short-range wireless topic.

**Infrared details**

Infrared is an older wireless standard from the Infrared Data Association, or **IrDA**. It provides about 4 megabits per second and works over roughly 3 feet. Because it uses infrared light, the devices must be able to “see” each other directly.

- Requires **line-of-sight**.
- Does not go around corners.
- Common on older laptops, printers, and cameras.
- Useful for transferring information between nearby devices.

> Infrared is practical only when the devices can be aligned directly and kept close together.

**Bluetooth and nearby wireless use**

Bluetooth is one of the short-range wireless technologies commonly grouped with NFC and infrared in A+ study material. The provided evidence does not give a specific speed or range value for Bluetooth, so keep your focus on its role as a short-range connection method rather than memorizing unsupported numbers.

- Used for nearby device connections.
- Fits the general category of short-range wireless communication.
- Often appears in the same study set as NFC and RFID.

**NFC fundamentals**

NFC stands for **Near Field Communication**. It is a short-range technology that works best over a few centimetres or less. Typical use requires you to deliberately touch or bring devices very close together to initiate contact.

- Supported speeds include 106, 212, and 424 kilobits per second.
- Designed for contactless interactions at very close range.
- Common in payment-related applications.
- Also used for travel tickets and close-proximity file sharing between phones.
- NFC Forum specifications include operating modes such as card emulation, reader/writer, wireless charging, and peer-to-peer.

**Common use cases to remember**

Short-range communication is often chosen for convenience, quick pairing, or intentional contact. Match the technology to the task rather than treating all wireless links the same.

- **Infrared**: older device transfers where direct alignment is possible.
- **NFC**: contactless payments, tickets, and close-range phone interactions.
- **Bluetooth**: short-range connections between nearby devices and peripherals.

**Exam focus**

For exam questions, identify the technology by its distance and behavior.

1. If the connection needs line-of-sight, think **infrared**.
2. If the devices must be almost touching, think **NFC**.
3. If the question describes a short-range wireless link between nearby devices, think **Bluetooth**.
4. If the prompt mentions contactless payments or tickets, choose **NFC**.

**Quick comparison**

| Technology | Range / behavior | Typical clue |
|---|---|---|
| Infrared | About 3 feet; line-of-sight | Older devices, direct alignment |
| Bluetooth | Short-range nearby connection | Wireless peripheral or device link |
| NFC | A few centimetres or less | Tap-to-pay, tickets, close contact |

**Key takeaways**

Short-range communication is about proximity and purpose. Infrared needs direct alignment, NFC works only at very close range, and Bluetooth serves nearby wireless connections. When you see a scenario question, use the distance and interaction style to identify the correct technology.
