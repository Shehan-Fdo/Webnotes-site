## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Power delivery basics**

A computer power supply unit (PSU) converts incoming power into the voltages that different components need. The key exam idea is that connectors are not interchangeable: each one is shaped and pinned for a specific device or motherboard header. When you identify a connector, think about both the device it powers and the voltages it carries.

- **SATA power** is the 15-pin connector used for modern storage devices.
- **Molex** is a more generic legacy power connector.
- **ATX** and **EPS** connectors supply motherboard and CPU power.
- **PCI Express** power interfaces support expansion cards that need extra power.

**Common connector types**

SATA power is the standard connector for many storage devices. It carries 3.3 volts, 5 volts, and 12 volts, although 3.3 volts is rarely used by the device. Its shape is keyed, so it only fits the SATA power socket.

Molex connectors are older and more general-purpose. They are still worth recognizing on the exam because they appear on legacy hardware and some adapters. Unlike SATA power, Molex is not tied to a single modern storage standard.

- **SATA power**
  - 15-pin connector
  - Used for storage devices
  - Carries 3.3 V, 5 V, and 12 V
- **Molex**
  - Legacy connector
  - More generic than SATA power
  - Common on older peripherals
- **ATX 12 V**
  - Motherboard power interface
  - Helps supply system power
- **EPS 12 V**
  - CPU power interface
  - Used where the processor needs dedicated power
- **PCI Express power**
  - Powers expansion cards
  - Used when the card needs more than the slot can provide

**Motherboard and CPU power**

The main motherboard power connector is the ATX connector. Modern boards commonly use a 24-pin ATX connector, which replaced the older 20-pin design to support higher power demands and better stability. In addition, the CPU often needs a separate EPS 12 V connector, so do not assume the main motherboard cable is enough for the processor.

> Use the correct motherboard and CPU power leads before powering on. A missing connector can prevent startup or cause unstable behavior.

**Storage and expansion power**

Storage devices usually need SATA power rather than Molex. The connector is small, keyed, and designed for current storage hardware. Expansion cards may need PCI Express power connectors when the motherboard slot cannot provide enough power on its own.

- Check the device type before choosing a connector.
- Match the connector shape to the socket.
- Verify that the PSU provides the required lead before installation.

**Troubleshooting power-related startup issues**

If a system does not power on, start with the basics: confirm the PSU is connected, the motherboard power connector is seated, and the CPU power connector is attached. A system can also fail to complete startup if core hardware is not receiving power, which may show up as no display, no fans, or no POST (Power On Self-Test).

1. Verify the PSU switch and wall power source.
2. Reseat the main motherboard power connector.
3. Reseat the CPU power connector.
4. Check storage and expansion card power leads.
5. Look for signs that POST begins, such as fans spinning or beep codes.

**Exam focus**

For A+ questions, focus on connector recognition and purpose rather than memorizing every pin. The most useful distinctions are:

- SATA power for modern storage
- Molex for legacy devices
- 24-pin ATX for motherboard power
- EPS 12 V for CPU power
- PCI Express power for graphics cards and other expansion devices

Understanding these roles helps you choose the right connector quickly and avoid mixing up motherboard, CPU, storage, and expansion power paths.
