## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**What M.2 is**

M.2 is a compact internal expansion standard used for storage and other add-in cards. It is commonly associated with solid-state drives (SSDs) because it fits modern systems that need small, fast, and flexible components.

The key exam idea is that M.2 is a **form factor**, not a storage protocol by itself. The module shape and connector style are separate from the interface it uses.

- M.2 is pronounced “em-dot-two.”
- It is also known historically as **NGFF** (Next Generation Form Factor).
- It was introduced to replace older compact standards such as **mSATA** and **mPCIe**.

**Why M.2 matters**

M.2 gained adoption because it reduces module footprint while supporting higher performance and more design flexibility. That makes it useful in thin laptops, ultrabooks, tablets, and other compact systems.

M.2 devices can support different interfaces, which is why two M.2 drives may look similar but behave differently. For exam purposes, focus on the distinction between the physical card and the signaling method it uses.

- M.2 supports both **SATA** and **PCIe**-based devices.
- Some M.2 modules are used for storage, while others can support other onboard expansion uses.
- The standard is designed for compact internal mounting on the motherboard.

**Sizes and physical layout**

M.2 modules come in multiple lengths, which helps manufacturers match capacity and space requirements. The width is commonly 22 mm, while the length varies by model.

Common lengths include:

- 30 mm
- 42 mm
- 60 mm
- 80 mm
- 110 mm

A common way to describe an M.2 module is by its width and length, such as `2280`, which indicates a 22 mm wide and 80 mm long module.

**Interfaces and compatibility**

M.2 supports more than one interface, so compatibility depends on both the card and the slot. A motherboard slot may accept certain key types and may support SATA, PCIe, or both.

> Do not assume every M.2 slot supports every M.2 drive. Check the slot’s supported interface and keying before installation.

- **SATA** M.2 devices use the SATA 6 Gb/s link.
- **PCIe** M.2 devices can provide higher performance.
- Compatibility depends on the motherboard slot and the module’s keying.

**Key exam distinctions**

M.2 questions often test whether you can separate the physical standard from the transport protocol. The same form factor can be used in different ways, so the label on the drive matters.

- **Form factor**: the physical size and connector style.
- **Interface**: the communication method, such as SATA or PCIe.
- **SSD**: solid-state drive, a common M.2 use case.

**Practical study takeaway**

When you see M.2 in an exam question, identify three things:

1. The module is compact and internally mounted.
2. The drive may use SATA or PCIe.
3. The slot and keying determine what is actually compatible.

That approach helps you avoid confusing M.2 with a specific protocol or assuming all M.2 devices perform the same way.
