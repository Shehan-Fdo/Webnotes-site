## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**HDD fundamentals**

A hard disk drive (HDD) is a **non-volatile** storage device, which means it keeps data even when power is off. It stores digital information on magnetic disks, also called platters, that are coated with magnetic material. An actuator arm with read/write heads moves over the spinning platters to access data.

HDDs are built for capacity and cost efficiency. They are commonly used where large storage space matters more than speed, including desktops, servers, and external storage such as NAS (Network Attached Storage) solutions.

- **Platters** hold the magnetic data surface.
- **Read/write heads** hover above the platters to access data.
- **Actuator arm** positions the heads over the correct track.
- **Random access** lets you retrieve data directly without sequential scanning.

**How HDDs behave**

HDDs rely on moving mechanical parts, so they are slower than solid-state drives (SSDs). The spinning platters and moving actuator arm create physical limits on access speed. Even so, the drive still provides direct access to stored data when the system powers on.

Because the data remains stored without power, HDDs are useful for long-term retention and general-purpose storage. The tradeoff is that mechanical complexity increases the chance of wear and failure over time.

> HDDs are vulnerable to mechanical wear, shock, heat, dust, and power-related damage.

**Common failure symptoms**

When an HDD begins to fail, the symptoms often show up in everyday use before total failure occurs. These signs usually point to problems with internal components such as read/write heads, spindle motor, firmware, or the drive surface itself.

Watch for these warning signs:

- Clicking or grinding noises
- Slow file access or slow system response
- Frequent crashes or freezes when accessing the drive
- Files that refuse to copy
- Data loss or corruption
- Boot errors
- The system failing to recognize the drive

Environmental and physical stress can accelerate failure. Heat, humidity, dust, power surges, drops, and normal wear all contribute to declining reliability. Early detection matters because it helps you protect data before the drive becomes unreadable.

**Troubleshooting approach**

When you suspect HDD problems, focus on symptoms that indicate mechanical or logical failure. A drive that makes unusual noises or causes repeated access delays often needs immediate attention and backup.

1. Confirm the reported symptom, such as slow access, crashes, or missing files.
2. Check whether the drive is detected by the system.
3. Look for physical warning signs, including clicking or grinding.
4. Back up important data as soon as possible.
5. Treat repeated errors, corruption, or boot issues as signs of possible drive failure.

**Exam focus**

For A+ purposes, remember the core contrasts:

- HDDs use **magnetic storage** on spinning platters.
- HDDs are **non-volatile** and retain data without power.
- HDDs offer **large capacity** at relatively low cost.
- HDDs are slower than SSDs because they contain moving parts.
- Failure symptoms often include noise, slowness, crashes, and data corruption.
