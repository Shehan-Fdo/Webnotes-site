## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**ATX overview**

ATX is a motherboard form factor, so it describes the board’s physical size and layout. In practice, you usually choose the motherboard first and then find a case that supports that form factor.

A motherboard’s layout is not something you can easily change later. Once you select a board, the number of expansion slots, memory slots, and connector placement are largely fixed by that model. That makes form factor an important early decision when you build or upgrade a system.

- **Form factor** defines the board’s physical dimensions and general layout.
- ATX is used as a standard desktop motherboard size.
- The motherboard choice affects case selection and overall build compatibility.
- Expansion and memory slot counts are tied to the selected board model.

**ATX size and compatibility**

A standard ATX motherboard is commonly listed as `305 mm x 244 mm` or `12 in x 9.6 in`. Case compatibility depends on whether the chassis supports that footprint and the board’s depth and width.

> Be careful with case listings that mention a size limit without stating which dimension they mean. A case may support ATX boards, but the exact fit still depends on the case’s supported dimensions.

When you compare parts, focus on the motherboard specification and the case specification together. The board must physically fit the case, and the case must provide the mounting and clearance needed for that form factor.

- Standard ATX dimensions are about `305 mm x 244 mm`.
- Case support should match the motherboard’s actual footprint.
- Some listings may describe support in a way that is not immediately clear.
- Physical fit matters even when the board and case are both labeled ATX.

**ATX in the motherboard family**

ATX is part of a broader set of motherboard form factors. Smaller systems may use other sizes, but they still share the same basic computer components such as CPU, memory, storage, and network connections. The main difference is the total footprint and how much room the board provides for expansion.

ATX boards generally give you a larger desktop-style layout than smaller form factors. That larger size can influence how many slots and connectors are available, which is why ATX is often associated with more traditional desktop builds.

- ATX is one option within the motherboard form factor family.
- Smaller systems may use different form factors for reduced footprint.
- The same core components can exist across different board sizes.
- Larger boards often provide more room for expansion features.

**Build planning tips**

Use the motherboard form factor as a compatibility checkpoint before you buy parts. If you know the board size, you can verify the case fit and avoid problems during assembly.

1. Check the motherboard’s form factor in the product specifications.
2. Confirm the case supports that form factor.
3. Compare the listed dimensions if the case support is unclear.
4. Verify that the board’s layout works for your expansion and memory needs.

ATX is less about a specific feature set and more about a standard physical design. For exam purposes, remember the relationship between motherboard size, case compatibility, and the fixed layout that comes with the selected board.

**Key takeaways**

- ATX is a motherboard form factor.
- It describes physical size and layout.
- Standard ATX size is `305 mm x 244 mm`.
- You match the motherboard to a compatible case.
- The board’s slot and connector layout is fixed once selected.
