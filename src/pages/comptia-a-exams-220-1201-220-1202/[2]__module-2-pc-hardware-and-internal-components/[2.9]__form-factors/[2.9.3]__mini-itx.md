## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Mini-ITX overview**

Mini-ITX is a small motherboard form factor used when you want a compact system footprint. In the evidence, it is described as a standard designed for small computers and as a fit for small desktop PCs or DIY custom systems.

The key exam idea is that motherboard form factor affects the total size of the build and the case you must choose. Once you select a motherboard, the layout is fixed, including the number of expansion slots and memory slots.

- **Compact size** is the defining trait of Mini-ITX.
- It is commonly used for small office systems, media-center-style PCs, and other space-limited builds.
- You usually choose the motherboard first, then find a case that supports that form factor.

**Physical dimensions and fit**

Mini-ITX motherboards are about `170 mm x 170 mm`. That small footprint makes them useful for compact systems, but it also means compatibility matters more than in larger builds.

A Mini-ITX board does not automatically fit every small case. The case must explicitly support Mini-ITX, and many mini PCs use custom motherboards instead of standard sizes. That means replacement or upgrade plans can be limited by the chassis design.

> A Mini-ITX motherboard still needs a compatible case; small size alone does not guarantee fit.

- Check the case specification for Mini-ITX support.
- Confirm that the chassis is designed for standard motherboard sizes, not only custom boards.
- Treat the case as a hard compatibility requirement, not an optional accessory.

**Build planning considerations**

Mini-ITX systems can use standard desktop hardware, but the smaller layout reduces room for expansion. The evidence emphasizes that motherboard selection fixes the basic layout, so you should plan around the available slots and connectors before buying parts.

This matters in exam scenarios where you compare a larger desktop to a much smaller system. Both may contain CPU, memory, storage, and network components, but the smaller system has a different physical footprint and usually tighter internal space.

- Expect fewer expansion options than on larger motherboard formats.
- Plan storage, memory, and add-in needs before choosing the board.
- Use the motherboard’s fixed layout to guide the rest of the build.

**Mini-ITX vs. larger form factors**

Mini-ITX is part of the broader motherboard form factor family that also includes ATX and Micro-ATX. The practical difference is size: Mini-ITX targets compact builds, while larger formats support larger cases and typically more room for components.

| Form factor | Main idea | Practical impact |
|---|---|---|
| ATX | Larger standard board | More room for expansion |
| Micro-ATX | Mid-size option | Smaller than ATX, less compact than Mini-ITX |
| Mini-ITX | Smallest of the three in the evidence | Best for compact systems and tight spaces |

**Exam takeaways**

For A+ purposes, focus on what Mini-ITX means in a build decision:

- It is a **motherboard form factor**.
- It is about `170 mm x 170 mm`.
- It is intended for compact systems.
- It requires a compatible case.
- It offers less physical room for expansion than larger boards.
