## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Micro-ATX overview**

Micro-ATX is a motherboard form factor, so the main thing you compare is the board’s physical size and layout. In practice, you usually choose the motherboard first and then find a case that fits it. Once you select a board, the number of expansion slots and memory slots is largely fixed.

Motherboard form factors matter because different systems can contain similar core components while still having very different footprints. A smaller desktop, office PC, or media-center style system may use a smaller board than a full-size desktop.

- **Form factor** describes the board’s size and layout.
- **Layout** is not easily changed after purchase.
- **Case compatibility** is a key planning step when building or upgrading.

**What Micro-ATX means in practice**

Micro-ATX is used when you want a more compact build without changing the basic motherboard role in the system. The board still serves as the foundation for the CPU, memory, storage, network connections, and expansion options.

The evidence emphasizes that motherboard connectors remain standard across form factors, even though the board size changes. That means the difference is mainly about footprint and available onboard space, not about the motherboard’s core purpose.

- **CPU** support remains part of the motherboard’s role.
- **Memory slots** are fixed by the chosen board model.
- **Expansion slots** are limited by the board layout.
- **Connectors** stay standardized across form factors.

**Build and compatibility considerations**

When you work with Micro-ATX, focus on fit and feature tradeoffs. A smaller board can suit compact systems, but the reduced size means fewer slots or connectors than a larger board may provide. That makes planning important before you buy parts.

Use the motherboard’s layout as a constraint, not a variable you can easily change later. If you need more expansion or memory capacity, you must choose a different motherboard model rather than trying to modify the existing one.

1. Choose the motherboard form factor that matches your build size.
2. Verify the case supports that form factor.
3. Check the board’s slot and connector layout before purchase.
4. Confirm the board provides the features you need for the system.

**Exam focus**

For A+ purposes, remember the relationship between motherboard size, case fit, and fixed layout. Micro-ATX is one of the common form factors you should be able to distinguish from larger desktop boards and smaller ITX-style boards.

- **Physical size** is the defining characteristic.
- **Feature count** depends on the specific motherboard model.
- **Standard connectors** help keep components compatible across form factors.
- **Selection order** is usually motherboard first, case second.
