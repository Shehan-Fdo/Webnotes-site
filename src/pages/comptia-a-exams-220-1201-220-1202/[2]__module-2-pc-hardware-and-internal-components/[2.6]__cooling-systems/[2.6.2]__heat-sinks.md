## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Heat sink purpose**

A heat sink is a passive cooling component that helps move heat away from a hot part and into the surrounding air. In A+ troubleshooting, you should connect heat sinks with overall system cooling, since overheating can reduce performance and, in severe cases, cause the system to halt.

Heat sinks are commonly used with CPUs and other high-power components. They work best when the system also has good airflow from case fans or other cooling methods.

- **Heat transfer** is the main job: pull heat away from the source and spread it out.
- **Airflow** matters because the heat sink depends on moving air to carry heat away.
- **System cooling** is a broader topic that includes heat sinks, fans, and liquid cooling.

**How heat sinks fit into cooling**

Computers stay cooler when air moves through the case in a controlled path. Cool air enters the system, passes over warm components, and exits as heated air. If cables, components, or blocked vents interrupt that path, cooling becomes less effective.

Heat sinks support this process by increasing the surface area available for heat dissipation. Their fins are designed to expose more metal to moving air, which improves cooling when the airflow is aligned correctly.

- Front intake and rear or top exhaust help move air through the case.
- A heat sink works better when air can pass across its fins.
- Poor cable placement can reduce cooling effectiveness.

**Installation and mounting considerations**

Proper installation is critical. A poorly mounted heat sink can leave air gaps, reduce heat transfer, and allow the component to overheat. The evidence emphasizes clean contact surfaces, correct thermal interface material, and even mounting pressure.

<blockquote>
<mark>Critical:</mark> If the heat sink is not seated correctly, thermal performance drops quickly and the component may overheat.
</blockquote>

Key installation points:

- Clean both contact surfaces before installation.
- Apply thermal paste or a thermal pad as directed.
- Use even pressure so the heat sink sits flat against the component.
- Tighten fasteners in a diagonal pattern when applicable.
- Align the heat sink with airflow so the fins can dissipate heat efficiently.

**Thermal interface and contact quality**

The interface between the component and the heat sink affects how well heat moves. Dust, oil, or excess thermal paste can interfere with that transfer. The goal is a thin, even connection that fills microscopic gaps without creating insulation.

<dl>
<dt>Thermal interface material</dt>
<dd>A material such as paste or a pad that improves contact between the heat source and the heat sink.</dd>
<dt>Contact pressure</dt>
<dd>The force that keeps the heat sink firmly seated against the component.</dd>
</dl>

Practical reminders:

- Too much paste can block heat transfer.
- Too little paste can leave air gaps.
- Uneven pressure can warp the sink or damage the chip.
- Clean, flat surfaces improve reliability.

**Troubleshooting signs**

When cooling is inadequate, the system may run hot, slow down, or shut off to protect itself. In A+ scenarios, overheating is often a symptom of blocked airflow, failed cooling hardware, or a heat sink that is not mounted correctly.

Look for:

- High temperatures during normal use.
- Sluggish performance under load.
- Unexpected shutdowns or instability.
- Dust buildup around fins, vents, or fans.
- Loose mounting hardware or poor contact.

**Exam focus**

For exam prep, remember the relationship between the heat sink and the rest of the cooling system. The heat sink does not work alone; it depends on airflow, proper installation, and good contact with the component.

1. Identify the cooling problem.
2. Check airflow paths, fans, and vents.
3. Inspect the heat sink for secure mounting and dust buildup.
4. Verify that thermal interface material is present and applied correctly.
5. Confirm that the component is not overheating due to another hardware issue.

**Quick comparison**

| Item | Role |
|---|---|
| Heat sink | Draws heat away from a component and spreads it into the air |
| Case fan | Moves air through the system |
| Thermal paste | Improves contact between the chip and the heat sink |
| Liquid cooling | Uses fluid to move heat away from hot components |

**Key takeaway**

A heat sink is simple in concept but important in practice. Good cooling depends on the full chain: clean contact, correct mounting, proper thermal interface material, and airflow that can carry heat away from the fins.
