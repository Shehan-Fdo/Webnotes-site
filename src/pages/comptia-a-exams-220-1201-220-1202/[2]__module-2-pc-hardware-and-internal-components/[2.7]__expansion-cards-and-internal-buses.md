## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Expansion slots and cards**

Expansion slots are internal motherboard connections that let you add hardware capabilities with expansion cards. They are also called bus slots or expansion ports. In practice, you match the card to a compatible slot before installation.

Common uses include graphics, networking, storage, sound, and specialized interfaces. The exact slot type matters because not every card fits every slot, and many older slot types are now obsolete.

- **Expansion slot**: the motherboard or riser-board connection used for an add-in card.
- **Expansion card**: the hardware you install into the slot.
- **Compatibility**: the card and slot must match physically and electrically.

**Common slot types**

You are most likely to encounter PCI, PCI Express, and sometimes AGP on older systems. Other legacy slot types exist, but they are less common in modern troubleshooting and upgrades.

| Slot type | Typical use | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| PCI | Network, SCSI, sound, video | Older general-purpose expansion bus |
| PCI Express | Video, network, storage controllers | Common modern internal expansion standard |
| AGP | Video | Legacy graphics-only slot |

PCI Express, often written as **PCIe**, stands for Peripheral Component Interconnect Express. It is a high-speed interface standard used to connect internal components to the motherboard.

**PCIe lanes and slot sizes**

PCIe slots come in different lane counts such as `x1`, `x4`, `x8`, and `x16`. The lane count describes how many data lanes are available for communication.

A higher lane count generally provides more bandwidth between the motherboard and the card. That is why graphics cards commonly use larger PCIe slots, while smaller add-in devices may use `x1`.

- `x1` slots are common for lower-bandwidth cards.
- `x16` slots are commonly used for graphics cards.
- More lanes generally mean higher transfer capability.

**Internal buses and motherboard traffic**

A motherboard contains many pathways that move data between components. These internal buses and traces carry traffic between adapter cards and other chipset components.

Older descriptions often refer to traffic flowing through a Northbridge or Southbridge. The key idea is that the motherboard routes data across separate internal pathways so components can communicate.

> Use the slot and bus type as a first check when a card does not fit, is not detected, or does not perform as expected.

**Practical installation checks**

Before you install an expansion card, verify the slot type, the card type, and the intended function. This reduces mismatches and avoids forcing incompatible hardware.

1. Identify the card you want to install.
2. Confirm the motherboard has a compatible expansion slot.
3. Match the physical slot size and the card’s lane or bus requirement.
4. Install the card firmly into the slot.
5. Verify the device is recognized after boot.

**Exam focus**

For A+ questions, focus on recognizing the slot name, the card type, and the most likely use case. You should also know that PCIe is the modern high-speed standard and that lane counts affect available bandwidth.

- **PCIe** is the most important modern slot family to know.
- **AGP** is a legacy graphics slot.
- **PCI** is older but still appears in hardware references and exam material.
- **Lane count** helps you compare PCIe slot capacity.
