## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Troubleshooting approach**

Network troubleshooting works best when you establish a baseline and then narrow the fault domain. Start with the simplest checks first: confirm the device has a link, verify local connectivity, and then move outward toward the network and internet.

A structured approach helps you avoid chasing symptoms. For example, a wireless issue may look like a bad adapter, but the cause can also be interference, weak signal, roaming behavior, or authentication failure. On Chromebooks, built-in diagnostics can reveal useful details without external tools.

- Check for a **link light** on wired connections.
- Test the local stack with `ping 127.0.0.1`.
- Use a step-by-step method so you can separate device, cable, wireless, and service issues.
- Treat intermittent problems as clues, not noise.

> If the device has no link light, suspect a cable, port, or physical connection problem before you assume a software fault.

**Common wireless causes**

Wireless connectivity problems often come from the environment rather than the device itself. Physical obstructions can weaken signal strength, and electronic interference can disrupt stable communication. This is especially relevant on the `2.4 GHz` band, which is more exposed to interference from nearby devices.

The `5 GHz` band can also be affected by range and penetration limits. It often performs better in congested environments, but it does not travel through walls and obstacles as well as lower-frequency signals.

- Dense objects can reduce signal quality.
- Microwaves, cordless phones, and Bluetooth equipment can interfere with Wi-Fi.
- A central, elevated, open router location improves coverage.
- Switching bands or choosing a less congested channel can improve stability.

**Chromebook Wi-Fi diagnostics**

Chrome OS includes built-in tools that help you inspect wireless behavior. The `crosh` shell opens with <kbd>Ctrl</kbd> + <kbd>Alt</kbd> + <kbd>T</kbd>, and it provides networking commands that can help you gather evidence before making changes.

Useful checks include connectivity statistics, system logs, and device logs. These tools help you spot patterns such as roaming, low received signal strength indicator (`RSSI`), and authentication failures.

```bash
crosh> connectivity show devices
crosh> connectivity show services
```

Additional places to inspect:

- `chrome://system` for full system logs, network state, and recent events.
- `chrome://device-log` with the `network` category and debug logging for more detail.
- `ping`, `route`, and `tracepath` for basic network path checks.

**What to look for in logs**

Logs are most useful when you know what symptoms matter. On Chromebooks, repeated roaming, weak signal, and authentication failures are strong indicators that the issue is wireless rather than general application behavior.

- **Frequent roaming** can point to unstable coverage.
- **Low RSSI** suggests weak signal strength.
- **Authentication failures** can indicate a credential, security, or access problem.
- Repeated patterns across devices can suggest an environmental or hardware issue.

**Hardware and device-level issues**

Not every wireless problem is caused by the network itself. Some Chromebook reports describe devices that stop finding networks or get stuck with Wi-Fi turned off, even after software resets. In those cases, reseating the network card may temporarily restore function, which suggests a possible hardware or connection issue.

This kind of symptom can return after a short time, so a temporary fix does not prove the root cause is software. If a device repeatedly fails after reloads or resets, you should consider hardware seating, adapter behavior, and recurring physical faults.

- A temporary fix does not always mean the problem is solved.
- Repeated failures after reloads can point away from software.
- Reseating a card may restore service for a while.
- Recurring symptoms across multiple devices can suggest a common hardware pattern.

**Practical troubleshooting flow**

1. Verify whether the issue is wired or wireless.
2. Check for a link light on wired connections.
3. Test local connectivity with `ping 127.0.0.1`.
4. Inspect signal quality, interference, and placement for Wi-Fi issues.
5. On Chromebooks, open `crosh` and review connectivity data.
6. Check logs for roaming, low RSSI, and authentication failures.
7. If the issue persists after resets, consider hardware or adapter seating.

**Key takeaways**

Network troubleshooting starts with simple verification and then moves toward more specific evidence. For Wi-Fi, signal quality, interference, and authentication are common fault areas. For Chromebooks, built-in diagnostics provide enough detail to identify many issues without external tools.
