## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Power and startup symptoms**

Power problems often show up as a device that will not turn on, will not hold a charge, or shuts down unexpectedly. On mobile devices, battery health matters because batteries do not last forever, and a worn battery can no longer maintain a reliable charge.

Startup issues can also look like a power problem. A device may appear dead when the real issue is charging, battery failure, or a boot-time fault. When you troubleshoot, separate the symptom into one of these categories before you replace parts.

- **No power** can mean the device is not receiving usable power.
- **Rapid battery drain** can point to battery wear or heavy power use.
- **Startup failure** may involve boot diagnostics, hardware checks, or operating system issues.
- **Overheating** can contribute to shutdowns and unstable behavior.

**Battery behavior and charging**

A battery that loses charge quickly is not always defective. Some devices consume more power when they are in poor reception areas because they keep polling for a better signal. In that case, reducing radio use can help preserve battery life.

Airplane mode can reduce power use when you do not need wireless connectivity. This is especially useful when the device is searching for a signal and draining the battery faster than expected.

> If a battery no longer maintains a charge well, replacement may be the practical fix.

**Troubleshooting approach**

Use a structured process so you do not confuse a charging issue with a startup issue. Start with the simplest checks, then move toward diagnostics and component replacement if needed.

1. Verify the device has a power source and is actually charging.
2. Check whether the battery is holding a charge or draining unusually fast.
3. Look for signs of overheating, liquid damage, or other physical problems.
4. If the device powers on, use diagnostics to check battery, processor, and memory status.
5. Review test results and logs before deciding on repair or replacement.

**POST and boot-time checks**

The Power On Self-Test (POST) is the startup self-check that verifies core components are installed and operational. It can help identify problems with the CPU, memory, or video subsystem before the operating system loads.

If POST detects a problem, you may see an on-screen error message or hear beep codes from the motherboard. These messages can vary by manufacturer, so use them as clues rather than final answers.

- **POST errors** point to hardware that may not be initializing correctly.
- **Beep codes** can indicate a failure before video output is available.
- **Blank screens** may occur when the system cannot complete startup normally.

**Diagnostics and real-time status**

ChromeOS diagnostics can help you investigate battery, processor, and memory status in real time. You can run troubleshooting routines and save telemetric information in a session log file for later review.

This is useful when the device powers on but behaves inconsistently. Real-time diagnostics help you confirm whether the issue is battery-related, hardware-related, or something else that affects startup stability.

**Common mobile-device clues**

Mobile devices often show power problems through everyday behavior rather than obvious failure. Watch for patterns that point to battery stress or charging trouble.

- The device charges slowly or not at all.
- The battery percentage drops faster than expected.
- The device shuts down before the battery meter reaches zero.
- The device powers on only when connected to power.
- The device becomes hot during charging or use.

**Practical exam focus**

For A+ troubleshooting, focus on matching the symptom to the likely cause. A dead device is not always a dead battery, and a startup failure is not always an operating system problem.

- Use **symptom isolation** to narrow the fault.
- Use **diagnostics** when the device can power on.
- Use **replacement** when the battery can no longer maintain a charge.
- Use **power-saving settings** such as airplane mode when excessive radio use drains the battery.
