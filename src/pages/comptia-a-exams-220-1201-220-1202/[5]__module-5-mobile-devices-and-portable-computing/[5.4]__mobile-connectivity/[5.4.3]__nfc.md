## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**NFC overview**

<abbr title="Near-field communication">NFC</abbr> is a short-range wireless communication technology used by mobile devices and other electronics. It works only when devices are very close together, typically within about 4 cm, which makes it useful for quick, low-speed exchanges without a complex setup.

NFC is based on inductive coupling between electromagnetic coils. In practical terms, you bring two NFC-capable devices or a device and a tag close together, and they can exchange data or trigger an action.

- **Short range** keeps communication limited to close proximity.
- **Low speed** is acceptable because NFC is used for simple exchanges, not large transfers.
- **Simple setup** makes it useful for fast interactions.
- **13.56 MHz** is the globally available unlicensed radio frequency associated with NFC.

**How NFC is used**

NFC appears in phones, tablets, wearables, and card readers. In A+ contexts, you should associate it with mobile connectivity, identification, synchronization, and other quick interactions between devices.

Common uses include:

- **Contactless payments** through card emulation behavior.
- **Device pairing** for quick setup of other wireless connections.
- **Data sharing** between nearby NFC-enabled devices.
- **Access control** and identification in mobile or card-based systems.

NFC is often described as a bootstrap technology because it can help start a connection that later uses another wireless method. That makes it useful when you want a fast, close-range handoff rather than a full data session over NFC itself.

**NFC operating modes**

NFC devices can work in different modes depending on the task.

- **Reader/writer mode**
  - One device reads from or writes to an NFC tag.
  - The tag is passive storage.
- **Peer-to-peer mode**
  - Two NFC-enabled devices exchange information directly.
- **Card emulation mode**
  - A device behaves like a contactless card.
  - This supports transactions such as mobile payments.

**What to remember for A+**

For exam prep, focus on the core traits that distinguish NFC from other mobile connectivity options:

- It is **wireless** and **very short range**.
- It is used for **quick, simple interactions** rather than high-volume transfers.
- It commonly supports **payments, pairing, and identification**.
- It is found in **mobile devices** and related peripherals.

> NFC is not the same as general Wi-Fi or Bluetooth. Think of it as a close-proximity technology that often starts an action rather than carrying large amounts of data.

**Quick comparison mindset**

When you see a question about a phone touching a reader, tapping to pay, or exchanging a small amount of data at very close range, NFC is the likely answer. If the scenario involves longer range or ongoing device communication, another wireless technology is probably a better fit.
