## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Mobile device categories**

Mobile devices include phones, tablets, and related portable hardware that users rely on for communication and productivity. In A+ contexts, you should recognize both the device type and the operating system because the platform affects app availability, management, and troubleshooting.

- **Smartphones** are handheld devices built for calling, messaging, apps, and location-aware features.
- **Tablets** provide a larger touch interface and are often used for media, work, and education.
- **Wearables** include devices such as smart watches and fitness monitors.
- **Specialized mobile devices** can also include VR/AR headsets and other portable computing devices.

Mobile device questions often focus on what the device is designed to do rather than on a single brand name. When you identify the use case first, the correct device type is usually easier to choose.

**Mobile operating systems**

The two major mobile operating systems in the evidence are Apple iOS and Google Android. They differ in source availability, hardware support, and app distribution.

- **iOS** runs on Apple devices such as iPhone and iPad.
- **Android** is based on Linux and is maintained by the Open Handset Alliance with Google involvement.
- iOS is **closed-source**, so you do not have access to the source code.
- Android is **open-source**, so the code is available.
- iOS apps are developed with the iOS software development kit and are approved by Apple before release.
- iOS apps are distributed through the Apple App Store.
- Android runs on many manufacturers’ devices, not just one vendor’s hardware.

> iOS is tied to Apple hardware, while Android appears across many device makers. That distinction is a common exam clue.

**iOS characteristics**

Apple iOS is designed for Apple’s mobile ecosystem and is based on Unix. The evidence emphasizes that it is exclusive to Apple products and that app distribution is controlled by Apple.

- Use iOS when the device is an Apple iPhone or iPad.
- Expect app approval before release.
- Expect app distribution through the Apple App Store.
- Expect development to use the iOS SDK on Mac OS X.

iOS is often described as tightly controlled. For exam purposes, the important practical point is that Apple manages both the platform and the app release process.

**Android characteristics**

Android is an open platform based on Linux and supported across many manufacturers’ devices. This makes it more broadly deployed than iOS.

- Use Android when the device is from a non-Apple manufacturer unless the question states otherwise.
- Expect open-source access to the code.
- Expect broad hardware variety across vendors.
- Expect the platform to be maintained through the Open Handset Alliance.

Android’s flexibility is a useful contrast to iOS. If a question highlights multiple hardware brands or an open platform, Android is usually the better fit.

**Location and display features**

Mobile operating systems use modern conveniences that improve usability and app behavior. The evidence specifically mentions display technologies and location services.

- **Location services** can use network information and GPS signals to determine position.
- Apps can use current location data when available.
- Mobile platforms support different display technologies and related user features.

Location-aware behavior is common in maps, navigation, and apps that need to adapt to where the device is being used. On the exam, location services are usually tied to GPS and network-based positioning.

**Calling and connectivity behavior**

Mobile devices are expected to stay connected, but that connectivity can affect power use. The evidence notes that phones may constantly check for a better signal when reception is poor.

- Poor reception can cause a device to poll repeatedly for a usable signal.
- Repeated signal checking can drain the battery quickly.
- Airplane mode can reduce radio activity when connectivity is not needed.

This matters because a battery problem is not always a battery failure. Sometimes the device is consuming power aggressively due to network conditions.

**Common mobile troubleshooting concerns**

Mobile troubleshooting often centers on physical damage, power issues, and input problems. The evidence highlights several recurring symptoms.

- **Battery issues** can include failure to hold a charge or rapid drain.
- **Broken screens** can affect usability and visibility.
- **Improper charging** can prevent normal power delivery.
- **Liquid damage** can cause unpredictable hardware failure.
- **Overheating** can indicate stress, damage, or excessive power use.
- **Digitizer issues** can affect touch input.
- **Cursor drift** can appear as unwanted pointer movement on supported devices.

When troubleshooting, start with the symptom and then narrow the cause. For example, rapid battery drain may point to a worn battery, poor signal conditions, or another power-hungry behavior.

**Quick comparison**

| Topic | iOS | Android |
|---|---|---|
| Source model | Closed-source | Open-source |
| Hardware support | Apple devices only | Many manufacturers |
| Base platform | Unix-based | Linux-based |
| App distribution | Apple App Store | Broad ecosystem, not limited to one vendor |
| Development note | Uses iOS SDK on Mac OS X | Built for a wide range of devices |

**Exam focus reminders**

- Match the OS to the hardware vendor first.
- Remember that iOS is Apple-only.
- Remember that Android is open-source and widely deployed.
- Connect battery drain to signal polling when reception is poor.
- Associate location services with GPS and network data.
