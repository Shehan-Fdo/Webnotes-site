## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Power adapter fundamentals**

A power adapter supplies the power a laptop or other device needs from an external source. In practice, you care about matching the adapter to the device’s electrical requirements so the device can run safely and reliably. The key idea is compatibility: the adapter must provide the right kind of power for the device it supports.

Power adapters are often discussed alongside adapters and converters because both involve changing how devices connect or receive power. In exam terms, keep the distinction practical: an adapter changes the physical interface or connection, while a converter changes the signal or power format when needed.

- **Wattage** tells you how much power the adapter can supply.
- **Voltage** and current work together to define the adapter’s output.
- **Compatibility** matters more than convenience when choosing a charger.
- A mismatched adapter can be a temporary workaround or a permanent solution only if it is electrically appropriate.

**Electrical ratings and compatibility**

When you evaluate a power adapter, focus on the label and the device requirements. The evidence emphasizes matching voltage, current, and wattage. Wattage is the power rating, and it is calculated from voltage multiplied by current. That makes wattage a useful summary, but it does not replace checking the underlying electrical values.

For laptop charging, the adapter must meet the device’s needs. A charger with the wrong rating may not charge correctly or may not be suitable at all. In study scenarios, the safest approach is to verify the device’s power label or manual before using a replacement adapter.

<dl>
<dt>Voltage</dt>
<dd>The electrical pressure supplied by the adapter.</dd>
<dt>Current</dt>
<dd>The amount of electrical flow the adapter can provide.</dd>
<dt>Wattage</dt>
<dd>The overall power available from the adapter, based on voltage and current.</dd>
</dl>

> Use only adapters that are electrically compatible with the device. Physical fit alone does not guarantee safe operation.

**Adapters versus converters**

The source material notes that adapters and converters are used when you do not have the right cable or interface for the job. In many cases, the systems are electrically compatible, so an adapter can bridge one connector type to another. In other cases, a converter changes signals from one format to another, such as converting between USB and Ethernet.

This distinction matters because not every connection problem is solved the same way. If the issue is only the connector shape, an adapter may be enough. If the signal format must change, you need a converter instead.

- **Adapter**
  - Changes the physical connection.
  - Often works when the systems are electrically compatible.
- **Converter**
  - Changes the signal or format.
  - Is used when the interface needs more than a connector change.

**Practical selection steps**

1. Check the device’s power label or manual for voltage, current, and wattage requirements.
2. Compare those requirements with the adapter’s output rating.
3. Confirm the connector type fits the device.
4. Use the adapter only if the electrical and physical requirements match.
5. Treat any temporary workaround as temporary unless it is clearly supported by the device specifications.

**What to remember for the exam**

Power adapters are about delivering the correct electrical power to a device. The most testable ideas are matching ratings, understanding wattage, and knowing when an adapter is different from a converter. If a question asks about charging a laptop, think first about compatibility and power requirements, not just plug shape.
