## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**What ESD is**

Electrostatic discharge (<abbr title="electrostatic discharge">ESD</abbr>) is the sudden movement of static electricity from one object to another. Static electricity can build up and remain in place until it finds a path to discharge.

For computer work, the important point is that the discharge is the hazard, not the static charge sitting there. A person may feel a static shock at around 3,500 volts, but computer components can be damaged by much less.

- Static electricity is harmless while it stays in place.
- A discharge can damage sensitive components in an instant.
- Even a small, unnoticed event can leave physical damage on a part.

**Why ESD matters**

Computer hardware is vulnerable because internal circuits are designed for low-voltage operation. The evidence shows that only 100 volts or less can damage components inside a computer.

That means you should treat ESD as a real handling risk any time you open a system, move parts, or store components outside the chassis.

> <mark>Important:</mark> A component may fail immediately or be weakened by ESD damage even if the discharge is not obvious to you.

**Common ESD protection methods**

Use ESD control tools and safe handling habits to reduce the chance of discharge. The evidence specifically identifies anti-static straps, pads, mats, and bags as protective measures.

- **Anti-static strap**: Helps equalize charge between you and the equipment.
- **Anti-static pad or mat**: Provides a safer work surface for parts.
- **Anti-static bag**: Protects components during storage and transport.
- **ESD-safe tools**: Use tools intended for electronics work when handling sensitive parts.

A practical setup usually combines personal grounding, a protected work surface, and proper storage for removed components.

**Handling and storage practices**

Proper component handling and storage reduce exposure to static buildup and accidental discharge. Keep parts in protective packaging when they are not installed, and avoid unnecessary contact with exposed circuitry.

- Handle components by their edges when possible.
- Store parts in anti-static bags when they are not in use.
- Keep the work area organized so parts do not sit unprotected on ordinary surfaces.
- Use grounded protection before touching internal hardware.

**Grounding and safety awareness**

Equipment grounding provides a safe path for excess electrical energy. The evidence notes that electrical energy takes the shortest path to ground, so proper grounding helps dissipate unwanted charge safely.

Use equipment with its grounding feature intact. Do not disable grounding by removing the ground prong from a three-pronged plug.

- A grounded connection helps protect both you and the equipment.
- A broken or bypassed ground is a safety hazard.
- High-voltage warning labels indicate a separate electrical hazard and deserve attention.

**Practical workflow for ESD-safe work**

1. Prepare a grounded, ESD-safe work area.
2. Put on anti-static protection before touching components.
3. Place parts on a pad or mat instead of an ordinary surface.
4. Store removed parts in anti-static bags.
5. Recheck that grounding remains intact before powering equipment.

**Key takeaways**

ESD is a discharge of static electricity that can damage computer components quickly and with very little voltage. Your goal is to prevent that discharge by controlling charge, grounding yourself and the equipment, and storing parts in protective materials.

- Static electricity itself is not the problem.
- The discharge is what causes damage.
- Anti-static straps, pads, mats, and bags are core protections.
- Proper grounding is part of safe component handling.
