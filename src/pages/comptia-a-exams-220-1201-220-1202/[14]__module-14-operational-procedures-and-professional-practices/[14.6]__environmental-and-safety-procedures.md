## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Safety first when working with hardware**

When you work inside powered electronic equipment, you reduce risk by removing all power sources before opening the system. If you are unsure what a component is, the safest choice is not to touch it. This is especially important around power supplies, which are designed to stay enclosed and may contain very high voltages.

- Remove all power sources before opening a device.
- Treat unknown components cautiously and avoid unnecessary contact.
- Do not open a power supply or place tools inside it.
- Be especially careful in devices such as servers and laser printers, where high voltages may be present.

> Never assume a component is safe to handle just because the system is off; verify power is removed first.

**Electrical safety and personal protection**

Safety procedures also include protecting yourself and the equipment from avoidable harm. The evidence emphasizes grounding, electrical fire protection, and the use of personal safety best practices. You should also use protective gear and safe handling habits when the task exposes you to dust, debris, or other hazards.

- Use proper grounding practices when working with electronic equipment.
- Follow electrical fire safety procedures.
- Wear safety goggles when the task creates eye hazards.
- Use an air filtration mask when dust or airborne particles are present.
- Disconnect power before repairing a PC.

**Environmental controls in the workspace**

A safe environment depends on more than electrical precautions. Temperature, humidity, ventilation, and equipment placement all affect reliability and safety. Dust cleanup matters as well, and the evidence specifically calls out compressed air and vacuums as part of maintenance and cleanup practices.

- Keep temperature and humidity within appropriate ranges.
- Maintain proper ventilation around equipment.
- Place equipment so airflow is not blocked.
- Clean up dust and debris regularly.
- Use compressed air and vacuums appropriately for cleanup.

**Material Safety Data Sheets and disposal**

A <abbr title="Material Safety Data Sheet">MSDS</abbr> provides disposal and handling guidance for components and materials. These sheets are provided with components and can usually be downloaded from the manufacturer if the original copy is unavailable. In the United States, the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (<abbr title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">OSHA</abbr>) mandates these safety data sheets.

- Check the MSDS before disposing of batteries, display devices, solvents, toner, or other assets.
- Download the sheet from the manufacturer if the original is missing.
- Follow the disposal instructions for the specific material or component.
- Use the MSDS as the primary reference for handling and disposal guidance.

**Common disposal concerns**

Different materials require different handling, so you should not treat all equipment waste the same way. Batteries, toner, and display devices are specifically called out as items that may require documented disposal steps. The practical takeaway is to verify the correct procedure before you discard anything.

- Verify battery disposal requirements before removal.
- Follow toner disposal guidance instead of using general trash disposal.
- Check disposal instructions for display devices and other assets.
- Use manufacturer documentation when local procedures are unclear.

**Practical workflow for safe handling**

1. Power down the device and remove all power sources.
2. Identify the component or material before touching or moving it.
3. Check the MSDS for disposal or handling requirements.
4. Use the right protective equipment for the task.
5. Restore the workspace by cleaning dust and confirming ventilation.

**Key takeaways**

Environmental and safety procedures focus on preventing injury, protecting equipment, and disposing of materials correctly. The safest habits are simple: remove power, avoid unknown components, use protective gear, maintain the workspace, and consult the MSDS before disposal.
