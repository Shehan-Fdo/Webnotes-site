## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Core concepts**

Disaster recovery (DR) and business continuity (BC) are related but not identical. DR focuses on restoring IT operations after a disruptive event, while BC focuses on keeping critical business operations running during and after the disruption. Both aim to reduce downtime and data loss.

A disaster can be large or small. It may involve natural events, cybersecurity threats, or system failures. Even a localized incident, such as a burst pipe damaging systems in a data center, can require a recovery plan.

- **Disaster recovery** restores IT services after disruption.
- **Business continuity** maintains essential business functions during disruption.
- Both rely on planning, documentation, backups, redundancies, and testing.
- Third-party facilities or geographically diverse sites may support recovery efforts.

**Planning goals**

The main goal of BC/DR planning is to keep the organization operational and limit impact when an incident occurs. In practice, you want to preserve availability, protect data, and restore services in a controlled way.

BC and DR often work together:
- BC keeps the business functioning at an acceptable level.
- DR brings systems and infrastructure back online.
- A strong plan accounts for both immediate response and longer-term recovery.

> Use conservative assumptions when planning. A recovery strategy should work for more than just major disasters; smaller incidents can also interrupt operations.

**Recovery metrics**

Several metrics help you measure recovery readiness and system resilience. These values help you evaluate incidents and set realistic recovery targets.

- **MTBF** means mean time between failures.
- **MTTF** means mean time to failure.
- **MTTR** means mean time to repair.
- **RPO** means recovery point objective.
- **RTO** means recovery time objective.

These metrics help answer different questions:
- How often does a system fail?
- How long does it take to repair or restore it?
- How much data loss is acceptable?
- How quickly must service return?

| Metric | Meaning | Practical use |
|---|---|---|
| MTBF | Mean time between failures | Estimates reliability over time |
| MTTF | Mean time to failure | Helps describe expected lifespan before failure |
| MTTR | Mean time to repair | Measures how quickly you can fix or restore service |
| RPO | Recovery point objective | Defines the maximum acceptable data loss |
| RTO | Recovery time objective | Defines the maximum acceptable downtime |

**Planning and testing**

Recovery plans are only useful if they are current and tested. Testing helps confirm that procedures, backups, and alternate resources actually work when needed.

1. Identify critical systems and business functions.
2. Define acceptable downtime and data loss targets.
3. Document recovery procedures and responsibilities.
4. Verify backup and recovery resources.
5. Test the plan and update it after changes or failures.

Testing should reflect realistic conditions. If you rely on a third-party data center or alternate site, confirm that it is available and that your team knows how to use it.

**Recovery resources**

Organizations may use external providers or alternate facilities to support disaster recovery. These resources are often located in a geographically diverse area so a regional event does not affect both the primary and recovery environments.

Common recovery support includes:
- Backup systems
- Redundant infrastructure
- Alternate sites
- Third-party recovery services
- Written procedures and contact lists

**Exam focus**

For A+ exam prep, be ready to distinguish the purpose of BC and DR and match the metrics to their definitions. You should also recognize that disaster recovery planning applies to both major and minor incidents.

- BC = keep operations going.
- DR = restore IT services.
- RPO = acceptable data loss.
- RTO = acceptable downtime.
- MTBF, MTTF, and MTTR describe reliability and repair timing.
