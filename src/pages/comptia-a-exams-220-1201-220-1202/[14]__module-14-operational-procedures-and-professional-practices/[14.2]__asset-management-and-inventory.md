## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Asset management overview**

Asset management creates a record of every asset in the organization. In IT, that usually includes hardware such as laptops, servers, switches, routers, and even cables. It can also include software and other tracked resources.

A complete inventory gives you visibility into what exists, where it is, and who uses it. That visibility supports help desk work, financial reporting, and compliance.

- **Asset record**: a structured list of organizational assets.
- **Inventory**: the set of tracked items, often including hardware and software.
- **Asset tag**: a physical identifier attached to an asset.

**Why inventory matters**

Inventory data helps you connect a device to a user and a ticket. If a user reports a laptop issue, you can identify the exact model and serial number assigned to that person. That makes troubleshooting faster and reduces confusion when multiple similar devices exist.

Inventory also supports business decisions. Organizations use asset data to understand depreciation, financial reporting, and tax-related concerns. When assets are tracked over time, you can see which items are still in service and which ones have lost value.

- Track devices by model and serial number.
- Associate assets with help desk tickets.
- Use inventory data for depreciation and financial reporting.
- Keep visibility into asset location and usage.

**Asset identification methods**

Many organizations attach an asset tag directly to the device. The tag may use a barcode or an RFID (radio-frequency identification) chip. The goal is to make the asset easy to identify and match to the inventory record.

A good asset tag system reduces manual lookup work and helps prevent confusion between similar devices. It also makes audits easier because you can scan or verify the physical item against the record.

> Use a consistent tagging method so physical assets and inventory records stay aligned.

**Lifecycle and procurement**

Asset management is not just about counting devices. It also follows the procurement life cycle, from purchase through use and eventual retirement. That lifecycle view helps you understand what you own and when it changes status.

A practical asset process includes:
1. Record the asset when it is acquired.
2. Assign it to a user, location, or department.
3. Update the record during maintenance or reassignment.
4. Mark it retired when it is no longer in service.

This lifecycle approach helps you keep records current instead of relying on outdated spreadsheets.

**Operational benefits**

Asset management improves day-to-day IT operations by giving support staff context. When tickets are linked to assets, agents can see the device history and respond more effectively. It also helps organizations monitor hardware and software more consistently.

Common benefits include:
- Better service delivery through accurate asset context.
- Improved visibility into hardware and software ownership.
- Reduced waste from unmanaged or forgotten assets.
- Stronger compliance through regular tracking and review.

**Practical inventory habits**

Inventory works best when it stays current. Regular updates help you avoid missing devices, duplicate records, and stale information. A structured process is more reliable than ad hoc tracking.

Useful habits include:
- Update records when devices are deployed, moved, or retired.
- Verify asset details during audits or self-checks.
- Keep software and hardware records in the same system when possible.
- Share accurate asset data across teams that need it.

**Exam focus**

For A+ purposes, focus on the purpose of asset management rather than a specific tool. You should recognize that it tracks hardware, software, and related details across the asset lifecycle. You should also know why asset tags, serial numbers, and inventory records matter in support and business workflows.
