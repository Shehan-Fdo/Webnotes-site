## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**Azure landing zone overview**

An Azure landing zone is the baseline environment you use to deploy workloads into Azure with consistent controls for identity, networking, governance, and operations. It is not a single product; it is an architecture and operating model that helps you scale cloud adoption safely.

A good landing zone gives you:
- **Scalability** for many subscriptions, teams, and workloads.
- **Security** through centralized identity, policy, and network controls.
- **Governance** with standards for naming, tagging, and compliance.
- **Operational excellence** with monitoring, logging, and automation.
- **Separation of duties** so platform teams and application teams can work independently.

Landing zones are especially important when you need to support multiple business units, regulated workloads, or a long-term cloud operating model. For AZ-305, focus on how the landing zone connects architecture decisions to enterprise requirements rather than memorizing a single reference diagram.

**Core design principles**

Azure landing zone design usually follows a few recurring principles:

- **Design for scale** so you can add subscriptions, regions, and workloads without redesigning the foundation.
- **Standardize by default** so teams inherit secure and compliant settings.
- **Use least privilege** so access is limited to what each role needs.
- **Separate platform from application concerns** so shared services do not become tightly coupled to app delivery.
- **Automate everything repeatable** so provisioning is consistent and auditable.
- **Prefer policy over manual review** so controls are enforced continuously.

These principles matter because landing zones are meant to reduce drift. If every team builds its own network, identity, and logging model, you lose consistency and increase operational risk.

**Enterprise-scale landing zone structure**

A common enterprise landing zone model uses a management group hierarchy with separate scopes for platform and application subscriptions. This supports centralized governance while allowing workload teams to deploy independently.

Typical hierarchy pattern:
- **Tenant root group**
  - **Platform**
    - Identity
    - Connectivity
    - Management
  - **Landing zones**
    - Corp
    - Online
    - SAP
    - Other workload groups
  - **Sandbox**
  - **Decommissioned**

This structure is useful because:
- Platform subscriptions host shared services such as connectivity, identity-related services, and monitoring.
- Application subscriptions host business workloads and are governed by inherited policy.
- Sandbox subscriptions allow experimentation with lighter controls.
- Decommissioned scopes help you isolate retired resources and reduce clutter.

> Avoid placing workload-specific resources directly at the tenant root unless you have a very deliberate reason. Most governance and policy should be assigned at management group or subscription scope, not at individual resource scope.

**Landing zone archetypes**

Landing zones often map to two broad archetypes:

- **Platform subscriptions**
  - Host shared services used by many workloads.
  - Common examples include hub networking, firewall, DNS, monitoring, and identity-related operational tooling.
  - Best when you need centralized control and shared operations.

- **Application subscriptions**
  - Host one or more business applications or product teams.
  - Common examples include dev/test/prod subscriptions for a product, or separate subscriptions per environment.
  - Best when teams need autonomy within guardrails.

A practical enterprise pattern is:
- Use a **platform landing zone** for shared services.
- Use **application landing zones** for business workloads.
- Use separate subscriptions for **production** and **nonproduction** when governance, billing, or risk separation matters.

When to choose each:
- Choose a **platform subscription** when the service is shared, security-sensitive, or needs centralized operations.
- Choose an **application subscription** when the workload lifecycle is owned by a product team and should be isolated for cost, policy, or blast-radius reasons.

**Management groups and scope strategy**

Management groups are the primary way to apply governance at scale. They let you assign Azure Policy, role-based access control (RBAC), and other controls to many subscriptions at once.

Best practices:
- Keep the hierarchy **simple and stable**.
- Use management groups to reflect **operating boundaries**, not every organizational chart detail.
- Assign broad policies at higher scopes and workload-specific policies lower in the tree.
- Use subscriptions as the main unit of **billing, quota, and workload isolation**.
- Use resource groups for **lifecycle and deployment grouping**, not for enterprise governance boundaries.

What to assign where:
- **Tenant root group**
  - Global guardrails that apply everywhere, when appropriate.
- **Platform management group**
  - Policies for shared services, connectivity, and management tooling.
- **Landing zones management group**
  - Baseline policies for application subscriptions.
- **Subscription**
  - RBAC for workload owners, budgets, and workload-specific policy exceptions.
- **Resource group**
  - Deployment permissions and resource lifecycle management.

What not to do:
- Do not put all governance in resource groups; that does not scale.
- Do not create overly deep hierarchies unless you have a clear operational need.
- Do not assign conflicting policies at multiple scopes without understanding inheritance and exclusions.

Example management group assignment with Azure CLI:
```bash
az account management-group create --name CorpApps --display-name "Corporate Applications"

az account management-group subscription add \
  --name CorpApps \
  --subscription 00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000
```

Example policy assignment at management group scope:
```bash
az policy assignment create \
  --name deny-public-ip \
  --display-name "Deny Public IPs" \
  --scope /providers/Microsoft.Management/managementGroups/CorpApps \
  --policy "/providers/Microsoft.Authorization/policyDefinitions/xxxxxxx-xxxx-xxxx-xxxx-xxxxxxxxxxxx"
```

**Governance baseline**

Governance in a landing zone usually includes policy, RBAC, tagging, budgets, and compliance reporting. The goal is to make the secure path the easy path.

Common governance controls:
- **Azure Policy** to enforce or audit standards.
- **RBAC** to control who can deploy and manage resources.
- **Tags** to support ownership, cost allocation, and operations.
- **Budgets** to detect overspend early.
- **Resource locks** for critical shared resources.
- **Compliance reporting** for audit and operational review.

Useful policy patterns:
- Deny public IP creation in sensitive subscriptions.
- Require diagnostic settings on supported resources.
- Enforce allowed regions.
- Require tags such as `Owner`, `CostCenter`, `Environment`, and `DataClassification`.
- Audit or deny unsupported SKUs.

Example policy initiative concept:
- Bundle related policies into an **initiative** so you can assign a baseline once and manage it centrally.
- Use initiatives for landing zone standards such as networking, logging, and tagging.

Example Azure Policy definition snippet:
```json
{
  "properties": {
    "displayName": "Require CostCenter tag",
    "policyType": "Custom",
    "mode": "Indexed",
    "parameters": {
      "tagName": {
        "type": "String",
        "defaultValue": "CostCenter"
      }
    },
    "policyRule": {
      "if": {
        "field": "[concat('tags[', parameters('tagName'), ']')]",
        "exists": "false"
      },
      "then": {
        "effect": "deny"
      }
    }
  }
}
```

**Identity and access design**

Identity is a foundational landing zone concern because it controls who can access the platform and workloads. In Azure, Microsoft Entra ID is the identity provider for users, groups, and service principals.

Key identity practices:
- Use **least privilege** for all roles.
- Prefer **group-based assignment** over direct user assignment.
- Separate **platform admin** roles from **workload owner** roles.
- Use **privileged access management** for elevated operations.
- Protect critical accounts with strong authentication and monitoring.

Important concepts:
- **Privileged Identity Management (PIM)** provides just-in-time elevation for privileged roles.
- **Break-glass accounts** are emergency access accounts used when normal identity systems fail.
- **Managed identities** reduce secret management for Azure resources.
- **Service principals** are useful for automation, but they need careful credential governance.

Break-glass account design:
- Create at least two emergency accounts.
- Exclude them from conditional access policies that could lock you out, but protect them with strong controls.
- Monitor their sign-ins and use.
- Store credentials securely and test recovery procedures.

Least-privilege pattern:
- Grant platform operators only the roles needed for networking, monitoring, or policy management.
- Grant application teams access only to their subscriptions or resource groups.
- Use custom roles when built-in roles are too broad.

Example role assignment:
```bash
az role assignment create \
  --assignee-object-id 11111111-1111-1111-1111-111111111111 \
  --role "Reader" \
  --scope /subscriptions/00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000
```

**Networking architecture**

Networking is one of the most important landing zone design areas because it affects security, connectivity, and workload isolation. A common enterprise pattern is hub-and-spoke, though some organizations use virtual WAN or other topologies depending on scale and connectivity needs.

Common patterns:
- **Hub-and-spoke**
  - A central hub hosts shared network services.
  - Spokes host application workloads.
  - Traffic can be inspected and controlled centrally.
- **Virtual WAN**
  - Useful for large-scale branch, remote user, and global connectivity scenarios.
  - Can simplify connectivity at scale.
- **Flat network**
  - Usually not preferred for enterprise landing zones because it weakens isolation and governance.

Hub-and-spoke components often include:
- Azure Firewall or another network virtual appliance.
- VPN or ExpressRoute gateways.
- Shared DNS services.
- Bastion or secure admin access.
- Central logging and monitoring endpoints.

Spoke design considerations:
- Use separate spokes for different applications or environments when isolation matters.
- Use network security groups and route tables to control east-west and north-south traffic.
- Avoid overlapping IP address spaces.
- Plan for future growth and peering limits.

Example hub VNet skeleton in Bicep:
```bicep
param location string = resourceGroup().location
param vnetName string = 'hub-vnet'
param addressPrefix string = '10.0.0.0/16'

resource hubVnet 'Microsoft.Network/virtualNetworks@2023-11-01' = {
  name: vnetName
  location: location
  properties: {
    addressSpace: {
      addressPrefixes: [
        addressPrefix
      ]
    }
    subnets: [
      {
        name: 'AzureFirewallSubnet'
        properties: {
          addressPrefix: '10.0.1.0/24'
        }
      }
      {
        name: 'GatewaySubnet'
        properties: {
          addressPrefix: '10.0.2.0/24'
        }
      }
      {
        name: 'SharedServicesSubnet'
        properties: {
          addressPrefix: '10.0.3.0/24'
        }
      }
    ]
  }
}
```

> `GatewaySubnet` and `AzureFirewallSubnet` have service-specific requirements. Size them intentionally and validate the design before deployment.

**Private connectivity and DNS**

Private access is a common landing zone requirement because it reduces exposure to the public internet and supports secure service consumption.

Key components:
- **Private endpoints** provide private IP access to PaaS services.
- **Private Link** is the broader capability that enables private access to supported services.
- **Private DNS zones** resolve private endpoint names to private IP addresses.
- **Azure DNS Private Resolver** helps bridge DNS between on-premises and Azure or between different DNS domains.

Why DNS matters:
- Private endpoints are not enough by themselves; clients must resolve the service name to the private IP.
- Without correct DNS, workloads may still try to reach public endpoints.
- Centralized DNS design reduces troubleshooting complexity.

Common landing zone DNS pattern:
- Host shared private DNS zones in the platform subscription.
- Link zones to spoke VNets that need resolution.
- Use Azure DNS Private Resolver when you need hybrid name resolution without custom DNS servers.

Example private DNS zone link:
```bash
az network private-dns zone create \
  --resource-group rg-platform-dns \
  --name privatelink.blob.core.windows.net

az network private-dns link vnet create \
  --resource-group rg-platform-dns \
  --zone-name privatelink.blob.core.windows.net \
  --name link-spoke1 \
  --virtual-network /subscriptions/00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000/resourceGroups/rg-spoke1/providers/Microsoft.Network/virtualNetworks/spoke1-vnet \
  --registration-enabled false
```

**Monitoring, logging, and operations**

Operational readiness is a core landing zone requirement. You need standards for collecting logs, metrics, alerts, and traces across subscriptions.

Operational baseline usually includes:
- **Azure Monitor** for metrics and alerts.
- **Log Analytics workspaces** for centralized log storage and querying.
- **Diagnostic settings** for resource logs and platform logs where supported.
- **Action groups** for alert routing.
- **Workbooks** for visualization and reporting.
- **Automation** for remediation and routine tasks.

Important exam point:
- Diagnostic settings are **resource-specific**. Not every resource supports the same categories, and some resources may not support diagnostic settings in the same way. Always verify the target resource and available categories before automating.

Example diagnostic settings command pattern:
```bash
az monitor diagnostic-settings create \
  --name send-to-law \
  --resource /subscriptions/00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000/resourceGroups/rg-app/providers/Microsoft.Storage/storageAccounts/mystorage \
  --workspace /subscriptions/00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000/resourceGroups/rg-platform-monitor/providers/Microsoft.OperationalInsights/workspaces/law-platform \
  --logs '[{"category":"StorageRead","enabled":true},{"category":"StorageWrite","enabled":true}]' \
  --metrics '[{"category":"Transaction","enabled":true}]'
```

Operational standards to define early:
- Which logs are mandatory.
- How long logs are retained.
- Which alerts are critical.
- Who owns incident response.
- How platform and application teams share responsibility.

**Tagging and cost management**

Tagging is a simple but powerful landing zone control. It helps with chargeback, ownership, automation, and compliance.

Common tags:
- `Owner`
- `CostCenter`
- `Environment`
- `Application`
- `DataClassification`
- `BusinessUnit`

Tagging guidance:
- Make a small set of tags mandatory.
- Keep tag values standardized.
- Use policy to enforce required tags where possible.
- Avoid overly complex tag taxonomies that teams will not maintain.

Cost management practices:
- Create budgets at subscription and management group scope.
- Use alerts for forecasted overspend.
- Separate production and nonproduction subscriptions when cost visibility matters.
- Review reserved capacity and savings opportunities for steady-state workloads.

Example budget concept:
- Set a monthly budget for each application subscription.
- Alert at 50%, 80%, and 100%.
- Route alerts to the workload owner and platform finance contact.

**Subscription vending**

Subscription vending is the automated process of creating new subscriptions with the right baseline controls already applied. It is a common enterprise landing zone capability because it reduces manual setup and drift.

A subscription vending workflow often includes:
1. Request submission with workload metadata.
2. Approval and validation.
3. Subscription creation.
4. Management group placement.
5. Policy assignment.
6. RBAC assignment.
7. Network and logging onboarding.
8. Budget and tag application.

Why it matters:
- It speeds up onboarding.
- It ensures consistent governance.
- It reduces human error.
- It supports self-service while preserving control.

Example automation idea with Bicep at subscription scope:
```bicep
targetScope = 'subscription'

param mgId string
param location string = deployment().location

resource subPolicy 'Microsoft.Authorization/policyAssignments@2022-06-01' = {
  name: 'baseline-policy'
  scope: subscription()
  properties: {
    displayName: 'Baseline Landing Zone Policy'
    policyDefinitionId: '/providers/Microsoft.Authorization/policySetDefinitions/landing-zone-baseline'
  }
}
```

In practice, subscription vending is often implemented with pipelines, templates, and approval workflows rather than a single manual command.

**Reference implementations and accelerators**

Microsoft provides landing zone guidance and reference implementations that help you deploy enterprise-scale foundations more quickly. These are useful because they encode common patterns for management groups, policy, identity, networking, and operations.

What these accelerators typically help with:
- Establishing a management group hierarchy.
- Deploying baseline policies and initiatives.
- Creating platform subscriptions.
- Setting up connectivity and monitoring foundations.
- Standardizing deployment patterns with Infrastructure as Code.

Use them as starting points, not as rigid blueprints. You still need to adapt them to your organization’s identity model, network topology, compliance requirements, and operating model.

**Common exam scenarios**

For AZ-305, expect scenario questions that ask you to choose the right landing zone pattern based on business requirements.

Typical decision cues:
- Need centralized control and shared services -> use a platform landing zone.
- Need workload autonomy and isolation -> use application subscriptions.
- Need strong governance across many subscriptions -> use management groups and policy.
- Need private access to PaaS services -> use private endpoints and private DNS.
- Need hybrid name resolution -> consider Azure DNS Private Resolver.
- Need emergency access resilience -> design break-glass accounts.
- Need repeatable onboarding -> use subscription vending automation.

**Practical design checklist**

Use this checklist when evaluating a landing zone design:

- Define the management group hierarchy.
- Decide which subscriptions are platform and which are application.
- Establish identity controls, including PIM and break-glass accounts.
- Choose the network topology, such as hub-and-spoke or virtual WAN.
- Plan private DNS and hybrid name resolution.
- Set policy baselines for regions, tags, and public exposure.
- Centralize logging and monitoring.
- Define budgets and cost ownership.
- Automate subscription onboarding and baseline deployment.
- Document separation of duties and operational responsibilities.

**Quick comparison of common scopes**

| Scope | Best for | Avoid using for |
|---|---|---|
| Management group | Governance, policy, RBAC inheritance | Per-resource operational details |
| Subscription | Billing, quota, workload isolation | Fine-grained app component grouping only |
| Resource group | Deployment lifecycle and resource grouping | Enterprise governance boundaries |
| Resource | Specific configuration and access control | Organization-wide standards |

**Key takeaways**

A landing zone is the foundation for enterprise Azure adoption. It combines identity, networking, governance, and operations into a repeatable model that supports scale and control.

For AZ-305, remember:
- Use management groups for inherited governance.
- Separate platform services from application workloads.
- Design for least privilege, automation, and observability.
- Treat private networking and DNS as first-class architecture concerns.
- Use subscription vending and reference implementations to standardize deployment.
