## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**Cost management fundamentals**

Azure cost management is the discipline of measuring, analyzing, allocating, and optimizing cloud spend. In practice, you use it to answer three questions:

- What did we spend?
- Who consumed it?
- How do we reduce or recover it?

**Showback** reports costs to teams without financial transfer. **Chargeback** assigns costs to teams or business units for internal billing. Both depend on accurate scope design, tagging, allocation rules, and reporting.

Azure cost data is not just about resource meters. It can also include:

- Compute, storage, networking, and managed service usage
- Marketplace purchases
- Support plans
- Reservations and savings plans
- Taxes and other billing-related charges, depending on agreement and scope

A good cost model separates:
- Direct costs that belong to one team
- Shared costs that must be split
- Central platform costs that may be redistributed
- Non-resource costs that still affect the internal bill

**Azure billing and scope hierarchy**

Azure cost visibility depends on the billing agreement and the scope you query. For exam purposes, understand both the **billing hierarchy** and the **management hierarchy**.

Typical billing hierarchy concepts include:

- **Billing account**: The top-level commercial container for invoices and payment relationships.
- **Billing profile**: A billing construct used for invoice generation and payment settings in many agreements.
- **Invoice section**: A subdivision used to organize charges on invoices and often align them to departments or cost centers.
- **Subscription**: The deployment and consumption boundary for Azure resources.

The exact hierarchy varies by agreement type, such as Microsoft Customer Agreement (MCA), Enterprise Agreement (EA), or Cloud Solution Provider (CSP). The important exam idea is that billing scope and management scope are not the same thing.

Management hierarchy concepts include:

- **Management group**
- **Subscription**
- **Resource group**
- **Resource**

Cost data can be queried at these scopes, but not all billing constructs map cleanly to management groups. Management groups are useful for governance and aggregation, but cost reporting often still rolls up from subscriptions and billing scopes.

> Management group-level reporting is useful for aggregation, but it does not replace billing-scope reporting. If a cost item is not attached to a subscription resource, you may need billing-scope views to see it accurately.

**Showback vs chargeback**

Showback and chargeback use the same underlying cost data, but they serve different business goals.

| Concept | Purpose | Financial impact | Typical audience |
|---|---|---:|---|
| Showback | Inform teams of their consumption | No internal billing transfer | Engineering, product, finance |
| Chargeback | Recover costs from consuming teams | Internal billing or journal allocation | Finance, IT, business units |

Use showback when you want transparency and behavior change. Use chargeback when you need formal cost recovery or budget enforcement.

A practical pattern is to start with showback, validate allocation logic, then move to chargeback once the organization trusts the numbers.

**Allocation models**

Cost allocation is the process of splitting shared or indirect costs across consumers. This is where most chargeback models succeed or fail.

Common allocation methods include:

- **Direct allocation**: Assign the full cost to the resource owner when ownership is clear.
- **Proportional allocation**: Split shared costs by a measurable driver such as CPU hours, storage used, requests, or headcount.
- **Fixed allocation**: Assign a predetermined percentage or monthly amount to each team.
- **Hybrid allocation**: Use direct allocation for obvious costs and proportional allocation for shared services.

Examples of shared cost drivers:

- Network hub and firewall costs split by spoke subscription
- Shared AKS control plane or platform services split by namespace or node usage
- Central monitoring costs split by log ingestion volume
- Shared identity or security tooling split by subscription count or user count

A simple allocation workflow looks like this:

1. Identify the cost pool.
2. Choose the allocation driver.
3. Collect usage metrics for each consumer.
4. Calculate each consumer’s share.
5. Publish the showback report.
6. Apply chargeback only after validation and approval.

Example allocation table:

| Cost pool | Monthly cost | Driver | Team A | Team B | Team C |
|---|---:|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Shared firewall | $900 | Traffic volume | 50% | 30% | 20% |
| Central logging | $1,200 | GB ingested | 40% | 35% | 25% |
| Platform support | $500 | Fixed split | $200 | $150 | $150 |

**Tagging strategy for cost attribution**

Tags are one of the most important tools for showback and chargeback. They help you map Azure resources to business dimensions such as owner, application, environment, and cost center.

Common tags include:

- `CostCenter`
- `Application`
- `Owner`
- `Environment`
- `BusinessUnit`
- `Project`

Good tagging practices:

- Use a standard tag dictionary.
- Enforce required tags at deployment time.
- Keep tag values consistent and machine-readable.
- Avoid free-form values that break reporting.
- Treat tags as metadata, not security boundaries.

Example Azure CLI commands for tag management:

```bash
# Add or update tags on a resource group
az tag update \
  --resource-id /subscriptions/<sub-id>/resourceGroups/<rg-name> \
  --operation Merge \
  --tags CostCenter=FIN-1001 Environment=Prod Owner=platform-team

# View tags on a resource group
az tag list --resource-id /subscriptions/<sub-id>/resourceGroups/<rg-name>
```

For resource-level tagging, many Azure resource providers also support resource-specific update commands that include tags. The exact command depends on the resource type.

A simple policy-driven tagging approach can help you enforce standards:

- Audit missing tags
- Deny deployments without required tags
- Append default tags where appropriate
- Remediate existing resources with noncompliant tags

A policy definition for required tags must include a full policy rule and parameters. A partial snippet is not enough for deployment. In practice, you use Azure Policy to evaluate compliance and optionally deny or modify resources during creation.

**Azure Policy and governance for cost control**

Azure Policy helps you prevent cost drift by enforcing standards that reduce waste and improve attribution.

Useful policy patterns include:

- Require tags such as `CostCenter` and `Owner`
- Restrict expensive SKUs in nonproduction environments
- Limit allowed regions to reduce data transfer and support consistency
- Deny public IP creation where not needed
- Enforce diagnostic settings to improve usage visibility
- Audit orphaned resources such as unattached disks or idle public IPs

Policy is especially useful when combined with management groups. You can apply governance at the top of the hierarchy and inherit it downward to subscriptions and resource groups.

**Cost Management tools and reporting**

Azure Cost Management and Billing provides the core reporting and analysis experience. Use it to:

- Analyze spend by scope, tag, service, resource group, or subscription
- Set budgets and alerts
- Forecast future spend
- Export data for external reporting
- Track reservation and savings plan utilization
- Identify anomalies and trends

Practical reporting views include:

- Cost by subscription
- Cost by resource group
- Cost by tag
- Cost by service name
- Cost by location
- Amortized cost views for reservations and savings plans

**Amortized cost** is important when you want a fairer internal allocation model. Instead of showing the full reservation purchase on the purchase date, amortization spreads the benefit across the period of use. This often produces a more accurate showback or chargeback report than raw actual cost.

**Reservations, savings plans, and allocation accuracy**

Reservations and savings plans change how costs appear in reports.

Key considerations:

- The upfront or recurring commitment may not align with the teams that consume the benefit.
- A reservation purchased by a central platform team may benefit multiple subscriptions.
- Amortized reporting helps distribute the effective cost across the consuming workloads.
- You may need a separate allocation rule for unused reservation capacity.
- Savings plan benefits are applied at the billing scope and can be harder to attribute directly to a single team.

For chargeback accuracy, decide whether you allocate:

- The purchase cost to the buyer
- The benefit to the consuming team
- Both, using a net cost model

A common internal model is:

- Platform team owns the commitment purchase
- Business teams receive the amortized benefit
- Unused commitment is reported back to the platform owner as waste

**Marketplace, support, and non-resource charges**

Not all costs come from Azure resources.

You should explicitly account for:

- Marketplace software charges
- Support plan fees
- Data transfer and bandwidth charges
- Subscription-level fees
- Tax and invoice adjustments where applicable

These costs can distort team-level reporting if you only look at resource usage. For chargeback, decide whether to:

- Allocate them to the subscription owner
- Spread them across all consuming teams
- Keep them as central overhead

This decision should be documented in your internal financial model.

**Budgets, alerts, and forecasting**

Budgets are a control mechanism, not just a reporting feature. They help you detect overspend early.

Use budgets to:

- Track monthly, quarterly, or annual spend
- Trigger alerts at thresholds such as 50%, 80%, 90%, and 100%
- Notify owners before costs exceed plan
- Support governance for departments or projects

Budgets are often paired with action groups or email notifications. In larger environments, you may also route alerts into ITSM or chat systems.

Example Azure CLI budget creation pattern varies by billing scope and agreement type. In practice, you must use the command family that matches your billing model and scope. The important concept is that budgets are created at a scope such as subscription, resource group, or billing scope, and then tied to thresholds and notifications.

> Budget commands and parameters differ across billing agreements and API versions. Always verify the current CLI syntax for your scope before using it in automation.

**Exports, Power BI, and APIs**

For recurring showback and chargeback, manual portal exports are not enough. Use automated data pipelines.

Common patterns:

- **Cost Management exports** to send daily or monthly cost data to a storage account
- **Power BI** to build executive dashboards and team scorecards
- **Azure Cost Management APIs** to integrate cost data into custom reporting systems
- **Logic Apps or Azure Functions** to move exported data into a data warehouse

A typical recurring reporting architecture:

1. Azure Cost Management exports daily cost data to a storage account.
2. An ETL process loads the data into a data lake or SQL database.
3. Power BI reads the curated dataset.
4. Finance publishes monthly showback reports.
5. Chargeback journals are generated from approved allocation rules.

Example export workflow using the portal conceptually:

- Choose the billing or subscription scope
- Select the export type, such as actual cost or amortized cost
- Target a storage account and container
- Schedule daily or monthly delivery
- Validate file arrival and schema consistency

Example Power BI model fields often include:

- Subscription name
- Resource group
- Resource name
- Service name
- Meter category
- Cost
- Currency
- Tag values
- Usage date
- Amortized cost

**Practical showback report example**

A useful showback report should be understandable by both engineers and finance.

Example monthly report output:

| Team | Direct Azure cost | Shared platform allocation | Reservation benefit | Non-resource charges | Total showback |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Retail App | $8,400 | $1,200 | -$600 | $150 | $9,150 |
| Data Platform | $12,000 | $2,500 | -$1,100 | $300 | $13,700 |
| Shared Services | $3,000 | $4,000 | -$200 | $900 | $7,700 |

This kind of report helps teams see:

- Their direct consumption
- Their share of shared services
- The effect of reservations or savings plans
- Overhead items that are not tied to a single resource

**Practical chargeback workflow**

Chargeback requires stronger controls than showback because it affects financial records.

A typical workflow is:

1. Define the cost policy with finance and engineering.
2. Identify chargeable and non-chargeable cost categories.
3. Map resources to owners using tags, subscriptions, or invoice sections.
4. Allocate shared costs using an approved formula.
5. Reconcile reservation, savings plan, and marketplace charges.
6. Publish the final chargeback statement.
7. Post internal journal entries or invoice transfers.

Important governance points:

- Keep the allocation formula stable for the reporting period.
- Version-control the logic used for calculations.
- Reconcile totals against the Azure invoice or billing export.
- Document exceptions such as one-time credits or refunds.

**Optimization inputs beyond Cost Management**

Cost management is not only about reporting. It also feeds optimization decisions.

Useful inputs include:

- **Azure Advisor** cost recommendations for right-sizing and idle resource cleanup
- Underutilized VM recommendations
- Unattached disk cleanup
- Reserved instance or savings plan purchase guidance
- Storage tier optimization
- App Service or SQL tier adjustments

Advisor is a practical companion to Cost Management because it turns spend analysis into action. Cost reports tell you where money goes; Advisor helps you reduce waste.

**Management group and subscription design for cost governance**

Your landing zone design affects how easy chargeback becomes.

Good design patterns include:

- Separate subscriptions by environment, business unit, or workload criticality
- Use management groups to apply governance consistently
- Keep shared platform services in dedicated subscriptions
- Use invoice sections or billing profiles to align commercial reporting with business ownership
- Avoid mixing unrelated workloads in the same subscription when chargeback precision matters

Trade-offs:

- More subscriptions improve attribution but increase operational overhead
- Fewer subscriptions simplify operations but make cost allocation harder
- Management groups help with policy inheritance, but they do not solve all billing attribution problems

**Common exam scenarios**

Expect questions that ask you to choose the best mechanism for a business goal.

Typical mappings:

- Need visibility only: use showback
- Need internal billing: use chargeback
- Need to enforce metadata: use tags and Azure Policy
- Need recurring reporting: use exports and Power BI
- Need to reduce waste: use budgets, alerts, and Azure Advisor
- Need fairer commitment reporting: use amortized cost

**Pitfalls and troubleshooting**

Common mistakes include:

- Using tags inconsistently across teams
- Allocating shared costs without a documented formula
- Ignoring marketplace and support charges
- Reporting raw reservation purchases instead of amortized cost
- Expecting management group views to replace billing-scope views
- Treating budgets as hard spending limits when they are primarily alerts and governance signals
- Building chargeback on incomplete data before validating the model

If your report totals do not match the invoice, check:

- Scope selection
- Currency and billing period
- Amortized versus actual cost
- Credits, refunds, and adjustments
- Reservation and savings plan treatment
- Missing non-resource charges

**Quick reference**

- **Showback**: report cost without billing transfer
- **Chargeback**: recover cost internally
- **Tags**: primary attribution metadata
- **Azure Policy**: enforce tagging and cost controls
- **Cost Management and Billing**: analyze, export, budget, and forecast
- **Amortized cost**: fairer view for reservations and savings plans
- **Azure Advisor**: optimization recommendations
- **Exports and Power BI**: recurring reporting pipeline

**Key takeaway**

A strong Azure cost model combines governance, attribution, and reporting. You use tags and policy to classify spend, Cost Management to measure it, exports and BI to publish it, and allocation rules to turn raw usage into showback or chargeback that the business can trust.
