## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**RBAC at scale: the design goal**

Azure <abbr title="Role-Based Access Control">RBAC</abbr> is the primary authorization model for Azure management-plane access. At scale, the goal is not just to grant access, but to make access predictable, auditable, and easy to operate across many subscriptions, teams, and environments.

A scalable design usually follows these principles:

- Assign access to **groups**, not individual users, whenever possible.
- Use the **lowest scope** that still meets the business need.
- Prefer built-in roles before creating custom roles.
- Separate **administration** of access from **ownership** of resources.
- Design for lifecycle events such as joiner, mover, leaver, project end, and emergency access.

RBAC design becomes difficult when teams create many one-off assignments, assign broad roles at subscription scope, or mix access control with unrelated governance tools. Good architecture reduces operational overhead and limits privilege creep.

**Core RBAC hierarchy and inheritance**

Azure scopes are hierarchical:

- **Management group**
- **Subscription**
- **Resource group**
- **Resource**

Permissions generally inherit downward. A role assignment at a management group can apply to many subscriptions beneath it, which is useful for platform teams and enterprise-wide standards.

Important exceptions and limitations matter in real designs:

- **Deny assignments** can block access even when an allow assignment exists. These are commonly used by managed services, Azure Blueprints, and some platform-managed scenarios.
- **Azure Blueprints** can create role assignments and deny assignments as part of a governed deployment pattern.
- **Managed applications** can also introduce deny assignments to protect provider-managed resources.
- Inheritance is not always a simple “more scope means more access” story; deny rules and service-managed boundaries can override expectations.

> A deny assignment is evaluated before effective access is granted, so a user may appear to have permission through RBAC but still be blocked from a specific action or resource.

**Scope strategy for enterprise design**

Choose scope based on operational intent:

- **Management group scope**
  - Use for platform-wide policies, baseline access, and centralized operations.
  - Best for shared services teams, security teams, and governance roles.
- **Subscription scope**
  - Use when a team owns an entire subscription boundary.
  - Common for application teams with isolated environments.
- **Resource group scope**
  - Use for app-specific or environment-specific delegation.
  - Good for tightly bounded workloads and temporary project access.
- **Resource scope**
  - Use for highly sensitive or narrowly delegated operations.
  - Example: granting access to one Key Vault, one storage account, or one AKS cluster.

A common enterprise pattern is:

1. Management groups define the organizational hierarchy.
2. Subscriptions separate billing, ownership, or environment boundaries.
3. Resource groups separate workloads.
4. RBAC assignments are made to groups at the highest practical scope.

**Built-in role selection: choosing the right role**

Role choice is one of the most common exam and design pitfalls.

- **Owner**
  - Full management-plane access, including the ability to assign access.
  - Use only when a person or team must fully control a scope.
  - Pitfall: it is often over-assigned when teams really need only resource management or access delegation.
- **Contributor**
  - Can create and manage resources, but cannot grant access.
  - Use for operators and application teams that deploy and manage resources.
  - Pitfall: it is not enough if the team must manage permissions.
- **User Access Administrator**
  - Can manage access assignments but not resource configuration.
  - Use for identity or platform teams that administer RBAC.
  - Pitfall: it is powerful because it can grant access, so it should be tightly controlled.

Other built-in roles are often better than broad roles:

- **Reader** for audit and visibility.
- Service-specific roles such as **Key Vault Administrator**, **Storage Blob Data Contributor**, or **Cosmos DB Built-in Data Contributor** when data-plane access is needed.
- Network or security roles when the task is limited to those domains.

A practical rule:

- If the user needs to **change resources**, use Contributor or a narrower built-in role.
- If the user needs to **grant access**, use User Access Administrator or a controlled privileged workflow.
- If the user needs both, consider whether that is a temporary elevation rather than a permanent assignment.

**Management group administration specifics**

Management groups are the backbone of large-scale RBAC, but they have their own administration model.

- The **tenant root group** sits at the top of the management group hierarchy.
- Access at the tenant root group is highly sensitive and should be limited.
- Only appropriately privileged identities can create and manage management groups.
- Management group creation and hierarchy changes are typically controlled by tenant-level or management-group-level permissions, not by ordinary subscription owners.

Design implications:

- Do not assume subscription owners can reorganize the management group tree.
- Keep management group administration separate from workload administration.
- Use a small set of platform administrators to manage hierarchy changes.
- Document who can create new management groups, move subscriptions, and assign roles at the root.

**Group-based access design at scale**

Azure RBAC works best when you assign roles to Microsoft Entra ID groups rather than users.

Benefits:

- Easier onboarding and offboarding.
- Fewer direct assignments.
- Better auditability.
- Cleaner separation between identity lifecycle and authorization.

Group design considerations:

- Use **security groups** for stable access control.
- Use **dynamic groups** when membership can be derived from attributes such as department, location, or device state.
- Avoid relying on **nested groups** for RBAC design; nested group behavior is limited and can complicate troubleshooting and governance.
- Keep group naming consistent, such as `az-rg-prod-app1-contrib` or `az-sub-platform-reader`.

At scale, group lifecycle matters:

- If a group is deleted, RBAC assignments tied to it become ineffective.
- If membership changes are delayed, access may lag behind HR or IAM events.
- Dynamic groups reduce manual work, but they require reliable attributes and careful rule design.
- Use access reviews to validate that group membership still matches business need.

**Role assignment scale and sprawl control**

Large tenants can accumulate thousands of role assignments. That creates operational and performance challenges.

Practical scale concerns:

- Too many direct user assignments increase audit complexity.
- Too many unique custom roles increase maintenance burden.
- Excessive scope fragmentation makes troubleshooting difficult.
- Repeated one-off assignments often indicate a missing group or missing role design pattern.

Ways to avoid RBAC sprawl:

- Standardize a small catalog of access groups.
- Reuse role assignment patterns across subscriptions and environments.
- Prefer inheritance from management groups when the same access applies broadly.
- Use custom roles only when built-in roles do not fit.
- Periodically inventory assignments and remove stale entries.

Custom role guidance:

- Create custom roles only for a specific gap in built-in permissions.
- Keep actions as narrow as possible.
- Avoid creating near-duplicate custom roles for each team.
- Review custom roles when services change, because provider operations evolve over time.

> In very large tenants, the design problem is often not “how do I grant access?” but “how do I prevent thousands of slightly different access patterns from becoming unmanageable?”

**Data-plane RBAC examples**

Azure RBAC covers both management-plane and, for many services, data-plane authorization. Data-plane access is often service-specific.

Common examples:

- **Storage**
  - Use roles such as `Storage Blob Data Reader`, `Storage Blob Data Contributor`, or `Storage Blob Data Owner`.
  - These control access to blob data, not just the storage account resource.
- **Key Vault**
  - Use Azure RBAC data-plane roles such as `Key Vault Secrets User` or `Key Vault Administrator` when the vault is configured for RBAC authorization.
  - Be aware that some environments still use access policies, so confirm the authorization model.
- **Azure SQL**
  - Azure RBAC can control management-plane access to the SQL server or database resource.
  - Actual database data access usually relies on SQL authentication, Microsoft Entra ID authentication, contained users, or database roles.
- **Azure Cosmos DB**
  - Use Cosmos DB data-plane roles for item-level or container-level access when supported by the account configuration.
  - Management-plane RBAC alone does not grant data access.
- **Azure Kubernetes Service (AKS)**
  - Azure RBAC can control cluster management access.
  - Kubernetes RBAC still governs in-cluster permissions unless Azure RBAC for Kubernetes is enabled and designed accordingly.

Design takeaway:

- Always distinguish between **resource management** and **data access**.
- Verify whether the service uses Azure RBAC, service-native authorization, or both.

**Azure Lighthouse and delegated resource management**

Azure Lighthouse is important for managed service providers and multi-tenant operations.

What it does:

- Delegates access to resources across tenant boundaries.
- Lets a managing tenant operate on customer subscriptions or resource groups without traditional guest-user sprawl.
- Supports centralized operations at scale.

Why it matters for RBAC design:

- It reduces the need to create many guest accounts in customer tenants.
- It enables standardized operational roles across many customers.
- It can simplify monitoring, patching, and support workflows.

Design cautions:

- Delegated access still needs least privilege.
- Scope carefully to the smallest practical boundary.
- Track which managing tenant identities have access to which customer scopes.

**Governance: PIM, access reviews, and entitlement management**

RBAC is stronger when paired with governance controls.

**Privileged Identity Management (PIM)**

PIM provides just-in-time elevation for privileged roles.

Use PIM to:

- Make high-privilege roles eligible instead of permanently active.
- Require approval before activation for sensitive roles.
- Enforce MFA, justification, and time limits.
- Reduce standing privilege.

Typical workflow:

1. A user is made eligible for a role.
2. The user requests activation when needed.
3. Approval and policy checks occur.
4. The role becomes active for a limited time.
5. The access expires automatically.

**Access reviews**

Access reviews help certify that access is still needed.

- Schedule periodic reviews for privileged groups and roles.
- Use managers, resource owners, or application owners as reviewers.
- Remove access when it is no longer justified.
- Use reviews for both direct assignments and group membership.

**Entitlement management and access packages**

Entitlement management can package access requests into governed workflows.

- Users request a package rather than individual roles.
- Approval chains can include managers, app owners, or security teams.
- Packages can include group membership, app access, and expiration rules.
- This is useful for contractors, project teams, and temporary access.

At scale, these tools reduce manual RBAC administration and improve audit readiness.

**RBAC, Azure Policy, and resource locks**

RBAC is not the only control plane governance tool.

- **Azure Policy** controls what can be deployed or how resources must be configured.
- **Resource locks** prevent deletion or modification of critical resources.
- **RBAC** controls who can perform actions.

How they interact:

- A user may have RBAC permission to create a resource, but Azure Policy can deny noncompliant deployment.
- A user may have Contributor access, but a delete lock can block removal.
- A user may have Owner access, but a lock or policy can still restrict certain operations.

Design guidance:

- Use RBAC for identity-based authorization.
- Use Policy for compliance and standardization.
- Use locks for protection against accidental change or deletion.
- Do not use RBAC as a substitute for policy enforcement.

**End-to-end enterprise pattern**

A scalable enterprise pattern often looks like this:

1. Create a management group hierarchy for platform, landing zones, and sandbox or dev/test areas.
2. Assign platform governance roles at the management group level.
3. Create security groups in Microsoft Entra ID for each job function.
4. Assign built-in roles to those groups at the narrowest practical scope.
5. Make privileged roles eligible through PIM.
6. Use access reviews for periodic certification.
7. Use entitlement management for temporary or request-based access.
8. Monitor for assignment drift and remove stale permissions.

Example pattern:

```bash
# Create a resource group-scoped role assignment for a group
az role assignment create \
  --assignee-object-id 11111111-2222-3333-4444-555555555555 \
  --assignee-principal-type Group \
  --role "Contributor" \
  --scope /subscriptions/aaaaaaaa-bbbb-cccc-dddd-eeeeeeeeeeee/resourceGroups/rg-app-prod
```

Example management group assignment:

```bash
az role assignment create \
  --assignee-object-id 11111111-2222-3333-4444-555555555555 \
  --assignee-principal-type Group \
  --role "Reader" \
  --scope /providers/Microsoft.Management/managementGroups/mg-platform
```

Example custom role definition skeleton:

```json
{
  "Name": "App Deployment Operator",
  "IsCustom": true,
  "Description": "Can deploy and manage app resources without access administration.",
  "Actions": [
    "Microsoft.Resources/subscriptions/resourceGroups/read",
    "Microsoft.Compute/virtualMachines/*",
    "Microsoft.Network/networkInterfaces/*"
  ],
  "NotActions": [
    "Microsoft.Authorization/*/write"
  ],
  "AssignableScopes": [
    "/subscriptions/aaaaaaaa-bbbb-cccc-dddd-eeeeeeeeeeee"
  ]
}
```

Example PIM-oriented operating model:

- Group `az-platform-security-admins` is eligible for `User Access Administrator`.
- Activation requires approval and MFA.
- Access is time-bound to 2 hours.
- Access reviews run monthly.
- The group is assigned at the management group scope, not per subscription.

**Troubleshooting and exam pitfalls**

Common mistakes include:

- Assuming Owner is required when Contributor plus a separate access-admin role would be safer.
- Forgetting that data-plane access may need a different role than management-plane access.
- Ignoring deny assignments from managed applications or blueprints.
- Overusing direct user assignments instead of groups.
- Designing nested group structures and expecting them to behave like flat RBAC groups.
- Giving subscription owners the ability to reorganize management groups without governance.
- Treating Azure Policy or locks as if they replace RBAC.

When evaluating a scenario, ask:

- What is the minimum scope?
- Is this management-plane or data-plane access?
- Does the service use Azure RBAC, service-native permissions, or both?
- Should the access be permanent, eligible, or temporary?
- Can a group-based pattern replace direct assignment?
- Are there governance controls that should complement RBAC?

**Quick comparison of common roles**

| Role | Best use | Common pitfall |
|---|---|---|
| Owner | Full control including access management | Over-assigned for routine operations |
| Contributor | Manage resources without granting access | Cannot delegate permissions |
| User Access Administrator | Manage role assignments | Too powerful for broad use |
| Reader | Audit and visibility | Not enough for troubleshooting changes |

**Practical design checklist**

- Define the management group hierarchy first.
- Map business functions to Entra ID groups.
- Use built-in roles before custom roles.
- Assign at the highest practical scope.
- Use PIM for privileged access.
- Review access regularly.
- Watch for deny assignments and service-specific authorization models.
- Control RBAC sprawl with standards, naming, and lifecycle management.

**Summary**

RBAC at scale is an operating model, not just a permission system. The best designs combine hierarchical scope planning, group-based assignments, least privilege, privileged access workflows, and governance controls such as Policy, locks, PIM, and access reviews. For AZ-305, focus on when to use each role, how scope inheritance works, and how enterprise constraints change the design.
