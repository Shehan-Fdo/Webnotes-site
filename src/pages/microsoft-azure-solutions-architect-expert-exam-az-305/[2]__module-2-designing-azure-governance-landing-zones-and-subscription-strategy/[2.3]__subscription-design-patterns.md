## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**Subscription design patterns overview**

An Azure subscription is a core governance, billing, and access boundary. You use it to separate workloads, delegate administration, apply policy, and control cost visibility. In enterprise design, the subscription is rarely just a billing container; it is a practical unit for lifecycle management, security baseline enforcement, and operational ownership.

A good subscription model balances isolation with manageability. Too few subscriptions can create noisy billing, broad permissions, and weak blast-radius control. Too many subscriptions can create sprawl, duplicated policy work, and operational overhead. The right pattern depends on organizational structure, compliance needs, environment separation, and how independently teams deliver and operate workloads.

**What a subscription is and is not**

A subscription is a logical container for Azure resources. It is associated with a Microsoft Entra tenant, and that tenant relationship matters for identity, access control, and governance. Resources in a subscription can span multiple regions, so a subscription is not a region boundary.

A subscription is useful when you need:
- **Billing separation** for chargeback or showback.
- **Access isolation** for different teams, business units, or environments.
- **Policy scope** for baseline controls and exceptions.
- **Quota and limit management** for workloads with distinct capacity needs.
- **Operational ownership** for independent lifecycle and support processes.

A subscription is not the right boundary when:
- You only need to separate resources by application component but still want shared administration.
- You want to isolate network traffic only; virtual networks, peering, and hub-spoke design may be enough.
- You need fine-grained access within a workload; resource groups, RBAC, and tags may be better.

**Enterprise hierarchy and where subscriptions fit**

Azure governance usually follows this hierarchy:

```text
Management group
  └─ Subscription
      └─ Resource group
          └─ Resource
```

Management groups provide policy and access inheritance across multiple subscriptions. Subscriptions sit below management groups and above resource groups. Resource groups are the deployment and lifecycle boundary for a set of related resources, but they do not require a single region and they do not replace subscription-level governance.

Use this hierarchy to separate concerns:
- **Management groups** for organization-wide governance and policy inheritance.
- **Subscriptions** for billing, access, and workload isolation.
- **Resource groups** for deployment units and lifecycle grouping.
- **Resources** for the actual services.

**Common subscription design patterns**

Different organizations use different patterns depending on scale and governance maturity.

| Pattern | Best for | Strengths | Tradeoffs |
|---|---|---|---|
| One subscription per environment | Clear dev/test/prod separation | Strong isolation, simpler environment-specific policy | Can increase subscription count quickly |
| One subscription per application | Independent app teams and release cycles | Good ownership and blast-radius control | Shared services become harder to centralize |
| One subscription per business unit | Large enterprises with distinct cost centers | Strong billing and accountability | App-level isolation may be weaker |
| Shared platform subscription | Central services like networking, identity, monitoring | Efficient shared operations | Requires careful RBAC and policy design |
| Landing zone subscriptions | Standardized enterprise onboarding | Repeatable governance and security baseline | Needs strong automation and lifecycle control |

**One subscription per environment**

This pattern is common when you want strong separation between development, test, and production. It works well when each environment has different access rules, compliance requirements, or release cadence.

Use it when:
- Production needs stricter controls than non-production.
- Teams deploy independently to each environment.
- You want separate budgets and alerts per environment.

Tradeoffs:
- Shared services may need duplication or cross-subscription access.
- Small teams can end up managing too many subscriptions.
- Environment parity can drift if you do not automate deployment.

A typical layout looks like this:

```text
Platform MG
  ├─ NonProd MG
  │   ├─ Dev subscription
  │   └─ Test subscription
  └─ Prod MG
      └─ Prod subscription
```

**One subscription per application**

This pattern gives each application its own boundary. It is useful when an app has its own team, release pipeline, security posture, and cost center.

Use it when:
- The application has independent ownership.
- You need clear cost attribution per product.
- The app has distinct compliance or data handling requirements.

Tradeoffs:
- Shared infrastructure such as logging or networking may need a separate platform subscription.
- Cross-application reporting can become more complex.
- Small applications may not justify a dedicated subscription.

**One subscription per business unit**

This pattern aligns with organizational finance and accountability. It is common in large enterprises where business units own budgets and governance decisions.

Use it when:
- Chargeback or showback is organized by business unit.
- Each unit has its own IT governance model.
- Central IT provides guardrails, but business units own delivery.

Tradeoffs:
- Business-unit boundaries may not match application boundaries.
- A single subscription can become crowded if the unit runs many workloads.
- Security and compliance exceptions may vary widely inside the same subscription.

**Shared platform subscription**

A shared platform subscription hosts common services such as:
- Hub networking
- DNS
- Bastion or jump services
- Monitoring and logging
- Shared automation
- Security tooling

This pattern reduces duplication and centralizes operations. It is especially useful in hub-spoke architectures and landing zone designs.

Use it when:
- Multiple workloads consume the same platform services.
- You want central control over shared infrastructure.
- You need a stable foundation for workload subscriptions.

Tradeoffs:
- Platform teams need strong RBAC boundaries.
- Shared subscriptions can become critical dependencies.
- Changes must be carefully governed to avoid broad impact.

**Landing zone and subscription vending**

A landing zone is a standardized environment for deploying workloads with the right governance, networking, identity, and security controls already in place. Subscription vending is the automated process of creating and configuring new subscriptions according to that standard.

A mature vending process usually includes:
- Subscription creation and naming.
- Management group placement.
- Policy assignment.
- RBAC role assignments.
- Budget and cost alert setup.
- Diagnostic settings and log routing.
- Network baseline attachment.
- Security baseline onboarding.

This approach reduces manual setup and helps enforce consistency.

Example onboarding flow:
1. Request a new subscription through a portal or pipeline.
2. Create or associate the subscription with the correct Microsoft Entra tenant.
3. Place the subscription in the right management group.
4. Apply policy initiatives and role assignments.
5. Configure monitoring, logging, and budget controls.
6. Hand over to the workload team.

**Subscription ownership and tenant association**

Every Azure subscription is linked to a Microsoft Entra tenant. That association affects identity, access, and governance. If you move a subscription between tenants, you must plan for identity changes, role assignments, managed identities, and service principals.

Important considerations:
- **Directory association** determines which tenant controls identity objects.
- Role assignments may need to be recreated after tenant changes.
- Managed identities and app registrations can be impacted.
- Cross-tenant moves require careful validation of dependencies.

> Be cautious with tenant transfers. They can break access assumptions, automation, and application authentication if you do not revalidate identity dependencies.

**Subscription types and offer models**

Subscription offer type affects procurement, billing, and governance. Common enterprise models include:
- **Pay-As-You-Go (PAYG)** for flexible consumption and smaller teams.
- **Enterprise Agreement (EA)** for large organizations with centralized billing and negotiated terms.
- **Microsoft Customer Agreement (MCA)** for modern commerce and flexible billing structures.
- **Cloud Solution Provider (CSP)** for partner-managed billing and support.

These models influence design decisions:
- Billing ownership and invoice structure.
- Who can create subscriptions.
- How budgets and cost management are organized.
- Whether central IT or a partner controls procurement.

For AZ-305, remember that subscription design is not only technical. It is also a financial and organizational decision.

**Naming and tagging standards**

Standardization makes subscriptions easier to manage at scale. Use naming conventions that encode ownership, environment, and purpose without becoming unreadable.

Common naming pattern:
- `<org>-<bu>-<app>-<env>-<region>-<number>`

Examples:
- `contoso-fin-payments-prod-eus-001`
- `contoso-platform-shared-nonprod-weu-001`

Good naming practices:
- Keep names consistent across subscriptions, resource groups, and resource tags.
- Avoid ambiguous abbreviations.
- Use a controlled vocabulary for environment names such as `dev`, `test`, `uat`, `prod`.
- Reserve tags for metadata that changes more often than the subscription name.

Useful tags at subscription scope:
- `Owner`
- `CostCenter`
- `BusinessUnit`
- `Environment`
- `DataClassification`
- `Criticality`
- `SupportGroup`

Example tag strategy:
```json
{
  "Owner": "platform-team",
  "CostCenter": "CC1024",
  "BusinessUnit": "finance",
  "Environment": "prod",
  "DataClassification": "confidential",
  "Criticality": "high"
}
```

**RBAC design at subscription scope**

Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) at subscription scope should follow least privilege. Subscription-level access is powerful, so you should assign it sparingly and prefer narrower scopes when possible.

Common patterns:
- **Reader** for audit, support, and visibility-only teams.
- **Contributor** for workload teams that manage resources but should not grant access.
- **Owner** only for a small set of platform administrators.
- **User Access Administrator** for teams that manage role assignments.
- **Custom roles** for specialized operations such as backup administration, network operations, or policy management.

Design guidance:
- Assign roles to groups, not individual users, whenever possible.
- Use separate groups for platform admins, workload operators, and auditors.
- Avoid giving `Owner` broadly at subscription scope.
- Use custom roles when built-in roles are too permissive.

Example custom role definition for limited subscription operations:
```json
{
  "Name": "Subscription Operator",
  "IsCustom": true,
  "Description": "Can manage resources but cannot assign access.",
  "Actions": [
    "Microsoft.Resources/subscriptions/resourceGroups/*",
    "Microsoft.Compute/*",
    "Microsoft.Network/*"
  ],
  "NotActions": [
    "Microsoft.Authorization/*/write"
  ],
  "AssignableScopes": [
    "/subscriptions/00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000"
  ]
}
```

**Policy inheritance, initiatives, and exceptions**

Azure Policy helps enforce standards. Policy assignments at management group scope inherit to subscriptions below them, which is the preferred way to apply baseline controls consistently. Subscription-level assignments are useful for workload-specific rules or exceptions.

Key ideas:
- **Initiatives** group multiple policy definitions into a single assignment.
- Management group assignments provide broad inheritance.
- Subscription assignments refine controls for a specific workload.
- Resource group or resource scope can be used for targeted exceptions, but use them carefully.

Typical enterprise pattern:
- Apply baseline initiatives at the management group level.
- Apply workload-specific policies at the subscription level.
- Use exemptions for approved exceptions with expiration and review.

Example CLI pattern for assigning a policy initiative at subscription scope:
```bash
az policy assignment create \
  --name "baseline-security" \
  --display-name "Baseline Security" \
  --scope "/subscriptions/00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000" \
  --policy-set-definition "/providers/Microsoft.Management/managementGroups/contoso/providers/Microsoft.Authorization/policySetDefinitions/baseline-security"
```

Example CLI pattern for assigning a single policy definition:
```bash
az policy assignment create \
  --name "allowed-locations" \
  --display-name "Allowed Locations" \
  --scope "/subscriptions/00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000" \
  --policy "/providers/Microsoft.Authorization/policyDefinitions/allowed-locations"
```

> Verify the exact parameter names in your Azure CLI version. Policy commands and flags can vary slightly across releases.

**Operational onboarding at subscription creation time**

A subscription should not be created in a blank state. The first hours of a new subscription are the best time to establish guardrails.

Recommended onboarding tasks:
- Assign the subscription to the correct management group.
- Apply baseline policy initiatives.
- Configure RBAC groups and break-glass access.
- Set budgets and alerts.
- Enable diagnostic settings for activity logs.
- Route logs to a central Log Analytics workspace or SIEM.
- Register required resource providers.
- Attach network and security baselines.
- Document ownership, support contacts, and escalation paths.

Example provider registration:
```bash
az provider register --namespace Microsoft.Compute
az provider register --namespace Microsoft.Network
az provider register --namespace Microsoft.KeyVault
```

Example budget creation:
```bash
az consumption budget create \
  --amount 5000 \
  --budget-name "prod-monthly-budget" \
  --category cost \
  --time-grain Monthly \
  --start-date 2026-01-01 \
  --end-date 2026-12-31 \
  --subscription-id 00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000
```

**CLI examples for subscription governance**

Use the Azure CLI to inspect and manage subscription placement and governance.

List subscriptions:
```bash
az account list -o table
```

Set the active subscription:
```bash
az account set --subscription "contoso-fin-payments-prod-eus-001"
```

Show the current subscription:
```bash
az account show -o jsonc
```

Move a subscription into a management group:
```bash
az account management-group subscription add \
  --name "contoso-prod" \
  --subscription "00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000"
```

View management group details:
```bash
az account management-group show --name "contoso-prod" -o jsonc
```

These commands are useful during subscription vending, governance audits, and reorganization projects.

**Subscription sprawl management**

Subscription sprawl happens when teams create subscriptions without a clear standard or lifecycle process. It increases operational burden and weakens governance.

Common causes:
- Teams create subscriptions for every small project.
- Old subscriptions remain active after workloads are retired.
- Temporary environments are never cleaned up.
- Ownership is unclear after team changes.

Controls that help:
- Require approval for new subscriptions.
- Use a standard request and onboarding workflow.
- Review subscriptions periodically for activity, cost, and ownership.
- Decommission unused subscriptions with a documented process.
- Enforce naming and tagging standards.
- Track subscriptions by lifecycle state such as `requested`, `active`, `suspended`, and `retired`.

A simple review checklist:
1. Confirm the subscription has an owner.
2. Check recent activity and spend.
3. Validate policy compliance.
4. Verify diagnostic and security baselines.
5. Decide whether to keep, archive, or retire it.

**Choosing the right pattern**

Use the following decision logic:

```text
Need strong environment isolation?
  └─ Yes → separate subscriptions by environment

Need independent app ownership and release cycles?
  └─ Yes → separate subscriptions per application

Need finance-aligned accountability?
  └─ Yes → separate subscriptions per business unit

Need shared services for many workloads?
  └─ Yes → add a platform subscription

Need repeatable onboarding at scale?
  └─ Yes → use landing zones and subscription vending
```

Practical tradeoffs:
- Choose **environment-based** separation when compliance and release control matter most.
- Choose **application-based** separation when teams own their own lifecycle.
- Choose **business-unit-based** separation when finance and reporting drive the model.
- Choose **platform subscriptions** when shared services need central control.
- Combine patterns when necessary; many enterprises use a hybrid model.

**Real-world design example**

A global company with multiple product teams might use:
- A management group hierarchy for platform, non-production, and production.
- One shared platform subscription for networking, logging, and security tooling.
- One subscription per product in production.
- One or more non-production subscriptions shared by smaller teams.
- Policy initiatives inherited from management groups.
- Subscription-level budgets and RBAC groups for each workload team.

This design gives the company:
- Central governance at scale.
- Clear cost attribution.
- Reduced blast radius.
- Repeatable onboarding.
- Room to grow without uncontrolled sprawl.

**Key takeaways**

Subscriptions are a strategic design boundary, not just an administrative container. The best pattern depends on ownership, compliance, billing, and operational independence. In enterprise Azure, you usually combine management groups, standardized subscriptions, policy inheritance, and automation to create a scalable governance model.
