## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**Separation of duties overview**

Separation of duties is a security and governance principle that divides critical responsibilities among different people, roles, or systems. In Azure architecture, you use it to reduce the chance that one identity can create, approve, deploy, and audit a change end to end.

The goal is not only to prevent malicious activity. It also reduces accidental misconfiguration, supports compliance, and creates clearer accountability. In practice, you combine identity controls, role-based access control, approval workflows, and logging.

- **Least privilege** limits each role to only the permissions needed.
- **Segregation** prevents one person from controlling the full lifecycle of a sensitive action.
- **Accountability** improves when actions map to distinct identities and audit trails.

**Why it matters in Azure**

Azure environments often span subscriptions, management groups, resource groups, and shared platform services. Without separation of duties, a single privileged account can:
- change network controls,
- deploy workloads,
- approve its own access,
- and delete evidence after the fact.

That concentration of power increases operational and security risk. Separation of duties helps you design controls so that:
- administrators manage infrastructure,
- developers deploy applications,
- security teams review and monitor,
- and approvers authorize sensitive changes.

This is especially important for regulated environments, production subscriptions, and landing zones that host shared services.

**Core design patterns**

A strong Azure implementation usually combines several patterns rather than relying on one control.

- **Role-based access control (RBAC)** assigns permissions by role instead of by individual.
- **Privileged Identity Management (PIM)** makes elevated access just-in-time and time-bound.
- **Management group hierarchy** separates platform, landing zone, and application ownership.
- **Subscription boundaries** isolate billing, policy, and administrative control.
- **Resource locks** protect critical resources from accidental deletion or modification.
- **Azure Policy** enforces guardrails even when users have broad permissions.

A practical pattern is to keep platform operations in one subscription or management group and application teams in separate subscriptions. That way, app teams can deploy workloads without being able to alter shared identity, networking, or security foundations.

**Common role separation model**

A typical enterprise model separates these responsibilities:

| Role | Typical responsibilities | What it should not do |
|---|---|---|
| Platform admin | Manage landing zones, networking, identity integration, policy | Approve its own exceptions or own application data |
| Security admin | Define security posture, monitor alerts, review access | Deploy production apps or bypass controls |
| Application owner | Deploy and operate app resources | Change shared network or identity settings |
| Auditor | Review logs, compliance, and change history | Modify resources or grant access |
| Approver | Review and authorize privileged actions | Execute the change being approved |

This model is flexible. The exact split depends on your organization, but the principle stays the same: no single role should control every stage of a sensitive operation.

**Azure RBAC and custom roles**

Azure RBAC is the main tool for implementing separation of duties. You assign built-in roles when they fit, and custom roles when you need narrower permissions.

Useful built-in roles include:
- **Owner**: full access including role assignment; use sparingly.
- **Contributor**: can manage resources but cannot grant access.
- **Reader**: can view resources without changing them.
- **User Access Administrator**: can manage access assignments; highly sensitive.
- **Security Admin**: can manage security-related settings in supported services.

For separation of duties, avoid giving broad roles like Owner to operational users unless absolutely necessary. Prefer:
- Contributor for deployment teams,
- Reader for auditors,
- custom roles for narrowly scoped tasks,
- and User Access Administrator only to dedicated access-management identities.

Example custom role definition:

```json
{
  "Name": "App Deployment Operator",
  "IsCustom": true,
  "Description": "Can deploy and manage application resources in a specific resource group.",
  "Actions": [
    "Microsoft.Web/sites/*",
    "Microsoft.Insights/alertRules/*",
    "Microsoft.Storage/storageAccounts/read"
  ],
  "NotActions": [
    "Microsoft.Authorization/*/Write",
    "Microsoft.Authorization/*/Delete"
  ],
  "AssignableScopes": [
    "/subscriptions/00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000/resourceGroups/app-prod-rg"
  ]
}
```

This kind of role lets a team operate its workload without being able to grant access or alter governance.

**Privileged Identity Management**

PIM is one of the most important tools for separation of duties in Azure. It provides just-in-time access, approval workflows, activation time limits, and access reviews for privileged roles.

Use PIM when you want:
- permanent low-privilege access,
- temporary elevation for maintenance,
- approval before activation,
- and an audit trail of who activated what and when.

A common pattern is:
- users have eligible, not permanent, access to privileged roles,
- they activate the role only when needed,
- activation requires MFA (multi-factor authentication),
- and activation expires automatically.

Example Azure CLI commands for role assignment and activation workflows:

```bash
# Assign a user as eligible for a privileged role at scope
az role assignment create \
  --assignee user@contoso.com \
  --role "Contributor" \
  --scope /subscriptions/00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000

# View role assignments at a scope
az role assignment list \
  --scope /subscriptions/00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000 \
  --output table
```

> PIM configuration is often managed through Microsoft Entra ID and Azure RBAC. Exact activation steps vary by role type and tenant settings.

**Management groups and subscription boundaries**

Management groups let you apply governance consistently across many subscriptions. This is useful for separation of duties because you can split control by function:
- platform subscriptions,
- application subscriptions,
- sandbox subscriptions,
- and security or audit subscriptions.

A common hierarchy looks like this:

```text
Tenant root group
├── Platform
│   ├── Connectivity subscription
│   ├── Identity subscription
│   └── Management subscription
├── LandingZones
│   ├── App-Prod subscription
│   └── App-NonProd subscription
└── Sandbox
    └── Dev subscription
```

This structure helps you:
- delegate ownership cleanly,
- apply different policies to different environments,
- and prevent app teams from changing shared infrastructure.

Use separate subscriptions when you need stronger isolation for:
- billing,
- access control,
- policy assignment,
- quota management,
- and blast-radius reduction.

**Policy and guardrails**

Azure Policy enforces rules even when users have permissions. This is important because separation of duties is not only about who can do something, but also about what they are allowed to do.

Examples of useful policies:
- require tags for ownership and cost center,
- restrict allowed regions,
- deny public IP creation in production,
- require private endpoints for sensitive services,
- enforce approved SKUs,
- and audit or deny insecure configurations.

Example policy assignment concept:

```bash
az policy assignment create \
  --name "deny-public-ip-prod" \
  --scope /subscriptions/00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000 \
  --policy "/providers/Microsoft.Authorization/policyDefinitions/<policy-definition-id>"
```

Policy is especially valuable when:
- developers need deployment freedom,
- but security or platform teams must enforce boundaries,
- and you want controls that survive role changes.

**Approval and change control**

Separation of duties often requires a workflow where one identity requests a change and another approves it. In Azure, this can be implemented with:
- PIM approval settings,
- Azure DevOps or GitHub pull request reviews,
- infrastructure as code (IaC) pipelines,
- and change management processes.

A secure deployment flow might look like this:
1. A developer submits an infrastructure change in Git.
2. A peer reviews the pull request.
3. A platform or security approver approves the release.
4. A pipeline identity deploys the change.
5. Logs and alerts record the deployment.

This pattern ensures the person who writes the change is not the only person who authorizes or executes it.

**Infrastructure as code and pipeline identities**

Infrastructure as code tools such as Bicep, ARM templates, Terraform, and Azure DevOps pipelines support separation of duties by moving deployment authority from humans to controlled service identities.

Benefits:
- humans do not need standing write access to production,
- deployments become repeatable and reviewable,
- and approvals can happen before execution.

Example Bicep deployment command:

```bash
az deployment group create \
  --resource-group app-prod-rg \
  --template-file main.bicep \
  --parameters environment=prod
```

A secure pipeline design usually uses:
- a dedicated service principal or managed identity,
- scoped permissions only to the target resource group or subscription,
- secretless authentication where possible,
- and approval gates before production deployment.

**Monitoring, logging, and auditability**

Separation of duties is incomplete without visibility. You need logs to prove that roles were separated and that privileged actions were reviewed.

Key telemetry sources:
- **Azure Activity Log** for subscription-level control plane events,
- **Microsoft Entra audit logs** for identity and access changes,
- **resource diagnostic logs** for service-specific events,
- **Microsoft Defender for Cloud** for security posture and alerts,
- **Azure Monitor** for centralized collection and alerting.

Useful practices:
- send logs to a central Log Analytics workspace,
- restrict log access to auditors and security staff,
- retain logs according to compliance requirements,
- and alert on role assignment changes, policy exemptions, and lock removals.

Example query idea in Kusto Query Language (KQL):

```kusto
AzureActivity
| where OperationNameValue has "roleAssignments/write"
| project TimeGenerated, Caller, OperationNameValue, ResourceGroup, ActivityStatusValue
| order by TimeGenerated desc
```

This helps you detect privilege changes that may violate separation of duties.

**Resource locks and protected operations**

Resource locks add another layer of control for critical resources. They do not replace RBAC, but they reduce accidental deletion or modification.

Lock types:
- **CanNotDelete** prevents deletion but allows updates.
- **ReadOnly** prevents changes and deletions.

Use locks for:
- key vaults,
- networking hubs,
- shared DNS zones,
- production resource groups,
- and critical monitoring resources.

Example:

```bash
az lock create \
  --name "prod-rg-lock" \
  --lock-type CanNotDelete \
  --resource-group app-prod-rg
```

Locks are useful when a team has legitimate access but should not be able to remove foundational resources casually.

**Practical architecture example**

Consider a production web application:
- Platform team owns the hub network, firewall, and identity integration.
- Security team owns policy, monitoring, and access reviews.
- Application team owns the app service, database, and deployment pipeline.
- Audit team has read-only access to logs and configuration.

In this design:
- the app team cannot change the firewall,
- the platform team cannot modify application code,
- the security team cannot deploy directly to production,
- and the audit team cannot alter anything.

This reduces blast radius and makes incident investigation easier.

**Common mistakes**

- Giving everyone **Owner** on the subscription.
- Using shared admin accounts instead of named identities.
- Allowing the same person to request, approve, and deploy changes.
- Granting permanent privileged access instead of eligible access.
- Forgetting to protect logs and audit data.
- Relying on process alone without technical enforcement.
- Applying policies only in nonproduction environments.

**Exam focus**

For AZ-305, expect separation of duties to appear in questions about:
- governance design,
- identity and access strategy,
- landing zone architecture,
- compliance requirements,
- and production workload isolation.

When choosing a solution, look for one that:
- minimizes standing privilege,
- separates platform and application ownership,
- uses RBAC and PIM,
- enforces policy at scale,
- and preserves auditability.

**Quick checklist**

- Define distinct roles for build, deploy, approve, and audit.
- Use RBAC with least privilege.
- Prefer eligible privileged access through PIM.
- Separate workloads by subscription or management group when needed.
- Enforce guardrails with Azure Policy.
- Protect critical resources with locks.
- Centralize logs and review privileged activity regularly.
