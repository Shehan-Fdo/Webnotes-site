## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**Overview**

In Azure landing zone design, subscriptions are the main unit for **billing**, **governance**, and **administrative isolation**. A common enterprise pattern is to separate subscriptions into two broad categories:

- **Platform subscriptions** for shared services and foundational controls.
- **Application subscriptions** for business workloads and product teams.

This split helps you apply different policies, access models, and lifecycle controls without forcing every workload into the same administrative boundary. It also supports the Azure landing zone guidance of using subscriptions as a scalable management boundary rather than treating a single subscription as the entire environment.

A subscription is not just a billing container. It is also where you scope Azure RBAC (Role-Based Access Control), policy assignments, resource quotas, and operational ownership. That makes the platform-versus-application decision important for architecture, not just organization.

**Core distinction**

A **platform subscription** hosts shared capabilities used by many workloads. An **application subscription** hosts one workload, one product, or one environment slice of a workload, depending on scale and governance needs.

| Aspect | Platform subscription | Application subscription |
|---|---|---|
| Primary purpose | Shared foundation services | Business workload hosting |
| Typical owners | Central IT / cloud platform team | App team / product team |
| Change cadence | Controlled, infrequent | Faster, workload-driven |
| Access model | Restricted, highly governed | Delegated to workload owners |
| Examples | Connectivity, identity, management | Web app, API, data tier, dev/test/prod |

Use this split when you need strong separation of duties, different policy baselines, or independent lifecycle management. Keep workloads in the same subscription only when the operational and governance requirements are simple enough that a separate boundary adds unnecessary overhead.

**When to use separate subscriptions instead of resource groups**

Resource groups are useful for organizing resources inside a subscription, but they do not provide the same level of isolation as subscriptions. Use separate subscriptions when you need one or more of the following:

- **Independent billing and chargeback** for a team, product, or environment.
- **Different policy enforcement** that should not be bypassed by resource group-level organization.
- **Separate RBAC boundaries** so platform admins and app teams do not share the same administrative plane.
- **Quota and limit isolation** for large-scale workloads.
- **Lifecycle independence** for moving, retiring, or restructuring workloads.
- **Blast-radius reduction** when a team should not be able to affect unrelated services.

Use resource groups when the resources belong to the same operational owner and the same governance model, and you mainly need logical grouping rather than isolation.

> A subscription boundary is stronger than a resource group boundary. If you need to separate ownership, policy, or billing, a subscription is usually the better design choice.

**Platform subscription types in landing zones**

Azure landing zone designs commonly use multiple platform subscriptions. The exact names vary by organization, but the purpose is usually consistent.

- **Connectivity subscription**
  - Hosts hub networking components such as Azure Virtual Network hubs, Azure Firewall, VPN gateways, ExpressRoute gateways, and route tables.
  - Centralizes network ingress, egress, and transit.
  - Often contains shared DNS forwarding or resolver components.

- **Identity subscription**
  - Hosts identity-related infrastructure that supports the landing zone, such as domain controllers in IaaS scenarios or identity integration services.
  - In many modern designs, identity services are partly SaaS or managed, so this subscription may be smaller or even unnecessary depending on the architecture.

- **Management subscription**
  - Hosts monitoring, logging, security tooling, automation, and governance services.
  - Common services include Log Analytics workspaces, Azure Monitor components, Microsoft Sentinel, Automation Accounts, update management, and backup vaults.
  - Centralizes operational visibility and response.

These platform subscriptions are usually owned by a central team and protected with stricter policy and RBAC than application subscriptions.

**Application subscription patterns**

Application subscriptions are where business workloads live. You can structure them in several ways:

- **One subscription per workload**
  - Good for clear ownership and strong isolation.
  - Useful when a workload has distinct compliance, cost, or lifecycle requirements.

- **One subscription per environment**
  - Common for separating `dev`, `test`, and `prod`.
  - Helps prevent accidental changes from lower environments affecting production.

- **One subscription per business unit or product**
  - Useful when a product team owns multiple related services.
  - Works well when the team manages its own release cadence and support model.

- **Multiple subscriptions per large workload**
  - Useful when a workload is large enough to need separate scaling, quotas, or operational boundaries.
  - For example, split front-end, API, and data platform subscriptions if the architecture and governance justify it.

A single application subscription can host multiple resource groups, but do not use resource groups as a substitute for a subscription when the workload needs real isolation.

**Decision factors**

Use these factors to decide whether to split subscriptions:

- **Scale**
  - Large enterprises often need multiple subscriptions because one subscription can become too crowded for governance, quota, and operational clarity.
  - Azure has service-specific limits and subscription-level quotas, so a single subscription can become a bottleneck.

- **Ownership**
  - If different teams manage different parts of the environment, separate subscriptions reduce coordination overhead.
  - Platform teams usually need tighter control than application teams.

- **Compliance**
  - Regulated workloads may require dedicated subscriptions to simplify audit scope and policy enforcement.

- **Cost management**
  - Separate subscriptions improve chargeback and showback.
  - They also make it easier to track platform costs versus application consumption.

- **Operational independence**
  - If one workload should be deployable, patchable, or recoverable without affecting others, separate subscriptions help.

- **Network design**
  - Shared services such as hub networking are easier to manage when centralized in platform subscriptions.
  - Application subscriptions then consume those services through peering, private endpoints, and DNS integration.

**How shared services are consumed across subscriptions**

A platform/application split works best when shared services are designed for cross-subscription consumption.

- **VNet peering**
  - Connects application spokes to a central connectivity hub.
  - Use it for low-latency private connectivity within a region.
  - Remember that peering is not transitive by default; the hub must be designed to route traffic appropriately.

- **Private endpoints**
  - Let application workloads access PaaS services privately without exposing public endpoints.
  - Common for Storage, Key Vault, SQL, and other managed services.
  - Private DNS zones are often centralized so name resolution works consistently across subscriptions.

- **DNS resolution**
  - Central DNS design is critical when private endpoints are used.
  - Application subscriptions often link to shared private DNS zones or use DNS forwarding to the platform DNS layer.

- **Shared security and monitoring**
  - Application subscriptions send logs and metrics to central workspaces in the management subscription.
  - Security teams can monitor multiple subscriptions from one place while still preserving workload ownership.

A practical pattern is to keep the shared network and observability services in platform subscriptions, then connect application subscriptions to them using private networking and centralized DNS.

**Governance and RBAC**

Subscription placement should align with governance. Azure RBAC inheritance flows from management group to subscription to resource group to resource. That means you can apply broad controls at the management group level and still delegate workload-specific access lower down.

Common governance patterns include:

- **Management group policies**
  - Apply baseline controls to all platform and application subscriptions.
  - Examples include allowed locations, required tags, diagnostic settings, and SKU restrictions.

- **Platform subscription restrictions**
  - Limit who can create or modify shared services.
  - Keep platform administration separate from application administration.

- **Application subscription delegation**
  - Give app teams Contributor or workload-specific custom roles within their subscription or resource groups.
  - Avoid granting broad rights in platform subscriptions unless the team truly owns those services.

Be careful with role names. Azure RBAC roles such as `Reader`, `Contributor`, `Owner`, and `Network Contributor` are different from Microsoft Entra ID or security product roles. Do not assume a security governance role is an Azure RBAC role.

**Example management group hierarchy**

A common hierarchy looks like this:

```text
Tenant Root Group
├── Platform
│   ├── Connectivity
│   ├── Identity
│   └── Management
└── LandingZones
    ├── Corp
    │   ├── App-Prod
    │   └── App-NonProd
    └── Online
        ├── App-Prod
        └── App-NonProd
```

This structure lets you:

- Apply common policy at the root or landing zone level.
- Apply stricter controls to platform subscriptions.
- Delegate application subscriptions to product teams.

**Practical Azure CLI examples**

Use the Azure CLI to place subscriptions under management groups and assign governance. Verify command syntax in your environment because CLI behavior can vary by version and tenant configuration.

List management groups:

```bash
az account management-group list -o table
```

Show a specific subscription:

```bash
az account show --subscription <subscriptionId>
```

Move a subscription to a management group:

```bash
az account management-group subscription add \
  --name <managementGroupId> \
  --subscription <subscriptionId>
```

Assign a policy at the management group scope:

```bash
az policy assignment create \
  --name require-location \
  --display-name "Require allowed locations" \
  --scope /providers/Microsoft.Management/managementGroups/<managementGroupId> \
  --policy-set-definition <policySetDefinitionId>
```

Assign an RBAC role at subscription scope:

```bash
az role assignment create \
  --assignee <user-or-group-object-id> \
  --role "Contributor" \
  --scope /subscriptions/<subscriptionId>
```

Create a resource group in an application subscription:

```bash
az group create \
  --name rg-app-prod-web \
  --location eastus \
  --subscription <subscriptionId>
```

> Subscription vending and billing-linked subscription creation are not the same as a normal ARM deployment. A management group deployment does not create a subscription by itself.

**Subscription vending and ownership nuances**

In enterprise environments, subscriptions are often created through a **subscription vending** process. This is usually handled by platform automation, IT service management, or billing APIs rather than by application teams directly.

Important ownership considerations:

- **Enterprise Agreement (EA)** and **Microsoft Customer Agreement (MCA)** billing models affect who can create subscriptions and how they are billed.
- Subscription ownership may be tied to a billing account, billing profile, or enrollment account depending on the agreement type.
- The subscription owner in Azure RBAC is not the same as the billing owner.
- Platform teams often create subscriptions on behalf of application teams, then delegate access after placement in the correct management group.

This matters because governance should start at subscription creation time. If a subscription is created outside the landing zone process, it may miss policy, logging, or network baselines.

**Scaling considerations and subscription limits**

Azure subscriptions have practical scaling limits even when the exact limit is service-specific. As environments grow, you may need more subscriptions to avoid operational friction.

Use multiple subscriptions when:

- You need to separate thousands of resources across teams or environments.
- A single subscription becomes difficult to govern with tags, policies, and access reviews.
- Quotas for a service or region become a constraint.
- Deployment pipelines need independent blast-radius boundaries.
- Cost allocation becomes too coarse in one subscription.

A good rule is to design for manageability first, not just for technical capacity. If one subscription becomes a shared dumping ground, governance usually degrades before the platform technically fails.

**Platform vs. application subscription comparison**

| Criterion | Platform subscription | Application subscription |
|---|---|---|
| Ownership | Central platform team | Workload or product team |
| Services | Shared infrastructure and controls | Business application components |
| Policy strictness | Highest | High, but workload-specific |
| Network role | Hub, transit, shared DNS | Spoke, workload access |
| Change frequency | Low | Medium to high |
| Cost model | Shared operational cost | Chargeback/showback to business units |
| Typical risk | Shared-service outage affects many workloads | Workload-specific outage affects one product |

This comparison is useful in design reviews because it forces you to ask whether a service is truly shared or merely convenient to centralize.

**End-to-end example: platform and application split**

Consider a company with one central platform team and one product team.

1. The platform team creates three subscriptions:
   - `sub-connectivity`
   - `sub-management`
   - `sub-identity`

2. The product team receives two application subscriptions:
   - `sub-app-prod`
   - `sub-app-nonprod`

3. The platform team places all subscriptions under the correct management groups:
   - `Platform`
   - `LandingZones/Corp`

4. The platform team assigns baseline policies at the management group level:
   - Allowed regions
   - Required tags
   - Diagnostic settings
   - Deny public IPs on selected workloads

5. The connectivity subscription hosts:
   - Hub virtual network
   - Azure Firewall
   - VPN or ExpressRoute gateway
   - DNS forwarding or resolver components

6. The management subscription hosts:
   - Log Analytics workspace
   - Microsoft Sentinel
   - Backup vaults
   - Automation and monitoring

7. The application subscriptions host:
   - App Service, AKS, or VM-based workloads
   - Private endpoints to shared PaaS services
   - Resource groups split by tier or component

8. The product team gets `Contributor` access only in the application subscriptions and `Reader` access to shared monitoring where appropriate.

This design keeps the platform stable while allowing the app team to deploy independently.

**Migration and refactoring scenarios**

You may need to move from a monolithic subscription to a platform/application split. Common triggers include:

- A single subscription has become too large to manage.
- Platform services and application workloads are mixed together.
- Different teams need different access models.
- Compliance requires stronger separation.

A practical migration approach is:

1. Inventory resources and classify them as platform or application.
2. Create the target management group structure.
3. Create new subscriptions for platform and application workloads.
4. Establish shared networking and DNS first.
5. Move or redeploy platform services into the platform subscriptions.
6. Recreate or migrate application workloads into application subscriptions.
7. Reapply policy, RBAC, and monitoring baselines.
8. Validate connectivity, identity, logging, and backup before decommissioning the old layout.

Not every resource can be moved cleanly between subscriptions. Some services require redeployment, DNS changes, or downtime planning. Treat migration as an architecture project, not just a resource move.

**Common exam pitfalls**

- Confusing **resource groups** with subscriptions as the main governance boundary.
- Putting shared hub networking inside an application subscription.
- Giving application teams broad access to platform subscriptions.
- Assuming one subscription is always enough for an enterprise landing zone.
- Treating billing ownership and Azure RBAC ownership as the same thing.
- Designing shared services without private DNS and peering considerations.
- Using a management group deployment as a way to create subscriptions.

**Design guidance for AZ-305**

For exam scenarios, choose platform subscriptions when the question emphasizes:

- Shared services
- Centralized governance
- Network transit
- Security and monitoring
- Separation of duties

Choose application subscriptions when the question emphasizes:

- Workload ownership
- Independent deployment
- Environment isolation
- Chargeback
- Compliance boundaries

If the scenario asks whether to use resource groups or subscriptions, prefer subscriptions when the requirement is about isolation, governance, or ownership. Prefer resource groups when the requirement is only about organization within the same operational boundary.

**Quick decision checklist**

- Use a **platform subscription** if the service is shared by many workloads.
- Use an **application subscription** if the service belongs to one workload or team.
- Use separate subscriptions if you need stronger isolation than resource groups provide.
- Use management groups to apply policy and RBAC consistently across both.
- Use private networking, DNS, and centralized monitoring to connect the split cleanly.
