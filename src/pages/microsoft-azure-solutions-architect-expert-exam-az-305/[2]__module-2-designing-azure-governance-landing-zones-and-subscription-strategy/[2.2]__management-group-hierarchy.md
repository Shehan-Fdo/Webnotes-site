## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**Management group hierarchy overview**

Azure management groups provide a governance layer above subscriptions. You use them to apply Azure Policy, role-based access control (RBAC), and organizational structure consistently across many subscriptions. In AZ-305 design questions, management groups are the tool you choose when a control must span multiple subscriptions, but not necessarily the entire Microsoft Entra tenant.

A management group hierarchy sits inside a single Microsoft Entra tenant. The tenant is the identity and administration boundary; management groups are the governance boundary for Azure resources. That distinction matters: tenant-level administration controls who can manage the hierarchy, while management group scope controls what policies and permissions flow to subscriptions beneath it.

**Hierarchy structure and limits**

Management groups form a tree, not a flat list. Each management group can have:
- Child management groups
- Subscriptions

A management group cannot contain:
- Resource groups
- Individual resources

That rule is important for exam scenarios. If a requirement targets a resource group or a single resource, you use subscription scope, resource group scope, or resource scope instead of a management group.

Key structural constraints to remember:
- A hierarchy can have up to **six levels** of management groups, including the root management group.
- A subscription can have only **one parent** management group at a time.
- A management group can have many child management groups and many subscriptions.
- All subscriptions in the tenant ultimately roll up under the root management group.

> The root management group is the top of the hierarchy and is created automatically for each new tenant. It is the default parent for subscriptions until you move them elsewhere.

**Root management group behavior**

The root management group is enabled by default in new tenants. Azure creates it so you have a single top-level governance anchor for the tenant. In practice:
- Every subscription belongs somewhere under the root.
- If you do not place a subscription into a child management group, it remains directly under the root.
- Policies or RBAC assignments at the root can affect the entire tenant’s subscription tree, subject to inheritance and exclusions.

This is often the right place for broad guardrails, such as:
- Denying unsupported regions
- Enforcing tagging standards
- Applying baseline security controls
- Delegating limited governance to platform teams

Use the root carefully. A broad assignment at the root can affect every subscription, so it is powerful but risky.

**Tenant scope versus management group scope**

Microsoft Entra tenant administration and Azure management group governance are related but not the same.

- **Tenant scope**
  - Controls identity, directory, and tenant-wide administration.
  - Includes permissions such as creating management groups and managing tenant-level settings.
  - Is where you manage the directory itself, not just Azure resource governance.

- **Management group scope**
  - Controls governance inheritance for subscriptions and nested management groups.
  - Is where you assign policies and RBAC for Azure resource management at scale.
  - Does not manage identities directly; it consumes identities from the tenant.

For AZ-305, this distinction helps you choose the right control plane:
- Use tenant-level permissions when you need to create or reorganize the hierarchy.
- Use management group scope when you need governance across multiple subscriptions.

**Creation and move prerequisites**

Creating and organizing management groups requires tenant-level permission. In general, you need a role that can manage management groups at the tenant root, such as a tenant-level management group administrator capability or equivalent privileged access in the directory.

To move a subscription into a management group, you typically need:
- Permission to move the subscription
- Permission to write to the target management group
- Appropriate access on the subscription itself, often Owner or a role with equivalent rights
- Tenant-level visibility into the hierarchy if the move crosses governance boundaries

Operationally, subscription moves are used when you are:
- Reorganizing an enterprise landing zone
- Separating platform and application subscriptions
- Applying different policy baselines to different business units

A move changes inherited governance immediately after the hierarchy updates. That can affect policy compliance, RBAC inheritance, and allowed locations.

**Governance inheritance**

Management groups are valuable because they let you define controls once and inherit them downward.

Typical inheritance behavior:
- Policies assigned at a parent management group apply to child management groups and subscriptions below it.
- RBAC assignments at a management group can grant access across all descendant subscriptions.
- Child scopes can add more restrictive controls, but they cannot bypass inherited deny effects unless the design explicitly allows exceptions.

Common inheritance outcomes:
- A policy at the root denies creation of resources in unsupported regions.
- A policy at a business-unit management group requires tags for that unit’s subscriptions.
- A role assignment at a platform management group lets a central team manage shared services subscriptions without granting access to every subscription in the tenant.

**Management groups versus subscriptions**

Use management groups when the boundary is governance across multiple subscriptions. Use subscriptions when the boundary is billing, quota, or a smaller administrative unit.

| Aspect | Management group | Subscription |
|---|---|---|
| Primary purpose | Governance and inheritance | Resource container and billing/quota boundary |
| Can contain resources | No | Yes |
| Can contain resource groups | No | Yes |
| Can contain subscriptions | Yes | No |
| Policy inheritance | Yes | Yes, from parent management group |
| RBAC inheritance | Yes | Yes, from parent management group |
| Billing boundary | No | Yes |
| Quota boundary | No | Yes |
| Typical use | Enterprise-wide guardrails | Workload isolation and chargeback |

Exam-style example:
- If a company wants one policy to deny public IPs across all workloads, place the policy at a management group.
- If a team needs separate billing and quota for a production workload, use a separate subscription.
- If a requirement is to isolate a single application’s resources from others, a subscription boundary is usually more appropriate than a management group boundary.

**Enterprise landing zone example**

A common AZ-305 design is an enterprise landing zone hierarchy like this:

```text
Tenant root management group
├─ Platform
│  ├─ Identity
│  ├─ Connectivity
│  └─ Management
├─ LandingZones
│  ├─ Corp
│  │  ├─ Corp-App1-Prod subscription
│  │  └─ Corp-App1-Dev subscription
│  └─ Online
│     ├─ Online-App2-Prod subscription
│     └─ Online-App2-Dev subscription
└─ Sandbox
   └─ Sandbox-Dev subscription
```

How this design works:
- The **root** applies global guardrails, such as allowed regions and security baselines.
- The **Platform** branch hosts shared services subscriptions and central IT controls.
- The **LandingZones** branch hosts application subscriptions grouped by business domain.
- The **Sandbox** branch allows experimentation with lighter controls.

Policy and RBAC inheritance outcomes:
- Root-level policy denies unsupported regions for every subscription.
- Platform subscriptions inherit stricter network and logging requirements.
- Application subscriptions inherit baseline security but can have workload-specific policies.
- Sandbox subscriptions can be assigned more permissive policies if the organization allows it.

This pattern is common because it separates platform governance from workload governance while keeping a single tenant-wide control plane.

**Naming and organization best practices**

Good hierarchy design makes governance understandable and maintainable.

Recommended practices:
- Use names that reflect business function, not technical trivia.
- Keep the hierarchy shallow unless you have a clear governance reason to add levels.
- Align management groups to organizational boundaries such as platform, business unit, environment, or geography.
- Use consistent naming conventions for subscriptions and management groups.
- Document ownership for each branch of the hierarchy.

Examples of useful naming patterns:
- `Platform`
- `LandingZones`
- `Sandbox`
- `Corp`
- `Online`
- `Prod`
- `NonProd`

Anti-patterns:
- Creating a management group per application when subscriptions would be enough
- Mirroring every team structure exactly, which creates a brittle hierarchy
- Using cryptic abbreviations that make policy placement hard to understand
- Putting everything directly under the root with no intermediate structure
- Creating too many levels without a governance need

A good rule is to model the hierarchy around how you govern, not just how you report.

**Practical CLI examples**

You can inspect and manage the hierarchy with Azure CLI.

List management groups:
```bash
az account management-group list --output table
```

Show a specific management group:
```bash
az account management-group show --name Contoso-Platform
```

Create a management group:
```bash
az account management-group create \
  --name Contoso-Platform \
  --display-name "Platform"
```

Move a subscription under a management group:
```bash
az account management-group subscription add \
  --name Contoso-Platform \
  --subscription 00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000
```

List subscriptions under a management group:
```bash
az account management-group subscription list --name Contoso-Platform
```

A simple policy assignment at a management group might look like this:

```bash
az policy assignment create \
  --name deny-nonapproved-regions \
  --display-name "Deny non-approved regions" \
  --scope /providers/Microsoft.Management/managementGroups/Contoso-Platform \
  --policy /providers/Microsoft.Authorization/policyDefinitions/<policyDefinitionId>
```

These commands are useful for understanding how hierarchy changes affect inherited governance.

**Design decisions and exam cues**

When you see an AZ-305 scenario, ask:
- Does the requirement apply to one workload, one subscription, or many subscriptions?
- Is the control about governance or about billing/quota?
- Should the control be inherited automatically?
- Does the organization need a central platform team with delegated control?

Choose a management group when:
- You need consistent policy across multiple subscriptions
- You need centralized RBAC for a platform team
- You want to separate platform, sandbox, and workload governance
- You need tenant-wide guardrails without touching each subscription individually

Choose a subscription when:
- You need a billing boundary
- You need quota isolation
- You want to separate production from nonproduction at the resource container level
- You need a clear administrative boundary for a workload

**Common pitfalls**

- Assuming management groups can hold resources directly
- Treating management groups as a billing construct
- Forgetting that all subscriptions ultimately roll up to the root management group
- Overusing deep hierarchies when a simpler structure is easier to manage
- Assigning broad permissions at the root without considering inheritance impact
- Moving subscriptions without checking policy effects and access dependencies

**Quick memory aid**

- **Tenant** = identity and directory boundary
- **Management group** = governance and inheritance boundary
- **Subscription** = billing, quota, and resource container boundary
- **Resource group** = deployment and lifecycle boundary

**Summary**

Management groups are the Azure governance backbone for multi-subscription environments. They let you organize subscriptions into a hierarchy, apply policy and RBAC through inheritance, and enforce tenant-wide guardrails from the root management group. For AZ-305, focus on the hierarchy limits, the root management group’s default behavior, the tenant-versus-management-group distinction, and the practical choice between management groups and subscriptions for enterprise design.
