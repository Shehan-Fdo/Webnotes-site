## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**Cost optimization as an architecture decision**

Cost optimization is not a single feature; it is a design discipline. In Azure, you reduce spend by choosing the right service model, right sizing resources, matching billing scope to workload behavior, and removing waste across compute, storage, networking, and operations.

For AZ-305, you should think in terms of tradeoffs:

- **Performance vs cost**: higher throughput, lower latency, and stronger resiliency usually cost more.
- **Flexibility vs commitment**: pay-as-you-go is flexible; reservations and savings plans lower cost but reduce agility.
- **Managed service vs control**: PaaS often reduces operational overhead, but some managed services have hidden platform costs.
- **Always-on vs on-demand**: nonproduction and bursty workloads often benefit from automation and shutdown schedules.

A good architecture answer explains *why* a service is cheaper in a given scenario, not just *what* the service is.

**Pre-deployment cost estimation**

Before you design, estimate cost with Azure’s planning tools. These are especially useful when comparing architectures that look similar on paper but differ in billing model.

- **Azure Pricing Calculator**: estimates monthly cost for Azure services based on region, SKU, usage, and configuration.
- **Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) Calculator**: compares on-premises costs with Azure migration scenarios, including hardware, software, labor, and facilities.
- **Azure Migrate**: helps assess servers, databases, and web apps, and can feed sizing and cost estimates for migration planning.
- **Azure Cost Management**: tracks actual spend after deployment and helps validate whether the design matches the estimate.

Use the pricing calculator when you need service-by-service estimates. Use the TCO calculator when you need to justify migration economics to stakeholders.

Example planning workflow:

1. Estimate baseline infrastructure in the **Azure Pricing Calculator**.
2. Compare against current on-premises spend in the **TCO Calculator**.
3. Validate assumptions with **Azure Migrate** assessments.
4. After deployment, compare forecast vs actual in **Cost Management**.

**Core cost levers in Azure**

Azure cost optimization usually comes from a combination of design choices rather than one “cheap” service.

- **Right sizing**: choose the smallest SKU that meets performance and availability requirements.
- **Elasticity**: scale out and scale in based on demand instead of provisioning for peak all the time.
- **Commitment discounts**: use reservations or savings plans when usage is stable enough.
- **License optimization**: reuse eligible licenses where allowed.
- **Lifecycle management**: move data to cheaper tiers or delete it when no longer needed.
- **Operational automation**: stop idle resources, schedule nonproduction environments, and automate cleanup.

A common exam pattern is to identify the cheapest solution that still satisfies the requirement for availability, performance, and governance.

**Compute cost optimization**

Compute is often the largest cost center, so architecture decisions here matter most.

- **Azure Virtual Machines**:
  - Best when you need OS-level control, custom software, or lift-and-shift compatibility.
  - Cost depends on VM size, region, disk type, uptime, and licensing.
  - Use smaller sizes, burstable SKUs for light workloads, and autoscale where possible.
- **Azure App Service**:
  - Often cheaper than VMs for web apps because Azure manages the platform.
  - Good for predictable web/API workloads that do not need full OS control.
  - Scale up for performance, scale out for concurrency.
- **Azure Functions**:
  - Best for event-driven or intermittent workloads.
  - Consumption-based billing can be very cost-effective for spiky traffic.
  - For steady high throughput, a dedicated plan may be more economical or predictable.
- **Azure Container Apps / AKS**:
  - Containers can improve density and utilization.
  - AKS adds cluster management overhead; it is not automatically cheaper than PaaS.
  - Use when container portability or orchestration is a requirement, not just for cost alone.

A useful rule: if the workload is idle much of the time, prefer serverless or scheduled shutdown. If the workload is steady and predictable, commitment-based pricing may be better.

**Reservations, savings plans, and commitment tradeoffs**

Azure offers discount models that reduce cost in exchange for commitment.

- **Reserved Instances (RIs)**:
  - Best for stable, predictable usage of specific resources.
  - Common for VMs, SQL Database, Cosmos DB, and other eligible services.
  - Commitment is typically tied to region, instance family, and term.
- **Savings Plan for Compute**:
  - Applies to eligible compute usage across supported services.
  - More flexible than VM-specific reservations because the discount follows compute spend rather than a single instance shape.
  - Useful when you expect steady compute spend but may change VM sizes or move between eligible compute services.
- **Spot VMs**:
  - Lowest cost for interruptible workloads.
  - Suitable for batch jobs, rendering, test, or fault-tolerant processing.
  - Not appropriate for critical always-on workloads.

Tradeoff guidance:

- Choose **reservations** when the workload is stable and you know the exact scope.
- Choose **savings plans** when you want discount flexibility across eligible compute usage.
- Avoid both when the workload is short-lived, uncertain, or likely to change region/family frequently.
- Do not commit capacity for workloads that may be retired, replatformed, or heavily seasonal.

> Be careful: commitment discounts reduce unit cost, but they can increase waste if you overcommit or if the workload changes before the term ends.

**Licensing optimization**

Licensing can materially change the economics of Azure architecture.

- **Azure Hybrid Benefit**:
  - Lets you apply eligible on-premises Windows Server or SQL Server licenses to Azure.
  - Can significantly reduce VM or database costs.
  - Often a key decision point when comparing lift-and-shift vs replatforming.
- **Existing software assurance or subscription licenses**:
  - May be reusable depending on product terms.
  - Important for Windows, SQL Server, and some third-party software.
- **License mobility**:
  - Some software licenses can move to Azure if the vendor terms allow it.

Use licensing optimization when the organization already owns eligible licenses and wants to reduce cloud spend without changing the application architecture.

**Storage cost optimization**

Storage costs are not just about capacity. Transaction volume, redundancy, access tier, and data movement can all affect the bill.

- **Access tiers**:
  - **Hot**: for frequently accessed data.
  - **Cool**: for infrequently accessed data with lower storage cost and higher access cost.
  - **Archive**: for long-term retention with very low storage cost and high retrieval latency/cost.
- **Redundancy choices**:
  - Locally redundant storage is usually cheaper than geo-redundant options.
  - Higher redundancy improves resilience but increases cost.
- **Transaction costs**:
  - Frequent reads, writes, and list operations can become expensive at scale.
  - This matters for logs, backups, and small-object workloads.
- **Data lifecycle**:
  - Move data to cheaper tiers as it ages.
  - Delete expired data automatically when retention ends.

Example lifecycle policy pattern:

```json
{
  "rules": [
    {
      "enabled": true,
      "name": "move-and-expire-logs",
      "type": "Lifecycle",
      "definition": {
        "filters": {
          "blobTypes": ["blockBlob"],
          "prefixMatch": ["logs/"]
        },
        "actions": {
          "baseBlob": {
            "tierToCool": { "daysAfterModificationGreaterThan": 30 },
            "tierToArchive": { "daysAfterModificationGreaterThan": 90 },
            "delete": { "daysAfterModificationGreaterThan": 365 }
          }
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

Use lifecycle management for logs, backups, and content archives where access patterns are predictable. For frequently restored data, archive may be too expensive because retrieval costs and latency can outweigh storage savings.

**Storage transaction and backup tradeoffs**

A low storage price does not always mean low total cost.

- **Backups**:
  - Frequent full backups increase storage and transaction costs.
  - Incremental or differential strategies can reduce cost.
  - Retention policies should match compliance needs, not default to “keep forever.”
- **Logging**:
  - High-volume diagnostic logs can generate significant ingestion and retention costs.
  - Send only required categories and set retention intentionally.
- **Data lake and analytics**:
  - Large numbers of small files increase transaction overhead.
  - Compacting files and using efficient formats can reduce cost.

For exam scenarios, choose the design that balances retention, retrieval frequency, and operational simplicity.

**Networking cost optimization**

Networking services can create hidden recurring costs, especially in hub-and-spoke or internet-facing designs.

- **Azure NAT Gateway**:
  - Provides outbound internet connectivity for private subnets.
  - Charges are based on gateway hours and data processed.
  - Useful when you need predictable outbound SNAT behavior, but it is not free.
- **Azure Application Gateway**:
  - Layer 7 load balancer with TLS termination, path-based routing, and optional WAF.
  - Costs more than simple load balancing because it is a managed application delivery service.
  - Good for regional web apps that need advanced routing and private backend integration.
- **Azure Firewall**:
  - Centralized network security and egress control.
  - Can be expensive, especially in always-on hub designs.
  - Use when you need policy enforcement, logging, and centralized inspection.
- **DDoS Protection**:
  - Adds protection for public IP resources and can be justified for internet-facing critical workloads.
  - Not every workload needs the premium tier; assess risk and business impact.
- **Private Link / Private Endpoints**:
  - Improve security by keeping traffic private, but they add endpoint and DNS complexity.
  - Can also affect cost depending on the number of endpoints and data processed.

Design guidance:

- Use the simplest network path that meets security and routing requirements.
- Avoid deploying expensive edge or firewall services when a lighter service is sufficient.
- Consolidate shared network services in a hub only when the operational and security benefits justify the cost.

**Choosing between Front Door, Application Gateway, and CDN**

These services solve different problems, and cost should follow the requirement.

| Service | Best for | Cost perspective | Common exam clue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Azure Front Door | Global HTTP(S) entry point, edge routing, TLS termination, WAF, failover | Higher than simple regional routing, but valuable for global edge delivery | “Global users,” “multi-region failover,” “edge-based routing” |
| Application Gateway | Regional Layer 7 routing to private backends, WAF, TLS offload | Regional managed service with steady cost | “Single region,” “path-based routing,” “private VNet backends” |
| Azure CDN | Caching static content close to users | Often best for static assets and cache-heavy content | “Images, scripts, videos, static files” |

Use Front Door when you need global HTTP(S) routing and edge-based failover. Use Application Gateway when the workload is regional and needs VNet integration. Use CDN when the main goal is caching static content and reducing origin traffic.

**Database cost optimization**

Database choices often drive both compute and storage spend.

- **Provisioned databases**:
  - Better for steady, predictable workloads.
  - Can be cheaper at sustained high utilization.
- **Serverless databases**:
  - Better for intermittent or unpredictable workloads.
  - Can auto-pause and scale with demand in supported services.
  - May cost more than provisioned options for always-on heavy usage.
- **Hyperscale or distributed options**:
  - Useful for scale, but not always the cheapest choice.
  - Choose for capacity and performance requirements, not cost alone.
- **Read replicas and geo-replication**:
  - Improve availability and read scale, but increase cost.
  - Use only when the business requirement justifies it.

A common architecture decision is whether to choose a serverless database for dev/test or low-traffic production, versus a provisioned tier for predictable enterprise workloads.

**Operational automation and shutdown scheduling**

Automation is one of the easiest ways to reduce waste in nonproduction environments.

- Stop VMs outside business hours.
- Deallocate test environments when not in use.
- Use schedules for labs, dev, and QA environments.
- Automate cleanup of temporary resources such as disks, snapshots, public IPs, and unused NICs.
- Use policy and tagging to identify resources that should be shut down or deleted.

Example Azure CLI pattern for stopping a VM:

```bash
az vm deallocate \
  --resource-group rg-dev \
  --name vm-dev-01
```

Example scheduling approach:

```bash
# Pseudocode pattern for automation
# 1. Tag nonproduction resources with environment=dev or environment=test
# 2. Run a scheduled job at 19:00 to deallocate them
# 3. Run a scheduled job at 07:00 to start them
```

This pattern is especially effective for labs, proof-of-concept environments, and developer subscriptions.

**Governance that supports cost control**

Governance does not directly lower unit price, but it prevents waste.

- **Tags**:
  - Use tags such as `costCenter`, `environment`, and `owner`.
  - Enable chargeback and accountability.
- **Azure Policy**:
  - Enforce allowed SKUs, regions, and required tags.
  - Prevent expensive or noncompliant deployments.
- **Budgets and alerts**:
  - Set budget thresholds to notify teams before overspend.
  - Use alerts to detect anomalies early.
- **Resource locks and approvals**:
  - Prevent accidental deletion of critical resources.
  - Use carefully so locks do not block cleanup of temporary environments.

Example policy idea:

```json
{
  "if": {
    "field": "tags['environment']",
    "exists": "false"
  },
  "then": {
    "effect": "deny"
  }
}
```

Use policy to enforce tagging and region restrictions, especially in large organizations where uncontrolled deployments create hidden cost.

**Monitoring and continuous optimization**

Cost optimization is ongoing. You should review actual usage and adjust the architecture over time.

- Compare forecasted cost with actual spend.
- Identify idle resources and underutilized SKUs.
- Review storage growth and transaction patterns.
- Check whether reservations are fully utilized.
- Reassess whether a service should be replatformed, downsized, or retired.

Useful Azure Cost Management actions include:

- Cost analysis by subscription, resource group, tag, or service.
- Budget alerts for threshold-based notifications.
- Recommendations for rightsizing and commitment purchases where available.
- Exporting cost data for deeper analysis.

**Exam-oriented decision patterns**

When you see a cost-related AZ-305 question, look for the hidden requirement behind the price.

- If the workload is **steady and predictable**, consider reservations or savings plans.
- If the workload is **bursty or intermittent**, consider serverless or autoscale.
- If the organization already owns **Windows or SQL licenses**, consider Azure Hybrid Benefit.
- If the workload is **nonproduction**, consider shutdown schedules and automation.
- If the data is **rarely accessed**, use cool or archive tiers with lifecycle rules.
- If the app is **global web traffic**, consider Front Door.
- If the app is **regional and VNet-integrated**, consider Application Gateway.
- If the content is **static**, consider CDN.
- If the database usage is **unpredictable**, consider serverless where supported.
- If the network security stack is **overbuilt**, simplify before adding expensive managed services.

**Practical architecture examples**

A few common scenarios help anchor the tradeoffs.

- **Dev/test environment**:
  - Use smaller VMs or App Service dev tiers.
  - Automate shutdown after hours.
  - Avoid reservations unless usage is stable and long-lived.
- **Enterprise web app with global users**:
  - Use Front Door for global entry and failover.
  - Use regional App Service or AKS behind it.
  - Use CDN for static assets if caching is important.
- **Line-of-business app with stable VM usage**:
  - Use Azure Hybrid Benefit if eligible.
  - Buy reservations for the VM family and region.
  - Right-size disks and avoid overprovisioning.
- **Archive and compliance data**:
  - Store in cool or archive tiers.
  - Apply lifecycle policies.
  - Validate retrieval cost and latency before choosing archive.
- **Batch processing**:
  - Use Spot VMs or serverless jobs if interruption is acceptable.
  - Scale only during processing windows.

**Common mistakes to avoid**

- Choosing the cheapest SKU without checking performance or availability requirements.
- Buying reservations for workloads that are likely to change soon.
- Ignoring licensing benefits that already exist in the organization.
- Using premium networking services when a simpler design meets the need.
- Forgetting transaction, retrieval, and egress costs in storage decisions.
- Keeping nonproduction resources running 24/7.
- Treating cost optimization as a one-time task instead of an operational process.

**Quick decision checklist**

Before you finalize an architecture, ask:

1. Is the workload steady enough for a commitment discount?
2. Can existing licenses reduce the Azure bill?
3. Can the workload scale down, pause, or shut off when idle?
4. Is the chosen storage tier aligned with access frequency?
5. Are networking and security services justified by the requirement?
6. Have you estimated cost with the pricing and TCO tools?
7. Will governance prevent future waste?

If you can answer these clearly, your design is usually cost-aware and exam-ready.
