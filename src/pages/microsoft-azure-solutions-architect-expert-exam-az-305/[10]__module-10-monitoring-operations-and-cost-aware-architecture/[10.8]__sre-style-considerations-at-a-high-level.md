## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**SRE-style operating model**

Site Reliability Engineering (SRE) applies software engineering practices to operations. In Azure architecture work, the goal is not just to deploy services, but to define how the service is owned, observed, recovered, and improved over time.

SRE-style thinking is useful when you need to design for:
- **Reliability** targets that are explicit and measurable.
- **Operational ownership** across teams and services.
- **Fast detection and response** to incidents.
- **Continuous validation** of resilience, not just one-time design.

A good Azure solution architecture includes the service itself plus the operating model around it: monitoring, alerting, escalation, recovery, and post-incident improvement.

**Service ownership and operating model**

A service catalog helps you identify what exists, who owns it, and how it is supported. This is especially important in distributed systems where one user-facing application depends on many internal services.

Key ownership concepts include:
- **RACI**: Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed. Use it to clarify who does the work, who approves decisions, and who needs updates.
- **On-call rotation**: Define who receives alerts outside business hours and how handoffs occur between shifts or teams.
- **Operational handoff**: Document what must be transferred when a project moves from build to run, including runbooks, dashboards, alert rules, and known risks.
- **Escalation path**: Specify when to page a senior engineer, service owner, or incident commander.

Practical expectations for handoff:
- The service has a named owner and backup owner.
- Alerts map to an actionable response, not just a notification.
- Runbooks describe common failures, dependencies, and recovery steps.
- Support boundaries are clear for platform, application, and third-party components.

**Reliability objectives and error budgets**

SRE uses service level indicators (SLIs), service level objectives (SLOs), and service level agreements (SLAs) to make reliability measurable.

- **SLI**: A metric that reflects user experience, such as availability, latency, or successful request rate.
- **SLO**: The target for an SLI, such as 99.9% successful requests over 30 days.
- **SLA**: A contractual commitment to a customer, often with penalties or credits.

Error budgets are the allowed amount of unreliability before you must slow feature delivery and focus on stability. If your SLO is 99.9%, your error budget is 0.1% of the time or requests in the measurement window.

Use error budgets to:
- Balance feature velocity against reliability work.
- Trigger reliability reviews when burn rate is too high.
- Prioritize fixes for recurring incidents and fragile dependencies.

A practical policy often includes:
- **Burn-rate alerting**: Alert when the error budget is being consumed too quickly, not only when the SLO is already violated.
- **Incident response thresholds**: Escalate faster for high burn rates on critical user journeys.
- **Release gating**: Pause risky deployments when reliability is below target.

Example SLO framing:
- Availability SLI: percentage of successful checkout requests.
- SLO: 99.95% monthly success rate.
- Error budget policy: if the service consumes 50% of its monthly budget in the first week, freeze non-essential changes and investigate.

**Monitoring, observability, and dependency mapping**

Observability is the ability to understand system behavior from telemetry. In Azure, this usually combines metrics, logs, traces, and dependency data.

Core telemetry types:
- **Metrics**: Numeric time-series data such as CPU, request count, or latency.
- **Logs**: Event records with context for troubleshooting.
- **Traces**: End-to-end request flow across services.
- **Dependencies**: External or internal services that a workload relies on.

Dependency mapping is critical in microservices and distributed systems because incidents often originate in a downstream service, database, queue, or identity provider. You need to know what depends on what before you can isolate impact.

Azure services commonly used for observability:
- **Azure Monitor** for metrics, logs, alerts, and dashboards.
- **Log Analytics workspace** for Kusto Query Language (KQL) queries over collected logs.
- **Application Insights** for application telemetry, request tracing, exceptions, and dependency tracking.
- **Workbooks** for interactive dashboards and operational views.
- **Service Health** and **Resource Health** for platform and resource status signals.

> Application Insights is a distinct Azure Monitor capability for application telemetry; treat it as part of the monitoring stack, not just a generic label for all monitoring.

Practical Azure Monitor examples:

```bash
# List Log Analytics workspaces
az monitor log-analytics workspace list -o table

# Show metric definitions for a resource
az monitor metrics list-definitions \
  --resource /subscriptions/<subId>/resourceGroups/<rg>/providers/Microsoft.Web/sites/<appName> \
  -o table
```

Example KQL for request failures:

```kusto
requests
| where timestamp > ago(24h)
| where success == false
| summarize failures = count() by cloud_RoleName, resultCode
| order by failures desc
```

Example KQL for dependency latency:

```kusto
dependencies
| where timestamp > ago(24h)
| summarize avgDuration = avg(duration), p95Duration = percentile(duration, 95) by target
| order by p95Duration desc
```

A useful workbook usually includes:
- Availability and latency trends.
- Top failing endpoints.
- Dependency health by downstream service.
- Error budget consumption.
- Recent deployments correlated with incident spikes.

**Alerting strategy and burn-rate design**

Good alerting is actionable, low-noise, and tied to user impact. Avoid alerting on every symptom; alert on conditions that require human intervention.

Alert design principles:
- **Symptom-based alerts**: Alert on user-visible failures, not only infrastructure thresholds.
- **Multi-window burn-rate alerts**: Use short and long windows to catch fast and slow SLO consumption.
- **Severity mapping**: Align alert severity with business impact and response expectations.
- **Deduplication**: Prevent alert storms during cascading failures.

A common pattern is:
- Page on rapid SLO burn for critical services.
- Create tickets for slower degradation or non-critical services.
- Use informational alerts for trend monitoring.

Example conceptual burn-rate logic:
- Fast burn: 5-minute window indicates severe outage.
- Slow burn: 1-hour window indicates sustained degradation.
- Both conditions together reduce false positives while still catching real incidents.

**Incident response and postmortems**

Incident response should be repeatable and documented. The goal is to restore service quickly, communicate clearly, and learn from the event.

Typical incident roles:
- **Incident commander**: Coordinates response and keeps priorities clear.
- **Communications lead**: Updates stakeholders and status pages.
- **Subject matter experts**: Investigate specific components.
- **Operations lead**: Executes mitigation and rollback steps.

A practical incident flow:
1. Detect the issue through alerting, user reports, or health signals.
2. Triage severity and scope.
3. Stabilize the service with rollback, failover, throttling, or feature disablement.
4. Communicate status and expected next update.
5. Investigate root cause after service is stable.
6. Track corrective actions to completion.

Postmortems should be:
- **Blameless**: Focus on system behavior and decision points, not personal fault.
- **Action-item driven**: Produce concrete follow-up work.
- **Tracked to completion**: Assign owners and due dates, then verify closure.

Good postmortem outputs include:
- Timeline of events.
- Customer impact summary.
- Root cause and contributing factors.
- Detection gaps and alert improvements.
- Preventive actions with owners.

**Resilience engineering and recovery objectives**

Resilience is the ability to continue operating during failures and recover quickly afterward. In Azure design, this includes redundancy, failover, backup, and tested recovery procedures.

Core recovery objectives:
- **RTO** (Recovery Time Objective): Maximum acceptable time to restore service.
- **RPO** (Recovery Point Objective): Maximum acceptable data loss measured in time.

Use RTO and RPO to choose the right recovery pattern:
- Short RTO and low RPO often require active-active or active-passive failover with frequent replication.
- Longer RTO and higher RPO may be acceptable for less critical workloads with backup-based recovery.

Resilience patterns to consider:
- Availability zones for intra-region fault tolerance.
- Multi-region deployment for regional outage resilience.
- Geo-redundant storage for data durability.
- Queue-based buffering to absorb spikes and downstream outages.
- Circuit breakers and retries with backoff to reduce cascading failures.
- Graceful degradation so non-critical features fail safely.

Backup validation matters as much as backup creation. Test restore procedures regularly and confirm:
- Data integrity after restore.
- Application startup after recovery.
- Dependency availability after failover.
- Access controls and secrets are restored correctly.

**Chaos engineering and validation**

Chaos engineering is the controlled introduction of failure to validate resilience assumptions. It goes beyond disaster recovery drills by testing how the system behaves under realistic faults.

Use chaos testing to validate:
- Instance loss.
- Zone failure.
- Network latency or packet loss.
- Dependency unavailability.
- Throttling or quota exhaustion.

Good practice:
- Start in non-production or low-risk environments.
- Define a hypothesis before the test.
- Measure the expected user impact and recovery behavior.
- Stop if the blast radius exceeds the approved limit.

Chaos testing complements:
- Disaster recovery exercises.
- Game days.
- Load testing.
- Failover drills.

**Operational dashboards and workbooks**

Dashboards and workbooks help operators see the service state quickly. They should answer the questions: Is the service healthy? What changed? What is failing? What should I do next?

A strong operational workbook often includes:
- Golden signals: latency, traffic, errors, saturation.
- SLO status and error budget consumption.
- Deployment markers and change history.
- Dependency health and downstream failures.
- Region-by-region or zone-by-zone status.

Example workbook use cases:
- Executive summary for service health.
- NOC view for current incidents.
- Engineering view for deep-dive troubleshooting.
- Release view for deployment correlation.

**Practical Azure monitoring setup**

A simple Azure monitoring baseline often includes:
- Enable platform diagnostics on key resources.
- Send logs to a Log Analytics workspace.
- Enable Application Insights for application telemetry.
- Create metric alerts for critical infrastructure signals.
- Create log alerts for user-impacting failures.
- Build a workbook for operational review.

Example diagnostic settings pattern:

```bash
az monitor diagnostic-settings create \
  --name send-to-law \
  --resource /subscriptions/<subId>/resourceGroups/<rg>/providers/Microsoft.Web/sites/<appName> \
  --workspace /subscriptions/<subId>/resourceGroups/<rg>/providers/Microsoft.OperationalInsights/workspaces/<lawName> \
  --logs '[{"category":"AppServiceHTTPLogs","enabled":true},{"category":"AppServiceConsoleLogs","enabled":true}]' \
  --metrics '[{"category":"AllMetrics","enabled":true}]'
```

Example alert rule concept for a failed request rate:
- Condition: failed requests exceed threshold for 5 minutes.
- Scope: application or service resource.
- Action group: on-call email, SMS, or webhook.
- Severity: high for customer-facing services.

**Service decomposition and incident isolation**

Breaking a system into smaller services improves ownership and scaling, but it also increases the need for clear dependency mapping. During an incident, you need to know whether the failure is in:
- The front-end.
- An API layer.
- A database.
- A message queue.
- An identity or third-party service.

Service decomposition helps you:
- Isolate blast radius.
- Assign ownership per component.
- Scale independently.
- Apply different SLOs to different user journeys.

A practical decomposition approach is to define services around business capabilities, not just technical layers. For example, checkout, catalog, and identity may have different reliability targets and different on-call owners.

**Operational governance and continuous improvement**

SRE is not a one-time design choice. It is a continuous operating discipline.

You should regularly review:
- Alert quality and noise.
- SLO attainment and error budget consumption.
- Incident trends and recurring root causes.
- Backup and restore test results.
- Failover and chaos test outcomes.
- Ownership clarity and handoff gaps.

A mature operating model uses these reviews to drive:
- Better alert thresholds.
- Safer release practices.
- Improved runbooks.
- Reduced mean time to detect and recover.
- Clearer accountability across teams.

**Exam focus summary**

For AZ-305, focus on the architectural intent behind SRE-style practices:
- Define measurable reliability with SLIs, SLOs, and error budgets.
- Design monitoring that combines metrics, logs, traces, and dependency mapping.
- Use alerting that is actionable and tied to user impact.
- Plan incident response with clear ownership, escalation, and communication.
- Validate resilience with backup tests, failover drills, and chaos engineering.
- Include RTO and RPO when discussing recovery design.
- Treat postmortems as blameless learning tools with tracked corrective actions.
