## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**Recovery objectives in Azure**

Recovery objectives define how much disruption your business can tolerate after an outage. In Azure architecture, the two core metrics are <abbr title="Recovery Time Objective">RTO</abbr> and <abbr title="Recovery Point Objective">RPO</abbr>. They guide decisions about backup frequency, replication mode, failover automation, and whether you need a single-region design or a multi-region disaster recovery (DR) strategy.

These objectives are business-driven, not product-driven. You start with the application’s tolerance for downtime and data loss, then map that tolerance to Azure services such as Azure Backup, Azure Site Recovery, geo-redundant storage, database replication, and traffic failover patterns.

**RTO: Recovery Time Objective**

RTO is the maximum acceptable time to restore a service after an incident. It answers: “How long can this application be unavailable?”

A low RTO means you need faster recovery mechanisms, such as:
- Warm standby or active-active architectures
- Automated failover
- Pre-provisioned infrastructure
- Minimal manual intervention

A higher RTO allows slower, cheaper recovery options, such as:
- Restore from backup
- Rebuild from infrastructure as code
- Manual validation before bringing the service back online

In practice, RTO includes more than just VM boot time. It also includes:
- Detection time
- Decision time
- Failover orchestration time
- DNS or traffic routing propagation
- Application warm-up and validation

For example, if a business can tolerate 2 hours of downtime, your design must restore the service within that window, including all operational steps required to make the app usable again.

**RPO: Recovery Point Objective**

RPO is the maximum acceptable amount of data loss measured in time. It answers: “How far back in time can we recover data?”

A low RPO means you need frequent backups or near-real-time replication. A higher RPO allows more data loss and usually reduces cost and complexity.

RPO is affected by:
- Backup schedule frequency
- Replication lag
- Transaction log shipping or database replication intervals
- Storage replication type
- Application consistency requirements

If the RPO is 15 minutes, the solution must ensure that no more than 15 minutes of data is lost in a disaster. That does not mean recovery takes 15 minutes; it means the recovered state should be no older than 15 minutes before the incident.

**RTO vs RPO**

These terms are often confused, but they measure different things.

| Metric | Measures | Question answered | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| RTO | Time to restore service | How long can the app be down? | Restore within 1 hour |
| RPO | Acceptable data loss window | How much data can be lost? | Lose at most 5 minutes of data |

A design can have:
- Low RTO and low RPO: expensive, highly available, highly automated
- Low RTO and higher RPO: fast recovery, but some data loss is acceptable
- Higher RTO and low RPO: data is protected well, but service restoration may take longer
- Higher RTO and higher RPO: lowest cost, least resilient

The key is that RTO and RPO are independent. A solution can recover quickly but still lose recent data, or preserve data well but take a long time to come back online.

**How Azure services map to recovery objectives**

Different Azure services help you meet different targets.

- **Azure Backup**
  - Best for point-in-time recovery of files, VMs, and supported workloads
  - Usually improves RPO through scheduled backups
  - Often has a longer RTO because restore operations take time

- **Azure Site Recovery (ASR)**
  - Replicates workloads to another region
  - Typically improves both RTO and RPO compared with backup-only designs
  - Supports orchestrated failover and test failover

- **Geo-redundant storage**
  - Protects storage data against regional failure
  - Useful for object and file data durability
  - Does not automatically restore application state or compute

- **Database replication**
  - Helps reduce RPO for transactional systems
  - Can reduce RTO when failover is automated or semi-automated
  - Examples include geo-replication patterns and failover groups where supported

- **Traffic management**
  - Azure Front Door, Traffic Manager, or DNS-based routing can reduce perceived downtime
  - Traffic routing helps meet RTO, but it does not by itself protect data

**Designing for a target RTO**

To meet a strict RTO, you reduce the time needed to detect, decide, and recover.

Common design choices include:
- Keep standby infrastructure ready in a secondary region
- Automate failover with runbooks or platform features
- Use health probes and traffic routing to shift users quickly
- Pre-stage dependencies such as networking, identity, and secrets
- Test failover regularly so the process is predictable

A practical pattern is:
1. Detect failure through monitoring.
2. Trigger failover orchestration.
3. Bring up compute or redirect traffic.
4. Validate application health.
5. Resume service and monitor closely.

Example Azure CLI commands for traffic validation and operational checks:

```bash
az monitor metrics list \
  --resource /subscriptions/<subId>/resourceGroups/<rg>/providers/Microsoft.Compute/virtualMachines/<vmName> \
  --metric Percentage CPU \
  --interval PT5M
```

```bash
az network public-ip show \
  --resource-group <rg> \
  --name <publicIpName> \
  --query "{ip:ipAddress, sku:sku.name}"
```

These commands do not perform recovery by themselves, but they help you verify the state of resources during incident response.

**Designing for a target RPO**

To meet a strict RPO, you reduce the amount of data that can be lost between protection points.

Common design choices include:
- Increase backup frequency
- Use application-consistent backups where needed
- Replicate data continuously or near-continuously
- Reduce write latency between primary and secondary copies
- Ensure logs and transaction streams are protected

For databases, RPO is often driven by replication lag rather than backup schedule alone. For file and object storage, it is driven by replication type and whether writes are committed to one or multiple locations.

Example backup policy concept for a VM:
- Daily full backup
- Multiple restore points retained
- Shorter backup interval for critical workloads if supported
- Longer retention for compliance, separate from RPO

A backup policy can improve recoverability, but it does not guarantee a low RPO if the backup interval is too long. If you back up every 24 hours, your RPO may be as high as 24 hours.

**Application consistency vs crash consistency**

Recovery objectives are only useful if the recovered workload is usable.

- **Crash-consistent** recovery captures the disk state as if the VM lost power suddenly
- **Application-consistent** recovery coordinates with the application to flush in-memory data and complete transactions where possible

Application-consistent backups usually improve the quality of recovery, especially for databases and transactional systems. They may take longer to create and can require guest agents or workload-specific integration.

Use application-consistent recovery when:
- The workload has transactional data
- You need a clean restore point
- The application cannot safely recover from a crash-consistent snapshot alone

Use crash-consistent recovery when:
- The workload is stateless or tolerant of abrupt interruption
- Speed and simplicity matter more than perfect application state

**How to choose between backup and replication**

Backup and replication solve different problems.

- Use **backup** when you need:
  - Point-in-time restore
  - Long-term retention
  - Protection against accidental deletion or corruption
  - Lower cost than continuous replication

- Use **replication** when you need:
  - Faster failover
  - Lower RTO
  - Lower RPO
  - A secondary site ready for immediate use

A common architecture combines both:
- Replication for fast disaster recovery
- Backup for retention, compliance, and protection from logical corruption

> Do not treat replication as a substitute for backup. Replication can copy deleted or corrupted data just as quickly as good data.

**Measuring and validating recovery objectives**

You should not assume your design meets its targets until you test it.

Validate RTO by measuring:
- Time to detect the incident
- Time to initiate failover
- Time to restore compute and dependencies
- Time to route users to the recovered environment
- Time until the application is fully functional

Validate RPO by measuring:
- Backup age at the point of failure
- Replication lag
- Transaction loss after failover
- Time between restore points

A simple test plan might include:
1. Record the current backup or replication state.
2. Simulate a failure or use a test failover.
3. Measure elapsed time until the app is available.
4. Compare the recovered data timestamp with the incident time.
5. Confirm the results meet the business target.

**Practical examples**

A file-sharing app used by an internal team may tolerate:
- RTO: 4 hours
- RPO: 24 hours

That design could use:
- Daily backups
- Manual restore procedures
- No secondary active site

A customer-facing payments system may require:
- RTO: 15 minutes
- RPO: 5 minutes

That design could use:
- Multi-region replication
- Automated failover
- Application-consistent data protection
- Continuous monitoring and runbooks

A reporting system may require:
- RTO: 8 hours
- RPO: 1 hour

That design could use:
- Scheduled backups every hour
- Restore automation
- Secondary region infrastructure deployed on demand

**Azure architecture considerations**

When you design for RTO and RPO, also account for:
- **Dependencies**: identity, DNS, Key Vault, networking, and shared services
- **State**: databases, queues, caches, and session stores
- **Consistency**: whether the app can tolerate stale or partial data
- **Cost**: lower RTO and RPO usually increase cost
- **Testing**: recovery plans must be exercised regularly
- **Regional support**: not every service or redundancy option is available in every region

A recovery plan is only as strong as its weakest dependency. If your app fails over but its secrets, DNS records, or database endpoints do not, the effective RTO is much worse than expected.

**Common exam points**

For AZ-305, focus on these distinctions:
- **RTO** is about downtime duration
- **RPO** is about data loss window
- Backup usually helps RPO more than RTO
- Replication usually helps both RTO and RPO
- Failover automation reduces RTO
- Backup retention is not the same as recovery speed
- Application-consistent recovery is preferred for transactional workloads

**Quick memory aid**

- **RTO** = time to recover service
- **RPO** = time to recover data

If you remember only one thing, remember this:
- RTO is measured from outage to restored service
- RPO is measured from outage back to the last recoverable data point
