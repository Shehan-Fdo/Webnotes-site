## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**Failover planning overview**

Failover planning is the process of designing how workloads continue operating when a component, zone, region, or entire service path becomes unavailable. In Azure, you usually combine platform capabilities, application design, data replication, and operational runbooks rather than relying on a single product.

A good plan answers four questions:

- **What fails?** A VM, subnet, database, region, identity dependency, or the whole application stack.
- **Where does traffic go?** Another zone, another region, or a degraded but functional path.
- **How fast must recovery happen?** Recovery Time Objective (RTO) defines acceptable downtime.
- **How much data loss is acceptable?** Recovery Point Objective (RPO) defines acceptable loss measured in time.

Failover planning is not only about disaster recovery. It also covers planned maintenance, zone outages, dependency failures, and partial service degradation. For AZ-305, focus on choosing the right Azure service and pattern for the workload’s availability target.

**Core resilience concepts**

Azure resilience design usually starts with the failure domain. A failure domain is the scope of an outage that can affect multiple resources at once. Smaller failure domains reduce blast radius.

- **Availability zone**: A physically separate datacenter within a region.
- **Region**: A geographic area containing one or more datacenters.
- **Paired region**: Azure-designated region pairing used for some platform recovery behaviors and coordinated updates.
- **Fault domain**: A set of hardware with shared power and network in some services.
- **Update domain**: A group of resources updated together during planned maintenance.

Use these concepts to decide whether you need:

- **High availability** within a region, such as zone-redundant services.
- **Disaster recovery** across regions, such as active-passive or active-active designs.
- **Backup and restore** for slower recovery scenarios.
- **Application-level failover** when the platform does not provide automatic switching.

A common exam trap is confusing high availability with disaster recovery. High availability reduces downtime from localized failures; disaster recovery restores service after a larger outage or data corruption event. They often work together, but they are not the same thing.

**Design inputs for failover planning**

Before choosing a pattern, define the workload requirements.

- **Business impact**: Which components are mission critical and which can be delayed?
- **Dependency map**: Identify compute, storage, database, identity, DNS, secrets, and third-party services.
- **Statefulness**: Stateless tiers fail over more easily than stateful tiers.
- **Data consistency**: Some workloads can tolerate eventual consistency; others require strong consistency.
- **Operational maturity**: Automatic failover is only useful if you can test and monitor it.
- **Cost tolerance**: Warm standby and active-active designs cost more than backup-only approaches.

A practical way to document this is to create a tiered recovery matrix.

| Tier | Example component | Target RTO | Target RPO | Typical pattern |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|
| 0 | Identity, DNS, secrets | Minutes | Near zero | Multi-region design, automation, redundancy |
| 1 | Transactional app and database | Minutes to hours | Seconds to minutes | Zone-redundant or cross-region replication |
| 2 | Reporting and batch jobs | Hours | Hours | Backup/restore or redeploy |
| 3 | Archive and noncritical tools | Best effort | Best effort | Restore on demand |

**Common failover patterns**

**Active-active**

In an active-active design, two or more sites serve traffic at the same time. This improves resilience and can reduce failover time because traffic is already flowing to multiple locations.

Use active-active when:

- The application is stateless or can synchronize state safely.
- The data layer supports multi-region reads or writes.
- You can handle split-brain risk and conflict resolution.

Benefits:

- Better resilience to regional failure.
- Lower recovery time than cold standby.
- Potentially better latency through geographic distribution.

Tradeoffs:

- Higher complexity.
- More expensive.
- Data consistency is harder to maintain.

**Active-passive**

In an active-passive design, one site serves traffic while another remains ready to take over. The passive site may be warm, hot, or cold.

- **Hot standby**: Fully provisioned and synchronized, ready for fast failover.
- **Warm standby**: Partially provisioned, can scale up quickly.
- **Cold standby**: Minimal resources, slower recovery.

Use active-passive when:

- You need simpler operations than active-active.
- The workload is stateful or difficult to distribute.
- Cost matters more than instant recovery.

**Pilot light**

Pilot light keeps only the critical core running in the secondary region, such as the database replica and minimal infrastructure. You scale out the rest during failover.

This pattern is useful when:

- You want lower cost than warm standby.
- You can tolerate some recovery delay.
- Infrastructure can be automated with templates or scripts.

**Backup and restore**

Backup and restore is the simplest recovery model. You restore data and redeploy infrastructure after an outage or corruption event.

Use it when:

- RTO and RPO are relatively loose.
- The workload is noncritical or infrequently used.
- You need protection from accidental deletion or ransomware.

This pattern is not a substitute for high availability. It is a recovery mechanism, not continuous service continuity.

**Traffic management and failover routing**

Traffic routing is often the first layer of failover. Azure offers several options, each with different scope.

- **Azure Traffic Manager**: DNS-based global traffic distribution and failover.
- **Azure Front Door**: Global HTTP/HTTPS entry point with layer 7 routing and health-based failover.
- **Azure Load Balancer**: Regional layer 4 load balancing for TCP/UDP traffic.

Use the right tool for the traffic type:

- Choose **Traffic Manager** for DNS-based failover across regions and endpoints.
- Choose **Front Door** for web applications needing global HTTP(S) routing, acceleration, and edge-based failover.
- Choose **Load Balancer** for regional internal or public load balancing, not global traffic steering.

> Azure Load Balancer is regional. It can support resilient designs, but it is not a global traffic manager by itself.

**Traffic Manager**

Traffic Manager resolves DNS to direct clients to the best endpoint based on routing method and health probes. Because it is DNS-based, failover depends on DNS caching and client behavior.

Routing methods include:

- **Priority**: Primary endpoint with failover to secondary endpoints.
- **Weighted**: Split traffic across endpoints.
- **Performance**: Route to the lowest-latency endpoint.
- **Geographic**: Route based on user location.
- **Subnet**: Route based on client IP ranges.
- **MultiValue**: Return multiple healthy endpoints.
- **Nested**: Combine profiles for complex topologies.

Health probes are configured on the endpoint or profile behavior, not as generic profile creation parameters. A typical profile uses a DNS name and endpoint monitoring settings.

Example:

```bash
az network traffic-manager profile create \
  --resource-group rg-global \
  --name tm-web \
  --routing-method Priority \
  --unique-dns-name tm-web-12345 \
  --ttl 30 \
  --monitor-protocol HTTPS \
  --monitor-port 443 \
  --monitor-path /health
```

A priority-based endpoint setup might look like this:

```bash
az network traffic-manager endpoint create \
  --resource-group rg-global \
  --profile-name tm-web \
  --name primary-web \
  --type azureEndpoints \
  --target-resource-id /subscriptions/<subId>/resourceGroups/rg-east/providers/Microsoft.Web/sites/app-east \
  --priority 1
```

Notes:

- DNS TTL affects how quickly clients observe failover.
- Health probes must reflect real application readiness, not just process liveness.
- Traffic Manager cannot instantly move every client because resolvers cache DNS responses.

**Azure Front Door**

Front Door is often the preferred global entry point for HTTP/HTTPS applications. It provides global anycast ingress, health probes, path-based routing, and fast failover at the edge.

Use Front Door when you need:

- Global web failover with low latency.
- Layer 7 routing by host, path, or header.
- TLS termination and web acceleration.
- WAF (Web Application Firewall) integration.

Front Door is especially useful for multi-region web apps because it can route around unhealthy origins without relying solely on DNS cache expiration.

**Azure Load Balancer**

Azure Load Balancer distributes traffic within a region. It supports:

- **Internal Load Balancer** for private traffic.
- **Public Load Balancer** for internet-facing traffic.
- **Zone-redundant frontend** in supported scenarios.

It is useful for VM-based tiers and clustered services, but it does not provide global failover across regions on its own. For cross-region designs, pair it with Traffic Manager, Front Door, or application-level logic.

**Compute failover options**

**Virtual Machines**

For VM-based workloads, failover planning usually involves:

- Availability sets for intra-datacenter fault and update domain separation.
- Availability zones for zonal resilience.
- Azure Site Recovery for cross-region replication and orchestration.

Azure Site Recovery (ASR) replicates VMs to a secondary region and can automate failover and failback. It is a common choice for IaaS disaster recovery.

Example ASR CLI flow:

```bash
az site-recovery fabric create \
  --resource-group rg-dr \
  --vault-name rsv-dr \
  --fabric-name eastus-fabric \
  --location eastus
```

ASR configuration is more involved than a single command. In practice, you define:

- Recovery Services vault
- Source fabric and protection container
- Replication policy
- Protected items
- Recovery plans

Use recovery plans to coordinate multi-tier application failover in the correct order, such as database first, then app tier, then web tier.

**App Service**

Azure App Service supports zone redundancy in some SKUs and can be deployed across regions for failover. Common patterns include:

- Active-active with Front Door or Traffic Manager.
- Active-passive with a warm secondary app.
- Slot-based deployment for safe updates, though slots are not a regional DR strategy by themselves.

Use deployment slots for release safety, not as a substitute for region failover.

**AKS and containers**

Azure Kubernetes Service (AKS) failover planning depends on cluster design and data dependencies.

- Use multiple node pools and zones for intra-region resilience.
- Use separate clusters across regions for disaster recovery.
- Externalize state to managed data services where possible.
- Replicate container images and configuration across regions.

For multi-region AKS, you usually need:

- Global ingress such as Front Door.
- Replicated persistent data.
- Infrastructure as code for rapid cluster rebuilds.

**Data layer failover**

Data is usually the hardest part of failover planning. Choose the replication model based on consistency and recovery needs.

**Azure SQL Database**

Azure SQL Database supports several resilience options:

- **Zone redundancy** for higher availability within a region.
- **Active geo-replication** for readable secondary databases in another region.
- **Auto-failover groups** for managed failover of one or more databases.

Auto-failover groups are useful when you want a managed listener endpoint and coordinated failover for Azure SQL Database workloads. They are not the only DR option, and they apply to specific Azure SQL deployment models.

Example concept:

- Primary region hosts the writable database.
- Secondary region hosts the replicated database.
- Application connects through the failover group listener.
- On failover, the listener changes to the secondary region.

A typical CLI pattern for creating a failover group is:

```bash
az sql failover-group create \
  --name fog-app \
  --partner-server sql-secondary \
  --resource-group rg-sql \
  --server sql-primary \
  --failover-policy Automatic \
  --grace-period 60
```

For manual failover, verify the exact command and resource type in current Azure CLI documentation before using it in production or exam answers. The syntax and supported operations can differ between Azure SQL Database and Azure SQL Managed Instance.

**Azure SQL Managed Instance**

Managed Instance supports failover groups for cross-region disaster recovery. This is often the preferred managed DR pattern for MI because it preserves instance-level features more closely than database-only replication.

Use it when:

- You need near-instance compatibility.
- You want managed replication and failover.
- You can accept the operational model of paired instances.

**Azure Cosmos DB**

Cosmos DB is designed for global distribution. You can:

- Add regions for low-latency reads.
- Configure multi-region writes in supported APIs and scenarios.
- Use automatic failover for read regions.

Cosmos DB failover planning focuses on:

- Consistency level.
- Write region strategy.
- Conflict resolution.
- Regional failover priority.

Because Cosmos DB is globally distributed, the failover question is often about write-region promotion and client routing rather than restoring a single database copy.

**Storage failover**

Azure Storage resilience depends on the redundancy option you choose.

- **LRS**: Locally redundant storage within one datacenter.
- **ZRS**: Zone-redundant storage across availability zones in a region.
- **GRS**: Geo-redundant storage with asynchronous replication to a paired region.
- **GZRS**: Zone-redundant primary with geo-redundant secondary.
- **RA-GRS / RA-GZRS**: Read access to the secondary endpoint.

Use geo-redundant options when you need regional disaster recovery for blobs and some storage scenarios. Remember that replication is asynchronous, so some data loss is possible during a regional outage.

**DNS and identity dependencies**

Failover often fails because supporting services are overlooked.

- **DNS**: Ensure authoritative DNS is highly available and automation can update records.
- **Microsoft Entra ID**: Most Azure services depend on identity, so plan for authentication continuity and token behavior.
- **Key Vault**: Secrets, certificates, and keys must be reachable from the failover region.
- **Private DNS**: Private endpoints and name resolution must work in both regions.
- **Certificates**: TLS certificates should be replicated or reissued in the secondary region.

If the application cannot authenticate or resolve names after failover, the compute and database layers are irrelevant.

**Network failover design**

Network design affects whether failover is actually reachable.

- Use redundant VPN or ExpressRoute connectivity where required.
- Consider dual circuits or dual tunnels for branch and on-premises connectivity.
- Replicate route tables, NSGs (Network Security Groups), and firewall rules.
- Validate that private endpoints and service endpoints exist in the secondary region.

Example of a resilient topology:

- Primary region hosts app, database, and ingress.
- Secondary region hosts warm standby app and replicated data.
- Global entry point uses Front Door or Traffic Manager.
- On-premises connectivity uses redundant paths.
- Automation updates DNS or traffic routing during failover.

**Operational runbooks**

A failover design is incomplete without a tested runbook. The runbook should define who does what, in what order, and how to verify success.

1. Detect the incident using monitoring and alerting.
2. Confirm scope and decide whether to fail over.
3. Freeze deployments and stop conflicting writes if needed.
4. Promote the secondary data store or enable write access.
5. Shift traffic using Front Door, Traffic Manager, or DNS.
6. Validate application health, authentication, and data integrity.
7. Communicate status to stakeholders.
8. Record the incident and begin failback planning.

A runbook should also include failback, because returning to the primary site can be more complex than failing over.

**Testing and validation**

Failover planning is only credible if you test it.

- Perform planned failover tests in nonproduction and, when safe, production windows.
- Validate RTO and RPO against actual measurements.
- Test partial failures, not just full-region outages.
- Confirm that monitoring detects unhealthy dependencies.
- Rehearse failback and data reconciliation.

Useful validation checks include:

```bash
az network watcher test-connectivity \
  --source-resource /subscriptions/<subId>/resourceGroups/rg-app/providers/Microsoft.Network/networkInterfaces/nic-app \
  --dest-address sql-secondary.database.windows.net \
  --dest-port 1433
```

```bash
az monitor metrics list \
  --resource /subscriptions/<subId>/resourceGroups/rg-app/providers/Microsoft.Web/sites/app-east \
  --metric Requests,Http5xx
```

These commands help you verify connectivity and service health, but they do not replace application-level testing.

**Failback considerations**

Failback is the process of returning traffic and data to the original primary site after recovery. It is often riskier than failover because both sites may have diverged.

Plan for:

- Data synchronization back to the primary.
- Change freeze during cutback.
- Validation of schema and application version parity.
- DNS or routing changes in the reverse direction.
- Rollback if the original site is not stable.

Do not assume failback is automatic just because failover was automated.

**Exam-focused distinctions**

| Topic | What it is | What to remember |
|---|---|---|
| High availability | Keeps service running during localized failures | Usually intra-region, often zone-based |
| Disaster recovery | Restores service after major outage | Usually cross-region, requires replication |
| Backup | Point-in-time recovery | Protects against deletion and corruption |
| Failover | Switching to a standby path | Can be manual or automatic |
| Failback | Returning to the original site | Needs validation and synchronization |

**Practical architecture examples**

**Web app with managed database**

- Front Door as global entry point.
- App Service in two regions.
- Azure SQL Database with auto-failover group.
- Key Vault replicated or deployed in both regions.
- Monitoring and alerts on both app and database health.

This pattern is common when you want low-latency global access and managed database failover.

**VM-based line-of-business app**

- Availability sets or zones in the primary region.
- Azure Site Recovery to a secondary region.
- Azure Load Balancer for regional distribution.
- Traffic Manager or Front Door for global routing.
- Recovery plan to start database, middleware, then application servers.

This pattern is common for legacy applications that cannot be refactored quickly.

**Containerized microservices**

- AKS clusters in two regions.
- Front Door for global ingress.
- Managed database with geo-replication.
- Shared configuration in Key Vault and App Configuration.
- GitOps or IaC for rapid rebuild.

This pattern works well when services are stateless and infrastructure is reproducible.

**Common pitfalls**

- Treating DNS failover as instant; client caching delays changes.
- Forgetting identity, secrets, and certificates.
- Using backup as the only DR strategy for a critical workload.
- Assuming zone redundancy protects against regional outages.
- Failing to test failback.
- Overlooking data consistency and write conflicts in active-active designs.
- Designing traffic failover without validating health probes.

**Quick decision guide**

- Use **zones** when you need resilience within a region.
- Use **cross-region replication** when you need disaster recovery.
- Use **Front Door** for global web traffic.
- Use **Traffic Manager** for DNS-based global routing.
- Use **ASR** for VM disaster recovery.
- Use **failover groups** for Azure SQL Database or Managed Instance where supported.
- Use **backup/restore** when recovery time can be longer and cost must stay low.

**Key takeaways**

Failover planning in Azure is a layered design problem. You combine traffic routing, compute redundancy, data replication, and operational procedures to meet business recovery targets. The best solution depends on workload criticality, statefulness, and acceptable RTO/RPO.

For AZ-305, focus on selecting the right pattern for the scenario, not memorizing a single product. The correct answer usually reflects the workload’s traffic type, data model, and recovery objectives.
