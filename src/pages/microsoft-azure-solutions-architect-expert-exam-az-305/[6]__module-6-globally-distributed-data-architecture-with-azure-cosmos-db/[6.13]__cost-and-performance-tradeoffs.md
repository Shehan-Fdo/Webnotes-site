## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**Cost and performance tradeoffs**

Azure architecture is rarely about choosing the fastest or cheapest option in isolation. You usually balance throughput, latency, availability, operational effort, and long-term spend. The right design depends on workload shape, business criticality, and how much variability you can tolerate.

A useful exam mindset is to ask:
- What is the workload’s steady-state demand versus burst demand?
- Which component is the bottleneck: compute, storage, database, or network?
- What level of resilience is required, and what does that resilience cost?
- Can you reduce spend with governance before changing the architecture?

**Core decision factors**

Cost and performance tradeoffs usually show up in four places: service model, tier/SKU, scaling strategy, and topology. A design that looks cheaper on paper can become expensive if it increases operational overhead, egress charges, or downtime risk.

- **Service model** affects how much you manage.
  - **IaaS** gives the most control but usually requires the most patching, sizing, and operations.
  - **PaaS** reduces management overhead and often improves time to value.
  - **SaaS** minimizes infrastructure management but offers the least customization.
- **Tier/SKU** changes limits, features, SLA, and price.
  - Basic tiers often suit dev/test or low-scale workloads.
  - Standard tiers add scale, HA features, and better throughput.
  - Premium tiers usually improve latency, IOPS, isolation, or advanced features.
- **Scaling strategy** determines whether you pay for peak capacity all the time or only when needed.
  - Vertical scaling increases size per instance.
  - Horizontal scaling adds instances to spread load.
- **Topology** influences both latency and spend.
  - Multi-region designs improve resilience but increase replication, traffic, and management costs.
  - Network-hub designs can centralize control but may add latency and transit charges.

**Azure cost governance**

Before you redesign a workload, use governance to make spend visible and controllable. Azure cost controls are often the fastest way to improve the cost/performance ratio without changing the application.

- **Microsoft Cost Management** helps you analyze spend by subscription, resource group, service, tag, and time period.
- **Budgets** let you set thresholds and trigger alerts before overspend becomes a problem.
- **Tags** support chargeback and showback by application, environment, owner, or cost center.
- **Resource groups** provide a coarse allocation boundary, but tags are usually better for shared subscriptions.
- **Azure Policy** can enforce cost-aware guardrails, such as allowed SKUs, required tags, or region restrictions.

A practical tagging model often includes:
- `CostCenter`
- `Application`
- `Environment`
- `Owner`
- `BusinessUnit`

Example Azure CLI budget creation:

```bash
az consumption budget create \
  --amount 5000 \
  --budget-name monthly-prod-budget \
  --category Cost \
  --time-grain Monthly \
  --start-date 2026-01-01 \
  --end-date 2026-12-31 \
  --resource-group my-rg
```

Example query to inspect spend by tag in Azure Cost Management exports or via Kusto-style analysis in a Log Analytics workspace that ingests cost data:

```kusto
CostManagementExports
| where TimeGenerated >= ago(30d)
| summarize TotalCost = sum(Cost) by tostring(Tags["Application"]), tostring(Tags["Environment"])
| order by TotalCost desc
```

> Cost data can lag behind real usage. Use it for trend analysis and governance, not for minute-by-minute autoscale decisions.

**Choosing between IaaS, PaaS, and SaaS**

The exam often tests whether you choose the right service model for the right tradeoff. The best answer is not always the lowest direct infrastructure cost.

| Model | Cost profile | Performance control | Operational effort | Typical fit |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|
| IaaS | Pay for VMs, disks, and network; can be efficient at scale | High | High | Legacy apps, custom OS needs, specialized agents |
| PaaS | Higher unit price sometimes, lower ops cost | Medium to high | Low to medium | Web apps, APIs, managed databases |
| SaaS | Subscription-based | Low | Lowest | Standard business capabilities |

Key exam guidance:
- Choose **IaaS** when you need OS-level control, unsupported software, or lift-and-shift compatibility.
- Choose **PaaS** when you want managed scaling, patching, backups, and faster delivery.
- Choose **SaaS** when the business problem matches an existing product and customization needs are limited.

A common trap is assuming IaaS is always cheaper. It may have lower sticker price for compute, but patching, monitoring, HA design, and admin time can make it more expensive overall.

**Tier and SKU tradeoffs**

Azure services often have meaningful differences between tiers. The right tier depends on throughput, latency, feature set, and SLA requirements.

- **App Service**
  - Basic tiers are suitable for small apps, dev/test, or limited scale.
  - Standard tiers add autoscale and deployment slots.
  - Premium tiers improve scale, isolation, and performance characteristics.
- **Azure SQL Database**
  - **General Purpose** balances cost and performance for most workloads.
  - **Business Critical** provides lower latency and higher IOPS through local SSD and multiple replicas, but costs more.
  - Serverless can reduce cost for intermittent workloads, but cold start and scaling behavior matter.
- **Storage**
  - Standard storage is cheaper and suitable for many workloads.
  - Premium storage improves latency and IOPS for demanding workloads.
  - Hot, cool, and archive access tiers trade retrieval cost for storage cost.
- **Azure Kubernetes Service (AKS)**
  - You pay for nodes and supporting services.
  - More advanced cluster features can improve portability and deployment flexibility, but they increase operational complexity.
- **Azure Functions**
  - Consumption plans are cost-efficient for sporadic workloads.
  - Dedicated or premium plans improve predictability, networking options, and warm performance.

**Scaling patterns**

Scaling is one of the most important cost/performance levers. You want to match capacity to demand without overprovisioning.

- **Vertical scaling**
  - Increase CPU, memory, disk, or SKU size on a single instance.
  - Useful when the app is not horizontally scalable or when you need more memory per process.
  - Can be simpler than redesigning the app, but may require downtime or service restart depending on the platform.
- **Horizontal scaling**
  - Add more instances to distribute load.
  - Better for stateless services and bursty traffic.
  - Requires load balancing, session handling, and shared state design.

Autoscale is usually the right answer when demand varies predictably or spikes based on metrics. Use it when the workload can scale out safely and when the platform supports it.

Example autoscale rule concept for a web app or VM scale set:
- Scale out when average CPU stays above 70% for 10 minutes.
- Scale in when average CPU stays below 40% for 15 minutes.
- Add cooldown periods to avoid oscillation.

Example Azure CLI for a VM scale set autoscale profile:

```bash
az monitor autoscale create \
  --resource-group my-rg \
  --resource myvmss \
  --resource-type Microsoft.Compute/virtualMachineScaleSets \
  --name vmss-autoscale \
  --min-count 2 \
  --max-count 10 \
  --count 2
```

Example autoscale rule:

```bash
az monitor autoscale rule create \
  --resource-group my-rg \
  --autoscale-name vmss-autoscale \
  --condition "Percentage CPU > 70 avg 10m" \
  --scale out 1
```

> Autoscale should follow workload signals, not just cost targets. Scaling too aggressively can increase latency, thrashing, and total spend.

**Performance metrics that drive decisions**

You should tune or re-architect when metrics show sustained saturation, not just occasional spikes. The most useful indicators are workload-specific and should be observed together.

- **CPU** saturation suggests compute pressure or inefficient code.
- **Memory** pressure can indicate leaks, poor caching, or undersized instances.
- **IOPS** and disk latency show storage bottlenecks.
- **Request latency** reveals user experience impact.
- **Queue depth** indicates backpressure and delayed processing.
- **Throughput** helps you understand whether the system is hitting a ceiling.
- **Connection count** can expose database or gateway limits.
- **Error rate** and timeout rate show whether performance issues are becoming failures.

A practical tuning workflow:
1. Establish a baseline under normal load.
2. Identify the bottleneck metric.
3. Test one change at a time.
4. Measure latency, throughput, and cost together.
5. Decide whether to scale, optimize code, or redesign the topology.

**Storage and database tradeoffs**

Storage and database choices often dominate both cost and performance.

- **Azure SQL Database**
  - General Purpose is usually the default starting point.
  - Business Critical is better for low-latency OLTP workloads.
  - Hyperscale can support very large databases and fast scaling patterns.
- **Azure Cosmos DB**
  - Offers predictable low latency and global distribution.
  - Costs depend on request units and replication, so overprovisioning can be expensive.
- **Azure Storage**
  - Use lifecycle management to move data to cooler tiers automatically.
  - Use premium disks or premium file shares when latency matters more than cost.
- **Caching**
  - Azure Cache for Redis can reduce database load and improve response times.
  - Caching is often cheaper than scaling the primary database, especially for read-heavy workloads.

Example SQL tier decision:
- Choose **General Purpose** for balanced cost and moderate latency.
- Choose **Business Critical** when you need faster failover, lower latency, and higher IOPS.
- Choose **serverless** when usage is intermittent and you can tolerate scaling behavior.

Example storage lifecycle policy:

```json
{
  "rules": [
    {
      "enabled": true,
      "name": "move-to-cool",
      "type": "Lifecycle",
      "definition": {
        "filters": {
          "blobTypes": ["blockBlob"],
          "prefixMatch": ["logs/"]
        },
        "actions": {
          "baseBlob": {
            "tierToCool": {
              "daysAfterModificationGreaterThan": 30
            },
            "tierToArchive": {
              "daysAfterModificationGreaterThan": 180
            }
          }
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

**Network cost and performance tradeoffs**

Network design affects both latency and spend. In Azure, traffic patterns can create hidden costs that are easy to miss during architecture review.

- **Bandwidth and egress**
  - Data leaving Azure is often charged.
  - Cross-region traffic can increase cost and latency.
  - Inbound traffic is usually cheaper or free, depending on the service and path.
- **Peering and transit**
  - Hub-and-spoke centralizes routing and inspection.
  - It can simplify governance but may add transit hops and cost.
- **VPN vs ExpressRoute**
  - VPN is usually cheaper and faster to deploy.
  - ExpressRoute offers more predictable connectivity, lower jitter, and private connectivity, but costs more.
- **CDN and edge optimization**
  - Azure Front Door and Azure CDN reduce latency by serving content closer to users.
  - They can also reduce origin load and improve global performance.
- **Load balancing**
  - Azure Load Balancer is efficient for Layer 4 traffic.
  - Application Gateway adds Layer 7 features such as TLS termination and web application firewall integration.
  - Azure Front Door is useful for global HTTP(S) acceleration and edge routing.

A common pattern is:
- Use **Front Door** for global entry, caching, and fast failover.
- Use **Application Gateway** for regional Layer 7 routing and WAF.
- Use **Load Balancer** for non-HTTP workloads or internal traffic distribution.

**Availability, resilience, and cost**

Higher availability usually costs more. The exam expects you to understand that resilience is not free and that the right design depends on business impact.

- **Availability zones**
  - Improve resilience against datacenter failure within a region.
  - Increase cost because you may run multiple instances and duplicate supporting resources.
- **Geo-replication**
  - Protects against regional outages.
  - Adds replication cost, network cost, and operational complexity.
- **Active-active**
  - Improves availability and can reduce failover time.
  - Usually costs more because both sites carry live traffic.
- **Active-passive**
  - Lower steady-state cost than active-active.
  - Failover may take longer, and the passive site may still incur standby costs.

Tradeoff examples:
- A public web app with strict uptime requirements may justify zone-redundant compute and geo-redundant data.
- An internal reporting app may accept single-region deployment with backups and a documented recovery plan.
- A mission-critical database may need synchronous replication, but that increases latency and cost.

**Reservations and savings plans**

Azure cost optimization often includes commitment-based pricing. These options reduce cost when usage is steady, but they reduce flexibility.

- **Reservations**
  - Best when you have predictable, steady usage for eligible resources.
  - Common for compute, storage, and some database offerings.
  - You commit to a term, usually one or three years, in exchange for lower rates.
- **Savings plans**
  - Offer flexibility across eligible compute usage.
  - Better when instance families or sizes may change over time.
  - Useful when you want discounting without locking into a specific resource shape.

Example CLI to view reservation-related options is service-specific, so verify the exact command for the resource type you are studying. In exams, focus on the concept:
- Use a commitment discount when utilization is stable.
- Avoid commitments when demand is uncertain or short-lived.
- Reassess commitments after major architecture changes.

**When to optimize code versus scale infrastructure**

Not every performance issue needs a bigger SKU. Sometimes the cheapest fix is in the application or data access layer.

- Optimize code when:
  - CPU is high due to inefficient algorithms.
  - Memory use grows because of leaks or oversized objects.
  - Database queries are chatty or unindexed.
- Scale infrastructure when:
  - The workload is already efficient but saturated.
  - You need more headroom quickly.
  - The platform offers a simple scale-up or scale-out path.
- Re-architect when:
  - The app is tightly coupled to a single instance.
  - State management prevents horizontal scaling.
  - Latency or availability requirements exceed the current design.

Example of a simple caching pattern:

```csharp
// Pseudocode
var cacheKey = $"product:{id}";
var product = await cache.GetAsync(cacheKey);

if (product == null)
{
    product = await repository.GetProductAsync(id);
    await cache.SetAsync(cacheKey, product, TimeSpan.FromMinutes(10));
}
```

This pattern often reduces database load more cheaply than increasing database tier immediately.

**Exam-style scenarios**

Use these patterns to reason through AZ-305 questions.

- A startup web app with unpredictable traffic and a small team:
  - Prefer PaaS over IaaS.
  - Use autoscale and managed services.
  - Avoid overcommitting to reserved capacity too early.
- A global e-commerce site with seasonal spikes:
  - Use Front Door or CDN for edge acceleration.
  - Use autoscale, caching, and zone redundancy.
  - Consider commitment discounts only for the predictable baseline.
- A line-of-business app with steady usage and strict latency:
  - Consider higher-tier PaaS or dedicated compute.
  - Use reservations or savings plans where eligible.
  - Optimize the database and storage tier before adding more compute.
- A legacy app that depends on custom OS components:
  - Start with IaaS.
  - Modernize later if cost and operations become too high.
  - Use monitoring to identify the best migration target.

**Practical validation checklist**

Before you finalize a design, validate both performance and cost assumptions.

1. Measure baseline CPU, memory, latency, IOPS, and queue depth.
2. Identify whether the bottleneck is compute, storage, database, or network.
3. Compare service tiers and SKUs for the required feature set.
4. Estimate the impact of scaling out versus scaling up.
5. Include network egress, replication, and HA costs in the estimate.
6. Apply tags, budgets, and policy controls for governance.
7. Re-test after changes and compare against the baseline.

**Key takeaways**

- Cost and performance are linked through service model, tier, scaling, topology, and governance.
- PaaS often reduces operational cost even when direct platform cost is higher.
- Autoscale works best for stateless, metric-driven workloads.
- Network design can create hidden spend through egress, transit, and multi-region traffic.
- Higher availability and resilience usually increase cost, but they may be required by the business.
- Use budgets, tags, and policy to control spend before making larger architectural changes.
