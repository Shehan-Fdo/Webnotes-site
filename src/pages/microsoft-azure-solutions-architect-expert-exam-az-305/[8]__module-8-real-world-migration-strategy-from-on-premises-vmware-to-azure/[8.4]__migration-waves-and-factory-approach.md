## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**Migration waves and the factory approach**

A migration program is easier to control when you break it into **waves** and run repeatable work through a **factory** model. A wave is a planned batch of applications, servers, databases, or workloads that move together based on dependency, risk, business priority, and readiness. The factory approach standardizes the steps, roles, tooling, and quality gates so each wave follows the same predictable process.

This model matters because large migrations fail when every workload is treated as a one-off project. A factory reduces variability, improves throughput, and makes it easier to measure progress, manage risk, and reuse patterns across many workloads.

- **Wave** planning groups similar workloads for coordinated migration.
- **Factory** execution turns migration into a repeatable operating model.
- **Gates** prevent unready workloads from moving too early.
- **Standardization** reduces cost, rework, and operational drift.

**Core migration concepts**

A migration program usually starts with discovery and assessment, then moves into planning, execution, validation, and optimization. The factory approach does not replace these phases; it operationalizes them. You use the same intake, assessment, remediation, migration, and cutover pattern for each wave.

Common inputs to wave planning include:

- Application criticality and business owner approval
- Technical dependencies between systems
- Compliance and data residency requirements
- Infrastructure complexity and platform fit
- Downtime tolerance and cutover window
- Readiness of landing zones, identity, networking, and security controls

A good wave plan balances speed and safety. If you group too aggressively, a single dependency can delay many workloads. If you make waves too small, you lose the efficiency benefits of a factory.

**Why the factory approach works**

The factory approach borrows from manufacturing: define a standard process, create repeatable workstations, and measure output quality. In Azure migration programs, the “stations” are the teams and tools that handle assessment, remediation, migration, testing, and cutover.

Benefits include:

- **Predictability** through repeatable runbooks and templates
- **Scale** by processing many workloads with the same operating model
- **Quality** through consistent validation and sign-off
- **Visibility** with dashboards, backlog tracking, and wave status reporting
- **Governance** with clear approvals, controls, and exception handling

The factory model is especially useful when you migrate hundreds or thousands of servers or many application components with similar patterns.

**Typical factory roles**

A migration factory usually includes a mix of business, technical, and operational roles. Exact titles vary, but the responsibilities are consistent.

- **Program leadership**
  - Owns scope, schedule, budget, and executive reporting
  - Resolves cross-team blockers and prioritization conflicts
- **Wave planner**
  - Groups workloads into waves
  - Tracks dependencies, readiness, and cutover sequencing
- **Application owner**
  - Confirms business impact, testing, and acceptance criteria
- **Platform team**
  - Builds landing zones, identity, networking, policy, and monitoring
- **Migration engineers**
  - Execute replication, rehosting, refactoring, or data movement
- **Operations and support**
  - Handle post-cutover monitoring, incident response, and stabilization

A factory works best when responsibilities are explicit. If ownership is unclear, cutover risk increases and remediation slows down.

**Wave design principles**

Wave design is the discipline of deciding what moves together and in what order. You usually start with low-risk workloads to validate the process, then scale to more complex systems.

Key principles:

- **Dependency awareness**
  - Keep tightly coupled systems in the same wave or sequence them carefully
  - Avoid moving a front-end without its required back-end unless the architecture supports it
- **Business priority**
  - Move workloads that deliver early value or reduce risk
  - Align with business calendars and blackout periods
- **Technical similarity**
  - Group workloads with similar OS, database, network, or security patterns
  - Reuse the same migration pattern to reduce effort
- **Readiness**
  - Only include workloads with completed assessment, remediation, and approvals
- **Risk balancing**
  - Mix low-risk and moderate-risk workloads to maintain throughput without overloading the team

A common mistake is to plan waves only by server count. Server count is useful for capacity planning, but it does not capture application complexity or dependency risk.

**Factory lifecycle**

A migration factory often follows a repeatable lifecycle for each wave.

1. **Intake**
   - Collect workload inventory, owners, dependencies, and business requirements
   - Confirm migration strategy such as rehost, replatform, refactor, retire, or retain
2. **Assess**
   - Evaluate readiness, compatibility, and remediation needs
   - Identify blockers such as unsupported OS versions, hard-coded IPs, or legacy authentication
3. **Prepare**
   - Build or validate landing zones, network connectivity, identity integration, and governance controls
   - Create runbooks, test plans, and rollback plans
4. **Migrate**
   - Replicate data, move compute, or deploy target resources
   - Execute cutover during the approved window
5. **Validate**
   - Confirm application functionality, performance, security, and monitoring
   - Obtain business sign-off
6. **Stabilize**
   - Monitor incidents, tune performance, and close defects
   - Transition ownership to operations

This lifecycle is repeated for every wave, which is why standard templates and checklists are so valuable.

**Planning a wave backlog**

A wave backlog is the prioritized list of workloads waiting to migrate. It is similar to a product backlog, but the items are migration candidates rather than features.

Useful backlog fields include:

- Workload name and owner
- Business criticality
- Migration strategy
- Dependency list
- Estimated effort
- Required remediation
- Target wave
- Cutover window
- Status and blockers

A practical backlog helps you answer three questions quickly:

- What is ready now?
- What is blocked and why?
- What should be prepared next?

You can manage the backlog in Azure DevOps, Jira, ServiceNow, or a spreadsheet for smaller programs. The tool matters less than the discipline of keeping the data current.

**Example backlog structure**


**Landing zones and factory readiness**

The factory depends on a stable Azure foundation. Before you migrate at scale, you need a landing zone that supports identity, networking, governance, logging, and security.

Typical readiness items include:

- **Identity**
  - Microsoft Entra ID integration
  - Role-based access control (RBAC) assignments
  - Privileged access controls
- **Networking**
  - Hub-spoke or equivalent topology
  - DNS design
  - Connectivity through VPN or ExpressRoute
- **Governance**
  - Management groups, subscriptions, and policy assignments
  - Naming and tagging standards
  - Resource locks where appropriate
- **Security**
  - Defender for Cloud recommendations
  - Key management and secrets handling
  - Baseline logging and alerting
- **Operations**
  - Monitoring, backup, patching, and incident processes
  - Cost management and chargeback/showback

> If the landing zone is not ready, the factory becomes a bottleneck. Migration speed is limited by platform readiness, not by the number of engineers.

**Common Azure migration tooling**

Azure migration programs often combine discovery, assessment, replication, and governance tools. The exact mix depends on workload type.

- **Azure Migrate**
  - Discovery and assessment for servers, apps, and databases
  - Migration orchestration for supported scenarios
- **Azure Site Recovery**
  - Replication and disaster recovery-based migration for virtual machines
  - Useful for rehosting with controlled cutover
- **Azure Database Migration Service**
  - Database migration with reduced downtime for supported engines
  - Often used for replatforming data tiers
- **Azure DevOps**
  - Work item tracking, pipelines, and release coordination
- **Azure Monitor**
  - Post-cutover telemetry, alerts, and dashboards

Example Azure CLI commands for common migration-adjacent tasks:

```bash
# Create a resource group for migration tooling
az group create \
  --name rg-migration-tools \
  --location eastus

# Create a Log Analytics workspace for monitoring
az monitor log-analytics workspace create \
  --resource-group rg-migration-tools \
  --workspace-name law-migration

# Assign a Reader role to a migration operator at the resource group scope
az role assignment create \
  --assignee user@contoso.com \
  --role Reader \
  --scope /subscriptions/<subscription-id>/resourceGroups/rg-migration-tools
```

**Factory governance and quality gates**

Quality gates are checkpoints that prevent avoidable failures. They are especially important when many workloads move in parallel.

Typical gates include:

- **Assessment gate**
  - Inventory is complete
  - Dependencies are documented
  - Migration strategy is approved
- **Readiness gate**
  - Landing zone and connectivity are validated
  - Security and identity requirements are met
  - Rollback plan exists
- **Cutover gate**
  - Test results are acceptable
  - Business owner approves the window
  - Support staff are on standby
- **Stabilization gate**
  - Monitoring is healthy
  - Critical incidents are resolved or accepted
  - Ownership transfer is complete

A gate should be objective. Use measurable criteria such as test pass rate, replication health, or change approval status rather than vague “looks good” language.

**Example cutover checklist**

1. Confirm replication is current and healthy.
2. Freeze source-side changes if required.
3. Validate DNS, firewall, and routing updates.
4. Execute the cutover runbook.
5. Run smoke tests and business validation.
6. Monitor logs, metrics, and alerts for the agreed stabilization period.
7. Record issues, rollback decisions, and final sign-off.

**Patterns for different migration strategies**

The factory approach supports multiple migration strategies, but the workflow changes slightly depending on the target outcome.

| Strategy | Typical use | Factory emphasis |
|---|---|---|
| Rehost | Move VMs with minimal change | Replication, cutover, and validation |
| Replatform | Move with limited optimization | Compatibility checks and service mapping |
| Refactor | Modernize application components | Code changes, testing, and release coordination |
| Retire | Decommission unused workloads | Business approval and data retention |
| Retain | Keep on-premises or defer | Exception tracking and future planning |

Rehosted workloads usually move fastest because the application changes are minimal. Refactoring takes longer because the factory must coordinate development, testing, and deployment pipelines.

**Practical example: organizing a migration wave**

Suppose you have 120 servers and 18 applications. A sensible wave plan might look like this:

- **Wave 0**
  - Pilot workloads
  - Validate landing zone, tooling, and runbooks
- **Wave 1**
  - Low-risk internal apps with simple dependencies
  - Use the results to refine the factory process
- **Wave 2**
  - Medium-complexity apps with shared services
  - Increase parallel execution carefully
- **Wave 3**
  - Business-critical workloads with stricter cutover controls
  - Require stronger rollback and executive oversight

This sequence lets you learn early, reduce uncertainty, and build confidence before moving critical systems.

**Example runbook snippet**

```yaml
wave: 2
workload: crm-api
strategy: rehost
cutover_window: "2026-05-14T22:00:00Z"
prechecks:
  - replication_healthy
  - dns_change_approved
  - support_on_call
steps:
  - freeze_changes
  - stop_source_vm
  - finalize_replication
  - start_target_vm
  - update_dns
  - run_smoke_tests
rollback:
  - revert_dns
  - restart_source_vm
  - notify_stakeholders
```

**Metrics that matter**

A factory should be measured like an operating system, not just a project. Useful metrics include:

- **Throughput**
  - Number of workloads migrated per week or per wave
- **Lead time**
  - Time from intake to cutover
- **First-pass success rate**
  - Percentage of workloads that cut over without major rework
- **Defect rate**
  - Number of issues found during validation or stabilization
- **Rollback rate**
  - Frequency of failed cutovers requiring reversal
- **Readiness aging**
  - How long workloads remain blocked before migration

These metrics help you identify whether the bottleneck is assessment, platform readiness, application remediation, or cutover execution.

**Common risks and mitigations**

- **Hidden dependencies**
  - Mitigation: perform discovery, network flow analysis, and application owner interviews
- **Incomplete inventory**
  - Mitigation: reconcile CMDB, virtualization platform data, and cloud discovery results
- **Landing zone delays**
  - Mitigation: build the platform early and treat it as a separate workstream
- **Change fatigue**
  - Mitigation: schedule waves realistically and avoid overloading business owners
- **Inconsistent runbooks**
  - Mitigation: use templates, peer review, and version control
- **Weak rollback planning**
  - Mitigation: define rollback criteria before cutover and test the process where possible

**Exam focus**

For AZ-305, focus on the architectural reasoning behind the factory approach:

- Use **waves** to control risk and sequence migration work.
- Use a **factory** to standardize execution and scale delivery.
- Ensure **landing zone readiness** before large-scale migration.
- Choose migration strategy based on workload fit, not just speed.
- Apply **governance** and **quality gates** to reduce failure during cutover.
- Track metrics to improve the program over time.

The key design question is usually not “Can you migrate it?” but “How do you migrate many workloads safely, repeatedly, and with predictable outcomes?”
