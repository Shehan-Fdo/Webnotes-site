## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**Pilot migration and cutover planning**

Pilot migration is a controlled, limited-scope move of a small workload slice to Azure so you can validate architecture, operations, security, performance, and user experience before full production migration. Cutover planning is the coordinated sequence that moves the remaining users, data, and dependencies to the target environment with minimal downtime and a clear rollback path.

Use pilot migrations to reduce uncertainty. They help you verify assumptions about identity, networking, application compatibility, data synchronization, monitoring, and support readiness. Use cutover planning to turn those findings into a repeatable go-live runbook.

**Why pilot first**

A pilot gives you evidence instead of estimates. It lets you test the migration pattern under realistic conditions without exposing the entire business to risk.

- **Scope control**: Move one app, one business unit, one region, or a low-risk subset of users.
- **Dependency discovery**: Identify hidden integrations, hard-coded endpoints, DNS assumptions, and firewall rules.
- **Operational validation**: Confirm monitoring, alerting, backup, patching, and support processes.
- **Performance validation**: Compare latency, throughput, and scaling behavior against the source environment.
- **Change readiness**: Measure user impact, training gaps, and support ticket volume before broad rollout.

A good pilot is representative enough to reveal real issues, but small enough that you can recover quickly if something fails.

**Common pilot migration patterns**

Choose the pilot pattern based on workload type, risk tolerance, and migration method.

- **Lift-and-shift pilot**: Move a small VM-based workload to Azure with minimal code changes.
- **Rehost with replication**: Use continuous replication tools to keep source and target in sync until cutover.
- **Replatform pilot**: Move a subset of the app to a managed service such as Azure App Service, Azure SQL Database, or Azure Kubernetes Service (AKS) to validate modernization assumptions.
- **Hybrid pilot**: Keep some components on-premises while moving front-end or noncritical services to Azure.
- **Canary-style pilot**: Route a small percentage of traffic to the Azure target and compare behavior with the source.

For exam scenarios, the best pilot pattern is usually the one that minimizes business risk while still validating the highest-risk dependencies.

**What to validate during a pilot**

A pilot should test more than whether the workload starts. Validate the full operating model.

- **Identity and access**: Microsoft Entra ID authentication, role assignments, managed identities, and conditional access.
- **Networking**: IP ranges, DNS resolution, private endpoints, routing, load balancers, and firewall rules.
- **Application behavior**: Session handling, file paths, local storage assumptions, and service-to-service calls.
- **Data consistency**: Replication lag, schema compatibility, transaction behavior, and backup/restore.
- **Observability**: Azure Monitor, Log Analytics, Application Insights, and alert routing.
- **Security controls**: Key Vault access, encryption, NSGs, Defender for Cloud, and policy compliance.
- **Operations**: Deployment automation, patching, scaling, incident response, and support handoff.

A pilot is successful when the team can operate the workload in Azure with acceptable risk, not just when the app launches.

**Planning inputs before you migrate**

Before you schedule a pilot, collect the information needed to design the migration and the cutover.

- **Business requirements**
  - Recovery time objective (RTO): how long the service can be unavailable.
  - Recovery point objective (RPO): how much data loss is acceptable.
  - Maintenance window: when you can perform the cutover.
  - Success criteria: what must be true for the pilot to be considered complete.
- **Technical inventory**
  - Servers, databases, storage, middleware, and external dependencies.
  - Network flows, ports, certificates, and DNS records.
  - Authentication methods and secrets.
  - Licensing and compliance constraints.
- **Operational readiness**
  - Support model, escalation path, and ownership.
  - Monitoring dashboards and alert thresholds.
  - Backup strategy and restore testing.
  - Rollback plan and decision authority.

A migration plan without these inputs often fails during cutover because the team discovers missing dependencies too late.

**Pilot design principles**

Keep the pilot realistic, measurable, and reversible.

- **Representative**: Include the riskiest dependency or user path, not just the easiest component.
- **Limited blast radius**: Restrict the number of users, data sets, or transactions affected.
- **Measurable**: Define metrics such as response time, error rate, replication lag, and support tickets.
- **Reversible**: Preserve the source environment and data synchronization so you can roll back.
- **Time-boxed**: Set a clear start, end, and review period.

> If the pilot cannot be rolled back quickly, it is too large or too early for a pilot.

**Cutover planning fundamentals**

Cutover is the point where production traffic, users, or data ownership moves from source to target. The goal is to minimize downtime and avoid split-brain behavior.

A strong cutover plan includes:

- **Pre-cutover checks**: Confirm readiness, replication health, backups, and approvals.
- **Freeze window**: Stop schema changes, code releases, or configuration drift before the move.
- **Final sync**: Replicate the last changes from source to target.
- **Traffic switch**: Update DNS, load balancers, routing rules, or connection strings.
- **Validation**: Test critical user journeys and system health immediately after the switch.
- **Rollback decision point**: Define the time or condition that triggers fallback.
- **Post-cutover monitoring**: Watch logs, metrics, and support tickets closely.

Cutover is not just a technical event. It is a coordinated business change with explicit ownership and communication.

**Typical cutover sequence**

Use a runbook with clear owners and timestamps.

1. Confirm the change window, approvers, and communication channels.
2. Verify backups, snapshots, and replication status.
3. Freeze nonessential changes in source systems.
4. Drain or stop inbound traffic to the source environment.
5. Perform the final data synchronization.
6. Switch DNS, traffic manager, load balancer, or application endpoints.
7. Validate authentication, application health, and data access.
8. Monitor telemetry and user reports for stabilization.
9. Decide whether to keep the source as fallback or decommission it later.

This sequence varies by workload, but the principle stays the same: stop drift, synchronize, switch, validate, and monitor.

**Azure services commonly involved**

Different migration scenarios use different Azure services and control points.

- **Azure Migrate**: Discovery, assessment, server migration, and dependency mapping.
- **Azure Site Recovery**: Replication and disaster recovery-style failover for VMs.
- **Azure Database Migration Service**: Database migration with reduced downtime for supported engines.
- **Azure App Service deployment slots**: Staged release and swap-based cutover for web apps.
- **Azure Traffic Manager**: DNS-based traffic distribution and failover.
- **Azure Front Door**: Global HTTP(S) routing, health probes, and gradual traffic shifting.
- **Azure Load Balancer**: Layer 4 traffic control for internal or external endpoints.
- **Azure DNS**: Record updates during endpoint changes.
- **Azure Monitor and Application Insights**: Health and performance validation after cutover.

Choose the service that matches the traffic model. DNS-based cutover is simple but can be affected by TTL (time to live) caching. Load balancer or Front Door changes can provide faster control and better health-based routing.

**Traffic switching options**

The right switch mechanism depends on the application architecture.

| Method | Best for | Strengths | Tradeoffs |
|---|---|---|---|
| DNS cutover | Web apps, public endpoints | Simple, widely supported | TTL delays, client caching |
| Load balancer update | Layer 4 or internal traffic | Fast control, predictable routing | Requires compatible architecture |
| Front Door / Traffic Manager | Global web workloads | Health-based routing, gradual shift | More design complexity |
| Connection string change | Databases or backend services | Direct and explicit | Requires app restart or redeploy |
| App Service slot swap | Web apps on App Service | Fast rollback, low downtime | Not all app settings should swap |
| Feature flag rollout | Application-level control | Fine-grained exposure | Requires app support |

For exam questions, prefer the method that best matches the workload and minimizes downtime while preserving rollback options.

**DNS and TTL considerations**

DNS cutovers often fail because teams underestimate caching.

- Lower the TTL well before cutover if you control the record.
- Verify that clients, resolvers, and intermediate caches honor the new value.
- Expect some users to continue hitting the old endpoint until caches expire.
- Keep the source environment available long enough to absorb late traffic.

Example DNS update with Azure CLI:

```bash
az network dns record-set a update \
  --resource-group rg-dns \
  --zone-name contoso.com \
  --name app \
  --set ttl=60
```

Example record change:

```bash
az network dns record-set a add-record \
  --resource-group rg-dns \
  --zone-name contoso.com \
  --record-set-name app \
  --ipv4-address 20.50.10.25
```

**Database cutover considerations**

Database migrations often determine the cutover window because they are sensitive to consistency and downtime.

- **Online replication**: Keep source and target synchronized until the final switch.
- **Schema compatibility**: Ensure the application can run against the target schema version.
- **Write freeze**: Stop writes long enough to capture the final delta.
- **Validation queries**: Compare row counts, key business tables, and critical aggregates.
- **Transaction handling**: Confirm that in-flight transactions are completed or safely retried.

Example Azure Database Migration Service pattern:

```bash
az dms project create \
  --resource-group rg-migration \
  --location eastus \
  --name sql-project \
  --source-platform SQL \
  --target-platform AzureSqlDatabase
```

For large databases, plan for log shipping, continuous replication, or staged synchronization rather than a single bulk copy at cutover time.

**VM migration and failover pattern**

For virtual machines, a common approach is replication followed by test failover and planned failover.

- **Test failover**: Validate the VM in an isolated network without affecting production.
- **Planned failover**: Perform a controlled switch during a maintenance window.
- **Commit**: Finalize the migration after validation.
- **Reprotect**: If needed, reverse replication for rollback or disaster recovery.

Example Azure Site Recovery-style workflow:

```bash
az recoveryservices vault create \
  --resource-group rg-dr \
  --name vault01 \
  --location eastus
```

The important concept is that replication reduces downtime, but you still need a cutover runbook to coordinate the final switch and validation.

**Application cutover with deployment slots**

For web applications on Azure App Service, deployment slots support staged release and quick rollback.

- Deploy the new version to a staging slot.
- Warm up the app and validate dependencies.
- Swap staging into production during the cutover window.
- Keep the previous version in the other slot for rollback.

Example:

```bash
az webapp deployment slot create \
  --resource-group rg-app \
  --name contoso-web \
  --slot staging
```

Slot swaps are useful when you want to reduce downtime and avoid redeploying during the cutover itself.

**Rollback planning**

Rollback is not a failure plan; it is a required part of cutover design.

A rollback plan should define:

- **Trigger conditions**: Error rate, failed validation, data mismatch, or user impact threshold.
- **Decision owner**: Who can authorize rollback.
- **Rollback steps**: How to restore traffic, data, and service state.
- **Data handling**: What happens to writes that occurred after the switch.
- **Communication**: How you notify stakeholders and support teams.

> Rollback becomes much harder after new writes land in the target system. If the workload is not designed for bidirectional sync, keep the rollback window short.

**Validation checklist after cutover**

Immediately after the switch, validate the most important business paths first.

- **Availability**
  - Application responds on the expected endpoint.
  - Health probes return success.
  - Authentication works for representative users.
- **Functionality**
  - Core transactions complete successfully.
  - Reports, uploads, and integrations work.
  - Background jobs and queues process normally.
- **Data**
  - New writes appear in the target system.
  - Key records match expected values.
  - Replication or sync has stopped or is no longer needed.
- **Operations**
  - Alerts are routed correctly.
  - Logs and metrics are visible.
  - Support teams can access the right dashboards.

A short validation script can help standardize checks:

```bash
curl -f https://app.contoso.com/health
nslookup app.contoso.com
az monitor metrics list \
  --resource /subscriptions/<subId>/resourceGroups/rg-app/providers/Microsoft.Web/sites/contoso-web \
  --metric Requests
```

**Communication and governance**

Migration success depends on coordination as much as technology.

- **Stakeholders**: Business owners, app owners, infrastructure teams, security, service desk, and leadership.
- **Change management**: Use approved change records and maintenance windows.
- **Status updates**: Send pre-cutover, start, validation, and completion messages.
- **Escalation path**: Define who handles incidents during the hypercare period.
- **Hypercare**: Increase monitoring and support coverage after go-live.

A clear communication plan reduces confusion when users see temporary issues or delayed DNS propagation.

**Practical cutover runbook template**

Use a runbook that is specific enough to execute under pressure.

1. Confirm scope, window, and approvers.
2. Verify backups, replication, and monitoring.
3. Freeze source changes.
4. Execute final sync.
5. Switch traffic.
6. Run smoke tests.
7. Compare key metrics to baseline.
8. Decide continue, rollback, or hold.
9. Document results and next actions.

Keep the runbook short enough to follow in real time, but detailed enough that a different engineer can execute it.

**Common risks and mitigations**

- **Hidden dependencies**
  - Mitigation: Use dependency mapping, packet capture, and application logs.
- **DNS propagation delays**
  - Mitigation: Lower TTL early and keep source online during transition.
- **Data loss during final sync**
  - Mitigation: Freeze writes and verify replication lag before switch.
- **Authentication failures**
  - Mitigation: Test Entra ID, certificates, secrets, and managed identities in the pilot.
- **Performance regression**
  - Mitigation: Benchmark before and after, then scale or tune the target.
- **Rollback complexity**
  - Mitigation: Keep the source intact and avoid irreversible changes until validation passes.

**Exam focus**

For AZ-305, expect questions that ask you to choose the best migration approach, traffic switch method, or validation strategy.

- Prefer a **pilot** when you need to reduce risk and validate assumptions before full migration.
- Prefer **continuous replication** when downtime must be minimized.
- Prefer **DNS or traffic manager cutover** when you need controlled traffic redirection.
- Prefer **deployment slots** for web app releases with fast rollback.
- Prefer **test failover** before planned failover for VM-based workloads.
- Include **rollback** and **post-cutover validation** in every serious migration plan.

The best answer usually balances business continuity, technical feasibility, and operational readiness rather than simply choosing the fastest migration path.

**Quick reference**

- **Pilot migration**: Small-scale production-like move used to validate the design.
- **Cutover**: The controlled switch from source to target production.
- **Rollback**: Reverting to the source or previous state if validation fails.
- **Hypercare**: Elevated support and monitoring immediately after go-live.
- **RPO**: Maximum acceptable data loss.
- **RTO**: Maximum acceptable downtime.
