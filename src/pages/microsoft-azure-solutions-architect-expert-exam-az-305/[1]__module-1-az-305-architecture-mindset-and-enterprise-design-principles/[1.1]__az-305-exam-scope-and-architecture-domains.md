## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**AZ-305 exam scope**

AZ-305 measures your ability to design Azure solutions, not to implement every service in depth. You are expected to choose the right architecture for a business requirement, justify tradeoffs, and recognize when a design is secure, resilient, cost-aware, and operationally manageable.

The exam focuses on architecture decisions across multiple domains:

- **Governance and compute**: subscriptions, management groups, policy, identity, and workload placement.
- **Networking**: connectivity, routing, DNS, load balancing, and private access.
- **Security**: identity, secrets, encryption, threat protection, and access control.
- **Data**: storage, databases, analytics, integration, and migration patterns.
- **Business continuity**: backup, disaster recovery, failover, and recovery testing.

A useful mindset is to ask:
- What is the business goal?
- What are the constraints?
- Which Azure service best fits the requirement?
- What tradeoff am I accepting?

> AZ-305 is architecture-first. If a question asks for the “best” design, you usually need to optimize for the stated requirement, not for maximum feature count.

**Official skill area weighting**

The exam weighting can change over time, so always verify the current Microsoft exam page before scheduling. For study planning, the common AZ-305 domains are:

| Domain | Typical focus |
|---|---|
| Design identity, governance, and monitoring solutions | Tenant structure, subscriptions, policy, RBAC, monitoring, and operational visibility |
| Design data storage solutions | Storage accounts, databases, analytics, replication, and data movement |
| Design business continuity solutions | Backup, restore, high availability, and disaster recovery |
| Design infrastructure solutions | Compute, networking, hybrid connectivity, and application architecture |

Study time should roughly follow the weighting published by Microsoft, but also your own experience. If you are weak in networking or governance, spend more time there because those areas often drive the best-answer choice.

**Azure architecture thinking**

AZ-305 questions often test whether you can connect requirements to architecture patterns. You should be comfortable with these decision drivers:

- **Availability**: keep services online during failures.
- **Scalability**: handle growth in users, traffic, or data.
- **Performance**: reduce latency and improve throughput.
- **Security**: protect identities, data, and network paths.
- **Cost**: avoid overprovisioning and unnecessary redundancy.
- **Operability**: make the solution supportable, observable, and automatable.
- **Compliance**: meet regulatory, residency, and audit requirements.

A strong answer usually balances these factors instead of maximizing one at the expense of the others.

**Azure landing zone reference architecture**

The Azure landing zone is the standard starting point for enterprise-scale Azure design. It provides a structured way to organize governance, identity, networking, and subscriptions before workloads are deployed.

Key landing zone ideas:
- **Management groups** create a hierarchy above subscriptions.
- **Subscriptions** separate billing, access, and policy boundaries.
- **Policy** enforces standards such as allowed regions, tagging, and private networking.
- **Identity** is centralized through Microsoft Entra ID.
- **Networking** is designed for hub-and-spoke or virtual WAN patterns.
- **Monitoring** and security are centralized for visibility and control.

Use landing zones when:
- You need multiple teams or workloads.
- You need consistent governance.
- You expect future scale or mergers/acquisitions.
- You want a repeatable enterprise pattern.

A simple management group layout might look like this:

```text
Tenant Root Group
├── Platform
│   ├── Identity
│   ├── Connectivity
│   └── Management
├── LandingZones
│   ├── Corp
│   └── Online
└── Sandbox
```

**Subscription design patterns**

Subscriptions are not just billing containers. They are also governance and isolation boundaries. In AZ-305, you should know when to split workloads across subscriptions.

Common patterns:
- **Platform subscription** for shared services such as networking, identity integration, and monitoring.
- **Application subscription** per workload or environment.
- **Environment-based subscriptions** for dev, test, and production.
- **Business-unit subscriptions** when chargeback or autonomy matters.
- **Regulatory boundary subscriptions** when compliance or data separation is required.

Use separate subscriptions when you need:
- Different access control boundaries.
- Different policy enforcement.
- Independent billing or chargeback.
- Reduced blast radius.
- Clear separation between production and nonproduction.

Do not split subscriptions unnecessarily if:
- The workload is small.
- Shared management would become harder.
- Cross-subscription networking and policy overhead outweighs the benefit.

**Tenant and directory considerations**

Microsoft Entra ID is the identity plane for Azure. Tenant design matters when you have multiple organizations, mergers, or external collaboration.

Important considerations:
- **Single tenant** is simplest for centralized governance.
- **Multi-tenant** may be required for separate legal entities or acquisitions.
- **Cross-tenant access** supports collaboration without fully merging directories.
- **B2B** (business-to-business) collaboration lets external users access resources with controlled identity.
- **B2C** is for consumer-facing identity scenarios and is a different design problem.

Design questions often ask whether to:
- Keep workloads in one tenant and separate with subscriptions.
- Use multiple tenants for isolation.
- Use cross-tenant access for collaboration.
- Centralize identity while delegating resource ownership.

**Identity and access architecture**

Identity is the first security boundary in Azure. You should understand how authentication, authorization, and privileged access fit together.

Core concepts:
- **Microsoft Entra ID** handles authentication and directory services.
- **RBAC** (role-based access control) authorizes actions on Azure resources.
- **PIM** (Privileged Identity Management) provides just-in-time elevation for supported privileged roles and scopes.
- **Managed identities** let Azure resources authenticate without stored secrets.
- **Conditional Access** enforces sign-in controls such as MFA and device compliance.

Use managed identities when:
- An app needs to call Azure services.
- You want to avoid secrets in code or configuration.
- You need a secure, automated identity for workloads.

Example: assign a system-assigned managed identity to a web app and grant it access to Key Vault.

```bash
az webapp identity assign \
  --resource-group rg-app \
  --name app-prod

az role assignment create \
  --assignee-object-id <principal-id> \
  --assignee-principal-type ServicePrincipal \
  --role "Key Vault Secrets User" \
  --scope /subscriptions/<sub-id>/resourceGroups/rg-sec/providers/Microsoft.KeyVault/vaults/kv-prod
```

**Governance architecture**

Governance ensures that Azure stays consistent as it grows. AZ-305 expects you to know how to design guardrails, not just how to deploy resources.

Main governance tools:
- **Management groups** for hierarchy and inheritance.
- **Azure Policy** for compliance and enforcement.
- **RBAC** for access control.
- **Tags** for organization, cost tracking, and automation.
- **Locks** for preventing accidental deletion or modification.

Policy is especially important for:
- Allowed locations.
- Required tags.
- Denying public IPs.
- Requiring private endpoints.
- Enforcing SKU restrictions.
- Auditing encryption or diagnostic settings.

Example policy assignment:

```bash
az policy assignment create \
  --name deny-public-ip \
  --display-name "Deny Public IPs" \
  --scope /subscriptions/<sub-id> \
  --policy /providers/Microsoft.Authorization/policyDefinitions/<policy-definition-id>
```

If you need to assign an initiative, use `--policy-set-definition` instead of a single policy definition.

A practical governance pattern:
1. Define standards at the management group level.
2. Apply policy assignments to subscriptions or resource groups.
3. Use exemptions sparingly and document them.
4. Monitor compliance continuously.

**Microsoft Defender for Cloud**

Microsoft Defender for Cloud is the security posture management and threat protection platform for Azure. It helps you assess configuration risk and protect workloads.

Use it for:
- Secure score and recommendations.
- Regulatory compliance views.
- Threat detection for supported resources.
- Just-in-time VM access in supported scenarios.
- Adaptive hardening guidance.

Defender for Cloud is not a replacement for architecture design. It complements secure design by showing where controls are missing or weak.

**Security architecture**

Security design in AZ-305 spans identity, secrets, encryption, network controls, and workload protection.

Key design areas:
- **Least privilege**: grant only the access required.
- **Segmentation**: isolate workloads by network, subscription, or resource group.
- **Secrets management**: store secrets in Key Vault, not in code.
- **Encryption**: protect data at rest and in transit.
- **Threat protection**: use Defender and logging to detect issues.
- **Privileged access**: use PIM and break-glass accounts carefully.

Key Vault patterns:
- Store secrets, keys, and certificates centrally.
- Use managed identities for access.
- Prefer private access when the workload is network-isolated.
- Separate vaults by environment or sensitivity when needed.

Encryption guidance:
- **At rest**: Azure encrypts many services by default, but some workloads require customer-managed keys (CMK).
- **In transit**: use TLS (Transport Layer Security) for service endpoints, APIs, and client connections.
- **Double encryption** may be required in regulated environments, but it adds complexity and cost.

> Do not assume every privileged role should be permanently assigned through PIM. PIM support and activation behavior depend on the role, scope, and tenant configuration.

**Network security architecture**

AZ-305 often tests whether you can choose the right control at the right layer.

Compare these controls:

| Control | Best use |
|---|---|
| NSG | Subnet or NIC-level traffic filtering inside a virtual network |
| ASG | Grouping VMs for simpler NSG rules |
| Azure Firewall | Centralized, stateful network filtering and egress control |
| DDoS Protection | Protection against volumetric attacks on public endpoints |
| WAF | Application-layer protection for HTTP/S traffic |
| Private Endpoint | Private access to PaaS services over a VNet |
| Service Endpoint | Secure service access from a subnet, but still uses the service’s public endpoint |

Use **NSGs** for basic segmentation. Use **ASGs** when you want rules based on workload groups instead of IP addresses. Use **Azure Firewall** when you need centralized inspection, logging, and egress control. Use **WAF** when the threat is web application attacks such as SQL injection or cross-site scripting.

**Private endpoints vs service endpoints**

Private endpoints are usually the stronger design choice when you need private connectivity to PaaS services.

- **Private Endpoint**
  - Gives the service a private IP in your VNet.
  - Keeps traffic on private networking.
  - Requires DNS planning.
  - Is preferred for strict isolation.

- **Service Endpoint**
  - Extends VNet identity to the service.
  - Traffic still reaches the public service endpoint.
  - Is simpler in some cases.
  - Is less isolated than private endpoints.

Use private endpoints when:
- Public access must be disabled.
- You need tighter network isolation.
- You want a private-only architecture.

Use service endpoints when:
- You need a simpler design.
- The service supports the endpoint and the scenario is acceptable.
- You do not need full private IP exposure.

Example private endpoint creation pattern:

```bash
az network private-endpoint create \
  --name pe-storage \
  --resource-group rg-net \
  --vnet-name vnet-hub \
  --subnet snet-private-endpoints \
  --private-connection-resource-id /subscriptions/<sub-id>/resourceGroups/rg-data/providers/Microsoft.Storage/storageAccounts/stprod \
  --group-ids blob \
  --connection-name pe-storage-conn
```

**DNS design**

DNS is a common source of architecture mistakes. Private connectivity only works well if name resolution is designed correctly.

Important patterns:
- Use **Azure Private DNS zones** for private endpoint name resolution.
- Link private DNS zones to the VNets that need resolution.
- In hybrid environments, forward DNS queries between on-premises and Azure as needed.
- Ensure custom DNS servers can resolve Azure private zones.

Common private endpoint DNS flow:
1. Client resolves the service name.
2. DNS returns the private endpoint IP.
3. Traffic stays inside the private network path.

If DNS is wrong, the client may still resolve the public endpoint and bypass the intended design.

**Hybrid connectivity**

AZ-305 expects you to compare VPN, ExpressRoute, and virtual WAN based on latency, bandwidth, reliability, and operational needs.

| Option | Best for | Tradeoff |
|---|---|---|
| Site-to-site VPN | Lower-cost hybrid connectivity | Internet-based, less predictable latency |
| Point-to-site VPN | Individual user or admin access | Not ideal for large-scale site connectivity |
| ExpressRoute | Private, predictable enterprise connectivity | Higher cost and provider dependency |
| Virtual WAN | Large-scale branch and hub connectivity | More abstraction and design complexity |

Use **ExpressRoute** when:
- You need private connectivity to Azure.
- You need predictable performance.
- You have enterprise networking requirements.

Use **VPN** when:
- Cost matters more than performance.
- You need a simpler or temporary connection.
- You are building a smaller hybrid footprint.

Use **virtual WAN** when:
- You have many branches or global connectivity needs.
- You want centralized routing and connectivity management.
- You need a scalable hub model.

**Peering vs VPN vs ExpressRoute**

- **VNet peering** connects Azure VNets privately and with low latency.
- **VPN** connects networks over the internet.
- **ExpressRoute** connects through a private circuit.

Choose peering when:
- Both networks are in Azure.
- You need low-latency east-west traffic.
- You want simple private connectivity between VNets.

Choose VPN or ExpressRoute when:
- You need on-premises connectivity.
- You need cross-site or branch connectivity.
- You need a dedicated enterprise circuit.

**Load balancing and application delivery**

AZ-305 questions often ask you to choose the right load balancing service.

| Service | Layer | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Azure Load Balancer | Layer 4 | TCP/UDP distribution, VM-based workloads |
| Application Gateway | Layer 7 | HTTP/S routing, TLS termination, WAF |
| Front Door | Global Layer 7 | Global entry point, acceleration, failover |
| Traffic Manager | DNS-based | Global routing by DNS response |
| Azure Firewall | Network security | Filtering, not load balancing |

Use **Application Gateway** when you need:
- Path-based routing.
- Host-based routing.
- TLS termination.
- Web Application Firewall integration.

Use **Front Door** when you need:
- Global HTTP/S entry.
- Fast failover across regions.
- Edge acceleration and global routing.

Use **Traffic Manager** when:
- DNS-based routing is sufficient.
- You want simple global endpoint selection.
- You do not need proxy-based features.

Use **Load Balancer** when:
- You need non-HTTP traffic distribution.
- You are balancing VM backends at Layer 4.

**Multi-region design**

Multi-region architecture is about balancing availability, latency, and cost.

Common patterns:
- **Active/passive**: one region serves traffic, another waits for failover.
- **Active/active**: multiple regions serve traffic simultaneously.
- **Pilot light**: minimal resources in the secondary region.
- **Warm standby**: scaled-down but ready secondary environment.

Choose active/passive when:
- Simplicity matters.
- You can tolerate failover time.
- You want lower cost.

Choose active/active when:
- You need higher availability.
- You can handle data synchronization complexity.
- Your application supports global traffic distribution.

**Storage architecture**

Storage design depends on durability, access patterns, and recovery requirements.

Key storage choices:
- **Blob Storage** for unstructured data, backups, and static content.
- **Azure Files** for SMB/NFS file shares.
- **Queue Storage** for simple messaging.
- **Table Storage** for NoSQL key-value patterns.
- **Managed disks** for VM storage.

Replication options:
- **LRS** (Locally Redundant Storage): copies within one datacenter.
- **ZRS** (Zone-Redundant Storage): copies across availability zones.
- **GRS** (Geo-Redundant Storage): copies to a paired region.
- **GZRS** (Geo-Zone-Redundant Storage): combines zone and geo redundancy.
- **RA-GRS / RA-GZRS**: read access to the secondary region for read-only workloads or reporting.

> Read access on RA-GRS and RA-GZRS is for read-only scenarios and reporting. It is not the same as active failover or a substitute for application resilience design.

Use ZRS when:
- You want zone-level resilience in a region that supports zones.
- You need higher availability than LRS.

Use GRS or GZRS when:
- You need regional disaster recovery.
- You want asynchronous replication to a paired region.

**Database architecture**

Database choices depend on workload type, consistency, scale, and operational model.

Common services:
- **Azure SQL Database** for managed relational workloads.
- **Azure SQL Managed Instance** for near-full SQL Server compatibility.
- **Azure Database for PostgreSQL** and **MySQL** for open-source relational workloads.
- **Azure Cosmos DB** for globally distributed NoSQL and multi-model scenarios.
- **SQL Server on Azure VM** when you need full OS and instance control.

Read replicas are mainly for:
- Read scaling.
- Offloading reporting queries.
- Some disaster recovery patterns, depending on the service.

Do not assume read replicas automatically provide failover. Promotion and regional resilience depend on the specific database service and configuration.

Example: Azure SQL failover group conceptually supports regional failover for supported databases.

```bash
az sql failover-group create \
  --name fog-prod \
  --partner-server sql-secondary \
  --resource-group rg-data \
  --server sql-primary \
  --failover-policy Automatic \
  --grace-period 1
```

Use failover groups when:
- You need managed geo-replication for Azure SQL.
- You want a simpler failover story than custom orchestration.

Use Cosmos DB when:
- You need global distribution.
- You need low-latency reads across regions.
- Your data model fits NoSQL or multi-model patterns.

**Analytics and data integration**

AZ-305 can include data platform design, especially when the question asks how to move, transform, or analyze data.

Service selection:
- **Azure Data Factory** for orchestration and data movement.
- **Azure Synapse Analytics** for integrated analytics, warehousing, and big data scenarios.
- **Azure Databricks** for Spark-based engineering, advanced transformations, and collaborative analytics.
- **Event Hubs** for streaming ingestion.
- **Stream Analytics** for real-time stream processing.

Choose Data Factory when:
- You need pipeline orchestration.
- You are moving data between systems.
- You need scheduled ETL/ELT workflows.

Choose Synapse when:
- You need a data warehouse or analytics platform.
- You want SQL-based analytics and integrated pipelines.
- You are designing a centralized analytics estate.

Choose Databricks when:
- You need advanced Spark processing.
- You have data engineering or machine learning workflows.
- You need flexible notebook-based collaboration.

A common analytics flow:
1. Ingest data from operational systems.
2. Land raw data in storage.
3. Transform data with pipelines or Spark.
4. Serve curated data to BI or downstream apps.

**Business continuity and disaster recovery**

Business continuity is broader than backup. It includes resilience, failover, recovery objectives, and validation.

Key terms:
- **RTO** (Recovery Time Objective): how long recovery can take.
- **RPO** (Recovery Point Objective): how much data loss is acceptable.
- **Backup**: restore data or systems to a prior point.
- **Failover**: switch to a secondary system or region.
- **Failback**: return to the primary site after recovery.

Design principles:
- Protect data first.
- Automate recovery where possible.
- Test recovery regularly.
- Document dependencies such as DNS, identity, and secrets.

Backup and restore patterns:
- Use Azure Backup for supported VMs, files, and workloads.
- Use native database backups or managed service backup features.
- Keep backup retention aligned with compliance and recovery needs.
- Validate restore procedures, not just backup success.

Failover orchestration:
- Use traffic management to redirect users.
- Ensure data replication is ready.
- Confirm DNS TTL values support timely cutover.
- Test application startup order and dependency recovery.

A practical DR checklist:
1. Define RTO and RPO for each workload.
2. Choose the recovery pattern: backup/restore, active/passive, or active/active.
3. Replicate data and configuration.
4. Automate failover steps where possible.
5. Run recovery tests and document results.

**Monitoring and operational design**

Monitoring is part of architecture, not an afterthought. AZ-305 expects you to design for observability.

Core tools:
- **Azure Monitor** for metrics, logs, and alerts.
- **Log Analytics** for centralized log queries.
- **Application Insights** for application telemetry.
- **Diagnostic settings** for sending resource logs to a destination.

Example diagnostic settings pattern:

```bash
az monitor diagnostic-settings create \
  --name diag-app \
  --resource /subscriptions/<sub-id>/resourceGroups/rg-app/providers/Microsoft.Web/sites/app-prod \
  --workspace /subscriptions/<sub-id>/resourceGroups/rg-monitor/providers/Microsoft.OperationalInsights/workspaces/law-prod \
  --logs '[{"category":"AppServiceConsoleLogs","enabled":true},{"category":"AppServiceHTTPLogs","enabled":true}]' \
  --metrics '[{"category":"AllMetrics","enabled":true}]'
```

Use diagnostic settings when:
- You need resource logs in Log Analytics, Storage, or Event Hubs.
- You want centralized troubleshooting and auditing.
- You need alerting and long-term retention.

**Infrastructure as Code**

AZ-305 does not test coding depth, but you should understand how infrastructure as code supports repeatable architecture.

Common tools:
- **Bicep** for Azure-native declarative deployment.
- **ARM templates** for JSON-based deployments.
- **Terraform** for multi-cloud or platform-agnostic workflows.

Bicep example:

```bicep
param location string = resourceGroup().location
param appName string
param planName string

resource plan 'Microsoft.Web/serverfarms@2023-12-01' = {
  name: planName
  location: location
  sku: {
    name: 'P1v3'
    tier: 'PremiumV3'
  }
}

resource app 'Microsoft.Web/sites@2023-12-01' = {
  name: appName
  location: location
  properties: {
    serverFarmId: plan.id
    httpsOnly: true
  }
}
```

Use IaC when:
- You need repeatability.
- You want version control and review.
- You need consistent environments.
- You want to reduce manual drift.

**Scenario-based decision patterns**

These patterns help you answer exam questions quickly.

- If the requirement is **private access to PaaS**, prefer private endpoints.
- If the requirement is **global HTTP/S routing**, consider Front Door.
- If the requirement is **regional web routing with WAF**, consider Application Gateway.
- If the requirement is **simple VM traffic distribution**, consider Load Balancer.
- If the requirement is **enterprise hybrid connectivity**, consider ExpressRoute.
- If the requirement is **policy enforcement**, use Azure Policy.
- If the requirement is **secret storage**, use Key Vault with managed identities.
- If the requirement is **read scaling for a database**, consider replicas where supported.
- If the requirement is **regional failover for managed SQL**, consider failover groups.
- If the requirement is **analytics orchestration**, consider Data Factory.
- If the requirement is **big data analytics**, consider Synapse or Databricks based on the workload.

**Common exam traps**

- Choosing the most feature-rich service instead of the simplest service that meets the requirement.
- Confusing **service endpoints** with **private endpoints**.
- Treating **RA-GRS** as active availability instead of read-only secondary access.
- Assuming **read replicas** automatically solve failover.
- Using **resource group location** as a design rule instead of a deployment convenience.
- Overlooking DNS when private networking is involved.
- Using permanent privileged access when just-in-time access is more appropriate.
- Ignoring operational needs such as logging, alerting, and recovery testing.

**Study approach**

To prepare effectively:
1. Learn the exam domains and how they map to Azure services.
2. Practice scenario questions that force tradeoff decisions.
3. Review landing zone, governance, and identity first.
4. Build a mental model for networking and private access.
5. Compare data services by workload type and recovery behavior.
6. Rehearse business continuity patterns with RTO and RPO in mind.
7. Validate your understanding with CLI, portal, and IaC examples.

A good final review is to explain each service in one sentence:
- What problem does it solve?
- When do you choose it?
- What is the main tradeoff?

That habit matches the way AZ-305 asks questions.
