## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**Architecture tradeoff analysis**

Architecture tradeoff analysis is the process of comparing design options against business and technical requirements, then choosing the option that best balances competing goals. In Azure, you rarely optimize for only one dimension. A design that maximizes availability may increase cost and operational complexity. A design that minimizes cost may reduce resiliency or performance. The architect’s job is to make those tradeoffs explicit and defensible.

A strong analysis starts with requirements:
- **Functional requirements** describe what the system must do.
- **Nonfunctional requirements** describe how well it must do it, such as availability, latency, security, and recoverability.
- **Constraints** include budget, compliance, region availability, legacy dependencies, and team skill.

A useful way to think about tradeoffs is to compare options across a consistent set of criteria:
- **Cost**: capital and operational expense, including compute, storage, networking, and licensing.
- **Availability**: ability to remain accessible during failures.
- **Resiliency**: ability to recover from component, zone, or region failures.
- **Performance**: latency, throughput, and scaling behavior.
- **Security**: identity, network isolation, data protection, and governance.
- **Operational complexity**: deployment, monitoring, patching, and incident response.
- **Scalability**: ability to handle growth without redesign.
- **Recoverability**: how quickly and completely you can restore service and data.

**How to structure the analysis**

A practical tradeoff analysis usually follows this pattern:
1. Identify the workload goals and constraints.
2. Define the candidate architectures.
3. Score each option against the same criteria.
4. Validate assumptions with Azure service behavior.
5. Choose the option that best fits the workload, not the one with the most features.

Use a decision matrix when you need to compare multiple options objectively.

| Criterion | Option A | Option B | Option C |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Cost | Low | Medium | High |
| Availability | Medium | High | High |
| Complexity | Low | Medium | High |
| Recovery time objective (RTO) | High | Medium | Low |
| Recovery point objective (RPO) | High | Medium | Low |

A matrix does not make the decision for you, but it makes the tradeoff visible. For exam scenarios, the best answer is often the one that satisfies the stated requirement with the least unnecessary complexity.

**Core decision dimensions**

**Cost**

Cost includes more than the monthly bill. You should consider:
- Compute size and uptime
- Storage redundancy and transaction costs
- Network egress and cross-region traffic
- Managed service premiums
- Licensing and support
- Human cost of operating the solution

A cheaper design may be acceptable when:
- The workload is noncritical.
- Downtime is tolerable.
- Recovery can be manual.
- Traffic is predictable and low.

A more expensive design may be justified when:
- The workload is revenue critical.
- The business requires low RTO and low RPO.
- Compliance requires stronger controls.
- The cost of downtime exceeds infrastructure savings.

**Availability and resiliency**

Availability is about keeping the service accessible. Resiliency is about surviving failures and restoring service. These are related but not identical.

Azure design choices that improve resiliency include:
- Availability Zones for datacenter-level fault isolation
- Zone-redundant services where supported
- Multiple instances behind a load balancer
- Geo-redundant storage for data durability
- Cross-region failover patterns for disaster recovery

Be precise about scope:
- A **zone** failure affects a datacenter group within a region.
- A **region** failure affects the entire Azure region.
- A **service** failure may affect only one platform component.

> Do not assume that backup alone is a disaster recovery architecture for low-RTO workloads. Backup protects data, but restore time may be too slow for business continuity requirements.

**Performance**

Performance tradeoffs often involve latency versus distribution. For example:
- Keeping compute and data in the same region reduces latency.
- Replicating data across regions improves resilience but can increase write latency or complexity.
- Caching improves response time but introduces consistency considerations.

Common performance decisions:
- Use **Azure Front Door** for global HTTP(S) acceleration and edge routing.
- Use **Azure Traffic Manager** for DNS-based global traffic distribution.
- Use **Azure Load Balancer** for regional Layer 4 distribution.
- Use **Azure Cache for Redis** to reduce repeated data access latency.
- Use **read replicas** or read-only patterns when read scale matters more than immediate write consistency.

**Security**

Security tradeoffs often involve usability and operational overhead. Stronger isolation can reduce attack surface but increase management effort.

Examples:
- Private endpoints improve network isolation but require DNS planning.
- Managed identities reduce secret handling but require correct role assignments.
- Just-in-time access improves security but adds operational steps.
- Customer-managed keys increase control but add key management responsibility.

A secure design should follow least privilege and defense in depth, but not every workload needs the most restrictive pattern. Match the control to the risk.

**Operational complexity**

Complexity is a real cost. A design with many moving parts can be harder to deploy, monitor, patch, and troubleshoot.

Complexity increases with:
- Multi-region active-active designs
- Custom failover logic
- Multiple identity and network boundaries
- Self-managed clusters or appliances
- Manual runbooks for frequent operations

Prefer managed services when they meet the requirement. For example:
- Use Azure SQL Database or Azure SQL Managed Instance instead of self-managed SQL Server when platform features fit.
- Use Azure App Service or Azure Container Apps when you do not need full cluster control.
- Use platform-native backup and monitoring where possible.

**Scalability**

Scalability is the ability to handle growth. It can be:
- **Vertical scaling**: larger instance sizes
- **Horizontal scaling**: more instances
- **Elastic scaling**: automatic adjustment based on demand

Tradeoffs:
- Vertical scaling is simple but has size limits and may require downtime.
- Horizontal scaling improves resilience and throughput but requires stateless design or shared state management.
- Elastic scaling reduces manual effort but may increase cost variability.

**Recoverability**

Recoverability is about restoring service and data after failure. It is often described using:
- **RTO**: Recovery Time Objective, the maximum acceptable downtime
- **RPO**: Recovery Point Objective, the maximum acceptable data loss

Lower RTO and RPO usually require:
- More replication
- More automation
- More cost
- More operational planning

**Common Azure architecture tradeoffs**

**Single region versus multi-region**

Single-region designs are simpler and cheaper. They are often appropriate when:
- The workload can tolerate regional outage impact.
- The business accepts longer recovery times.
- The team wants lower operational complexity.

Multi-region designs improve disaster recovery and sometimes availability, but they add:
- Data replication complexity
- Traffic routing complexity
- Higher cost
- More testing requirements

Typical patterns:
- **Active-passive**: one region serves traffic, another stands by.
- **Active-active**: both regions serve traffic simultaneously.

Active-passive is usually simpler and cheaper. Active-active can provide better utilization and faster failover, but it is harder to design correctly, especially for stateful workloads.

**Availability Zones versus region pairs**

Availability Zones protect against datacenter failures within a region. They are a good fit when:
- The service supports zone redundancy.
- You need high availability without full multi-region complexity.

Region pairs are useful for disaster recovery and platform recovery planning. They are not a substitute for zone design, and zone design is not a substitute for regional DR.

**Managed service versus self-managed infrastructure**

Managed services reduce operational burden and often improve baseline reliability. Self-managed infrastructure gives more control but increases patching, scaling, and recovery work.

Choose managed services when:
- The service meets the workload requirements.
- You want to reduce administrative overhead.
- You need built-in HA, backups, or scaling.

Choose self-managed infrastructure when:
- You need unsupported OS or software features.
- You require deep customization.
- You must control the full stack.

**Synchronous versus asynchronous replication**

Synchronous replication improves data consistency and reduces data loss, but it can increase latency and reduce geographic flexibility. Asynchronous replication improves performance and distance tolerance, but it can lose recent writes during failover.

Use synchronous replication when:
- You need very low RPO.
- The replicas are close enough for acceptable latency.

Use asynchronous replication when:
- You need geographic separation.
- Some data loss is acceptable.
- You want better write performance.

**Load balancing and traffic routing choices**

Different Azure services solve different problems:
- **Azure Load Balancer** distributes TCP/UDP traffic regionally at Layer 4.
- **Application Gateway** provides Layer 7 routing, TLS termination, and web application firewall integration.
- **Front Door** provides global HTTP(S) routing and edge acceleration.
- **Traffic Manager** uses DNS to direct clients to endpoints.

Choose based on protocol, scope, and routing needs:
- Use Load Balancer for non-HTTP regional workloads.
- Use Application Gateway for web workloads needing path-based routing or WAF.
- Use Front Door for global web entry points.
- Use Traffic Manager when DNS-based failover is sufficient.

**Storage redundancy tradeoffs**

Azure storage redundancy options balance cost and durability:
- **LRS**: locally redundant storage, lowest cost, protects against local hardware failure.
- **ZRS**: zone-redundant storage, protects against zone failure within a region.
- **GRS**: geo-redundant storage, replicates to a paired region asynchronously.
- **GZRS**: combines zone and geo redundancy.

Higher redundancy improves durability and recovery options, but increases cost. Choose based on the workload’s data loss tolerance and recovery requirements.

**Practical examples**

**Example 1: E-commerce web app**

Requirements:
- High availability
- Low latency for global users
- Moderate budget
- Stateless web tier
- Shared database

Likely tradeoff choice:
- Use **Front Door** for global entry and acceleration.
- Use **App Service** or containerized web apps in two regions.
- Use a database with geo-replication or failover support.
- Keep session state externalized.

Why:
- Global routing improves user experience.
- Multi-region improves resilience.
- Managed services reduce operational burden.

**Example 2: Internal line-of-business app**

Requirements:
- Single-country user base
- Business-hours usage
- Limited budget
- Acceptable short outages

Likely tradeoff choice:
- Single region deployment
- Zone-redundant components where available
- Automated backups
- Basic monitoring and alerting

Why:
- Multi-region would add cost and complexity without enough business value.
- Zone redundancy gives a good balance of resilience and cost.

**Example 3: Regulated workload**

Requirements:
- Strong data protection
- Auditability
- Restricted network access
- Defined recovery objectives

Likely tradeoff choice:
- Private endpoints
- Managed identities
- Key Vault with strict access control
- Backup and restore testing
- Possibly zone or region redundancy depending on RTO/RPO

Why:
- Security and compliance may outweigh simplicity.
- Operational controls must be documented and testable.

**CLI and configuration examples**

Use Azure CLI to inspect service capabilities before making tradeoff decisions.

```bash
az account show
az group list --output table
az network lb list --resource-group rg-app --output table
az network application-gateway list --resource-group rg-app --output table
az front-door list --output table
az storage account show --name mystorageacct --resource-group rg-data --query "sku.name"
```

Check whether a service supports zone redundancy or regional deployment options before committing to a design.

```bash
az vm list-skus --location eastus --size Standard_D2s_v5 --output table
az storage account show-usage --location eastus
```

For cost analysis, use Azure Cost Management to inspect actual spend and trends.

```bash
az costmanagement query \
  --scope "/subscriptions/<subscription-id>" \
  --timeframe MonthToDate \
  --type ActualCost \
  --dataset '{
    "granularity": "Daily",
    "aggregation": {
      "totalCost": {
        "name": "PreTaxCost",
        "function": "Sum"
      }
    }
  }'
```

For policy-driven tradeoffs, use Azure Policy to enforce guardrails while allowing flexibility.

```bash
az policy assignment list --scope "/subscriptions/<subscription-id>" --output table
az policy definition list --query "[?policyType=='Custom'].[displayName,name]" --output table
```

A simple ARM template parameter can help compare deployment options without rewriting the whole template.

```json
{
  "$schema": "https://schema.management.azure.com/schemas/2019-04-01/deploymentTemplate.json#",
  "contentVersion": "1.0.0.0",
  "parameters": {
    "enableZoneRedundancy": {
      "type": "bool",
      "defaultValue": false
    }
  }
}
```

**How to evaluate a tradeoff**

When you compare two architectures, ask:
- Which requirement is mandatory?
- Which requirement is desirable but flexible?
- What failure mode is most likely?
- What is the cost of downtime?
- What is the cost of added complexity?
- Can Azure manage this for you?
- Can you test the recovery path regularly?

A good architecture is not the one with the most redundancy. It is the one that meets the business objective with acceptable cost and manageable operations.

**Exam-focused guidance**

For AZ-305, expect questions that ask you to choose between:
- Cost and resiliency
- Simplicity and availability
- Latency and consistency
- Security and usability
- Regional and global routing

Look for keywords in the scenario:
- **Low RTO** usually points toward active-active, zone redundancy, or automated failover.
- **Low cost** usually points toward single-region or simpler managed services.
- **Global users** often points toward Front Door or Traffic Manager.
- **Non-HTTP traffic** often points toward Load Balancer.
- **Strict isolation** often points toward private endpoints, NSGs, and managed identities.
- **Minimal administration** often points toward PaaS over IaaS.

**Common mistakes**

- Treating backup as equivalent to high availability.
- Choosing multi-region when zone redundancy is sufficient.
- Choosing the most secure option without considering operational feasibility.
- Ignoring data consistency when designing active-active systems.
- Using a global routing service when the workload only needs regional load balancing.
- Overlooking DNS, identity, and data replication dependencies.

**Decision checklist**

Before finalizing a design, verify:
- The workload’s RTO and RPO.
- The acceptable budget range.
- The expected traffic pattern.
- The failure domains you must survive.
- The operational skills available.
- The compliance and data residency requirements.
- The Azure service limits and regional availability.

**Summary**

Architecture tradeoff analysis is the discipline of making design choices explicit and defensible. In Azure, the best solution usually balances availability, cost, performance, security, and complexity rather than maximizing one dimension. For exam scenarios, choose the simplest architecture that satisfies the stated requirements, then add resilience or security only where the business need justifies it.
