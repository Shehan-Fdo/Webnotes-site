## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**Requirements gathering in Azure architecture**

Requirements gathering is the bridge between business intent and technical design. In AZ-305 scenarios, you are rarely asked to build a solution from a blank slate; instead, you must interpret business goals, constraints, and risks, then choose Azure services and patterns that satisfy them.

A strong architecture answer shows that you can:
- identify stakeholders and their competing needs,
- elicit functional and nonfunctional requirements,
- prioritize tradeoffs,
- translate business language into measurable technical targets,
- document assumptions and decisions clearly.

The goal is not to collect every possible requirement. The goal is to collect the right requirements, validate them, and turn them into an architecture that is secure, resilient, scalable, compliant, and cost-aware.

**Stakeholder analysis**

Stakeholder analysis tells you who influences the solution, who uses it, and who approves it. Different stakeholders often optimize for different outcomes, so you need to surface those differences early.

Common stakeholder groups include:
- **Business sponsors** who care about cost, time to market, and business outcomes.
- **Application owners** who care about feature delivery, performance, and maintainability.
- **Operations teams** who care about supportability, monitoring, and incident response.
- **Security and compliance teams** who care about identity, data protection, auditability, and policy enforcement.
- **Data owners** who care about classification, retention, sovereignty, and lifecycle control.
- **Network teams** who care about connectivity, routing, DNS, and segmentation.
- **End users** who care about latency, availability, and usability.

A practical stakeholder analysis asks:
- Who approves the architecture?
- Who funds it?
- Who operates it?
- Who is impacted if it fails?
- Who owns the data?
- Who can accept risk?

When stakeholders conflict, you do not guess. You document the conflict, quantify the impact where possible, and escalate for decision-making. For example, security may require private endpoints and restricted egress, while application teams may prefer faster delivery with public access. The architecture should reflect the approved tradeoff, not the loudest opinion.

**Requirement elicitation techniques**

Requirement elicitation is the process of drawing out needs that are often incomplete, implicit, or contradictory. In exam scenarios, you should recognize which technique fits the situation and what kind of information it produces.

Common techniques include:
- **Interviews** for detailed, role-specific needs and hidden constraints.
- **Workshops** for cross-functional alignment, dependency discovery, and conflict resolution.
- **Questionnaires** for collecting input from many users or distributed teams quickly.
- **Reviewing existing documentation** such as current architecture diagrams, runbooks, incident reports, SLAs, policies, audit findings, and migration plans.
- **Observation and shadowing** for understanding real operational workflows and pain points.
- **Prototyping** for validating assumptions about user experience, integration, or performance.

Use each technique intentionally:
- Use interviews when you need depth from a specific stakeholder.
- Use workshops when multiple teams must agree on scope or priorities.
- Use questionnaires when you need broad input with limited time.
- Use documentation review when you need evidence of current-state behavior, constraints, or technical debt.

A useful workshop agenda often includes:
1. Business goals and success criteria.
2. Current-state pain points.
3. Functional requirements.
4. Nonfunctional requirements.
5. Constraints and assumptions.
6. Risks and open questions.
7. Prioritization and next actions.

**Functional and nonfunctional requirements**

Functional requirements describe what the solution must do. Nonfunctional requirements describe how well it must do it and under what constraints.

Functional examples:
- Users must authenticate with Microsoft Entra ID.
- The application must process orders from a web front end.
- Data must replicate from on-premises SQL Server to Azure SQL Managed Instance.
- Branch offices must reach internal applications through secure connectivity.

Nonfunctional examples:
- The service must be available 99.9% of the time.
- Page response time must be under 200 ms for 95% of requests.
- Data must remain in a specific geography.
- Recovery time objective must be 30 minutes.
- The solution must support 10,000 concurrent users.

In AZ-305, nonfunctional requirements often drive the architecture more strongly than functional ones. For example, a requirement for low latency may push you toward regional deployment, caching, or content delivery. A requirement for strict data residency may eliminate some global services or require region-specific storage and processing.

**Prioritizing requirements**

Not all requirements can be satisfied equally. Prioritization helps you decide what must be delivered first and what can be deferred or negotiated.

Common prioritization methods include:
- **MoSCoW**:
  - **Must have**: non-negotiable requirements.
  - **Should have**: important but not critical.
  - **Could have**: desirable if time and budget allow.
  - **Won't have**: explicitly out of scope for now.
- **Tradeoff analysis** to compare options across cost, performance, security, resilience, and complexity.
- **Weighted scoring** to rank requirements or solution options against agreed criteria.
- **Risk-based prioritization** to address the highest business or technical risks first.

When stakeholders disagree, resolve conflicts by:
- tying the decision to business outcomes,
- quantifying impact where possible,
- identifying the least risky compromise,
- documenting the final decision and rationale.

For example, if one team wants rapid migration and another requires zero downtime, you may choose a phased migration with parallel run, temporary coexistence, and a controlled cutover. That approach may cost more, but it reduces business risk.

**Translating business needs into technical requirements**

Translation is the core AZ-305 skill. You take a business statement and convert it into measurable technical requirements that can guide service selection and design.

Examples:
- “The app must be always available” becomes:
  - target availability percentage,
  - multi-zone or multi-region design,
  - health probes and failover strategy,
  - backup and restore objectives.
- “Users complain the app is slow” becomes:
  - latency targets,
  - throughput expectations,
  - concurrency estimates,
  - caching or scaling requirements.
- “We need to be compliant” becomes:
  - data classification,
  - encryption requirements,
  - audit logging,
  - retention and deletion policies,
  - access control and policy enforcement.

A good translation produces measurable targets, not vague statements. Instead of “fast,” define response time, throughput, and concurrency. Instead of “secure,” define identity, network, encryption, logging, and governance controls.

**SLAs, SLOs, and SLIs**

<abbr title="Service Level Agreement">SLA</abbr>, <abbr title="Service Level Objective">SLO</abbr>, and <abbr title="Service Level Indicator">SLI</abbr> are related but distinct.

- **SLA** is the contractual or formal commitment to a customer.
- **SLO** is the internal target you design and operate against.
- **SLI** is the measured metric that shows whether you are meeting the objective.

A practical chain looks like this:
- Business need: “The customer portal must be highly available.”
- SLA: 99.9% monthly availability.
- SLO: 99.95% internal service availability to provide buffer.
- SLI: percentage of successful requests over time, measured by synthetic tests and application telemetry.

Other useful SLIs include:
- request latency,
- error rate,
- queue depth,
- CPU and memory saturation,
- replication lag,
- backup success rate,
- restore success rate.

These metrics influence Azure design decisions:
- Higher availability targets may require availability zones, zone-redundant services, or multi-region failover.
- Latency SLOs may require regional placement, traffic routing, caching, or edge delivery.
- Error-rate SLOs may require retries, circuit breakers, and health-based routing.
- Backup and restore SLOs may require more frequent backups and tested recovery procedures.

> A service can meet an SLA on paper and still fail the business if the SLOs and SLIs do not reflect real user experience.

**Data requirements**

Data requirements go beyond security. They shape storage choice, replication, governance, migration, and lifecycle management.

Key data considerations include:
- **Classification**: identify whether data is public, internal, confidential, regulated, or highly sensitive.
- **Retention**: define how long data must be kept for business, legal, or regulatory reasons.
- **Sovereignty**: determine where data must reside and whether it can cross borders.
- **Lifecycle**: define creation, active use, archival, deletion, and legal hold stages.
- **Archival**: decide what data can move to lower-cost tiers and when.
- **Migration**: determine how data moves from on-premises or another cloud, including cutover, synchronization, and validation.
- **Backup and restore**: define backup frequency, retention period, restore point objectives, and test frequency.

Examples:
- Financial records may require long retention and immutable storage controls.
- Personal data may require regional storage and strict access logging.
- Historical logs may be moved to archive tiers after a defined period.
- Large databases may require staged migration with replication and validation before cutover.

Data requirements also affect service selection:
- Use Azure SQL Database or Azure SQL Managed Instance when relational compatibility matters.
- Use Azure Blob Storage for unstructured data and tiering.
- Use Azure Data Lake Storage for analytics-oriented large-scale data.
- Use Azure Backup and Recovery Services vaults for backup governance.
- Use Azure Site Recovery for workload replication and disaster recovery.

**Capacity, sizing, and service limits**

Capacity planning turns business growth assumptions into concrete sizing decisions. If you ignore capacity, you risk underprovisioning, throttling, or expensive redesign later.

Estimate:
- **Throughput**: requests per second, transactions per second, messages per minute, or data ingestion rate.
- **Storage growth**: current size, daily growth, retention impact, and archive volume.
- **Concurrency**: number of simultaneous users, sessions, or jobs.
- **Peak patterns**: seasonal spikes, batch windows, and failover load.
- **Service limits and quotas**: subscription limits, SKU limits, API throttles, and regional capacity constraints.

Practical questions:
- How many users are active at peak?
- How much data is generated per day?
- What is the expected growth over 12 to 36 months?
- What happens during month-end processing or marketing campaigns?
- Which Azure service quotas might block scale-out?

Example translation:
- Business need: “Support 50,000 users during peak sales.”
- Technical requirements:
  - estimate concurrent users, not just total users,
  - size App Service, AKS, or VM scale sets accordingly,
  - use autoscaling rules,
  - validate database throughput and connection limits,
  - consider caching and queue-based buffering.

For Azure design, capacity planning often leads to:
- horizontal scaling instead of vertical scaling,
- partitioning or sharding for data-heavy workloads,
- asynchronous processing for bursty workloads,
- pre-warming or reserved capacity for predictable peaks,
- quota checks before deployment.

**Business continuity and disaster recovery**

Business continuity planning is broader than RTO and RPO.

Important concepts:
- **RTO** <abbr title="Recovery Time Objective">RTO</abbr>: maximum acceptable downtime.
- **RPO** <abbr title="Recovery Point Objective">RPO</abbr>: maximum acceptable data loss.
- **Backup retention**: how long backups must be kept.
- **Restore testing frequency**: how often you verify that backups can actually be restored.
- **Failover planning**: how you move traffic to a secondary site or region.
- **Failback planning**: how you return to the primary site after recovery.
- **Runbooks**: documented recovery steps for operators.

A strong design answer includes:
- backup frequency and retention policy,
- restore validation schedule,
- failover trigger criteria,
- manual versus automated failover,
- DNS or traffic manager behavior,
- data consistency considerations during failback.

Example:
- A customer-facing app with a 15-minute RTO and 5-minute RPO may need active-passive multi-region design, frequent backups, database replication, and tested failover automation.
- A reporting system with a 24-hour RTO may use simpler backup-based recovery and lower-cost storage.

**Security, identity, and network translation**

Security requirements often become architecture constraints. Translate them into identity, network, encryption, and governance controls.

Common translations:
- “Only employees can access the app” becomes Microsoft Entra ID authentication, conditional access, and role-based access control.
- “No public exposure” becomes private endpoints, private DNS, and restricted inbound access.
- “Traffic must stay on private networks” becomes VPN, ExpressRoute, or hub-and-spoke routing.
- “All data must be encrypted” becomes encryption at rest, TLS in transit, and key management requirements.
- “We need auditability” becomes centralized logging, immutable retention where needed, and access reviews.

Identity integration scenarios often include:
- on-premises Active Directory with Microsoft Entra ID synchronization,
- single sign-on for hybrid users,
- managed identities for Azure-to-Azure service authentication,
- role-based access control for least privilege.

Hybrid connectivity scenarios often require:
- site-to-site VPN for lower-cost connectivity,
- ExpressRoute for private, predictable connectivity,
- hub-and-spoke network design for shared services and segmentation,
- DNS integration for name resolution across environments.

**Common AZ-305 scenario translations**

Hybrid connectivity example:
- Business need: branch offices must access Azure-hosted line-of-business apps securely.
- Translation:
  - use site-to-site VPN for initial deployment or ExpressRoute for higher reliability and bandwidth,
  - centralize shared services in a hub VNet,
  - route branch traffic through the hub,
  - use private endpoints for PaaS access where appropriate,
  - define bandwidth and latency targets.

Identity integration example:
- Business need: employees should use existing corporate credentials.
- Translation:
  - integrate on-premises identity with Microsoft Entra ID,
  - enable single sign-on,
  - enforce MFA (multi-factor authentication) and conditional access,
  - use managed identities for application-to-database access,
  - avoid hard-coded secrets.

Migration from on-premises to Azure example:
- Business need: move a legacy app with minimal downtime.
- Translation:
  - assess dependencies and data gravity,
  - choose lift-and-shift, replatform, or refactor based on time and risk,
  - use Azure Migrate for discovery and assessment,
  - stage data replication and test cutover,
  - define rollback and validation steps,
  - plan for coexistence during transition.

A migration plan often includes:
1. Discover servers, apps, and dependencies.
2. Assess readiness, sizing, and compatibility.
3. Prioritize workloads by business value and complexity.
4. Choose migration method and target Azure services.
5. Pilot, validate, and refine.
6. Execute cutover and monitor.
7. Decommission or repurpose legacy resources.

**Documenting requirements and decisions**

Good architecture work is traceable. You should be able to show how each requirement influenced a design choice.

Useful documentation artifacts include:
- **Requirements traceability matrix** to map business needs to technical requirements, design decisions, and validation tests.
- **Architecture decision record** style notes to capture the decision, context, alternatives, and rationale.
- **Assumptions log** to record what you accepted as true.
- **Risk register** to track unresolved issues and mitigations.

A simple traceability matrix might look like this:

| Business need | Technical requirement | Design decision | Validation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 99.9% availability | Multi-zone deployment | Zone-redundant App Service and database | Failover test and uptime monitoring |
| Private access only | No public endpoints | Private Link and VPN | Connectivity test from branch network |
| 30-minute recovery | Fast restore and failover | Backup + secondary region replication | DR drill and restore test |

An architecture decision record can be as simple as:
- Context: why the decision is needed.
- Decision: what you chose.
- Alternatives: what you considered.
- Consequences: tradeoffs and risks.

This style is valuable in exam answers because it shows justification, not just service names.

**Practical Azure translation examples**

Example 1: e-commerce platform
- Business need: handle holiday traffic spikes without downtime.
- Technical translation:
  - autoscale web tier,
  - cache product catalog data,
  - use queue-based order processing,
  - size the database for peak write throughput,
  - define SLOs for checkout latency and error rate,
  - test failover before peak season.

Example 2: regulated healthcare workload
- Business need: protect patient data and meet retention rules.
- Technical translation:
  - classify data by sensitivity,
  - restrict access with least privilege,
  - use private networking,
  - encrypt data at rest and in transit,
  - define retention and deletion policies,
  - keep audit logs and validate restore procedures.

Example 3: manufacturing telemetry ingestion
- Business need: ingest millions of device events daily.
- Technical translation:
  - estimate event rate and burst size,
  - use event-driven ingestion,
  - partition storage for scale,
  - define retention and archive tiers,
  - monitor throughput and throttling,
  - plan for regional failover if production is time-sensitive.

**How to answer AZ-305 requirement questions**

When you see a scenario, work through it systematically:
1. Identify stakeholders and business goals.
2. Separate functional from nonfunctional requirements.
3. Extract constraints, assumptions, and risks.
4. Prioritize requirements using a method such as MoSCoW.
5. Translate vague needs into measurable targets.
6. Map requirements to Azure services and design patterns.
7. Document tradeoffs and justify the final choice.

A strong answer usually mentions:
- identity and access,
- network connectivity,
- data protection and lifecycle,
- availability and disaster recovery,
- capacity and scaling,
- monitoring and validation,
- cost and operational complexity.

**Exam-focused takeaways**

- Requirements gathering is not just asking questions; it is structured discovery, prioritization, and validation.
- Stakeholder conflicts are normal, so you must resolve them with business context and documented tradeoffs.
- Translate vague goals into measurable targets such as availability, latency, throughput, retention, and recovery objectives.
- Data requirements include classification, sovereignty, retention, archival, and migration, not just security.
- Capacity planning must account for growth, concurrency, throughput, and Azure service limits.
- Business continuity includes backup retention, restore testing, failover, and failback, not only RTO and RPO.
- Traceability matters: show how each requirement led to a specific architecture decision.
