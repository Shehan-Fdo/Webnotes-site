## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Solutions Architect Expert (Exam AZ-305)

**Role of the solutions architect in enterprise delivery**

A solutions architect turns business intent into a deliverable, supportable, and governable cloud solution. In enterprise delivery, the role is not limited to design; you also shape decision-making, manage trade-offs, and ensure the solution can move through review, build, test, release, and operations with clear ownership.

The architect typically works across business, security, platform, application, and operations teams. You translate requirements into architecture choices, define guardrails, validate nonfunctional requirements, and help the organization accept risk consciously rather than by accident.

**Enterprise delivery responsibilities**

The architect’s responsibilities usually span the full delivery lifecycle:

- **Discovery**: clarify business goals, constraints, assumptions, and success criteria.
- **Design**: produce target-state architecture, integration patterns, and deployment approach.
- **Governance**: align with standards, policies, and approval processes.
- **Validation**: prove the design meets functional and nonfunctional requirements.
- **Release readiness**: confirm operational, security, and support readiness before go-live.
- **Handover**: transfer ownership to the right service teams with documentation and controls.

In practice, you are often the person who keeps the solution coherent when multiple teams optimize for different goals. For example, a product team may want speed, a security team may want tighter controls, and operations may want simpler support. The architect balances these concerns and records the decision rationale.

**Requirements management and traceability**

Enterprise delivery depends on traceability from business need to technical implementation. You do not just collect requirements; you decompose them into testable technical requirements and map them to controls, tests, and deliverables.

A practical flow looks like this:

1. Capture business requirements in outcome-oriented language.
2. Decompose them into functional and nonfunctional requirements.
3. Convert them into architecture decisions, design constraints, and acceptance criteria.
4. Map each requirement to a control, test, or deliverable.
5. Maintain traceability through design, build, test, and release.

Examples of traceability:

- Business requirement: “Customer data must remain in-region.”
  - Technical requirement: use region-scoped services and restrict replication.
  - Control: policy assignment and deployment guardrails.
  - Test: verify resource locations and failover behavior.
  - Deliverable: architecture decision record and deployment template.

- Business requirement: “The app must support peak seasonal traffic.”
  - Technical requirement: define scale targets, autoscaling rules, and capacity thresholds.
  - Control: performance budget and scaling policy.
  - Test: load test and stress test.
  - Deliverable: performance validation report.

A simple traceability matrix can help:

| Business requirement | Technical requirement | Control / test | Deliverable |
|---|---|---|---|
| Data residency | Region-bound deployment | Policy + deployment validation | Architecture decision record |
| Availability | Multi-zone design | Failover test | Resilience test report |
| Security logging | Centralized audit logs | Log retention check | Monitoring standard |

**Governance, review boards, and approval gates**

Enterprise delivery often includes formal governance checkpoints. The architect prepares the solution for review and presents the rationale behind key decisions.

Common governance mechanisms include:

- **Architecture review board (ARB)**: a forum that reviews design alignment with enterprise standards, patterns, and risk posture.
- **Design authority**: a group or role that approves deviations from standards and resolves architectural disputes.
- **Formal approval gates**: stage-based checkpoints such as concept approval, design approval, build readiness, and go-live approval.
- **Exception process**: a documented path for accepting a deviation from policy or standard.

You should expect to provide evidence at each gate:

- business context and scope
- architecture diagrams and data flows
- security and compliance considerations
- dependency and risk analysis
- test strategy and validation results
- operational readiness and support model

> If a design cannot meet a standard, do not hide the gap. Document the exception, the compensating control, the risk owner, and the expiry or review date.

A typical exception workflow includes:

1. Identify the standard or policy being violated.
2. Describe the business justification.
3. Assess risk, impact, and compensating controls.
4. Obtain approval from the designated authority.
5. Record the exception in a tracked register.
6. Revisit the exception on a defined schedule.

**Shared responsibility, operating model, and ownership boundaries**

Enterprise solutions usually rely on a shared operating model between central platform teams and product teams. The architect helps define who owns what so the solution is supportable after go-live.

Typical boundaries include:

- **Central platform team**
  - landing zones
  - identity baseline
  - network connectivity
  - policy and guardrails
  - shared observability and logging
- **Product or application team**
  - application code
  - application configuration
  - data model and business logic
  - app-level monitoring
  - service-specific runbooks

The architect often helps define a RACI matrix, which stands for **Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed**. This clarifies ownership for build, deployment, incident response, patching, and change approval.

Example ownership questions you should settle early:

- Who owns the Azure subscription and policy assignments?
- Who approves firewall or private endpoint changes?
- Who responds to application alerts after hours?
- Who performs backup restore tests?
- Who owns service decommissioning?

A handoff is successful only when the receiving team can operate the service without relying on the project team for routine support.

**Architecture validation and proof of concept decisions**

Validation is more than functional testing. The architect verifies that the design works under realistic constraints and that the chosen pattern is justified.

Use validation activities to answer questions such as:

- Does the design meet the required availability and recovery objectives?
- Can the solution handle expected load and growth?
- Are latency, throughput, and dependency constraints acceptable?
- Do security controls work as intended without breaking the user experience?
- Are operational tasks feasible for the support team?

Common validation methods include:

- **Architecture review**: evaluate the design against requirements and standards.
- **Proof of concept (PoC)**: test a specific technology choice or integration.
- **Spike**: time-boxed investigation to reduce uncertainty.
- **Load and resilience testing**: verify performance and failure behavior.
- **Security validation**: confirm identity, access, logging, and segmentation controls.

Use a PoC when the risk is about feasibility. Use a spike when the risk is about uncertainty or missing information. Avoid building a full solution just to answer a narrow question.

Example decision:

- If you are unsure whether a managed service supports a required private connectivity pattern, run a PoC.
- If you need to compare two messaging patterns for latency and operational complexity, run a spike and document the trade-off.

**Nonfunctional requirements and design trade-offs**

Nonfunctional requirements (NFRs) shape architecture as much as functional requirements do. The architect must make them explicit and measurable.

Common NFRs include:

- availability
- performance
- scalability
- recoverability
- security
- maintainability
- observability
- compliance
- cost efficiency

You should convert vague statements into measurable targets. For example:

- “Fast response” becomes “95th percentile response time under 300 ms for the primary transaction.”
- “Highly available” becomes “99.9% service availability with defined failover behavior.”
- “Recover quickly” becomes “RTO of 1 hour and RPO of 15 minutes,” where **RTO** is recovery time objective and **RPO** is recovery point objective.

Trade-offs are unavoidable. The architect documents why one option is chosen over another:

- stronger isolation may increase cost and operational overhead
- managed services may reduce maintenance but limit customization
- active-active designs improve resilience but increase complexity
- centralized control improves governance but can slow delivery

A good architecture decision record explains the options considered, the decision made, and the reason it best fits the business constraints.

**Change management, release governance, and production readiness**

Enterprise delivery usually includes formal change control. The architect helps ensure the release is safe, reversible, and supportable.

Important practices include:

- **CAB-style approval**: change advisory board review for significant production changes.
- **Production readiness review**: confirm monitoring, rollback, support, and security readiness before release.
- **Rollback governance**: define how to revert safely if deployment fails.
- **Release windows**: align deployment timing with business risk tolerance and support coverage.

Before go-live, verify:

- deployment automation is repeatable
- secrets and identities are in place
- monitoring and alerting are active
- backup and restore procedures are tested
- rollback steps are documented and feasible
- support teams know escalation paths
- change records and approvals are complete

Example production readiness checklist:

- [ ] Architecture approved
- [ ] Security sign-off complete
- [ ] Monitoring dashboards available
- [ ] Alerts routed to the correct team
- [ ] Rollback plan tested
- [ ] Runbooks published
- [ ] Service owner assigned
- [ ] CAB approval recorded

A release should not proceed if the team cannot explain how to detect failure, contain impact, and restore service.

**Monitoring, SLOs, SLIs, and SLAs**

Operational design should be driven by service objectives. The architect uses monitoring requirements to ensure the platform supports the business promise.

Key terms:

- **SLI**: service level indicator, a measured metric such as latency or error rate.
- **SLO**: service level objective, the target value for an SLI.
- **SLA**: service level agreement, the contractual or formal commitment to a customer.

The architect aligns telemetry with these objectives:

- define the critical user journeys
- choose metrics that reflect user experience
- set alert thresholds based on SLO burn rate or error budget consumption
- ensure logs, metrics, and traces support incident investigation

Example:

- SLI: successful checkout requests per minute
- SLO: 99.9% successful checkout availability over 30 days
- SLA: customer-facing commitment tied to service credits

Operational decisions should follow from these targets:

- If the SLO is strict, you may need multi-zone or multi-region resilience.
- If the SLA is customer-facing, you need stronger incident response and reporting.
- If the SLI is noisy, you may need better instrumentation before adding more alerts.

**Practical Azure delivery examples**

A solutions architect often uses Azure-native controls to enforce enterprise delivery decisions.

Examples include:

- **Azure Policy** to enforce location, tagging, SKU, and security standards
- **Management groups** to apply governance at scale
- **Role-based access control (RBAC)** to separate duties
- **Azure Monitor** and Log Analytics for observability
- **Azure Blueprints** concepts historically, though many organizations now prefer policy, templates, and landing zone patterns
- **Bicep** or ARM templates for repeatable deployment
- **Azure DevOps** or GitHub Actions for controlled release pipelines

Example policy assignment with Azure CLI:

```bash
az policy assignment create \
  --name enforce-location \
  --display-name "Enforce allowed locations" \
  --scope /subscriptions/<subscription-id> \
  --policy /providers/Microsoft.Authorization/policyDefinitions/<policy-definition-id>
```

Example Bicep snippet for a governed deployment:

```bicep
param location string = 'eastus'
param tags object = {
  environment: 'prod'
  owner: 'platform-team'
}

resource stg 'Microsoft.Storage/storageAccounts@2023-01-01' = {
  name: 'stg${uniqueString(resourceGroup().id)}'
  location: location
  tags: tags
  sku: {
    name: 'Standard_GRS'
  }
  kind: 'StorageV2'
}
```

Example Azure Monitor alert rule concept:

```json
{
  "criteria": {
    "allOf": [
      {
        "metricName": "Percentage CPU",
        "timeAggregation": "Average",
        "operator": "GreaterThan",
        "threshold": 80
      }
    ]
  }
}
```

**Scenario-based decision making**

Scenario 1: A business unit wants to launch quickly, but the platform team says the requested region is not approved.

- You assess the business urgency and the policy rationale.
- You check whether an approved region can meet latency and compliance needs.
- If not, you raise an exception request with compensating controls.
- You document the risk owner and the review date.
- You do not bypass governance informally.

Scenario 2: A team wants to deploy a new integration without a rollback plan.

- You require a rollback strategy before production approval.
- You verify whether the deployment is backward compatible.
- You ask for a production readiness review.
- If rollback is impossible, you may require a phased rollout or feature flag.

Scenario 3: Operations wants a single central monitoring team, but the product team needs app-specific alerts.

- You define shared platform telemetry and service-specific telemetry.
- You assign ownership for alert triage and escalation.
- You ensure the product team owns application-level remediation.
- You keep the central team focused on platform health and baseline controls.

**Common architect deliverables**

The architect usually produces artifacts that support delivery and governance:

- architecture diagrams
- decision records
- requirements traceability matrix
- risk register
- exception log
- test and validation plan
- production readiness checklist
- operational handoff document
- RACI matrix
- runbooks and support model

These deliverables are valuable because they make decisions visible, auditable, and repeatable. In enterprise environments, undocumented decisions often become operational risk.

**Exam focus reminders**

For AZ-305, focus on how the architect enables delivery, not just how Azure services work.

- connect requirements to design choices
- show how governance influences architecture
- explain ownership and handoff boundaries
- validate nonfunctional requirements with evidence
- use formal approval and exception workflows appropriately
- align monitoring and release practices with operational objectives

A strong answer usually shows that you can balance business value, technical feasibility, security, and operational support in one coherent design.
