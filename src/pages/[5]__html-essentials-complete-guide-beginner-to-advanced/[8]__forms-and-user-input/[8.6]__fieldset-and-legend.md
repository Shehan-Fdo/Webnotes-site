## **Course:** HTML Essentials: Complete Guide (Beginner to Advanced)

**Fieldset and legend basics**

- Use `<fieldset>` to group related form controls and their labels.
- Use `<legend>` as the caption for the grouped controls.
- Place the `<legend>` as the first child of the `<fieldset>`.
- Keep related inputs together when they belong to one form section.
- Use grouping to make the form easier to scan and understand.

**Why this structure matters**

- Grouping adds semantic meaning to a form section.
- A clear caption helps users understand what the controls are for.
- Screen readers can benefit from the grouped structure.
- Related controls become easier to manage and style as a unit.
- The pattern supports more usable and accessible forms.

**Basic markup pattern**

- Wrap the related controls in `<fieldset>`.
- Add a `<legend>` that describes the group.
- Put labels and inputs inside the fieldset.
- Keep the caption short and specific.
- Use the group for one topic, such as a preference or choice set.

**Example structure**

- A radio-button group can sit inside one `<fieldset>`.
- The `<legend>` can name the choice set.
- Each option can use its own `<label>` and `<input>`.
- The group stays visually and semantically connected.

**Practical notes**

- Use this pattern when a form has multiple related controls.
- Use it when you want a clear section boundary in a form.
- Use it when you need a caption for a control group.
- Keep the markup simple and consistent.
- Prefer semantic grouping over visual-only separation.

**Accessibility focus**

- Give the group a meaningful `<legend>`.
- Keep labels tied to their controls.
- Use the structure to support assistive technologies.
- Avoid splitting related controls across separate groups.
- Make the form’s purpose clear without relying on styling alone.
