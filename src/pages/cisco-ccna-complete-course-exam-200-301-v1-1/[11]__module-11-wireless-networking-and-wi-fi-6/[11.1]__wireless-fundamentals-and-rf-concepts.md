## **Course:** Cisco CCNA Complete Course (Exam 200-301 v1.1)

**Wireless networking overview**

Wireless LANs use radio frequency (RF) instead of copper or fiber to connect clients to the network. In a typical enterprise WLAN, a wireless client associates to an access point (AP), and the AP bridges traffic into the wired LAN or forwards it to a wireless controller depending on the design.

Wireless is fundamentally different from Ethernet:

- It is a **shared medium**, so multiple devices contend for airtime.
- It is usually **half-duplex**, so a device cannot transmit and receive at the same time on the same channel.
- Performance depends on signal quality, interference, channel width, client capability, and airtime contention, not just raw signal strength.

**RF basics**

RF is electromagnetic energy used to carry data over the air. The main concepts you need for CCNA are frequency, wavelength, power, attenuation, interference, and noise.

- **Frequency** is the number of wave cycles per second, measured in hertz (Hz).
- **Wavelength** is inversely related to frequency; higher frequency means shorter wavelength.
- **Power** is the strength of the transmitted signal.
- **Attenuation** is signal loss as the wave travels through air, walls, furniture, and other materials.
- **Noise** is unwanted energy that reduces signal quality.
- **Interference** is RF energy from other transmitters that overlaps or disrupts the channel.

A useful rule of thumb is that lower frequencies often propagate differently than higher frequencies, but range is not determined by frequency alone. Building materials, antenna design, transmit power, channel width, and local interference all matter.

**Wi-Fi generations and naming**

Wi-Fi generation labels are often used in study materials and troubleshooting discussions. The common mapping is:

| IEEE standard | Wi-Fi generation |
|---|---|
| 802.11a | Wi-Fi 2 |
| 802.11b | Wi-Fi 1 |
| 802.11g | Wi-Fi 3 |
| 802.11n | Wi-Fi 4 |
| 802.11ac | Wi-Fi 5 |
| 802.11ax | Wi-Fi 6 / 6E |

A few practical points:

- 802.11b/g/n commonly operate in 2.4 GHz.
- 802.11a/ac/ax commonly operate in 5 GHz.
- 802.11ax can also operate in 6 GHz when deployed as Wi-Fi 6E, subject to regulatory support and compatible hardware.

**2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, and 6 GHz bands**

Each band has tradeoffs in coverage, capacity, and interference.

- **2.4 GHz**
  - Better obstacle penetration than higher bands in many environments.
  - More crowded because of Bluetooth, microwaves, legacy Wi-Fi, and many IoT devices.
  - Only a few non-overlapping channels are practical in most regions.
- **5 GHz**
  - More channels and typically less congestion than 2.4 GHz.
  - Supports wider channels and higher throughput.
  - Some channels may require DFS (Dynamic Frequency Selection) depending on region.
- **6 GHz**
  - Large amount of additional spectrum for newer Wi-Fi deployments.
  - Requires compatible client and AP support.
  - Designed to reduce congestion and improve capacity in dense environments.

**Channel planning and overlap**

Channel planning is critical because wireless devices share airtime. In 2.4 GHz, channels overlap heavily if you choose adjacent channels.

- In most regions, channels **1, 6, and 11** are used because they do not overlap with each other under standard 20 MHz channel planning.
- Adjacent-channel interference occurs when nearby APs use overlapping channels.
- Co-channel interference occurs when multiple APs use the same channel and must share airtime.

Co-channel interference is not always as harmful as adjacent-channel interference, but too many APs on the same channel reduce throughput because clients must take turns using the medium.

> Channel availability and transmit power are affected by the regulatory domain. Country settings, local laws, and DFS requirements determine which channels and power levels are allowed.

**Regulatory domain and DFS**

Wireless design is constrained by regional rules.

- **Regulatory domain** defines which channels and transmit power levels are legal in a country or region.
- **DFS** helps avoid interference with radar systems on certain 5 GHz channels.
- If an AP detects radar on a DFS channel, it must vacate that channel and move to another allowed channel.

For exam prep, remember that channel planning is not just a technical preference; it is also a compliance issue.

**Wireless access method and contention**

Wi-Fi uses **CSMA/CA** (Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Avoidance), not CSMA/CD like classic Ethernet.

- A device listens before transmitting.
- If the channel is busy, it waits a random backoff period.
- This reduces the chance of collisions on a shared medium.
- Wireless cannot reliably detect collisions while transmitting, so it avoids them instead of detecting them after the fact.

This is why airtime is a scarce resource. Even a fast AP can feel slow if many clients compete for the same channel.

**SSID, BSSID, and association**

A wireless network name is the **SSID** (Service Set Identifier). A specific radio on an AP advertises a **BSSID** (Basic Service Set Identifier), usually tied to the AP radio MAC address.

- Clients scan for SSIDs.
- Clients choose a BSS on a specific band and channel.
- Association is the process of joining the AP.
- Authentication and encryption requirements must match on both sides before traffic flows.

A dual-band AP may advertise the same SSID on 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz, but the client still associates to one specific BSS on one band and channel at a time. Compatibility depends on supported PHY capabilities, security settings, and the client’s radio support.

**Wireless security fundamentals**

CCNA-level wireless security usually centers on WPA2, WPA3, PSK, and enterprise authentication.

- **WPA2** uses AES-based encryption with CCMP in common enterprise and personal deployments.
- **WPA3** improves security and is the newer standard, especially for personal networks and modern enterprise designs.
- **PSK** (Pre-Shared Key) uses a shared passphrase for all users.
- **Enterprise authentication** uses 802.1X with a RADIUS server for per-user authentication.

Key distinctions:

- **PSK**
  - Simpler to deploy.
  - Common in small offices and home networks.
  - Harder to manage securely at scale because everyone shares the same secret.
- **Enterprise**
  - Uses 802.1X authentication with a backend **RADIUS** server.
  - Supports individual credentials and better access control.
  - Better suited to organizations that need user-level accountability.

A typical enterprise flow is:

1. The client connects to the SSID.
2. The AP forwards authentication requests to the RADIUS server.
3. The RADIUS server validates the user or device.
4. The AP and client establish encryption keys and allow traffic.

**Antenna concepts**

Antennas shape how RF energy is transmitted and received.

- **Omnidirectional antennas** radiate broadly in a horizontal pattern and are common for general indoor coverage.
- **Directional antennas** focus energy in a specific direction and are useful for hallways, outdoor links, warehouses, and point-to-point designs.
- **Antenna gain** does not create power; it concentrates energy in a pattern, improving coverage in some directions while reducing it in others.
- **Polarization** refers to the orientation of the RF wave. Matching antenna polarization improves signal quality.

Placement matters as much as antenna type:

- Mount APs where coverage is needed, not hidden in cabinets or behind metal obstructions.
- Avoid placing APs near large sources of interference.
- Keep antennas oriented consistently when the design depends on polarization.
- Use directional antennas when you need to shape coverage and reduce spillover.

**Signal quality: RSSI, SNR, and throughput**

Do not confuse signal strength with usable performance.

- **RSSI** (Received Signal Strength Indicator) measures received power.
- **SNR** (Signal-to-Noise Ratio) measures how much stronger the signal is than the noise floor.
- **Throughput** is the actual data rate delivered to applications.

A client can show a decent RSSI but still perform poorly if noise or interference is high. SNR is often a better indicator of whether a client can sustain higher modulation and coding rates.

Wireless devices adapt data rates dynamically:

- Stronger, cleaner signals allow higher modulation and coding schemes.
- Weaker or noisier signals force lower data rates.
- Lower data rates improve reliability but reduce throughput.

**Practical design tradeoffs**

Wireless design is always a balance between coverage and capacity.

- Larger cells increase coverage but can increase contention.
- Smaller cells improve capacity by allowing more APs and more reuse, but they require careful channel planning.
- Higher channel widths can increase peak throughput but reduce the number of non-overlapping channels available.
- Dense environments often benefit from narrower channels and more APs rather than wide channels and fewer APs.

A common mistake is to increase transmit power too much. That can create sticky clients, poor roaming behavior, and excessive co-channel interference.

**Cisco wireless architecture concepts**

Cisco wireless deployments may use different architectures depending on platform and generation.

- **Autonomous APs** handle wireless functions locally.
- **Controller-based WLANs** centralize policy and management on a wireless LAN controller.
- **Cloud-managed systems** may use a cloud dashboard for configuration and monitoring.

For CCNA, focus on the concepts rather than memorizing one platform’s exact CLI. Commands vary by AP type, controller model, and IOS/IOS XE release.

**Practical configuration concepts**

A WLAN usually maps an SSID to a VLAN so wireless clients land in the correct Layer 2 segment.

Example concept for a controller-based design:

```text
SSID: CORP-WIFI
VLAN: 20
Security: WPA2-Enterprise
Authentication: 802.1X via RADIUS
```

A simple logical flow is:

1. Create the VLAN on the wired network.
2. Define the SSID/WLAN.
3. Map the SSID to the VLAN.
4. Configure security.
5. Ensure the AP or controller has trunk connectivity to carry the VLAN.

Example switch port toward an AP:

```cisco
interface GigabitEthernet1/0/10
 description AP uplink
 switchport mode trunk
 switchport trunk allowed vlan 10,20,99
 spanning-tree portfast trunk
```

Example VLAN creation:

```cisco
vlan 20
 name CORP-WIFI
```

Example router-on-a-stick or gateway subinterface for wireless clients:

```cisco
interface GigabitEthernet0/0.20
 encapsulation dot1Q 20
 ip address 192.168.20.1 255.255.255.0
```

These examples show the wired side of wireless integration, which is often what exam questions test: the SSID is wireless, but the client still needs a VLAN, gateway, and security policy on the wired network.

**Basic verification and troubleshooting**

Wireless troubleshooting usually starts with confirming the radio, channel, power, and client state.

Useful checks include:

- Verify the AP is broadcasting the expected SSID.
- Check which band and channel the AP uses.
- Confirm transmit power is reasonable for the design.
- Confirm the client is associated and authenticated.
- Check whether the client is on the intended VLAN.
- Look for interference, low SNR, or channel overlap.

Example verification commands vary by platform, but common Cisco-style checks may include:

```cisco
show interface status
show vlan brief
show ip interface brief
show running-config interface
```

On wireless controller or wireless-capable platforms, you may also see platform-specific commands for:

- AP summary
- Client summary
- WLAN/SSID summary
- Radio status
- Neighbor or rogue AP information

Because these commands are platform dependent, always match the command to the device type you are using.

**Common wireless troubleshooting symptoms**

- **Low RSSI**
  - Client is too far from the AP or blocked by obstacles.
  - Solution: improve placement, add APs, or adjust antenna type.
- **Low SNR**
  - Noise or interference is high.
  - Solution: change channel, reduce overlap, remove interference sources.
- **Slow throughput**
  - Airtime contention, wide-area interference, or low data rates may be the cause.
  - Solution: optimize channel plan, reduce cell size, and verify client capability.
- **Frequent disconnects**
  - Roaming issues, power problems, or security mismatches may be involved.
  - Solution: verify authentication, coverage overlap, and AP stability.

**Real-world CLI examples**

These examples are illustrative and may differ by platform.

Check wireless client association on a controller-based system:

```cisco
show wireless client summary
show wireless client mac-address 0011.2233.4455
```

Check AP status:

```cisco
show ap summary
show ap name AP-01 config general
show ap name AP-01 dot11 5ghz
```

Check WLAN/SSID status:

```cisco
show wlan summary
show wlan id 10
```

Check radio and channel details:

```cisco
show ap name AP-01 config 802.11a
show ap name AP-01 config 802.11b
```

If your platform uses different syntax, use the equivalent command set for that controller or IOS XE wireless implementation.

**Exam-focused takeaways**

- Wireless uses a shared, half-duplex medium and relies on CSMA/CA.
- 2.4 GHz commonly uses channels 1, 6, and 11 to avoid overlap in most regions.
- Co-channel interference and adjacent-channel interference are different problems.
- Regulatory domain settings affect channel availability and transmit power.
- WPA2 and WPA3 are the main modern security families; PSK and 802.1X/RADIUS are the key authentication models.
- RSSI, SNR, and throughput are related but not the same.
- Antenna type, gain, polarization, and placement strongly affect coverage.
- Cisco wireless commands are platform dependent, so know the concepts and interpret outputs carefully.
