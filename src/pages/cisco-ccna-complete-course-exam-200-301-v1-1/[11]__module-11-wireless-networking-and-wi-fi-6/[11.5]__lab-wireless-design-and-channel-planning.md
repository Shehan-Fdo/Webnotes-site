## **Course:** Cisco CCNA Complete Course (Exam 200-301 v1.1)

**Wireless design goals**

Wireless design is about balancing coverage, capacity, and roaming. You want enough signal for reliable client connectivity, enough channel separation to reduce co-channel interference, and enough access point (AP) density to handle user load. In practice, the “best” design depends on the environment: open office, classroom, warehouse, hallway, or multi-floor building all behave differently.

A good design starts with requirements, not hardware. You estimate where users are, what applications they run, and how many clients each AP must support. Then you choose bands, channels, transmit power, and AP placement to meet those needs.

- **Coverage** means clients can hear the AP and the AP can hear the clients.
- **Capacity** means the WLAN can support the expected number of active devices and traffic.
- **Roaming** means clients can move between APs without losing sessions unnecessarily.
- **Interference** includes co-channel interference, adjacent-channel interference, and non-Wi-Fi noise sources.

**Key RF concepts**

Radio frequency (RF) planning depends on signal strength, noise, and channel reuse. The most useful client-side measurements are received signal strength indicator (RSSI) and signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). RSSI tells you how strong the signal is; SNR tells you how much stronger the signal is than the background noise. A strong RSSI with poor SNR can still produce unreliable performance.

For CCNA-level design, remember that roaming and voice-like applications need stronger signal quality than simple web browsing. A common planning target is to keep client RSSI around `-67 dBm` or better for reliable roaming and real-time applications, with SNR typically `25 dB` or higher when possible. These are design targets, not universal absolutes.

- **RSSI** is the received signal level in dBm.
- **SNR** is the difference between signal and noise, in dB.
- **Noise floor** is the ambient RF noise level.
- **Cell size** is the coverage area of one AP radio.

> Do not assume that higher transmit power always improves the WLAN. Too much power can enlarge cells, reduce channel reuse, and increase co-channel interference.

**2.4 GHz and 5 GHz planning**

The 2.4 GHz band has fewer non-overlapping channels and is more prone to interference from legacy devices and non-Wi-Fi sources. In most enterprise designs, you prefer 5 GHz for capacity and cleaner channel reuse. The 2.4 GHz band is often kept as a compatibility band, not the primary design band.

In 2.4 GHz, only channels 1, 6, and 11 are typically used in North America because they do not overlap. In other regulatory domains, channel availability differs, but the design principle is the same: use non-overlapping channels whenever possible. In 5 GHz, you have more channels, which makes channel reuse easier and usually improves capacity.

- **2.4 GHz**
  - Better range through walls than 5 GHz in many cases.
  - More interference and fewer usable channels.
  - Best reserved for legacy or low-bandwidth clients when needed.
- **5 GHz**
  - More channels and better capacity.
  - Usually preferred for modern clients.
  - Often supports wider channel widths, but wider is not always better.

Channel width affects capacity and reuse. Wider channels can increase peak throughput, but they consume more spectrum and reduce the number of available channels. In dense environments, narrower channels such as 20 MHz often provide better overall performance than 40 MHz or 80 MHz because they improve channel reuse.

**Channel reuse across APs and floors**

Channel reuse means reusing the same channel in different cells only when the cells are far enough apart that they do not interfere heavily. In a simple single-floor office, you stagger channels so neighboring APs do not use the same channel. In a multi-floor building, you also consider vertical overlap: an AP on one floor can interfere with APs above or below it if the signal penetrates the floor slab.

A practical design pattern is to offset channels between floors and avoid stacking APs directly above each other on the same channel when possible. If APs are aligned vertically, their cells may overlap through the ceiling/floor structure, which increases co-channel contention. You want a reuse pattern that spreads same-channel APs apart both horizontally and vertically.

Example planning ideas:

- On one floor, APs may alternate channels `1`, `6`, and `11` in 2.4 GHz.
- On adjacent floors, shift the pattern so the AP directly above is not on the same channel.
- In 5 GHz, use a larger channel pool to separate neighboring APs and reduce contention.
- In high-density areas, reduce transmit power before increasing channel width.

A simple conceptual floor pattern:

```text
Floor 3:  AP1 ch 36   AP2 ch 40   AP3 ch 44   AP4 ch 48
Floor 2:  AP1 ch 44   AP2 ch 48   AP3 ch 36   AP4 ch 40
Floor 1:  AP1 ch 40   AP2 ch 36   AP3 ch 48   AP4 ch 44
```

This is not a universal template, but it shows the idea: same-channel APs should not be immediate neighbors, and vertical reuse should be planned deliberately.

**Transmit power and cell sizing**

Transmit power controls how far an AP’s signal reaches. Lower power shrinks the cell, which can improve channel reuse and roaming behavior in dense deployments. However, lowering power too much creates coverage holes and forces clients to roam too often or connect at weak signal levels.

Use transmit power as one part of the design, not the only solution. If the environment is capacity-limited, you may need more APs. If it is coverage-limited, you may need better placement or higher power. The right answer depends on whether the bottleneck is signal reach or client density.

- **Lower power**
  - Smaller cells.
  - Better reuse in dense deployments.
  - Risk of coverage gaps if overdone.
- **Higher power**
  - Larger cells.
  - Can help coverage in sparse areas.
  - Risk of sticky clients and co-channel interference if overdone.

**Roaming design targets**

Roaming works best when adjacent AP cells overlap just enough for clients to discover the next AP before the current signal becomes unusable. Too little overlap causes drops; too much overlap causes clients to hear many APs on the same channel and contend more heavily.

A common design target is modest overlap between neighboring cells, often around 15% to 20% for general data networks, with careful tuning for voice or real-time applications. The exact percentage depends on building materials, AP antenna patterns, and client behavior. For CCNA study, focus on the principle: enough overlap for seamless roaming, not so much that cells compete excessively.

Useful roaming-related targets:

- Keep client RSSI near `-67 dBm` or better for stable roaming.
- Aim for SNR of at least `25 dB` when possible.
- Avoid large coverage overlaps on the same channel.
- Place APs so clients can see the next AP before the current one becomes weak.

**Site survey workflow**

A wireless site survey validates the design before or after deployment. A predictive survey uses floor plans and assumptions; an active survey measures the real RF environment. For exam and lab purposes, know the workflow and the measurements you collect at each stage.

1. **Gather requirements**
   - Identify user count, device types, and applications.
   - Note special areas such as conference rooms, hallways, stairwells, and exterior walls.
   - Record any constraints such as cabling limits, mounting restrictions, or regulatory limits.

2. **Inspect the floor plan**
   - Mark wall materials, elevators, glass, metal shelving, and other attenuation sources.
   - Identify likely AP mounting points and cable paths.
   - Note areas where signal may leak between floors.

3. **Perform a predictive layout**
   - Estimate AP locations and antenna types.
   - Choose initial channels and power levels.
   - Plan for 2.4 GHz compatibility and 5 GHz capacity.

4. **Measure the RF environment**
   - Walk the site with a survey tool.
   - Record RSSI, SNR, noise floor, and channel utilization.
   - Check for dead zones, excessive overlap, and interference sources.

5. **Validate roaming and capacity**
   - Test movement between APs.
   - Verify that signal remains above target thresholds during transitions.
   - Simulate user load if possible.

6. **Adjust and document**
   - Move APs, change power, or alter channel assignments.
   - Update the design record with final AP locations and settings.
   - Save before-and-after measurements for comparison.

Common tools include a laptop with survey software, a spectrum analyzer, and a Wi-Fi client adapter that supports monitor or survey modes. On Cisco wireless platforms, you may also use controller and AP status commands to verify radio health and channel assignments.

**Cisco wireless verification commands**

Cisco wireless commands vary by platform, so use the commands that match the controller or AP software you are working with. On modern Cisco Catalyst 9800-style workflows, you often inspect AP radio summaries and wireless client state rather than relying on older controller-specific controller-radio commands.

Examples:

```bash
show wireless summary
show wireless client summary
show ap summary
show ap dot11 24ghz summary
show ap dot11 5ghz summary
show ap name AP-1 config general
show ap name AP-1 dot11 5ghz
```

These commands help you confirm:

- Which APs are joined and operational.
- Which radios are enabled.
- What channels and power levels are in use.
- Whether clients are associated and on which band.

If you are troubleshooting a specific AP, check its radio status and client associations first. Then compare the observed channel plan against the intended design.

**Example controller-style configuration concepts**

Exact syntax depends on the Cisco wireless platform, but the design intent is consistent: define the WLAN, map it to a policy or interface, and tune radio settings for channel and power behavior. In many environments, automatic radio resource management handles channel and power selection, while you set guardrails and verify the outcome.

Example conceptual configuration on a controller:

```text
WLAN name: Corp-WiFi
SSID: CorpWiFi
Security: WPA2/WPA3 Enterprise
Band preference: 5 GHz preferred
2.4 GHz: enabled for legacy support only
Channel width: 20 MHz in dense areas
Transmit power: auto with limits
```

Example CLI-style checks after deployment:

```bash
show ap name AP-1 dot11 24ghz
show ap name AP-1 dot11 5ghz
show wireless client mac-address 0011.2233.4455
show wireless stats client detail
```

If you are using a lab AP or controller, the exact command names may differ, but the workflow stays the same: verify radio band, channel, power, and client association.

**Practical channel planning example**

Suppose you have a small office with six APs on one floor and moderate client density. A reasonable starting plan is:

- Prefer 5 GHz for primary service.
- Use 20 MHz channels for better reuse.
- Stagger neighboring APs across different channels.
- Reduce power enough to keep cells from overlapping too heavily.
- Keep 2.4 GHz enabled only if needed for compatibility.

A simple 5 GHz reuse pattern might look like this:

```text
AP1 -> ch 36
AP2 -> ch 40
AP3 -> ch 44
AP4 -> ch 48
AP5 -> ch 36
AP6 -> ch 40
```

This works only if same-channel APs are far enough apart. If AP5 is too close to AP1, you need a different pattern or lower power. Channel planning is always tied to physical placement.

**Troubleshooting symptoms and likely causes**

Wireless problems often look like “bad Wi-Fi,” but the root cause is usually specific. Use the symptom to narrow the issue.

- **Low throughput**
  - Too much co-channel interference.
  - Excessive channel width in a dense area.
  - Weak SNR or high retransmissions.
- **Frequent drops while moving**
  - Insufficient overlap between cells.
  - APs too far apart.
  - Client roaming thresholds not met.
- **Sticky clients**
  - AP transmit power too high.
  - Overlapping cells too large.
  - Client driver roaming behavior is slow.
- **Poor 2.4 GHz performance**
  - Interference from Bluetooth, microwaves, or neighboring WLANs.
  - Overuse of overlapping channels.
  - Too many clients on the band.

**Lab checklist**

Use this checklist when you practice wireless design and channel planning:

- Identify the coverage area and user density.
- Choose the primary band, usually 5 GHz.
- Select a channel width appropriate for density.
- Plan non-overlapping channels for neighboring APs.
- Check vertical reuse across floors.
- Set transmit power to balance coverage and reuse.
- Verify RSSI and SNR at representative client locations.
- Confirm roaming overlap is sufficient but not excessive.
- Validate the design with controller and AP status commands.

**Exam focus reminders**

For CCNA-level questions, focus on the relationship between signal, channel reuse, and roaming. The exam is more likely to test design reasoning than vendor-specific tuning details.

- Use 2.4 GHz sparingly when possible.
- Prefer 5 GHz for capacity and cleaner channel planning.
- Use non-overlapping channels in 2.4 GHz.
- Keep channel reuse separated across nearby APs and floors.
- Lower power can improve reuse, but it does not replace AP density when capacity is the limit.
- Verify design with RSSI, SNR, and AP/channel status commands.
