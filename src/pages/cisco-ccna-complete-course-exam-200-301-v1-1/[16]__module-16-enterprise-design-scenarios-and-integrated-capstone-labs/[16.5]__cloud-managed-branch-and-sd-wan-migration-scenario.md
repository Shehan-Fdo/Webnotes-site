## **Course:** Cisco CCNA Complete Course (Exam 200-301 v1.1)

**Cloud-managed branch overview**

Cisco SD-WAN (Software-Defined WAN) separates the control plane from the data plane so you can centrally manage branch connectivity, policy, and path selection. In a migration scenario, you usually keep the legacy WAN running while you introduce SD-WAN at one or more branches, validate overlay tunnels, and then move traffic in stages.

The main idea is simple: the branch device builds secure tunnels over one or more underlay transports, and centralized controllers distribute routes and policy. This gives you better visibility and more flexible path control than a traditional static or policy-based WAN.

- **Underlay** is the physical or provider network, such as Internet, MPLS, or LTE.
- **Overlay** is the encrypted SD-WAN tunnel network built on top of the underlay.
- **TLOC** means Transport Locator; it identifies a device’s reachable tunnel endpoint on a specific transport.
- **OMP** means Overlay Management Protocol; it distributes routes, TLOCs, and policy information in the overlay.

**Deployment models and device terminology**

Cisco SD-WAN commonly uses a controller-based architecture with branch edge devices and centralized management. For CCNA-level understanding, you should know the difference between the older vEdge terminology and the newer cEdge terminology.

| Term | Meaning | Typical platform behavior |
|---|---|---|
| **vEdge** | Cisco SD-WAN virtual or purpose-built edge running the SD-WAN software stack | Uses SD-WAN-native control and forwarding behavior |
| **cEdge** | Cisco IOS XE SD-WAN edge device | Runs IOS XE with SD-WAN features integrated |
| **vManage** | Centralized management and orchestration | Templates, monitoring, and policy deployment |
| **vSmart** | Control-plane controller | Exchanges OMP routes and policy |
| **vBond** | Orchestrator | Helps devices discover controllers and establish secure onboarding |

A key exam point is that **Cisco IOS XE SD-WAN is not the same as legacy IOS routing behavior**. The device still supports familiar routing concepts such as static routes, OSPF, and BGP in many designs, but SD-WAN adds a controller-driven overlay, centralized policy, and tunnel management. In practice, the branch may look like a normal router from the CLI, but its forwarding and route advertisement are influenced by SD-WAN control connections and templates.

> A cEdge can participate in traditional routing protocols, but SD-WAN policy and OMP determine how routes and transports are advertised and preferred in the overlay.

**Control connections and their roles**

SD-WAN devices form secure control connections to the controllers before they can exchange overlay information. These control connections are essential for onboarding, route distribution, and policy enforcement.

- **vBond connection**
  - Helps the edge discover the rest of the control plane.
  - Assists with NAT traversal and initial orchestration.
  - Is often the first controller contact during bootstrap.
- **vSmart connection**
  - Carries OMP control information.
  - Distributes routes, TLOCs, service routes, and policy.
  - Is the main control-plane relationship for overlay route exchange.
- **vManage connection**
  - Provides management, templates, monitoring, and configuration workflows.
  - Does not normally carry route exchange like vSmart does.
  - Is used to push device templates and policies.

A useful mental model is:

1. The edge comes online and learns how to reach the controllers.
2. The edge authenticates and establishes secure control connections.
3. The edge exchanges OMP information with vSmart.
4. The edge builds data-plane tunnels to remote sites based on advertised TLOCs and policy.

**OMP and route distribution**

OMP is the SD-WAN control protocol that distributes overlay reachability information. It is not a replacement for all routing protocols, but it acts as the overlay distribution mechanism that tells devices what networks exist and how to reach them.

OMP commonly carries:

- **OMP routes** for prefixes learned from connected, static, or dynamic sources.
- **TLOCs** that identify how to reach a site over a specific transport.
- **Service routes** when service chaining or centralized services are used.
- **Policy information** that influences route preference and advertisement.

In a migration, OMP is what allows a branch to learn remote branch prefixes without building full-mesh static tunnels. The branch advertises its local routes into OMP, vSmart distributes them, and other branches learn the best path based on policy and transport attributes.

**Transport colors and TLOC behavior**

Transport colors are labels that identify the type of WAN transport used by a tunnel endpoint. They help the controller and edge devices classify paths and make path-selection decisions.

Common examples include:

- **biz-internet** for business Internet
- **public-internet** for public Internet
- **mpls** for MPLS transport
- **lte** for cellular backup
- **metro-ethernet** for Ethernet-based WAN access

A TLOC is usually identified by:

- System IP
- Transport color
- Encapsulation type, such as IPsec

Transport color matters because it affects:

- **Tunnel formation**: devices form tunnels only over transports that are configured and reachable.
- **Path preference**: policy can prefer MPLS over Internet, or Internet over LTE, depending on business goals.
- **Migration behavior**: you can keep legacy MPLS active while adding Internet-based SD-WAN tunnels, then shift traffic gradually.

For example, a branch may have both MPLS and Internet circuits. During migration, you might keep MPLS as the primary path for critical traffic while enabling Internet as a secondary SD-WAN transport. Later, you can reverse the preference if Internet performance and policy allow it.

**Certificate lifecycle and onboarding**

SD-WAN devices use certificates to authenticate to controllers and establish trust. If certificates are not already installed, the device must be onboarded through a bootstrap process.

Common onboarding concepts include:

- **ZTP** (Zero Touch Provisioning): the device contacts a provisioning service and receives initial configuration automatically.
- **PNP** (Plug and Play): a similar onboarding workflow often associated with Cisco device provisioning.
- **Bootstrap file**: a file or initial config that provides controller addresses, system parameters, and trust information.
- **Factory default onboarding**: the device starts with minimal config and learns the rest from the provisioning workflow.

If certificates are not preinstalled, the device typically needs:

- A valid system identity
- Controller reachability
- Bootstrap information for vBond or provisioning services
- Trust chain material so it can validate controller certificates

In practice, the certificate lifecycle includes:

- Initial trust establishment
- Device authentication to controllers
- Renewal or replacement before expiration
- Troubleshooting when trust fails due to time, reachability, or mismatched identity

> If certificate trust is broken, the device may fail to establish control connections even when basic IP connectivity exists.

**Migration workflow from legacy WAN to SD-WAN**

A staged migration reduces risk. You usually do not replace the entire WAN in one step unless the environment is small and simple.

A practical migration sequence looks like this:

1. **Assess the existing WAN**
   - Inventory branch routers, circuits, routing protocols, and critical applications.
   - Identify which sites can tolerate Internet-based transport and which need MPLS continuity.
2. **Prepare the SD-WAN controllers**
   - Bring up vManage, vBond, and vSmart.
   - Confirm controller certificates and reachability.
3. **Onboard the first branch**
   - Bootstrap the device with system IP, site ID, and controller information.
   - Verify control connections and OMP adjacency.
4. **Attach templates**
   - Use vManage device templates to standardize interfaces, VPNs, and routing.
   - Apply feature templates for transport, VPN, and policy settings.
5. **Validate overlay tunnels**
   - Confirm that tunnels form over the intended colors.
   - Check that routes are exchanged and preferred paths match policy.
6. **Move traffic gradually**
   - Start with low-risk sites or noncritical applications.
   - Shift default routes or application traffic to the SD-WAN overlay.
7. **Retire legacy WAN paths**
   - Remove old static tunnels or routing dependencies after validation.
   - Keep rollback options until the new design is stable.

A common branch migration scenario is:

- Keep the legacy router active on MPLS.
- Add a cEdge in parallel or replace the edge at the branch.
- Bring up SD-WAN over Internet and MPLS.
- Validate that branch-to-branch and branch-to-data-center traffic uses the intended overlay.
- Cut over user VLANs or default routes in phases.

**vManage templates and device attachment**

vManage is the operational center for SD-WAN deployment. You usually do not configure every branch manually; instead, you build reusable templates and attach them to devices or device groups.

Typical workflow:

1. Create feature templates for:
   - System settings
   - VPN interfaces
   - Routing protocols
   - Centralized policy elements
2. Build a device template from those features.
3. Attach the template to a device or device group.
4. Enter site-specific variables such as system IP, hostname, and interface addresses.
5. Push the configuration and verify the result.

Example of a template-driven configuration concept:

```text
system
 host-name BRANCH-01
 system-ip 10.255.1.1
 site-id 101
 organization-name ACME-WAN
 vbond 203.0.113.10
!
vpn 0
 interface ge0/0
  ip address 198.51.100.2/30
  tunnel-interface
   encapsulation ipsec
   color biz-internet
   allow-service all
```

This is not a complete production config, but it shows the relationship between system identity, transport interface, and tunnel color. In a real deployment, vManage usually renders these values from templates rather than requiring hand-built configs on every device.

**Data policy vs control policy**

These two policy types affect different parts of the SD-WAN system, and the distinction matters for exam questions.

| Policy type | What it affects | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| **Control policy** | OMP route exchange and route advertisement behavior | Influence which routes are learned, preferred, or redistributed |
| **Data policy** | Actual traffic forwarding decisions on the data plane | Match applications, set preferred paths, drop traffic, or steer traffic to a service |

Control policy is about what information enters or leaves the overlay control plane. Data policy is about what happens to packets after they are forwarded.

Examples:

- Use **control policy** to prevent certain prefixes from being advertised to specific sites.
- Use **data policy** to send voice traffic over MPLS and bulk traffic over Internet.
- Use **data policy** to block unwanted traffic or steer it through a security service.

A simple conceptual example:

```text
policy
 control-policy BLOCK-DEFAULT-ROUTE
  sequence 10
   match route 0.0.0.0/0
   action reject
 !
 data-policy APP-STEERING
  sequence 10
   match application voice
   action prefer mpls
  sequence 20
   match application bulk
   action prefer biz-internet
```

The exact syntax varies by platform and release, but the exam takeaway is the functional difference: **control policy changes route distribution; data policy changes packet forwarding**.

**Practical CLI validation**

During migration, you should verify both control-plane and data-plane health. Good outputs usually show established control connections, valid OMP peers, and active tunnels.

Useful commands include:

```bash
show sdwan control connections
show sdwan omp peers
show sdwan omp routes
show sdwan bfd sessions
show sdwan tunnel statistics
show ip route
show running-config
```

What “good” often looks like:

- **Control connections** show `up` or `established` to vBond, vSmart, and vManage.
- **OMP peers** show the expected controller peer state and route counts.
- **OMP routes** include local and remote prefixes with valid next hops or TLOCs.
- **BFD** (Bidirectional Forwarding Detection) sessions are up for active tunnels.
- **Tunnel statistics** show traffic counters increasing on the intended transport.
- **IP routing table** includes learned routes that match the overlay design.

Example interpretation:

```text
show sdwan control connections
```

Expected indicators:

- Controller IPs are reachable
- State is `up`
- Certificates are valid
- Transport interfaces are bound to the correct colors

```text
show sdwan omp peers
```

Expected indicators:

- vSmart peers are established
- Route counts are nonzero where expected
- Site IDs and system IPs match the design

```text
show sdwan bfd sessions
```

Expected indicators:

- Sessions are `up`
- Loss and latency values are reasonable
- The preferred transport is the one you intended to use

**Common migration validation checks**

Before cutover, confirm that the branch behaves correctly in both the control plane and the forwarding plane.

- **Identity**
  - Hostname, site ID, and system IP match the design.
  - The organization name is correct.
- **Transport**
  - Each WAN interface has the correct color.
  - The tunnel interface is enabled on the intended transports.
- **Control plane**
  - vBond, vSmart, and vManage connections are established.
  - Certificates are trusted and not expired.
- **Routing**
  - Local prefixes are advertised into OMP.
  - Remote prefixes are learned from the overlay.
- **Forwarding**
  - BFD sessions are up.
  - Traffic uses the preferred path.
  - Failover works when a transport is disabled.

**Example staged branch cutover**

Suppose Branch A currently uses a legacy router with static default routing to an MPLS provider. You want to migrate it to SD-WAN without downtime.

1. Keep the legacy router active and introduce the SD-WAN edge in parallel.
2. Bootstrap the SD-WAN edge with controller information and site identity.
3. Bring up Internet and MPLS transports on the edge.
4. Confirm that the edge forms control connections and learns remote routes through OMP.
5. Attach a vManage template that sets:
   - `site-id`
   - transport colors
   - VPN interfaces
   - routing policy
6. Test branch-to-branch traffic over the overlay.
7. Move the default route or user VLAN gateway to the SD-WAN edge.
8. Monitor application performance and tunnel health.
9. Remove the legacy WAN dependency after the new path is stable.

This staged approach lets you validate each layer before you commit to full cutover. It also gives you a rollback path if certificate trust, transport reachability, or policy behavior is not correct.

**Troubleshooting patterns**

When SD-WAN migration fails, the issue is often in one of four areas:

- **Bootstrap**
  - Wrong controller address
  - Missing organization name
  - Incorrect site ID or system IP
- **Certificates**
  - Expired trust chain
  - Time mismatch
  - Device not authorized
- **Transport**
  - Wrong color assignment
  - NAT or firewall blocking controller reachability
  - Underlay routing failure
- **Policy and routing**
  - Routes not advertised into OMP
  - Control policy filtering too aggressively
  - Data policy steering traffic to the wrong path

A practical troubleshooting mindset is to start from the outside in:

1. Confirm underlay reachability.
2. Confirm controller connectivity.
3. Confirm OMP route exchange.
4. Confirm tunnel formation and BFD.
5. Confirm application traffic follows policy.

**Exam focus summary**

For CCNA study, focus on these relationships:

- **vBond** helps with onboarding and controller discovery.
- **vSmart** is the main OMP control-plane controller.
- **vManage** handles templates, orchestration, and monitoring.
- **cEdge** runs Cisco IOS XE SD-WAN; **vEdge** is the older SD-WAN edge terminology.
- **Transport colors** identify and classify WAN paths.
- **TLOCs** represent reachable tunnel endpoints on a transport.
- **Control policy** changes route distribution.
- **Data policy** changes packet forwarding.
- **ZTP/PNP-style onboarding** helps automate initial device setup.
- **Staged migration** reduces risk and supports rollback.
