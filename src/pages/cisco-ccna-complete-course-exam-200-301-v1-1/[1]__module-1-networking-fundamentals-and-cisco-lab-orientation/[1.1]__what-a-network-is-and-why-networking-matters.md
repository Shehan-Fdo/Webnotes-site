**What a network is**

A computer network is a group of two or more independent computers and devices connected so they can share data, resources, and services. The connection can use wired media or wireless technologies. In practice, a network links systems physically and logically so they can communicate and collaborate.

- **Devices** in a network remain independent but can exchange information.
- **Connections** may use cables or wireless links.
- **Sharing** includes data, hardware, software, and services.

**Why networking matters**

Networking makes communication and resource sharing possible across personal, local, and wide environments. It supports efficient work by letting multiple devices use the same infrastructure instead of operating in isolation. This is why networks are central to digital collaboration, business operations, and modern IT services.

- Networks help users communicate across devices and locations.
- Networks improve efficiency by sharing resources.
- Networks support collaboration in home, office, and enterprise settings.

**Common ways to classify networks**

Networks are often classified by the area they cover, the transmission medium they use, and ownership or access control. For CCNA, the geographical-area view is especially useful because it helps you compare network scope.

| Type | Scope | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| **PAN** | Personal Area Network, very short range around one user | Personal devices such as a smartphone and wireless earbuds |
| **LAN** | Local Area Network, within a limited local area | Devices in a home, office, or building |
| **MAN** | Metropolitan Area Network, across a city or large campus area | City-scale connectivity |
| **WAN** | Wide Area Network, across large geographic distances | Connecting sites over regions or countries |
| **CAN** | Campus Area Network, across a campus or organization site | Multiple buildings in one organization |

**PAN, LAN, MAN, and WAN at a glance**

A **Personal Area Network (PAN)** connects personal devices within about 1–10 meters. A **Local Area Network (LAN)** connects devices in a small local area. A **Metropolitan Area Network (MAN)** spans a larger city-scale area. A **Wide Area Network (WAN)** covers the broadest distances and connects networks over large regions.

- **PAN** is for short-range personal connectivity.
- **LAN** is for local device sharing.
- **MAN** extends connectivity across a metropolitan area.
- **WAN** links distant networks together.

**Wired and wireless connectivity**

Networks can use either wired or wireless technologies. The choice depends on the environment, the required range, and the type of communication needed. Both approaches serve the same core purpose: moving data between devices.

- **Wired** connections use physical media.
- **Wireless** connections use radio-based technologies.
- The connection method affects mobility, range, and deployment style.
