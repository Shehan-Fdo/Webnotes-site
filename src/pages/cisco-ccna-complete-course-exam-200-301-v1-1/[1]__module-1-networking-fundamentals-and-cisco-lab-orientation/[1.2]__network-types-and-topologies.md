## **Course:** Cisco CCNA Complete Course (Exam 200-301 v1.1)

**Network type basics**

Networks are commonly classified by the area they cover and the purpose they serve. For CCNA, you should recognize the main scope-based categories and understand that the same network can also be described by its topology, or layout.

- **PAN** (Personal Area Network) connects personal devices over a very short range, often using Bluetooth.
- **LAN** (Local Area Network) covers a small area such as a room, office, or building.
- **MAN** (Metropolitan Area Network) spans a city or campus-sized area.
- **WAN** (Wide Area Network) connects sites across large geographic distances, including remote branches.
- **WLAN** (Wireless LAN) is a LAN that uses wireless connectivity instead of only wired links.

A network’s size is usually described by the geographic area it occupies and the number of devices it supports. In practice, you choose the type based on coverage needs, performance goals, and how the sites must communicate.

**Common topology concepts**

A topology describes how devices and links are arranged. You should be able to identify the common physical and logical layouts used in small and enterprise networks.

- **Star** topology connects devices through a central device, which is common in small business LANs.
- **Bus** topology places devices along a shared communication path.
- **Ring** topology links devices in a closed loop.
- **Mesh** topology provides multiple paths between devices for resilience.
- **Hybrid** topology combines two or more topology styles.

> A topology choice affects troubleshooting, redundancy, and how traffic moves through the network.

**LAN and star as a practical pairing**

A common design pattern is a LAN built with a star topology. This combination is popular because it is easy to manage and troubleshoot, and it supports high-speed local connectivity.

- End devices connect to a central switch or similar device.
- A failure on one access link usually affects only that device.
- Centralized design makes fault isolation simpler than in many shared-medium layouts.

This is a practical CCNA takeaway: network type describes the scope, while topology describes the arrangement. You often need both to explain a design correctly.

**WAN topology considerations**

WANs connect geographically separated sites, so the topology must match service requirements such as bandwidth, availability, and speed. Service providers may use different WAN topologies depending on how many sites must be interconnected and what level of connectivity the customer needs.

- **Point-to-point** connects two sites directly.
- WAN designs may use technologies such as Metro Ethernet to extend Ethernet connectivity across provider infrastructure.
- The chosen topology should reflect the enterprise need for reach, performance, and resilience.

**How to classify a network**

1. Identify the physical area the network covers.
2. Decide whether the network is personal, local, metropolitan, or wide area.
3. Determine whether the connection is wired, wireless, or a mix.
4. Describe the layout using a topology term such as star, bus, ring, mesh, or hybrid.

**Quick comparison**

| Type | Typical scope | Common use |
|---|---|---|
| PAN | Very short range | Personal devices and nearby sharing |
| LAN | Small site | Office, home, or building network |
| MAN | City-scale | Regional connectivity |
| WAN | Large geographic area | Branch and remote-site interconnection |
| WLAN | Local area, wireless | Wireless access within a LAN |

**Exam focus**

For exam prep, be ready to match the network type to its coverage and to identify the topology from a diagram or description. If a question mentions a small office with a central switch, think **LAN** with a **star** topology. If it describes multiple remote sites, think **WAN**.
