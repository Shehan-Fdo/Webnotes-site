## **Course:** Cisco CCNA Complete Course (Exam 200-301 v1.1)

**WAN purpose and business need**

A <abbr title="Wide Area Network">WAN</abbr> connects networks across large geographic distances, such as between branches, data centers, cloud environments, and remote users. Unlike a <abbr title="Local Area Network">LAN</abbr>, which usually stays within a building or campus, a WAN depends on a service provider or public infrastructure to carry traffic between sites.

The main purpose of a WAN is to let users and applications communicate as if they were on one larger network, even when the endpoints are far apart. In practice, you use WANs to support:

- Branch-to-headquarters access for shared applications
- Inter-site voice, video, and collaboration traffic
- Cloud access to SaaS, IaaS, and hosted services
- Remote connectivity for mobile or home users
- Business continuity and backup paths between locations

A WAN design is usually driven by business requirements, not just bandwidth. You choose the service based on cost, latency, availability, security, and how much control you need over the path.

**Common WAN service models**

WAN connectivity is often grouped into private, public, and hybrid approaches. The right choice depends on whether you want dedicated provider-managed transport, Internet-based transport, or a mix of both.

- **Private WAN**
  - Uses provider-managed circuits that are logically or physically isolated from the public Internet.
  - Common examples include leased lines, MPLS <abbr title="Multiprotocol Label Switching">MPLS</abbr> services, and point-to-point circuits.
  - Best when you need predictable performance, strong service-level agreements, and simpler routing between sites.
- **Public WAN**
  - Uses the Internet as the transport medium.
  - Common examples include site-to-site VPNs and remote-access VPNs.
  - Best when cost matters and you can tolerate variable latency and jitter.
- **Hybrid WAN**
  - Combines private and public links.
  - Often used to keep critical traffic on a private path while sending less sensitive or bursty traffic over broadband.
  - Useful when you want resilience, cost control, and application-aware path selection.
- **SD-WAN**
  - <abbr title="Software-Defined Wide Area Network">SD-WAN</abbr> overlays policy-based control on top of multiple transports.
  - Can use MPLS, broadband Internet, LTE/5G, or a mix of links.
  - Best when you need centralized policy, dynamic path selection, and easier branch scaling.

> A WAN technology choice is usually a tradeoff: dedicated services improve predictability, while Internet-based services improve flexibility and cost efficiency.

**WAN topologies and service arrangements**

WAN topologies describe how sites connect to each other. These patterns matter because they affect cost, resilience, routing complexity, and traffic flow.

- **Point-to-point**
  - Connects exactly two sites with a dedicated circuit or logical link.
  - Simple to design and troubleshoot.
  - Common for direct branch-to-data-center links, backup circuits, or provider handoffs.
  - Scales poorly if you need many sites because each new site requires additional links.
- **Hub-and-spoke**
  - All branch sites connect to a central hub, such as headquarters or a data center.
  - Easier to manage than many direct links.
  - Reduces circuit count, but branch-to-branch traffic may hairpin through the hub.
  - Common in traditional enterprise WANs and still used in hybrid designs.
- **Full mesh**
  - Every site has a direct connection to every other site.
  - Provides the best direct path options and can reduce latency between sites.
  - Expensive and operationally complex as the number of sites grows.
  - More common in small environments or as a logical overlay with SD-WAN.

A quick rule of thumb:

- Use **point-to-point** when you need a simple dedicated link between two locations.
- Use **hub-and-spoke** when centralized services matter more than direct branch-to-branch traffic.
- Use **full mesh** when many sites need direct communication and the cost/complexity is acceptable.

**WAN demarcation point and provider handoff**

The <abbr title="demarcation point">demarc</abbr> is the boundary between the customer network and the service provider network. It is the point where responsibility changes from the provider to the customer. In many deployments, the provider delivers service to a handoff device or interface, and the customer connects their router, firewall, or switch to that handoff.

Why the demarc matters:

- It defines who owns troubleshooting for each side of the connection.
- It helps you identify whether a fault is inside your network or in the provider circuit.
- It determines where physical media, signaling, and interface expectations must match.

Common handoff examples include:

- A provider-installed <abbr title="Network Interface Device">NID</abbr> or smart jack for a circuit
- An Ethernet handoff from a carrier-managed device
- A serial handoff on older leased-line services
- A fiber or copper termination at a customer edge device

In troubleshooting, you often verify link status at the customer edge first, then confirm the provider handoff, then escalate if the issue appears beyond the demarc.

> If the link is down on your router but the provider says the circuit is up, the demarc helps you prove where the failure likely sits.

**WAN connectivity types**

CCNA-level WAN study usually focuses on recognizing the major transport types and what they are used for. The exact service names vary by provider, but the underlying ideas are consistent.

**Leased lines and point-to-point circuits**

A leased line is a dedicated connection between two endpoints. It is typically always on and provides predictable performance because the bandwidth is reserved for your use.

- Good for stable, low-latency connectivity between two sites
- Often used for critical applications or backup paths
- Usually more expensive than shared services
- Historically associated with serial interfaces and provider-managed circuits

On older serial WAN links, you may see encapsulation such as:

- **HDLC** (<abbr title="High-Level Data Link Control">HDLC</abbr>) on Cisco serial links
- **PPP** (<abbr title="Point-to-Point Protocol">PPP</abbr>) when authentication or multi-vendor support is needed

Example serial interface configuration:

```cisco
interface serial0/0/0
 description WAN link to Branch A
 encapsulation ppp
 ip address 10.10.10.1 255.255.255.252
 no shutdown
```

Use PPP when you need features such as authentication or when the environment requires a standard point-to-point protocol. HDLC is simpler, but PPP is more flexible and more commonly referenced in labs and exam questions.

**MPLS**

MPLS is a provider-managed WAN service that forwards traffic using labels rather than only IP lookups. Enterprises often use MPLS to connect multiple sites with predictable performance and provider-managed routing.

- Common in private WAN designs
- Supports multiple sites with provider-controlled transport
- Often chosen for voice, video, and business-critical traffic
- Usually more expensive than Internet-based VPNs

MPLS is not a security feature by itself. If you need confidentiality, you may still add encryption on top of it.

**Ethernet WAN and Metro Ethernet**

Ethernet WAN services use Ethernet as the handoff and transport model between the customer and provider. <abbr title="Metro Ethernet">Metro Ethernet</abbr> is a common example, especially for campus-to-campus or branch-to-data-center connectivity.

- The provider delivers an Ethernet interface instead of a serial circuit
- The customer edge device connects with standard Ethernet framing
- Bandwidth options are often higher and easier to scale than legacy serial services
- The service may appear as point-to-point or multipoint depending on the provider design

Ethernet WAN is important because many modern WAN handoffs look like ordinary Ethernet interfaces on the router, even though the provider network behind them is not part of your LAN.

Example interface configuration for an Ethernet handoff:

```cisco
interface gigabitEthernet0/0
 description Provider handoff to Metro Ethernet circuit
 ip address 172.16.50.2 255.255.255.252
 no shutdown
```

**Broadband Internet access**

Broadband services such as cable, DSL, fiber Internet, or business Internet circuits are common WAN transports for branch offices and remote sites.

- Lower cost than private circuits
- Widely available
- Performance can vary more than private WAN services
- Often paired with VPN or SD-WAN for secure site-to-site connectivity

Broadband is a strong choice when you need fast deployment and lower cost, especially for smaller branches or backup links.

**VPN over the Internet**

A <abbr title="Virtual Private Network">VPN</abbr> creates an encrypted tunnel across a public network. In WAN design, VPNs are often used to secure traffic over the Internet between sites or for remote users.

- **Site-to-site VPN**
  - Connects two networks over the Internet
  - Common for branch-to-headquarters or branch-to-branch connectivity
  - Usually built with IPsec
- **Remote-access VPN**
  - Connects individual users to the enterprise network
  - Common for teleworkers and mobile users

VPNs are attractive because they combine low-cost transport with encryption. The tradeoff is that performance depends on the underlying Internet path.

**Wireless WAN options**

Wireless WAN services provide connectivity where wired circuits are unavailable or impractical.

- **LTE/5G**
  - Useful for backup connectivity, temporary sites, and mobile deployments
  - Often used as a secondary path in SD-WAN
- **Microwave or point-to-point wireless**
  - Can connect buildings or remote facilities
  - Requires line of sight and careful planning
- **Satellite**
  - Useful in remote areas with limited terrestrial infrastructure
  - Higher latency than terrestrial options

Wireless WAN is often chosen for reach and deployment speed rather than raw performance.

**Choosing the right WAN type**

When you compare WAN options, think in terms of business requirements first.

- Choose a **private circuit** when you need predictable latency, strong provider SLAs, and stable performance for critical traffic.
- Choose **Internet VPN** when cost is the main concern and you can tolerate variable performance.
- Choose **Metro Ethernet** when you want Ethernet-style handoff, higher bandwidth, and simpler integration with modern edge devices.
- Choose **MPLS** when you want provider-managed multi-site connectivity with predictable service characteristics.
- Choose **SD-WAN** when you need centralized control across multiple transports and want to mix private and public links.
- Choose **wireless WAN** when wired access is unavailable, temporary, or used as backup.

A practical comparison:

| Requirement | Better fit | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Lowest cost | Broadband Internet + VPN | Uses commodity transport |
| Predictable performance | Leased line or MPLS | Dedicated or provider-managed service |
| Fast branch deployment | Broadband or LTE/5G | Quick to install and activate |
| High bandwidth handoff | Metro Ethernet | Ethernet-based scaling is common |
| Resilience across links | Hybrid WAN or SD-WAN | Multiple transports and path selection |
| Direct branch-to-branch traffic | Full mesh or SD-WAN overlay | Avoids hub hairpinning |

**WAN encapsulation and interface behavior**

On WAN links, the encapsulation determines how Layer 2 frames are carried across the link. In labs and exam scenarios, you should recognize that interface behavior can depend on the encapsulation type.

- **PPP**
  - Point-to-point encapsulation
  - Supports authentication such as PAP and CHAP
  - Common in serial WAN examples
- **HDLC**
  - Cisco default on many older serial links
  - Simple and efficient
  - Less flexible than PPP
- **Ethernet**
  - Common on modern WAN handoffs
  - Often used with provider-delivered Ethernet services
  - May require VLAN tagging or subinterfaces in some designs

Useful verification commands:

```cisco
show interfaces serial0/0/0
show interfaces gigabitEthernet0/0
show controllers serial0/0/0
show ip interface brief
show running-config interface serial0/0/0
```

What to look for:

- `up/up` indicates the physical and data link layers are operational
- `encapsulation` confirms the Layer 2 method in use
- `line protocol down` often points to encapsulation mismatch, clocking issues, or provider-side problems on serial links
- `administratively down` means the interface is shut down locally

**Practical lab examples**

A branch router using a provider Ethernet handoff might look like this:

```cisco
interface gigabitEthernet0/1
 description WAN handoff to ISP
 ip address dhcp
 no shutdown
```

A point-to-point serial link with PPP might look like this:

```cisco
interface serial0/0/0
 description Link to HQ
 encapsulation ppp
 ip address 192.0.2.1 255.255.255.252
 no shutdown
```

A site-to-site VPN over broadband often uses the Internet-facing interface as the transport and encrypts traffic between private subnets. In that design, the WAN link itself is the public Internet, but the enterprise traffic remains protected by the tunnel.

**Exam-style distinctions to remember**

- **Private vs public**
  - Private WAN services are provider-managed and usually more predictable.
  - Public WAN services use the Internet and usually cost less.
- **Point-to-point vs multipoint**
  - Point-to-point connects two endpoints only.
  - Multipoint supports multiple sites on a shared service or overlay.
- **Hub-and-spoke vs full mesh**
  - Hub-and-spoke is simpler and cheaper.
  - Full mesh gives better direct connectivity but scales poorly.
- **Serial vs Ethernet handoff**
  - Serial is associated with older WAN circuits and PPP/HDLC.
  - Ethernet is common in modern WAN services such as Metro Ethernet.
- **MPLS vs VPN**
  - MPLS is a provider WAN service.
  - VPN is an overlay that can run over the Internet or other transports.
- **SD-WAN vs traditional WAN**
  - SD-WAN adds centralized policy and dynamic path selection across multiple links.
  - Traditional WANs usually rely more on static service design and manual routing decisions.

**Troubleshooting mindset**

When a WAN link fails, start by separating local issues from provider issues.

1. Check the interface state with `show ip interface brief`.
2. Verify the physical connection and link LEDs.
3. Confirm the configured encapsulation matches the provider or remote device.
4. Check for IP addressing and routing consistency.
5. Test reachability to the next hop and beyond the demarc.
6. Escalate to the provider if the fault appears outside your control boundary.

Common causes include:

- Wrong encapsulation on serial links
- Incorrect IP addressing or subnet mask
- Interface shutdown
- Provider circuit outage
- Mismatched speed, duplex, or handoff type on Ethernet services
- Missing tunnel or VPN configuration on Internet-based WANs

**Key takeaways**

WANs exist to connect distant networks and support business applications across geographic boundaries. The most important exam ideas are the service model, the topology, the handoff, and the transport type.

- **Point-to-point** is simple and direct.
- **Hub-and-spoke** reduces cost and centralizes services.
- **Full mesh** improves direct connectivity but increases complexity.
- The **demarcation point** separates customer responsibility from provider responsibility.
- **PPP** and **HDLC** are classic serial WAN encapsulations.
- **Metro Ethernet** and other Ethernet WAN services are common modern handoffs.
- **MPLS, VPN, broadband, LTE/5G, and SD-WAN** each solve different business problems.
