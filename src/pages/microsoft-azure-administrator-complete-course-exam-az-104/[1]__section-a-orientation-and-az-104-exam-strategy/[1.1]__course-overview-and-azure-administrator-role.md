## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Administrator Complete Course (Exam AZ-104)

**Azure Administrator role overview**

An Azure Administrator operates the day-to-day control plane of Microsoft Azure. You manage subscriptions, identities, storage, compute, networking, monitoring, and governance so workloads stay available, secure, and cost-aware. In practice, you are the person who turns business requirements into working Azure resources and keeps them healthy after deployment.

The role sits between design and operations. You usually do not own every architectural decision, but you do implement, validate, and maintain the environment. That means you often collaborate with:
- **Security teams** for identity, access control, policy, and incident response.
- **Networking teams** for IP planning, DNS, routing, VPN, and connectivity to on-premises or other clouds.
- **DevOps teams** for deployment pipelines, infrastructure as code, and release automation.
- **Application teams** for sizing, availability, backup, and troubleshooting application-facing issues.

A useful mental model is: architects decide *what* should exist, administrators make it *work*, and specialists or engineers may own deeper design areas such as advanced networking, security engineering, or platform automation.

**AZ-104 exam expectations**

AZ-104 is a hands-on administrator exam, not a theory-only test. Expect scenario-based questions that ask you to choose the best action, interpret command output, or identify the correct portal path for a task. You may need to reason under time pressure from partial information, such as a failed deployment, a permission issue, or a monitoring alert.

Typical exam expectations include:
- Reading a scenario and selecting the correct Azure service or administrative action.
- Interpreting output from Azure CLI, Azure PowerShell, or portal blades.
- Troubleshooting common issues such as access denied, resource deployment failures, or network reachability problems.
- Understanding when to use the portal versus command-line tools.
- Knowing the limits of AZ-104 depth: you should be comfortable with operational automation, but not expected to build advanced platform engineering solutions.

> AZ-104 focuses on administration and operations. You should know how to deploy and manage resources, but not expect deep coverage of advanced infrastructure-as-code design, custom DevOps platform engineering, or highly specialized security architecture.

**What you do in the Azure Administrator role**

Your daily work is operational and cross-functional. You keep subscriptions organized, enforce access and governance, and respond to changes in workload demand or incidents. Common responsibilities include:
- Creating and organizing subscriptions, resource groups, and tags.
- Managing users, groups, role assignments, and privileged access.
- Deploying and maintaining virtual machines, storage accounts, and networking components.
- Monitoring health, investigating alerts, and responding to service issues.
- Managing quotas, limits, and capacity-related constraints.
- Supporting backup, recovery, and business continuity tasks.
- Reviewing cost trends and making basic optimization recommendations.

These tasks are often repetitive but important. For example, you may create a resource group for a new application, assign a contributor role to a deployment identity, check whether a region quota blocks a VM deployment, and then review whether the environment is overprovisioned after the application goes live.

**Subscription, resource group, and lifecycle management**

Subscription and resource group management are foundational because they define scope, billing boundaries, and operational organization. A subscription is a billing and access boundary; a resource group is a logical container for related resources that usually share lifecycle, ownership, and deployment cadence.

Operational examples:
- Create a resource group for a project and apply tags such as `Owner`, `Environment`, and `CostCenter`.
- Move resources between resource groups when ownership changes, while checking for service-specific move limitations.
- Delete a resource group to remove an entire test environment after validation.
- Lock a resource group or subscription to prevent accidental deletion or modification.

Practical CLI examples:
```bash
az group create \
  --name rg-app-prod \
  --location eastus \
  --tags Owner=Platform Team=Payments Environment=Prod

az group delete \
  --name rg-app-test \
  --yes --no-wait
```

You should understand that lifecycle tasks are not just cleanup. They are part of governance, cost control, and operational hygiene.

**Identity and access management boundaries**

Azure administrators commonly manage Microsoft Entra ID identities at the resource access layer, even if a separate identity team owns tenant-wide identity strategy. You should know how to assign Azure roles, use groups instead of individual users where possible, and apply least privilege.

Key ideas:
- **Microsoft Entra ID** provides identity for users, groups, and service principals.
- **Azure role-based access control (RBAC)** controls access to Azure resources.
- **Role assignments** should usually target groups or managed identities, not individual users.
- **Privileged Identity Management (PIM)** may be used for just-in-time elevation in more mature environments.

Example role assignment:
```bash
az role assignment create \
  --assignee user@contoso.com \
  --role "Reader" \
  --scope /subscriptions/<subscription-id>/resourceGroups/rg-app-prod
```

In real environments, you often coordinate with security teams when access needs to be audited, when conditional access affects sign-in behavior, or when privileged access must be time-bound. Your job is to implement the operational side of access control, not to redesign the entire identity architecture.

**Compute, storage, and networking responsibilities**

Azure administrators manage core platform services that applications depend on. For compute, this often means virtual machines, VM scale sets, availability settings, and extensions. For storage, it includes storage accounts, blobs, file shares, access tiers, redundancy, and secure access. For networking, it includes virtual networks, subnets, network security groups, public IPs, DNS, load balancing, and connectivity.

Common operational tasks:
- Resize a VM to match workload demand.
- Add or remove data disks and manage disk performance settings.
- Configure storage account access keys, SAS tokens, or Azure AD-based access where appropriate.
- Create subnets and network security group rules for application tiers.
- Troubleshoot connectivity with effective routes, NSG rules, and DNS resolution.

Example network inspection commands:
```bash
az network vnet show -g rg-network -n vnet-prod
az network nsg rule list -g rg-network --nsg-name nsg-web
az network nic show-effective-route-table -g rg-network -n nic-web01
```

You should be able to explain why a VM cannot reach a service endpoint, why a port is blocked, or why name resolution fails. The exam often rewards methodical troubleshooting over memorized facts.

**Monitoring, metrics, logs, and alerts**

Monitoring is a core administrative skill because it tells you whether resources are healthy and whether action is needed. In Azure, it helps to distinguish between **metrics**, **logs**, and **alerts**:
- **Metrics** are numeric time-series measurements, such as CPU percentage, disk IOPS, or request count.
- **Logs** are detailed records of events, such as activity logs, diagnostic logs, or guest OS logs.
- **Alerts** are notifications triggered when a metric, log query, or activity condition matches a rule.

Use them differently:
- Use **metrics** when you want fast visibility into performance trends or thresholds.
- Use **logs** when you need forensic detail, correlation, or historical investigation.
- Use **alerts** when you need proactive notification or automated response.

Example:
- A VM shows high CPU in **metrics**.
- You inspect the **logs** to see whether a deployment, process crash, or disk issue occurred.
- You create an **alert** so the team is notified if CPU stays above a threshold for 15 minutes.

Azure Monitor is the umbrella service for these capabilities. You may also use Log Analytics workspaces, diagnostic settings, and action groups to centralize and route telemetry.

Example KQL (Kusto Query Language) query:
```kusto
AzureActivity
| where ActivityStatusValue == "Failed"
| project TimeGenerated, Caller, OperationNameValue, ResourceGroup, Properties
| order by TimeGenerated desc
```

**Cost management and optimization**

Cost awareness is part of administration even when finance or FinOps teams own formal budgeting. You should know how to identify waste, right-size resources, and use Azure tools to understand spend.

Useful tools:
- **Azure Cost Management** for cost analysis, budgets, and alerts.
- **Azure Advisor** for recommendations on cost, reliability, security, and performance.
- Tags and resource organization for chargeback or showback.

Practical examples:
- Identify idle VMs and recommend shutdown schedules.
- Review oversized VM SKUs and suggest resizing.
- Detect unattached disks or unused public IPs.
- Use budgets to alert when a subscription approaches a threshold.

Example CLI for a budget-related workflow is limited compared with portal usage, so in practice you often review cost data in the portal and export reports for analysis. For AZ-104, know the purpose of these tools and when to use them rather than memorizing advanced billing workflows.

**Azure CLI, PowerShell, portal, and Cloud Shell**

AZ-104 expects you to be comfortable with the Azure portal and command-line tools. The portal is useful for discovery, visual troubleshooting, and one-off changes. Azure CLI and Azure PowerShell are better for repeatable tasks, scripting, and quick verification.

Tool guidance:
- Use the **portal** when you need to inspect settings, follow a guided workflow, or confirm a resource state visually.
- Use **Azure CLI** when you want concise commands, cross-platform scripting, or quick checks.
- Use **Azure PowerShell** when you work in PowerShell-heavy environments or need object-oriented pipeline behavior.
- Use **Cloud Shell** when you want browser-based access without local setup.

Example CLI workflow:
```bash
az login
az account show
az vm list -d -g rg-app-prod -o table
```

Example PowerShell workflow:
```powershell
Connect-AzAccount
Get-AzContext
Get-AzVM -ResourceGroupName rg-app-prod
```

You should know the level of depth expected for AZ-104:
- You should be able to run common administrative commands and interpret output.
- You should not expect deep coverage of advanced scripting frameworks, custom modules, or complex automation platform design.
- You may see simple deployment templates or automation snippets, but the exam usually emphasizes operational understanding over software engineering depth.

**Infrastructure as code and automation depth**

Infrastructure as code (IaC) means defining infrastructure in files so it can be deployed consistently. In Azure, common tools include ARM templates, Bicep, Azure CLI scripts, and PowerShell scripts. DevOps teams often own the pipeline design, while administrators may validate deployments, troubleshoot failures, or make small changes.

At AZ-104 level, you should understand:
- What IaC is and why it improves repeatability.
- How Bicep and ARM templates describe resources declaratively.
- How scripts can automate routine admin tasks.
- How to read a simple deployment snippet and understand what it creates.

Example Bicep snippet:
```bicep
resource stg 'Microsoft.Storage/storageAccounts@2023-01-01' = {
  name: 'stappdemo001'
  location: resourceGroup().location
  sku: {
    name: 'Standard_LRS'
  }
  kind: 'StorageV2'
}
```

This is enough to recognize intent and troubleshoot basic deployment issues. You are not usually expected to design complex module architectures or enterprise-scale CI/CD pipelines for AZ-104.

**Governance, policy, and compliance basics**

Governance helps keep Azure environments consistent and compliant. Azure Policy can enforce rules such as allowed locations, required tags, or approved SKUs. Resource locks help prevent accidental deletion or modification. Management groups help organize subscriptions at scale.

Practical governance examples:
- Require the `Environment` tag on all resources.
- Deny deployment of resources outside approved regions.
- Audit whether storage accounts use secure transfer.
- Apply a lock to production resource groups.

Example policy concept:
- If a resource is created without a required tag, the policy can deny or audit it.
- If a VM is deployed in an unapproved region, the policy can block it.

This is important because administrators often implement policy decisions made by governance or security teams. You should know how to apply and verify these controls, and how to troubleshoot when policy blocks a deployment.

**Troubleshooting approach**

A strong administrator uses a repeatable troubleshooting method. Start by identifying the scope, then check the most likely control plane and data plane causes, and finally verify with logs or command output.

A practical sequence:
1. Confirm the symptom and affected scope.
2. Check recent changes, deployments, or policy actions.
3. Verify identity and RBAC if access is denied.
4. Inspect networking if connectivity fails.
5. Review metrics, logs, and alerts for evidence.
6. Validate the fix and document the outcome.

Example troubleshooting commands:
```bash
az monitor activity-log list --resource-group rg-app-prod --max-events 10
az vm show -g rg-app-prod -n vm-web01 -d
az network watcher test-connectivity \
  --source-resource /subscriptions/<subscription-id>/resourceGroups/rg-app-prod/providers/Microsoft.Compute/virtualMachines/vm-web01 \
  --dest-address 10.10.2.4 --dest-port 443
```

This method matters on the exam because many questions are scenario-based and require you to infer the next best action rather than recall a single fact.

**How to think about the exam**

AZ-104 rewards operational judgment. When you study, focus on:
- Knowing which Azure service solves a given administrative problem.
- Understanding the difference between configuration, monitoring, and remediation.
- Recognizing when a task belongs to the portal, CLI, PowerShell, or automation.
- Interpreting outputs from commands, alerts, and logs.
- Knowing the boundaries between administrator responsibilities and specialist roles.

A good rule is to ask:
- Is this an access issue, a network issue, a deployment issue, or a monitoring issue?
- Is the best fix immediate, preventive, or automated?
- Do I need to change the resource, the permission, the policy, or the alert?

**Quick reference for role boundaries**

| Area | Administrator focus | Often coordinated with |
|---|---|---|
| Identity and access | RBAC, groups, PIM, resource permissions | Security, identity teams |
| Networking | VNets, NSGs, DNS, routing, connectivity checks | Network engineers |
| Automation | Basic scripts, deployment validation, simple IaC | DevOps teams |
| Monitoring | Metrics, logs, alerts, action groups | Operations, SRE teams |
| Cost | Budgets, tagging, right-sizing, cleanup | Finance, FinOps |
| Governance | Policy, locks, subscription organization | Security, compliance |

**Key takeaways**

- AZ-104 is an operations-focused exam with scenario-based questions.
- You should be comfortable using the portal, CLI, PowerShell, and Cloud Shell.
- The Azure Administrator role spans identity, compute, storage, networking, monitoring, governance, and cost awareness.
- Metrics, logs, and alerts serve different monitoring purposes and often appear together in troubleshooting.
- Collaboration with security, networking, and DevOps teams is part of the job, but your focus is on implementation and day-to-day administration.
- Know the practical depth expected: enough automation and IaC to manage resources, not advanced platform engineering.
