## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Administrator Complete Course (Exam AZ-104)

**Governance hierarchy and scope**

Azure governance is built around a hierarchy of scopes. You use this hierarchy to organize access, policy, and billing boundaries in a way that matches your operating model.

- **Management groups** let you organize subscriptions into a tree for centralized governance.
- **Subscriptions** are the primary billing and quota boundary for most Azure resources.
- **Resource groups** organize resources within a subscription for lifecycle management.
- **Resources** inherit the controls applied at higher scopes, subject to the feature being used.

A common pattern is to place subscriptions under management groups by environment, business unit, or platform function. This makes it easier to apply Azure Policy and Azure role-based access control (Azure RBAC) consistently across many subscriptions.

**Management groups**

Management groups sit above subscriptions and are designed for governance at scale. They are generally available in Azure tenants by default, so you do not normally need to “enable” them before use.

- A management group can contain:
  - Subscriptions
  - Child management groups
- A subscription can have only one parent management group at a time.
- The hierarchy can be several levels deep, but keep it simple to avoid operational complexity.
- The special **Tenant Root Group** is the top management group in the hierarchy, not the Entra ID tenant root itself.

Management groups are useful when you want to apply controls once and have them flow down to many subscriptions. This is especially valuable for platform teams, security teams, and compliance teams.

**Why use management groups**

Use management groups when you need governance that spans multiple subscriptions.

- Apply policy to all subscriptions in a business unit.
- Delegate subscription administration to different teams while keeping central guardrails.
- Separate platform, sandbox, and production subscriptions.
- Standardize access and compliance across a large estate.

A practical hierarchy might look like this:

```text
Tenant Root Group
├── Platform
│   ├── Identity
│   ├── Connectivity
│   └── Management
├── LandingZones
│   ├── Prod
│   ├── NonProd
│   └── Sandbox
└── Decommissioned
```

This structure supports both central control and team autonomy. You can assign policies at the `Platform` or `LandingZones` level and still allow application teams to manage their own subscriptions.

**Subscriptions**

A subscription is the main container for Azure resource deployment and billing. It is also a common boundary for access delegation and quota management.

- Each subscription has:
  - A billing relationship
  - A unique subscription ID
  - Resource quotas and limits
  - Role assignments and policy assignments
- A subscription can be moved between management groups.
- A subscription can be transferred between billing contexts only when the subscription type and billing model support it.
- Directory transfer, billing transfer, and ownership changes are different operations.

Be careful not to treat all subscription moves as the same thing. For example, moving a subscription to another management group changes governance scope, while changing billing ownership is a separate process with different constraints.

**Subscription lifecycle and practical administration**

You often manage subscriptions through the Azure portal, Azure CLI, or Azure PowerShell.

Common CLI tasks include listing subscriptions and changing the active subscription context:

```bash
az account list --output table
az account set --subscription "Production-Sub"
az account show --output json
```

If you need to inspect the current context, `az account show` is the fastest check. If you work across multiple subscriptions, setting the correct context before running deployment or access commands helps prevent accidental changes in the wrong subscription.

> Subscription rename and transfer behavior depends on the subscription type and billing arrangement. Do not assume every subscription can be renamed or moved in the same way.

**Delegation patterns**

Delegation in Azure usually combines scope, role assignment, and policy. The goal is to give teams enough access to do their work without granting unnecessary control.

Common delegation patterns include:

- **Central IT with delegated app teams**
  - Central team owns management groups and policies.
  - App teams receive access only to their subscription or resource group.
- **Platform landing zone model**
  - Platform team manages shared services subscriptions.
  - Workload teams manage application subscriptions under governed management groups.
- **Environment separation**
  - Production subscriptions receive stricter policy and access.
  - Nonproduction subscriptions allow broader experimentation.
- **Break-glass administration**
  - A small number of highly privileged accounts are reserved for emergency access.

A good delegation model uses the principle of least privilege. You should grant only the permissions needed for the task and at the narrowest practical scope.

**Azure RBAC and scope inheritance**

Azure RBAC controls who can do what at a given scope. Role assignments can be made at management group, subscription, resource group, or resource scope.

- A role assignment at a parent scope is inherited by child scopes.
- Inheritance is powerful, but it can also create broad access if you assign roles too high in the hierarchy.
- Built-in roles such as **Owner**, **Contributor**, and **Reader** are commonly used.
- The **User Access Administrator** role can manage role assignments but does not grant resource management permissions by itself.

Example of assigning a role at subscription scope:

```bash
az role assignment create \
  --assignee user@contoso.com \
  --role Reader \
  --scope /subscriptions/00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000
```

Example of assigning a role at management group scope:

```bash
az role assignment create \
  --assignee user@contoso.com \
  --role Reader \
  --scope /providers/Microsoft.Management/managementGroups/contoso-platform
```

Use management group scope when you want the assignment to apply to all child subscriptions and child management groups. Use subscription or resource group scope when you want tighter control.

**Important distinction: Azure RBAC vs Entra ID roles**

Azure RBAC and Microsoft Entra ID directory roles are not the same thing.

- **Azure RBAC** controls access to Azure resources.
- **Entra ID roles** control directory-level tasks such as user management, app registration, and tenant settings.
- A management group is an Azure governance scope, not the same as the Entra tenant root.

This distinction matters in exam scenarios. For example, granting `Owner` at a management group does not make someone a directory administrator. Likewise, a directory role does not automatically grant Azure resource access.

**Azure Policy and inheritance**

Azure Policy helps enforce standards and assess compliance. Like RBAC, policy can be assigned at higher scopes and inherited by lower scopes.

- Assign policy at management group scope to cover many subscriptions.
- Use policy at subscription scope for workload-specific requirements.
- Use resource group or resource scope for exceptions or targeted controls.
- Use policy initiatives to group related policies into a single assignment.

Policy inheritance is similar to RBAC inheritance in that higher-scope assignments affect lower scopes, but the behavior is not identical.

- Policy can include effects such as `deny`, `audit`, `append`, `modify`, and `deployIfNotExists`.
- Exemptions can be used to document approved exceptions.
- Initiatives can bundle multiple policies, which changes how compliance is evaluated.
- Some policies are more effective at management group scope because they establish guardrails before resources are created.

Example policy assignment at management group scope:

```bash
az policy assignment create \
  --name "deny-public-ip" \
  --display-name "Deny Public IPs" \
  --scope /providers/Microsoft.Management/managementGroups/contoso-platform \
  --policy "/providers/Microsoft.Authorization/policyDefinitions/xxxxxxx-xxxx-xxxx-xxxx-xxxxxxxxxxxx"
```

In practice, you often combine policy with RBAC. Policy defines what is allowed, while RBAC defines who can perform actions within those rules.

**Delegation design patterns**

A strong Azure design separates governance from workload ownership.

- **Central governance, distributed ownership**
  - Central team manages management groups, policy, and baseline RBAC.
  - Application teams manage resources inside approved subscriptions.
- **Subscription vending**
  - A platform team provisions standardized subscriptions for workloads.
  - Each subscription starts with baseline policy, logging, and access.
- **Shared services model**
  - Shared infrastructure lives in dedicated subscriptions.
  - Workload subscriptions consume shared services through controlled network and identity patterns.
- **Environment-based segmentation**
  - Production, test, and development are separated into different subscriptions or management groups.
  - This reduces blast radius and simplifies policy targeting.

When you design delegation, think about:
- Who needs to create resources?
- Who needs to approve access?
- Which controls must be consistent across all workloads?
- Which exceptions are acceptable and how are they documented?

**Practical examples**

A simple governance workflow might look like this:

1. Create a management group structure for platform and workloads.
2. Move subscriptions into the correct management group.
3. Assign baseline policy at the management group level.
4. Delegate subscription or resource group access to workload teams.
5. Review compliance and access regularly.

Example of moving a subscription to a management group:

```bash
az account management-group subscription add \
  --name contoso-platform \
  --subscription 00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000
```

Example of viewing management groups:

```bash
az account management-group list --output table
```

Example of listing role assignments at a scope:

```bash
az role assignment list \
  --scope /subscriptions/00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000 \
  --output table
```

**Common exam traps**

Watch for wording that mixes up scopes or permissions.

- **Management group** is not the same as tenant root.
- **RBAC** is not the same as directory roles.
- **Policy inheritance** is not identical to RBAC inheritance.
- **Subscription transfer** is not a single universal operation.
- **Owner** at a management group does not automatically imply directory administration.
- **Tenant Root Group** is a management group, not a billing boundary.

If a question asks where to apply a control for many subscriptions, management group scope is often the best answer. If it asks about billing or quotas, the subscription is usually the relevant boundary. If it asks about resource lifecycle, resource groups are often the right level.

**Troubleshooting and operational tips**

If a role assignment or policy does not seem to apply, check the scope first.

- Confirm the subscription is under the expected management group.
- Verify the assignment is at the correct scope.
- Check for policy exemptions or conflicting assignments.
- Confirm the user is looking at the correct directory and subscription context.
- Use `az account show` to verify the active subscription before making changes.

A useful troubleshooting habit is to map the request to the correct boundary:

- Access issue: check RBAC scope.
- Compliance issue: check policy scope and exemptions.
- Billing issue: check subscription and billing account.
- Organization issue: check management group placement.

**Summary**

Management groups provide the governance hierarchy, subscriptions provide the operational and billing boundary, and delegation patterns determine how teams safely receive access. For AZ-104, focus on scope, inheritance, and the difference between Azure RBAC, Azure Policy, and Entra ID roles. The best design usually combines centralized guardrails with delegated workload ownership.
