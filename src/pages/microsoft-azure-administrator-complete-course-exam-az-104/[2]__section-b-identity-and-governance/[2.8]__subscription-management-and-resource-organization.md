## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Administrator Complete Course (Exam AZ-104)

**Subscription and scope fundamentals**

An Azure **subscription** is the primary billing and administrative boundary for Azure resources. It is also a key scope in Azure Resource Manager (ARM), which means access control, policy, and locks can all be applied at the subscription level and inherited downward.

A useful mental model is:

```text
Tenant
└── Management group
    └── Subscription
        └── Resource group
            └── Resource
```

This hierarchy matters because Azure evaluates many governance settings from the top down. If you understand where a setting is applied, you can predict what a user can do and why a deployment succeeds or fails.

- **Tenant**: The Microsoft Entra ID directory that owns identity and governance boundaries.
- **Subscription**: The billing and quota boundary for resources.
- **Resource group**: A logical container for related resources with a shared lifecycle.
- **Resource**: The actual service instance, such as a VM, storage account, or virtual network.

**Azure subscription types and billing models**

Azure subscription creation and management depend heavily on the billing context. Exam scenarios often ask who can create subscriptions, how billing is charged, and what administrative rights exist after creation.

Common billing models include:

- **Pay-As-You-Go (PAYG)**:
  - You pay monthly for what you use.
  - A user with the right billing permissions can create subscriptions directly from the billing account.
  - This model is common for small teams, labs, and ad hoc workloads.
- **Enterprise Agreement (EA)**:
  - Designed for larger organizations with negotiated enterprise billing.
  - Subscription creation is usually controlled by EA enrollment roles and billing administrators.
  - Subscriptions are often created under an enrollment account or department structure.
- **Microsoft Customer Agreement (MCA)**:
  - A modern commerce model used by many organizations and partners.
  - Subscription creation is tied to billing account roles and invoice sections.
  - Billing and ownership are separated more explicitly than in older models.
- **Cloud Solution Provider (CSP)**:
  - A partner manages billing and often subscription provisioning for the customer.
  - The partner controls the commerce relationship, while the customer may receive Azure RBAC access to resources.
  - Subscription creation and transfer behavior can differ from direct billing models.

A practical exam takeaway is that **billing ownership is not the same as Azure RBAC ownership**. A person may be able to pay for or create a subscription without automatically being a subscription administrator in the Azure portal.

> In exam scenarios, always separate **billing permissions** from **resource permissions**. A billing admin may create a subscription, but Azure RBAC roles still determine who can manage resources inside it.

**Who can create and manage subscriptions**

Subscription creation and administration depend on the billing model and the role assigned in the billing account or directory.

Typical patterns:

- In **PAYG**, a billing account owner or user with billing permissions can create subscriptions.
- In **EA**, users with enrollment-level permissions can create subscriptions within the enterprise billing structure.
- In **MCA**, billing account owners, invoice section owners, or users with appropriate billing roles can create subscriptions.
- In **CSP**, the partner usually provisions the subscription, and customer access is then granted through Azure RBAC.

After creation, subscription management in Azure often requires one of these roles:

- **Owner** at the subscription scope:
  - Full control over resources and access delegation.
  - Can assign roles and manage most subscription settings.
- **Contributor** at the subscription scope:
  - Can create and manage resources.
  - Cannot grant access to others.
- **User Access Administrator**:
  - Can manage role assignments without full resource control.
- **Billing roles**:
  - Control commerce and invoice-related actions, not necessarily resource administration.

A common pitfall is assuming that the person who created the subscription automatically has full Azure access. That is not always true, especially in partner-managed or enterprise billing contexts.

**Azure Resource Manager scope hierarchy**

Azure Resource Manager is the deployment and management layer for Azure. It uses a scope hierarchy that determines where governance settings apply.

The main scopes are:

- **Management group**
- **Subscription**
- **Resource group**
- **Resource**

This hierarchy affects:

- **RBAC**: Role assignments inherit downward unless overridden by a more specific scope.
- **Azure Policy**: Policies assigned at a higher scope apply to child scopes.
- **Locks**: Locks at a higher scope can block changes at lower scopes.
- **Deployments**: ARM templates and Bicep deployments target a specific scope.

Example of inheritance:

- If you assign a policy at the management group level, all subscriptions under that group inherit it.
- If you assign a role at the subscription level, the user can access all resource groups and resources beneath it, unless a narrower scope changes the effective access pattern.
- If you place a lock on a resource group, it can prevent deletion or modification of resources in that group.

**Management groups**

Management groups provide a way to organize multiple subscriptions under a single governance layer. They are especially useful when you need consistent policy and access control across many subscriptions.

Key points:

- Management groups sit above subscriptions in the hierarchy.
- They are tenant-level constructs, not billing constructs.
- They are used for governance, not for resource deployment.
- Subscriptions can be moved between management groups.

Practical uses:

- Separate production, development, and test subscriptions.
- Apply a common policy baseline to all business units.
- Delegate administration by department while keeping central governance.

Common constraints and exam-relevant behavior:

- A management group can contain subscriptions and child management groups.
- The hierarchy is rooted in the tenant’s management group structure.
- A subscription can belong to only one management group at a time.
- Moving a subscription between management groups changes inherited policy and access behavior.
- If a subscription is moved, inherited policies and role assignments from the old path may no longer apply as expected.

A simple hierarchy example:

```text
Tenant Root Group
├── Platform
│   ├── Shared-Services-Sub
│   └── Security-Sub
└── Workloads
    ├── Prod-Sub
    └── Dev-Sub
```

Use management groups when you need:

- Centralized governance across many subscriptions.
- Policy consistency for compliance.
- A clean separation between platform and workload subscriptions.

**Resource groups**

A resource group is a logical container for resources that share a lifecycle. It is one of the most tested concepts in AZ-104 because it affects deployment, management, and deletion behavior.

Resource group characteristics:

- Resources in a group can be deployed, updated, and deleted together.
- A resource group is scoped to a single subscription.
- A resource can belong to only one resource group at a time.
- Resources in the same group can still be in different regions.
- The resource group location stores metadata, not the resources themselves.

Why resource groups matter:

- They simplify lifecycle management for related resources.
- They provide a natural boundary for ARM template deployments.
- They are a common scope for RBAC, policy, and locks.

ARM template behavior at resource group scope:

- A deployment to a resource group can create or update multiple resources in that group.
- The template can reference existing resources in the same subscription or other scopes, but the deployment target still matters.
- If a template tries to deploy a resource to a different subscription, you must use a subscription-scope deployment or a different deployment strategy.

Example deployment:

```bash
az deployment group create \
  --resource-group rg-app-prod \
  --template-file main.bicep \
  --parameters environment=prod
```

Resource group deletion behavior:

- Deleting a resource group deletes the resources inside it.
- Most resources are deleted with the group, but some services may have soft-delete, retention, or dependency behavior that affects final cleanup.
- If a resource has dependencies outside the group, deletion can fail until those dependencies are removed or updated.

This is why resource groups are best used for resources that share a lifecycle, such as:

- A web app, app service plan, and database for one application.
- A VM, NIC, public IP, and disk set for one server.
- A test environment that can be removed as a unit.

**When to use subscription vs management group vs resource group**

Choose the scope based on governance and lifecycle needs.

| Scope | Best for | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| Management group | Cross-subscription governance | Apply policy and access across many subscriptions |
| Subscription | Billing, quotas, and isolation | Separate environments, departments, or chargeback boundaries |
| Resource group | Shared lifecycle and deployment unit | Group resources for one app or workload |
| Resource | Fine-grained control | Manage one service instance directly |

Practical examples:

- Use a **management group** for all production subscriptions so you can enforce security policy and auditing.
- Use a **subscription** for each business unit if you need separate billing, quotas, or administrative isolation.
- Use a **resource group** for one application stack so you can deploy and delete it together.
- Use a **resource** scope when you need to lock or assign access to a single storage account or VM.

**RBAC, Policy, and locks by scope**

Azure governance features behave differently depending on where you apply them.

- **RBAC**:
  - Role assignments inherit from parent scopes to child scopes.
  - A role at the management group level can grant access across many subscriptions.
  - A role at the resource group level limits access to that group.
- **Azure Policy**:
  - Policies can deny, audit, or modify resource behavior.
  - A policy assigned at a higher scope applies to all child scopes unless excluded.
  - Policy is commonly used to enforce location, SKU, tagging, or allowed resource types.
- **Locks**:
  - `CanNotDelete` prevents deletion.
  - `ReadOnly` prevents changes and deletions.
  - Locks can block operations even when the user has RBAC permissions.

Example scenario:

- A user has **Contributor** on a resource group.
- A **ReadOnly** lock exists on the group.
- The user cannot update or delete resources in that group, even though the RBAC role would normally allow it.

Another example:

- A policy at the management group denies creation of public IP addresses.
- A user tries to deploy a VM with a public IP in a child subscription.
- The deployment fails because the policy is inherited from the parent scope.

**Subscription ownership and access administration**

Subscription ownership is often misunderstood. In Azure, the person who can manage billing is not always the person who can manage resources.

Important distinctions:

- **Billing ownership** controls commerce and invoicing.
- **Azure RBAC ownership** controls resource access.
- **Directory ownership** can affect who can assign access and manage identity-related settings.

Common administrative roles:

- **Owner**:
  - Full control over the subscription.
  - Can assign roles and manage resources.
- **Contributor**:
  - Can manage resources but not access.
- **User Access Administrator**:
  - Can assign roles.
- **Billing account roles**:
  - Can create or manage subscriptions depending on the billing model.

In practice, organizations often use a small set of subscription owners and delegate day-to-day work through resource group-level roles.

**Moving subscriptions between management groups**

Moving a subscription is a governance operation, not a billing transfer.

What happens when you move a subscription:

- The subscription changes its parent management group.
- Inherited policies and role assignments from the old path may no longer apply.
- New inherited policies and role assignments from the destination path begin to apply.
- Existing direct assignments at the subscription scope remain unless changed.

Practical implications:

- If the destination management group has a deny policy, resources in the subscription may become noncompliant or blocked from future changes.
- If the source group had a security baseline, moving the subscription away can remove that baseline.
- You should review policy compliance after the move.

A common exam scenario is moving a subscription into a stricter management group and then seeing deployments fail because a new policy denies the requested resource type or region.

**Resource moves between resource groups or subscriptions**

Azure supports moving many resources, but not all. Moves are often tested because they require dependencies to be considered.

Before moving resources, you should verify:

1. The resource type supports move operations.
2. Dependent resources are included in the move if required.
3. Role assignments, identities, and references will still be valid after the move.
4. Locks and policies will not block the operation.
5. The source and destination subscriptions are in the same tenant for many move scenarios.

Common move considerations:

- Virtual machines often require related resources such as NICs, disks, and public IPs to move together.
- Some resources have service-specific restrictions or cannot be moved at all.
- References in scripts, DNS records, Key Vault access policies, managed identities, or application settings may need updates after the move.
- Role assignments at the old scope may not automatically produce the intended access at the new scope.

Example CLI move pattern:

```bash
az resource move \
  --destination-group rg-new \
  --ids /subscriptions/<sub-id>/resourceGroups/rg-old/providers/Microsoft.Compute/virtualMachines/vm01 \
       /subscriptions/<sub-id>/resourceGroups/rg-old/providers/Microsoft.Network/networkInterfaces/vm01-nic
```

If a move fails, common causes include:

- A lock on the source or destination resource group.
- A policy that denies the target resource type.
- Missing dependent resources in the move set.
- Unsupported resource type combinations.
- Cross-subscription constraints or provider limitations.

> If a resource move is blocked, check locks first, then policy, then dependency completeness. That order often saves time in troubleshooting.

**Deletion, cleanup, and lifecycle planning**

Lifecycle planning is a major reason to organize resources carefully.

Best practices:

- Put resources with the same lifecycle in the same resource group.
- Avoid mixing long-lived shared services with short-lived test resources in one group.
- Use separate subscriptions when you need billing isolation or strong administrative separation.
- Use management groups to enforce standards across many subscriptions.

Deletion behavior to remember:

- Deleting a resource group removes the resources in it.
- Deleting a subscription removes access to the subscription and its resources, but billing and retention behavior can vary by agreement and service.
- Some services retain data or snapshots due to soft delete, backup, or retention settings.

**Azure Blueprints context**

Azure Blueprints has been de-emphasized in modern Azure governance design. For exam purposes, understand the concept, but focus more on management groups, Azure Policy, RBAC, and ARM/Bicep deployments.

What to know:

- Blueprints was intended to package governance artifacts such as policy, role assignments, and ARM templates.
- Modern governance patterns usually rely on management groups plus policy and infrastructure as code.
- If Blueprints appears in a scenario, treat it as a legacy or less central governance approach unless the question explicitly requires it.

**Practical deployment and governance examples**

Example 1: Enforce tagging at scale

- Assign a policy at the management group level that requires an `Environment` tag.
- All subscriptions under that group inherit the requirement.
- Resource group deployments fail if the tag is missing and the policy is set to deny.

Example 2: Separate production and development

- Create two subscriptions: `Prod-Sub` and `Dev-Sub`.
- Place them under different management groups or under a common parent with different policy assignments.
- Use resource groups inside each subscription for application stacks.

Example 3: Shared services subscription

- Put networking, identity integration, and monitoring in a dedicated subscription.
- Grant platform admins access at the subscription scope.
- Keep workload teams limited to their own resource groups.

Example 4: Move a workload to a new governance baseline

- Move the subscription from `Workloads` to `Platform-Secured`.
- Recheck policy compliance.
- Validate that role assignments still allow the required operations.
- Confirm that no deny policy blocks future deployments.

**Troubleshooting checklist**

When a deployment, move, or deletion fails, check the following:

- **Scope**: Are you operating at the correct level?
- **RBAC**: Do you have the required role at that scope?
- **Policy**: Is a deny or modify policy blocking the action?
- **Locks**: Is a `CanNotDelete` or `ReadOnly` lock present?
- **Dependencies**: Are all related resources included?
- **Billing context**: Is the subscription created and managed under the expected billing model?
- **Inheritance**: Did a parent management group change the effective behavior?

A quick verification command set:

```bash
az account show
az group show --name rg-app-prod
az lock list --resource-group rg-app-prod
az policy assignment list --scope /subscriptions/<sub-id>
az role assignment list --scope /subscriptions/<sub-id>
```
