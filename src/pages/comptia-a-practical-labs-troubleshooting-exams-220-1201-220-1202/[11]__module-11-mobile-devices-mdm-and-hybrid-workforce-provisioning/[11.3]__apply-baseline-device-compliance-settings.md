## **Course:** CompTIA A+ Practical Labs & Troubleshooting (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Baseline compliance overview**

Baseline device compliance settings give you a consistent way to judge whether enrolled devices meet your organization’s security requirements. In Microsoft Intune, compliance settings help protect organizational resources by checking device conditions such as encryption, operating system version, and security risk.

These settings are part of a mobile device management (<abbr title="mobile device management">MDM</abbr>) approach, where you centrally define rules and then evaluate devices against them. The goal is not just to detect problems, but to create a repeatable standard for what counts as acceptable device posture.

- **Compliance policy**: the rule set you create to define acceptable device status.
- **Baseline**: a standard set of settings used as a benchmark for comparison.
- **Noncompliant**: a device that fails one or more required checks.

**What Intune can evaluate**

Microsoft Intune provides Windows compliance settings that let you require specific protections and health conditions. The evidence highlights several common checks you can use when building a baseline.

- Require **BitLocker** to protect data at rest.
- Set minimum and maximum operating system versions.
- Use Microsoft Defender for Endpoint risk level as part of the compliance decision.
- Apply device health checks to help ensure devices boot to a trusted state.

For Windows, these settings apply to Windows 10 and later, and the source also notes support for Windows Holographic for Business and Surface Hub. That makes the compliance model useful across multiple managed Windows device types, not just standard laptops and desktops.

> Use conservative settings when you are unsure. A stricter baseline can improve security, but it can also block devices that are not fully updated or not properly configured.

**Building a practical baseline**

A baseline works best when you start with your organization’s actual requirements and translate them into enforceable settings. The evidence describes compliance baselines as predefined sets of policies that help determine device status and make remediation easier.

1. Identify the security or regulatory requirements you need to satisfy.
2. Translate those requirements into device settings that can be checked automatically.
3. Create the compliance policy for the correct platform, such as Windows 10 and later.
4. Assign the policy to the devices or users you want to evaluate.
5. Review compliance results and remediate devices that fail the baseline.

This approach helps you move from a general security goal to a measurable policy. Instead of relying on manual inspection, you use the management platform to compare each device against the same standard.

**Default compliance behavior**

Intune also evaluates enrolled devices against built-in compliance checks even before you deploy custom policies. This default behavior can mark devices as noncompliant and may affect access decisions if the device does not meet the tenant’s requirements.

That means you should treat compliance as an ongoing state, not a one-time setup task. If a device suddenly appears noncompliant, check whether the issue comes from the default policy, a custom compliance policy, or a missing requirement such as encryption or a supported operating system level.

**Troubleshooting compliance status**

When a device does not show the expected compliance state, focus on the specific requirement that failed rather than the overall label. A device can be noncompliant for a single missing control, even if most other settings are correct.

- Verify the device is enrolled in the correct management scope.
- Check whether the platform matches the policy target.
- Confirm required protections such as BitLocker are enabled.
- Review operating system version requirements.
- Look for endpoint risk signals if your policy uses Microsoft Defender for Endpoint.

If you need to test noncompliance, change one required condition in a controlled way and then re-evaluate the device. This gives you a predictable way to confirm that the policy is working as intended.

**Key exam takeaways**

- Intune compliance settings help enforce device security standards.
- Baselines provide a repeatable benchmark for comparing device posture.
- Windows compliance settings can require encryption, OS version limits, and risk checks.
- Default compliance checks may apply even before you create custom policies.
- Noncompliance usually points to a specific failed requirement, not a general device failure.
