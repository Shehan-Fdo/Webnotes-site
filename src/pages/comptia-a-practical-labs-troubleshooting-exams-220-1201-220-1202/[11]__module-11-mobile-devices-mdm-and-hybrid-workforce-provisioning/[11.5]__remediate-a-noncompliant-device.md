## **Course:** CompTIA A+ Practical Labs & Troubleshooting (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Compliance basics**

Microsoft Intune uses **compliance policies** to evaluate whether managed devices meet your organization’s security requirements. A device is considered compliant only when it satisfies the conditions defined in the policy. When compliance results are connected to Microsoft Entra Conditional Access, access to corporate resources can depend on the device’s current compliance status.

- Compliance policies define the rules and conditions for managed devices.
- Noncompliant devices can be blocked from organizational resources.
- Conditional Access adds an extra layer of enforcement based on compliance status.
- The goal is to protect data and resources from devices that do not meet requirements.

**Actions for noncompliance**

Intune compliance policies can include **actions for noncompliance**, which are time-ordered actions that help protect the device and the organization. These actions can respond automatically when a device falls out of compliance.

- Actions are applied in a defined order.
- A policy can remotely lock a device.
- A policy can send a notification to the device or user.
- Notifications help users understand and resolve the noncompliant status.

> Important: Android device administrator (DA) management is deprecated and no longer available for devices with access to Google Mobile Services (GMS). If you still use DA management, move to another supported management approach.

**How remediation works**

Remediation starts by identifying why the device is noncompliant, then applying the appropriate fix and allowing the device to report a compliant state again. In practice, you often verify the device meets the policy requirements, then trigger or wait for compliance to update.

1. Check the device’s compliance status and identify the failing requirement.
2. Correct the underlying issue on the device.
3. Re-evaluate compliance so the management system can update the status.
4. Confirm that access to protected resources is restored if Conditional Access is in use.

**Common remediation patterns**

Some noncompliance cases are caused by stale status or integration issues rather than a true security problem. In those cases, the device may appear noncompliant even when checks pass.

- A device may need a fresh compliance update after changes are made.
- Integration between management systems can affect the reported status.
- Duplicate or inconsistent device records can create confusing compliance results.
- If a device is already fixed, the remaining task is often to refresh the compliance signal.

**Practical troubleshooting focus**

When a device remains noncompliant, focus on the policy result first, then the device record, then any management integration that reports compliance. This keeps you from changing settings that are not related to the actual failure.

- Verify the policy conditions that the device must meet.
- Check whether the device has recently updated its status.
- Look for mismatched or duplicate device entries if the status seems inconsistent.
- Use the compliance result as the source of truth for access decisions.

**Key takeaway**

A noncompliant device is not just a status label; it is a control point that can restrict access until the device meets policy requirements again. Your job is to identify the failing condition, correct it, and make sure the compliance state updates cleanly.
