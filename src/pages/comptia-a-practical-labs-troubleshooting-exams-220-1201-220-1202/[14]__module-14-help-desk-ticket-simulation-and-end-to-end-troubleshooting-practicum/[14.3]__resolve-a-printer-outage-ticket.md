## **Course:** CompTIA A+ Practical Labs & Troubleshooting (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202)

**Printer outage triage**

A printer outage often shows up as an offline device, a stalled queue, or a service that is not running. In practice, the issue may be a mismatch between hardware, software, and network settings rather than a failed printer. Your goal is to restore printing quickly, then document what changed so the ticket can be closed cleanly.

- Check whether the printer is actually connected but reported as offline.
- Look for stalled print jobs that block the queue.
- Verify that the print spooler service is running in Windows.
- Confirm that drivers and settings match the current print environment.

**Start with the most likely causes**

When a printer says offline but is connected, the problem is often a sync issue rather than a broken device. Misaligned settings, outdated drivers, and stalled queues are common triggers. A practical first pass focuses on the service, the queue, and the connection path before you move to deeper changes.

- **Print spooler** issues can stop jobs from reaching the printer.
- **Queue** buildup can make the device appear unavailable.
- **Drivers** may need to be updated if the printer and system are out of sync.
- **Settings** can cause the printer to report offline even when the link is active.

> If the printer outage affects multiple users, treat it as a service-impacting incident and verify restoration before you close the ticket.

**Windows spooler check**

Microsoft documents print spooler service errors as a common Windows printing problem. If the spooler is not running, print jobs cannot process normally. Restarting or correcting the service is a key troubleshooting step when the queue is stuck or the printer is unresponsive.

```powershell
Get-Service spooler
Restart-Service spooler
```

- Use the service state to confirm whether the spooler is running.
- Restart the service after you clear obvious queue issues.
- Re-test printing after the service comes back online.

**Troubleshooting flow**

Use a simple, repeatable sequence so you do not skip the obvious causes.

1. Confirm the symptom with the user and identify whether the printer is offline, stalled, or not printing at all.
2. Check the print queue for stuck jobs and clear or release them as needed.
3. Verify that the print spooler service is running.
4. Review printer connectivity and settings to make sure the device is in sync with the environment.
5. Check for driver problems if the outage persists after the basic service and queue checks.
6. Test a new print job to confirm recovery.

**Incident handling and documentation**

A good resolution is not complete until the service is restored and the ticket record is accurate. Incident closure should include the actions taken, the support group or person responsible, the time of resolution, and the time of closure. Documentation should also capture any workaround, the root cause if known, and the applied fix.

- Record the actions you took during diagnosis and recovery.
- Note the status changes from open to resolved to closed.
- Include the workaround if you used one to restore service quickly.
- Capture the root cause and the final resolution when they are known.

**What to verify before closure**

Before you close the ticket, confirm that printing works and that the user agrees the issue is resolved. Closure notes should reflect the completed work, not just the symptom. This helps keep the incident history useful for future troubleshooting and trend analysis.

- The printer produces a successful test page or user print job.
- The spooler and queue are stable after recovery.
- The ticket includes clear resolution notes and closure details.
- Any follow-up work is assigned if the issue is not fully eliminated.
