## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Security Engineer Associate (Exam AZ-500)

**Policy enforcement in sign-in logs**

Azure sign-in logs in Microsoft Entra ID show how authentication and authorization decisions are applied to each sign-in attempt. You use them to confirm whether a policy blocked access, required extra verification, or allowed the session with conditions applied. For AZ-500, the key skill is reading the log details and connecting them to the policy that caused the outcome.

Sign-in logs are especially useful when you need to answer questions such as:

- Why was a user blocked?
- Which policy required <abbr title="Multi-Factor Authentication">MFA</abbr>?
- Did Conditional Access evaluate as expected?
- Was the failure caused by the user, the device, the app, or the policy?

**Where to review logs**

You typically review sign-in activity in the Microsoft Entra admin center.

```text
Microsoft Entra admin center
  -> Identity
  -> Monitoring & health
  -> Sign-in logs
```

You can also use Microsoft Graph or Azure Monitor workflows for automation and long-term analysis, but the portal is the most direct place to inspect policy enforcement details during troubleshooting.

**What sign-in logs tell you**

A sign-in record usually includes the identity, application, client, location, device, authentication method, and policy evaluation results. The most important fields for policy enforcement are the ones that explain *why* access was granted or denied.

- **Status** shows whether the sign-in succeeded or failed.
- **Conditional Access** shows which policies applied and their results.
- **Authentication Details** shows step-by-step authentication events, such as password validation or MFA challenge.
- **Device and location context** helps explain why a policy triggered.
- **Risk information** may appear if Identity Protection is involved.

A single sign-in can be influenced by multiple controls, so you should read the record as a sequence of decisions rather than a single pass/fail result.

**Conditional Access evaluation**

Conditional Access is the most common policy framework you will inspect in sign-in logs. It evaluates signals such as user, group, app, device state, location, client app, and risk, then applies controls like block, grant, require MFA, require compliant device, or require approved client app.

A useful mental model is:

1. The sign-in request arrives.
2. Entra ID evaluates the policy conditions.
3. Matching policies are applied.
4. The strictest effective control wins when policies overlap.
5. The log records the outcome and the reason.

> If a policy is in report-only mode, the sign-in log shows the simulated result without enforcing it. This is useful for testing before you turn on a policy for real users.

**Common policy outcomes in logs**

| Outcome | Meaning | Typical troubleshooting use |
|---|---|---|
| Success | Access was granted | Confirm the policy allowed the intended user |
| Failure | Access was denied | Identify the blocking policy or authentication issue |
| Interrupted | User must complete another step | Often MFA or device compliance |
| Report-only | Policy was evaluated but not enforced | Validate impact before rollout |
| Not applied | Policy conditions did not match | Check scope, exclusions, or client type |

**Reading the Conditional Access tab**

The Conditional Access tab is the fastest way to see policy enforcement. It usually lists each policy and its result, such as success, failure, or not applied. This is where you determine whether the policy matched the sign-in and whether the control was satisfied.

Look for these patterns:

- A policy shows **Failure** when the user did not meet the requirement, such as MFA or compliant device.
- A policy shows **Success** when the user satisfied the control.
- A policy shows **Not applied** when the sign-in did not match the policy conditions.
- A policy shows **Report-only** when it was tested but not enforced.

If multiple policies apply, the log helps you identify whether one policy blocked access even though another policy would have allowed it.

**Authentication details and MFA**

Authentication details show the sequence of authentication steps. This is important when a policy requires MFA or when a sign-in fails after the primary credential succeeds.

Common examples include:

- Password accepted, then MFA required
- Password accepted, MFA completed successfully
- Password accepted, MFA failed or timed out
- Passwordless sign-in completed with a supported method

A sign-in can succeed at the primary authentication stage but still fail overall if the policy requires an additional control that is not met.

```text
Authentication Details
1. Password entered successfully
2. MFA required by Conditional Access
3. MFA challenge completed
4. Sign-in succeeded
```

**Device and compliance signals**

Many policies depend on device state. Sign-in logs can show whether the device is compliant, hybrid joined, or managed. If a policy requires a compliant device and the device is not compliant, the log should reflect that mismatch.

Use the device information to verify:

- Whether the device is registered or joined as expected
- Whether compliance status is current
- Whether the client app can present the required device claims
- Whether the user is on a managed or unmanaged endpoint

This is especially important for browser-based access, mobile apps, and legacy clients, because not every client can satisfy every device-based control.

**Location and network-based enforcement**

Policies often use named locations, trusted IP ranges, or country/region conditions. Sign-in logs help you confirm whether the request came from the expected network path.

You should check:

- The public IP address seen by Entra ID
- Whether the IP matches a named location
- Whether the sign-in came from a trusted or untrusted location
- Whether VPN or proxy behavior changed the apparent source

A common troubleshooting issue is that the user believes they are on a corporate network, but the log shows a public IP from a VPN exit node or home ISP, which causes the policy to apply differently.

**Risk-based enforcement**

If Microsoft Entra ID Protection is enabled, sign-in logs may include risk signals such as sign-in risk or user risk. These signals can trigger policies that require password change, MFA, or block access.

Use risk information to determine whether:

- The sign-in was flagged as risky
- A risk-based policy was applied
- The user completed the required remediation
- The sign-in was blocked due to risk

Risk-based controls are useful for adaptive security, but they can also create false positives, so the log is essential for understanding the exact trigger.

**Practical troubleshooting workflow**

When a user reports access problems, use a repeatable process.

1. Find the sign-in event for the affected user and time window.
2. Open the failed or interrupted sign-in record.
3. Review the **Status** and error code.
4. Check the **Conditional Access** tab for applied policies.
5. Inspect **Authentication Details** for MFA or password issues.
6. Review device, location, and client app context.
7. Compare the result with the intended policy design.
8. Adjust the policy, exclusions, or user state if needed.

This workflow helps you separate policy problems from identity problems and client problems.

**Useful error patterns**

Some failures are policy-related, while others are authentication-related. The log details help you distinguish them.

- **Policy block**: A Conditional Access rule denies access.
- **MFA failure**: The user cannot complete the second factor.
- **Device compliance failure**: The endpoint is not compliant or not recognized.
- **Legacy authentication**: The client cannot satisfy modern controls.
- **Token or session issue**: The user may need to sign out and sign in again.

> Legacy authentication is a frequent cause of policy enforcement failures because it does not support modern controls such as MFA and many Conditional Access requirements.

**Example sign-in interpretation**

Suppose a user signs in to Microsoft 365 from an unmanaged laptop outside the corporate network. A policy requires MFA and a compliant device for the app.

The log might show:

- Sign-in status: Success for password, then overall failure
- Conditional Access: One policy succeeded, another failed
- Authentication details: MFA completed
- Device: Not compliant
- Location: Untrusted network

In this case, MFA alone was not enough. The device requirement caused the failure.

**CLI and API examples**

You can inspect sign-in logs with Microsoft Graph when you need automation or repeatable analysis. The exact permissions and query shape depend on your tenant configuration, but the general pattern is to query sign-in records and filter by user, time, or app.

```http
GET https://graph.microsoft.com/v1.0/auditLogs/signIns?$top=10
Authorization: Bearer <access_token>
```

A more targeted query can help during troubleshooting:

```http
GET https://graph.microsoft.com/v1.0/auditLogs/signIns?$filter=userPrincipalName eq 'alice@contoso.com'
Authorization: Bearer <access_token>
```

If you use the Azure CLI, you typically rely on Microsoft Graph access rather than a dedicated `az` command for sign-in logs. For quick checks, the portal remains the most practical tool.

**Best practices for policy validation**

- Test policies in **report-only** mode before enforcing them.
- Use targeted pilot groups instead of broad tenant-wide rollout.
- Exclude emergency access accounts from restrictive policies.
- Review sign-in logs after every policy change.
- Correlate sign-in logs with audit logs when you change policy settings.
- Document expected outcomes so you can compare them with actual log results.

**What to remember for the exam**

- Sign-in logs show the real enforcement result for authentication and Conditional Access.
- The Conditional Access tab is the primary place to see which policy applied and why.
- Authentication details help you trace MFA and other step-by-step challenges.
- Device, location, client app, and risk signals often explain unexpected failures.
- Report-only mode is for validation, not enforcement.
- A successful password check does not guarantee overall access if another control fails.
