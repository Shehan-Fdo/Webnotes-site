## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Security Engineer Associate (Exam AZ-500)

**Overview**

Scoping security policies to a pilot group lets you validate impact before broad rollout. In Azure, this is especially important for controls that can block sign-in, require MFA (multifactor authentication), enforce device compliance, or restrict administrative actions. You reduce operational risk by testing with a small, representative set of users, then expanding only after you confirm the policy behaves as intended.

A **break-glass account** is an emergency access account used only when normal administrative access is unavailable. You exclude these accounts from most conditional access and governance policies so you always retain a recovery path. The key design goal is balance: enforce strong controls for everyone else while preserving a reliable emergency escape hatch.

**Why pilot first**

Pilot scoping helps you catch unintended side effects before they affect the whole tenant. Common issues include:

- Blocking legacy apps or service workflows
- Requiring controls that some users cannot satisfy yet
- Accidentally locking out administrators
- Creating conflicting policy combinations
- Overlooking service accounts or emergency accounts

A pilot group should be small, intentional, and representative. Include users from different roles, devices, and locations so you can observe how the policy behaves across real usage patterns.

**How Azure policy scoping works**

In Microsoft Entra ID, many security controls are applied through assignments and exclusions. You typically scope a policy by:

- **Include** specific users or groups
- **Exclude** specific users or groups
- Target all users, then exclude exceptions
- Use report-only mode where available to observe impact without enforcement

For exam purposes, remember that scoping is not just about who gets the policy; it is also about who must never be affected by it. That distinction matters for break-glass accounts, service principals, and other critical identities.

**Pilot group design**

A good pilot group is not random. It should be deliberate and easy to manage.

- Use a dedicated security group such as `CA-Pilot-Users`
- Keep membership small enough to troubleshoot quickly
- Include users who represent:
  - Standard office workers
  - Remote workers
  - Mobile users
  - Privileged administrators, if the policy affects admin workflows
- Review membership regularly so the pilot stays current

If you are testing a policy that affects privileged access, consider a separate admin pilot group rather than mixing admins with general users. This makes troubleshooting easier and reduces the chance of broad administrative disruption.

**Break-glass account principles**

Break-glass accounts are emergency-only accounts with highly controlled access. They are usually cloud-only, strongly protected, and monitored continuously. They should not depend on the same controls that might fail during an outage or misconfiguration.

Typical characteristics include:

- Strong, unique credentials stored securely
- No day-to-day use
- Excluded from conditional access policies that could block emergency sign-in
- Excluded from password expiration policies where appropriate, depending on your governance model
- Monitored for any sign-in or directory change activity
- Protected with alerting and strict operational procedures

> Do not use break-glass accounts for routine administration. If they become normal admin accounts, they lose their value as emergency access.

**Common exclusion patterns**

You usually exclude break-glass accounts from policies that could prevent recovery. Examples include:

- Conditional Access policies requiring MFA
- Policies requiring compliant or hybrid joined devices
- Location-based restrictions
- Sign-in risk or user risk policies
- Administrative role activation workflows, if they would block emergency access

A common pattern is to create a dedicated group such as `CA-Exclusions-BreakGlass` and place the emergency accounts there. Then exclude that group from the relevant policies.

**Example conditional access design**

A practical rollout pattern is:

1. Create a pilot group.
2. Assign the policy to the pilot group only.
3. Validate sign-in behavior and app access.
4. Expand to broader groups after testing.
5. Exclude break-glass accounts from enforcement policies.

Example policy intent:

- Users in `CA-Pilot-Users` must use MFA
- All other users are not yet affected
- `BreakGlass-Admin-1` and `BreakGlass-Admin-2` are excluded from the policy

This approach lets you test enforcement without risking tenant-wide lockout.

**Microsoft Entra admin center workflow**

A typical configuration path is:

1. Open the Microsoft Entra admin center.
2. Go to `Protection` > `Conditional Access`.
3. Create a new policy or edit an existing one.
4. Under **Assignments**, select **Users or workload identities**.
5. Choose **Include** and select the pilot group.
6. Choose **Exclude** and add break-glass accounts or the break-glass exclusion group.
7. Configure **Grant** or **Session** controls as needed.
8. Start in **Report-only** if you want to observe impact first.
9. Enable the policy after validation.

**Example Azure CLI pattern**

You may automate group creation and membership management with Azure CLI. Conditional Access policy creation is more commonly handled through Microsoft Graph, but group preparation is often done with CLI or PowerShell.

```bash
# Create a pilot group
az ad group create \
  --display-name "CA-Pilot-Users" \
  --mail-nickname "CA-Pilot-Users"

# Create a break-glass exclusion group
az ad group create \
  --display-name "CA-Exclusions-BreakGlass" \
  --mail-nickname "CA-Exclusions-BreakGlass"
```

Example Microsoft Graph PowerShell pattern for adding a user to a group:

```powershell
Connect-MgGraph -Scopes "Group.ReadWrite.All","User.Read.All"

$group = Get-MgGroup -Filter "displayName eq 'CA-Pilot-Users'"
$user = Get-MgUser -UserId "pilot.user@contoso.com"

New-MgGroupMemberByRef -GroupId $group.Id -DirectoryObjectId $user.Id
```

**Report-only mode**

Report-only mode is useful when you want to understand policy impact before enforcement. It helps you answer questions such as:

- Would this policy block sign-in?
- Which users would be affected?
- Which apps or locations trigger the control?
- Are there unexpected exclusions or conflicts?

Use report-only mode during pilot testing whenever the policy type supports it. Then review sign-in logs and policy results before turning on enforcement.

**Monitoring and validation**

After scoping a policy to a pilot group, validate the results with sign-in logs and policy insights.

Look for:

- Successful sign-ins from pilot users
- Failed sign-ins caused by the new control
- Unexpected exclusions
- Legacy authentication attempts
- Break-glass account activity

Useful validation checks include:

```text
Sign-in logs -> Conditional Access tab -> policy result
Audit logs -> group membership changes
Identity Protection -> risk events, if applicable
```

If a pilot user is blocked unexpectedly, confirm:

- The user is actually in the pilot group
- The policy targets the correct cloud apps
- Another policy is not conflicting
- The user meets the required device or MFA state
- The account is not accidentally excluded

**Operational safeguards for break-glass accounts**

Break-glass accounts need more than just exclusion. They require operational discipline.

- Store credentials in a secure vault with restricted access
- Use at least two emergency accounts
- Assign strong, unique passwords
- Monitor sign-in alerts continuously
- Test recovery procedures periodically
- Document when and how the accounts may be used
- Review exclusions after every policy change

A common best practice is to keep the accounts cloud-only and outside normal administrative workflows. This reduces dependency on external identity systems that might fail during an outage.

**Example policy topology**

A simple rollout topology looks like this:

```text
All users
  ├─ Pilot group: CA-Pilot-Users
  │    └─ Policy enforced or report-only
  ├─ General users
  │    └─ Not yet targeted
  └─ Break-glass accounts
       └─ Excluded from enforcement policies
```

This structure gives you a controlled test surface while preserving emergency access.

**Pitfalls to avoid**

- Excluding too many accounts, which weakens the policy
- Using break-glass accounts for daily administration
- Forgetting to exclude emergency accounts from new policies
- Piloting with only IT staff, who may not represent real usage
- Enabling enforcement before reviewing logs
- Leaving pilot groups unmanaged after rollout
- Assuming one exclusion covers every policy type

**Exam focus**

For AZ-500, focus on the relationship between policy scope, pilot validation, and emergency access. You should be able to explain:

- Why pilot groups reduce risk
- How include and exclude assignments work
- Why break-glass accounts must be excluded
- When to use report-only mode
- How to verify policy impact before full deployment

The safest design is usually: pilot first, validate with logs, then expand carefully, while keeping break-glass accounts outside enforcement paths.
