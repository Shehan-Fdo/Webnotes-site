## **Course:** Microsoft Azure Security Engineer Associate (Exam AZ-500)

**Effective permissions: what you are validating**

Effective permissions are the permissions a principal can actually use at a specific scope after Azure evaluates all applicable access sources. In practice, you validate more than a single role assignment. You check direct assignments, inherited assignments, group membership, deny assignments, custom role definitions, and whether the service uses control-plane or data-plane authorization.

A useful mental model is:

- **Identity**: who is requesting access.
- **Scope**: where the request is made.
- **Authorization model**: Azure RBAC, resource-specific roles, or service-native permissions.
- **Constraints**: network rules, locks, policy, PIM, and token freshness.

> Effective access can differ from what you expect if the identity is in a group, has just activated a PIM role, or is blocked by a deny assignment or network restriction.

**Azure portal tools for checking access**

The Azure portal gives you two different views that are easy to confuse:

- **View my access**
  - Use this when you want to see what permissions *your signed-in account* currently has.
  - It is useful for self-service troubleshooting.
  - It reflects the current portal evaluation of your access, which may lag behind recent changes if tokens, group membership, or directory state have not fully refreshed.

- **Check access**
  - Use this when you want to inspect a specific resource, resource group, or subscription and see which principals have access.
  - It is the better choice when you are troubleshooting another user, a managed identity, or a service principal.
  - It helps you confirm direct assignments and inherited assignments at the selected scope.

Typical portal workflow:

1. Open the target resource, resource group, or subscription.
2. Select **Access control (IAM)**.
3. Use **Check access** to search for the principal.
4. Review role assignments, scope inheritance, and any visible deny assignments.
5. If needed, open the role assignment details to inspect the assignment scope and condition.

The portal is often the fastest first check, but it is not the only source of truth. For deeper troubleshooting, combine it with CLI, PowerShell, and directory checks.

**Azure RBAC evaluation basics**

Azure Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) evaluates permissions by combining assignments and scope inheritance. A principal can receive access from multiple places:

- Direct role assignment at the resource.
- Role assignment at the resource group.
- Role assignment at the subscription.
- Role assignment at the management group.
- Group membership, including nested or transitive group membership.
- Privileged Identity Management (PIM) activation, if the role is eligible rather than permanent.

Azure evaluates the most specific scope first, but broader scopes still matter because they can grant access to child resources. For example, a subscription-level `Reader` assignment applies to all resource groups and resources in that subscription unless a deny assignment or service-specific rule blocks the action.

Important distinctions:

- **Control plane** permissions govern management actions such as creating a resource, changing settings, or reading metadata through Azure Resource Manager.
- **Data plane** permissions govern access to the data inside a service, such as blobs, secrets, queues, messages, or database data.
- A role that works for the control plane may not grant data access.

**Direct, inherited, and transitive access**

When a user says “I have the role,” verify how the role is assigned.

- **Direct assignment**
  - The role is assigned directly to the user, group, service principal, or managed identity.
  - This is easiest to validate.

- **Inherited assignment**
  - The role is assigned at a parent scope and inherited by child resources.
  - Common when a subscription-level role appears to “magically” grant access everywhere.

- **Group-based assignment**
  - The role is assigned to a Microsoft Entra ID group, not the user directly.
  - You must validate group membership, including nested groups if your tenant uses them.

- **Transitive membership**
  - A user may be a member of a group through another group.
  - Portal and directory tools may need time to reflect this, and token refresh may be required before the access is usable.

Practical validation steps for group-based access:

1. Confirm the role assignment is on the group, not just the user.
2. Confirm the user is a current member of the group.
3. Check whether the group is nested inside another group.
4. Refresh the user token or sign out and sign back in.
5. Re-test the target action.

**Deny assignments and why they matter**

A deny assignment blocks specific actions even when a role assignment appears to allow them. Deny assignments are less common than allow assignments, but they are important in troubleshooting because they can override expected access.

You may encounter deny assignments in scenarios such as:

- Managed application deployments.
- Certain platform-managed resources.
- Azure Blueprints or other governance patterns in older environments.

How to interpret them:

- A deny assignment usually targets specific actions or data actions.
- It can apply at a scope and affect child resources.
- It may not be obvious from a simple role list, so you need to inspect the access details carefully.

If a user can see a resource but cannot modify it, check whether a deny assignment blocks the write action even though a reader or contributor role exists.

**Custom roles and missing actions**

When built-in roles do not fit, custom roles are often used. If access is missing, validate the role definition itself, not just the assignment.

A custom role can fail to work because:

- The required `actions` are missing.
- The required `dataActions` are missing.
- The role is scoped too narrowly.
- The role definition was updated, but the assignment or token has not refreshed.
- The service requires a specific operation name that was not included.

Example custom role definition fragment:

```json
{
  "Name": "Storage Blob Metadata Reader",
  "IsCustom": true,
  "Description": "Can read blob metadata and list containers.",
  "Actions": [
    "Microsoft.Storage/storageAccounts/blobServices/containers/read",
    "Microsoft.Storage/storageAccounts/blobServices/read"
  ],
  "DataActions": [
    "Microsoft.Storage/storageAccounts/blobServices/containers/blobs/read"
  ],
  "NotDataActions": [],
  "AssignableScopes": [
    "/subscriptions/00000000-0000-0000-0000-000000000000"
  ]
}
```

When troubleshooting a custom role:

- Compare the failed operation with the role definition.
- Check whether the operation is a control-plane action or a data-plane data action.
- Verify the role is assignable at the target scope.
- Confirm the assignment exists at the correct scope.

**CLI and PowerShell validation patterns**

Azure CLI and PowerShell are useful for confirming assignments, scope, and principal identity. They do not replace service-specific authorization checks, but they are essential for narrowing the problem.

Common Azure CLI commands:

```bash
# Show the signed-in account
az account show

# List role assignments for a principal
az role assignment list \
  --assignee user@contoso.com \
  --all \
  --output table

# List role assignments at a scope
az role assignment list \
  --scope /subscriptions/<subscription-id>/resourceGroups/<rg-name> \
  --output table

# Show role definition details
az role definition list \
  --name "Storage Blob Data Reader" \
  --output jsonc
```

Useful PowerShell commands:

```powershell
# Current context
Get-AzContext

# Role assignments for a principal
Get-AzRoleAssignment -SignInName user@contoso.com

# Role assignments at a scope
Get-AzRoleAssignment -Scope "/subscriptions/<subscription-id>/resourceGroups/<rg-name>"

# Role definition details
Get-AzRoleDefinition -Name "Storage Blob Data Reader"
```

What to look for in the output:

- The exact `Scope`.
- The `RoleDefinitionName`.
- The `ObjectType` or principal type.
- Whether the assignment is direct or inherited.
- Whether the role is eligible through PIM rather than active.

> If the role assignment exists but the action still fails, do not assume the assignment is wrong. Check token refresh, data-plane authorization, network access, and service-specific requirements next.

**Validating managed identities and service principals**

Managed identities and service principals are common in automation, so you need a repeatable way to validate them.

For a **managed identity**:

- Confirm the identity is enabled on the resource.
- Find the identity’s principal ID.
- Check role assignments at the target scope.
- Verify the target service supports that identity type for the operation.
- Test from the workload that uses the identity, not only from your admin account.

For a **service principal**:

- Confirm the app registration or enterprise application exists.
- Check the object ID and application ID carefully.
- Validate role assignments on the service principal object.
- Ensure the client secret or certificate is valid if the workload authenticates with one.
- Confirm the token audience matches the target service.

Example Azure CLI lookup for a managed identity:

```bash
az resource show \
  --ids /subscriptions/<subscription-id>/resourceGroups/<rg-name>/providers/Microsoft.ManagedIdentity/userAssignedIdentities/<identity-name> \
  --query "{name:name, principalId:properties.principalId, clientId:properties.clientId}" \
  --output jsonc
```

Then validate assignments:

```bash
az role assignment list \
  --assignee <principal-id> \
  --scope /subscriptions/<subscription-id> \
  --output table
```

**Data-plane validation examples**

Many exam and real-world issues happen because the control plane works but the data plane fails.

**Storage**

For Azure Storage, `Reader` or `Contributor` on the storage account does not automatically grant blob data access. You need a data-plane role such as `Storage Blob Data Reader` or `Storage Blob Data Contributor`, and the storage account must allow Azure AD authorization for the operation.

Example:

```bash
az storage blob list \
  --account-name <storage-account> \
  --container-name <container-name> \
  --auth-mode login \
  --output table
```

Troubleshooting notes:

- `--auth-mode login` uses the signed-in identity.
- The identity must have the correct data-plane role.
- Some operations may require additional parameters or a different auth flow depending on account configuration.
- Network rules, private endpoints, and firewall settings can still block access even when RBAC is correct.

**Azure Key Vault**

Key Vault has two authorization models:

- **Vault access policy model**
- **Azure RBAC model**

In Azure RBAC mode, control-plane roles like `Contributor` do not grant secret read access. You need a data-plane role such as `Key Vault Secrets User` for secret retrieval, but access still depends on network access and the exact operation.

Example:

```bash
az keyvault secret show \
  --vault-name <vault-name> \
  --name <secret-name>
```

If the command fails:

- Confirm the vault uses Azure RBAC or access policies, and validate against the correct model.
- Check the role assignment scope.
- Check firewall, private endpoint, and trusted service settings.
- Confirm the secret operation is allowed by the assigned role.

**Azure SQL**

Azure SQL uses a mix of Azure RBAC for management and database permissions for data access. A user may be able to see the SQL server resource in Azure but still fail to connect to the database.

Validation points:

- Confirm the identity is configured as an Azure AD admin or database principal where required.
- Check database-level roles and permissions.
- Verify the connection string uses the correct authentication method.
- Confirm the client token is for the SQL resource audience.

Example connection test with Azure AD authentication:

```bash
sqlcmd -S <server-name>.database.windows.net -d <database-name> -G -U user@contoso.com
```

If access fails, check both Azure-side assignment and database-side permissions.

**Event Hubs and Cosmos DB**

Service-specific data-plane authorization is common in messaging and database services.

- **Event Hubs**
  - Use Azure RBAC data roles such as `Azure Event Hubs Data Receiver` or `Azure Event Hubs Data Sender`.
  - Validate namespace scope and entity scope carefully.
  - Confirm the client is using the correct endpoint and authentication method.

- **Cosmos DB**
  - Validate whether the account uses Azure RBAC, keys, or a resource token model.
  - Check whether the role includes the required data actions for read or write operations.
  - Confirm the request targets the correct API and account endpoint.

These services are useful exam examples because they reinforce the difference between management access and data access.

**AKS and Kubernetes authorization nuance**

Azure Kubernetes Service (AKS) has two separate layers of authorization:

- **Azure RBAC on the AKS resource**
  - Controls who can manage the cluster resource in Azure.
  - Examples include reading cluster metadata or changing cluster settings.

- **Kubernetes RBAC inside the cluster**
  - Controls what a user or workload can do inside Kubernetes.
  - This is separate from Azure RBAC unless Azure RBAC for Kubernetes is explicitly enabled.

Do not assume that an Azure role such as `Azure Kubernetes Service Cluster User Role` authorizes all in-cluster actions. It may allow access to cluster credentials or cluster-related operations, but pod, namespace, and workload permissions are still governed by Kubernetes authorization rules or Azure RBAC for Kubernetes, depending on the cluster configuration.

Validation approach:

1. Confirm whether the cluster uses Kubernetes RBAC, Azure RBAC for Kubernetes, or both.
2. Check the Azure role assignment on the cluster resource.
3. Check the Kubernetes `Role`, `ClusterRole`, `RoleBinding`, or `ClusterRoleBinding`.
4. Test with `kubectl auth can-i` after obtaining credentials.

Example:

```bash
az aks get-credentials \
  --resource-group <rg-name> \
  --name <aks-name>

kubectl auth can-i get pods -n <namespace>
kubectl auth can-i create deployments -n <namespace>
```

**Portal, directory, and token refresh issues**

A common troubleshooting trap is assuming the portal always shows the latest effective access immediately.

Delays can come from:

- Microsoft Entra ID group membership propagation.
- Token caching in the browser or client.
- PIM activation state not yet reflected in the current token.
- Cached directory data in the portal.
- Recent role assignment changes that have not been re-evaluated by the service.

Practical response:

- Sign out and sign back in.
- Refresh the access token by opening a new browser session or using a new CLI login.
- Re-run the access check after waiting for propagation.
- If PIM is involved, confirm the role is active, not merely eligible.

**Troubleshooting workflow for missing access**

Use a structured process instead of guessing.

1. Identify the exact failing action.
2. Determine whether it is control plane or data plane.
3. Confirm the principal type: user, group, service principal, or managed identity.
4. Check direct assignments at the target scope.
5. Check inherited assignments from parent scopes.
6. Check group membership, including nested groups.
7. Review deny assignments.
8. Inspect the role definition for missing `actions` or `dataActions`.
9. Validate network, firewall, private endpoint, policy, and lock conditions.
10. Refresh the token and retest.

**Case study: user can see a storage account but cannot list blobs**

Scenario: A user has `Reader` on a storage account and can open the resource in the portal, but `az storage blob list --auth-mode login` fails.

Diagnosis:

- `Reader` is a control-plane role only.
- Blob listing is a data-plane operation.
- The user likely needs `Storage Blob Data Reader` or a higher data role.
- If the storage account has firewall restrictions, the user’s network path must also be allowed.

Resolution:

1. Assign `Storage Blob Data Reader` at the storage account or container scope.
2. Confirm the storage account supports Azure AD authorization for the operation.
3. Ensure the user is on an allowed network path.
4. Sign out and sign back in, then retry the command.

**Case study: user has a role but portal still says access denied**

Scenario: A user was added to a group that has `Contributor` on a resource group, but the portal still denies access.

Diagnosis:

- The user may be relying on a new group membership that has not propagated.
- The browser token may still contain old claims.
- The group assignment may be nested and not yet reflected in the portal.
- A deny assignment or lock may block the specific action.

Resolution:

1. Confirm the group assignment exists at the correct scope.
2. Confirm the user is a member of the group, including nested membership if applicable.
3. Sign out and sign back in to refresh the token.
4. Re-check access in the portal.
5. If the action is still blocked, inspect deny assignments and resource locks.

**Locks and Azure Policy as confounders**

Sometimes the issue is not authorization in the RBAC sense.

- **Resource locks**
  - `CanNotDelete` blocks deletion.
  - `ReadOnly` blocks write operations and some management changes.
  - A user may have the right role but still be blocked by a lock.

- **Azure Policy**
  - Policy can deny creation or modification of resources that violate rules.
  - A denied deployment can look like a permissions problem even when RBAC is correct.

When troubleshooting, check whether the failure message mentions policy, lock, or deny behavior. If so, do not keep changing RBAC roles blindly.

**Useful validation checklist**

- Confirm the exact resource scope.
- Confirm whether the action is control plane or data plane.
- Confirm the principal type and object ID.
- Check direct and inherited role assignments.
- Check group membership and nested groups.
- Check PIM activation and token refresh.
- Check deny assignments.
- Check custom role `actions` and `dataActions`.
- Check service-specific authorization model.
- Check network, firewall, private endpoint, lock, and policy constraints.

**Quick reference: when to use each tool**

| Tool | Best use | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Azure portal `View my access` | Self-check for your own account | Good first step, but may lag after recent changes |
| Azure portal `Check access` | Inspect a resource and principal assignments | Best for troubleshooting another identity |
| `az role assignment list` | Confirm assignments and scopes | Fast and scriptable |
| `Get-AzRoleAssignment` | PowerShell-based validation | Useful in admin automation |
| `kubectl auth can-i` | Kubernetes authorization checks | Only for in-cluster permissions |
| Service-specific CLI | Validate data-plane access | Must match the service and auth model |

**Exam-focused takeaways**

- Azure RBAC assignment does not always equal effective access.
- Data-plane access often requires a different role than control-plane access.
- Group membership, nested groups, and PIM can change what is effectively available.
- Deny assignments, locks, and policy can block actions even when roles look correct.
- Token refresh and directory propagation matter after changes.
- Always validate the service’s own authorization model before assuming RBAC is enough.
