## **Course:** CompTia Security Plus

**Security control purpose**

- Security controls help you prevent events from occurring.
- They also help you minimize impact when events occur.
- Controls can limit damage after an attacker gets in.
- Controls protect data, systems, buildings, and people.

**Control categories**

- **Technical controls** use technology to enforce security.
- **Administrative controls** rely on policies and procedures.
- **Physical controls** protect facilities and tangible assets.
- A strong strategy uses multiple control types together.

**Control functions**

- **Preventive** controls stop or reduce attacks before they succeed.
- **Detective** controls identify suspicious activity or incidents.
- **Corrective** controls help restore systems after an incident.
- **Countermeasures** are techniques and strategies used to prevent, detect, and respond to threats.

**Examples of security technologies**

- Firewalls help protect networks from unauthorized access.
- Intrusion detection systems help identify suspicious activity.
- Endpoint security helps protect individual devices.
- Secure protocols help protect communication and architecture.

**Identity and access safeguards**

- Authentication verifies a user or system identity.
- Authorization limits what an authenticated entity can do.
- Accounting tracks actions for review and accountability.
- These concepts support secure access control.

**Risk and continuity safeguards**

- Risk analysis helps you assess threats and vulnerabilities.
- Mitigation strategies reduce the effect of identified risks.
- Business continuity planning supports operations during disruption.
- Recovery planning helps restore normal service after an incident.

**Policy-based safeguards**

- An acceptable use policy defines allowed and disallowed use of company IT assets.
- An AUP helps set clear expectations for employees and stakeholders.
- Policies support security awareness training.
- Policies matter for both on-site and remote use.

**Practical exam focus**

- Know the broad control categories first.
- Match each control to its main function.
- Use layered defenses instead of a single safeguard.
- Think in terms of prevention, detection, response, and recovery.
