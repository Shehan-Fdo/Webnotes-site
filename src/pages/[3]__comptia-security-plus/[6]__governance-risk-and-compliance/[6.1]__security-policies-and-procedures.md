## **Course:** CompTia Security Plus

**Security policy basics**

- Policies are the foundation of security processes and procedures.
- Policies support **confidentiality**, integrity, and availability.
- Policies state what you should do and why you should do it.
- Policies can be broad, such as data storage requirements.
- Policies can also be detailed, such as Wi-Fi use or remote access rules.
- Policies help define acceptable behavior across the organization.

**Security standards**

- Standards provide the formal requirements for handling situations.
- Standards reduce risk by making expectations clear.
- Standards often document password requirements.
- Standards can cover access control and physical security.
- Organizations may create their own standards for unique needs.
- Organizations may also adopt standards from groups such as ISO and NIST.

**Security procedures**

- Procedures define the steps you follow to carry out security tasks.
- Procedures help control data and services.
- Change management is a common security procedure.
- Change management adds checks and balances before changes go live.
- Change management helps prevent downtime, confusion, and mistakes.
- Change management starts by defining the scope of the change.
- Change management also evaluates the risk of the change.
- Change management requires a formal plan for the change.
- Procedures also support onboarding and offboarding.
- Procedures can include playbooks for repeatable response actions.

**Governance, risk, and compliance**

- **GRC** means governance, risk, and compliance.
- GRC aligns IT and business objectives.
- GRC helps manage risk while supporting business operations.
- GRC also supports adherence to applicable laws and industry rules.
- Governance, policies, standards, and procedures work together as part of security governance.

**Roles and policy control**

- Security governance includes policies, regulations, standards, and frameworks.
- Governance also includes risk management and privacy concerns.
- Data roles matter in policy enforcement.
- Common roles include data owners, controllers, processors, custodians, and stewards.
- Policies often define how sensitive data is handled.
- Policies can also define data classification and retention.

**Practical exam focus**

- Know the difference between a policy, a standard, and a procedure.
- Treat policies as direction.
- Treat standards as required specifics.
- Treat procedures as step-by-step execution.
- Use change management as the clearest example of a security procedure.
- Use password rules as a common example of a security standard.
