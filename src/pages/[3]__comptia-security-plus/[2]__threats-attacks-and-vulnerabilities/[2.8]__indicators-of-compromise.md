## **Course:** CompTia Security Plus

**Indicators of Compromise**

- An **indicator of compromise** (`IOC`) is evidence that a system or network may be breached.
- Use IOCs to spot harmful activity early and support investigation.
- IOCs can appear in logs, files, traffic, or endpoint behavior.
- A single IOC may not prove an attack by itself.
- Look for patterns that match known compromise behavior.

**What IOCs help you identify**

- IOCs help you determine whether malware or another compromise has occurred.
- They support analysis of suspicious changes in systems or data.
- They help you connect symptoms to a likely attack type.
- They can point to unauthorized access or data exfiltration.
- They give security teams clues for incident response.

**Common IOC examples**

- Unusual file changes can indicate tampering.
- Changed hash values can show that a file was modified.
- Large or unexpected traffic volumes can signal suspicious transfer activity.
- Traffic from unusual geographic locations can suggest compromise.
- Modified DNS settings can redirect traffic to malicious destinations.
- Account lockouts can indicate abuse or credential attacks.
- Impossible travel can suggest a stolen account.
- High resource consumption can indicate malicious activity.

**Malware-related indicators**

- **Malware** is software designed for a harmful purpose.
- Malware may damage data or systems.
- Malware may grant unauthorized access.
- Different malware types can produce different compromise signs.
- Security+ expects you to match indicators with the likely malware type.

**Examples of malware types**

- A **virus** is unwanted malicious software that can reproduce itself.
- **Crypto-malware** encrypts files or programs to extort money.
- **Ransomware** denies access to systems or data until payment.
- A **worm** spreads itself through networks, email, or messages.
- A **trojan** pretends to be a harmless application.
- A **rootkit** can provide backdoor access to a system.
- A **keylogger** records keystrokes from an infected machine.
- **Adware** produces ads and pop-ups in a browser.
- **Spyware** installs itself to spy on the infected machine.

**How to analyze indicators**

- Start with the observed symptom.
- Compare the symptom to known compromise patterns.
- Decide whether the evidence suggests malware, credential abuse, or another intrusion.
- Use multiple indicators before drawing a conclusion.
- Treat IOC analysis as part of broader incident investigation.

**Exam focus**

- Security+ asks you to analyze indicators of compromise and determine the type of malware.
- You should recognize both technical signs and behavioral signs.
- You should distinguish between similar malware categories.
- You should use conservative judgment when evidence is incomplete.

> An IOC suggests compromise, but it does not always identify the exact attack on its own.
