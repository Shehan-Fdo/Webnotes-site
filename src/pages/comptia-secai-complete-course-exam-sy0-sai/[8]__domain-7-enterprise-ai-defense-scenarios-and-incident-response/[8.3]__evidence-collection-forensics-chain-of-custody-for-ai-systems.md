## **Course:** CompTIA SecAI+ Complete Course (Exam SY0-SAI+)

**Evidence handling in AI incidents**

AI systems create evidence across many layers: endpoints, cloud services, model registries, vector databases, feature stores, prompt logs, API gateways, and identity systems. Your goal is to preserve what happened, when it happened, who accessed it, and how the system behaved without contaminating the evidence.

In practice, you collect evidence for several reasons:

- Support incident response and root-cause analysis.
- Preserve material for legal, regulatory, or HR action.
- Reconstruct model misuse, data leakage, or prompt injection.
- Demonstrate integrity of logs, artifacts, and custody records.

AI incidents often involve both traditional security evidence and AI-specific artifacts. That means you may need to preserve:

- **Model artifacts** such as weights, checkpoints, adapters, and configuration files.
- **Training and inference data** such as datasets, prompts, completions, labels, and feedback.
- **Retrieval artifacts** such as vector indexes, embeddings, chunking pipelines, and document stores.
- **Operational telemetry** such as API logs, audit logs, traces, and rate-limit events.
- **Identity and access records** such as tokens, service accounts, secrets usage, and role changes.

**Core forensic principles**

Forensic work is about defensibility, not convenience. You want evidence that is relevant, authentic, complete enough for the purpose, and handled in a way that can be explained later.

Key principles include:

- **Preservation first**: prevent alteration before you collect.
- **Minimize handling**: touch only what you need.
- **Document everything**: record who, what, when, where, why, and how.
- **Maintain integrity**: use hashes and controlled storage.
- **Keep provenance**: show the origin and path of each item.
- **Separate facts from interpretation**: notes should distinguish observation from analysis.

> If you cannot explain how evidence was collected, verified, stored, and transferred, it is much harder to defend in court or in an internal review.

**Legal hold and preservation requests**

A legal hold, sometimes called a litigation hold, is a directive to preserve potentially relevant information when litigation, investigation, audit, or regulatory action is reasonably anticipated. Once a hold is active, you suspend normal deletion, rotation, and disposal processes for the covered data.

For AI environments, a legal hold may apply to:

- Chat transcripts and prompt histories.
- Model outputs and moderation decisions.
- Training datasets and labeling records.
- Vector database contents and retrieval logs.
- Cloud audit logs, IAM changes, and access tokens.
- Notebook files, experiment runs, and model registry entries.

Practical preservation steps:

1. Identify the scope of the hold with legal or compliance.
2. Freeze deletion policies for covered systems where possible.
3. Preserve relevant accounts, buckets, repositories, and logs.
4. Record the hold start time, scope, and approving authority.
5. Verify that retention settings and backups align with the hold.

For cloud and SaaS platforms, preservation often requires provider-side action. You may need to open a formal preservation request through the vendor’s legal or trust channel so that logs or content are retained beyond normal retention windows.

**Chain of custody**

Chain of custody is the documented history of evidence from collection to final disposition. It proves who had the evidence, when they had it, and what they did with it.

A strong chain of custody record typically includes:

- Unique evidence identifier.
- Description of the item.
- Source location and system.
- Date and time of collection.
- Collector name and role.
- Hash values before and after transfer.
- Storage location and access controls.
- Every transfer, review, or analysis event.
- Final disposition or return.

A simple custody log can look like this:

```text
Evidence ID: AI-INC-2026-014
Item: Exported prompt logs from inference gateway
Source: prod-gw-02 / /var/log/gateway/prompts.log
Collected by: J. Rivera
Collected at: 2026-04-26T14:12:33Z
Hash: SHA-256 9f2c...a81d
Stored in: WORM vault / case-014 / evidence-01
Transfers:
- 2026-04-26T14:20Z to IR lead M. Chen for review
- 2026-04-26T16:05Z returned to vault
```

Best practices:

- Use unique IDs for each item or container.
- Seal physical media and record seal numbers.
- Require signatures or electronic approvals for transfers.
- Restrict access to need-to-know personnel.
- Keep the original evidence immutable when possible.

**AI-specific evidence sources**

AI incidents often require collecting artifacts that traditional incident response may overlook. These sources can reveal prompt injection, data exfiltration, model tampering, or unauthorized retrieval.

Common AI evidence sources include:

- **Prompt and completion logs** from chatbots, copilots, or agent systems.
- **System prompts and tool instructions** used by orchestration layers.
- **Vector database contents** including embeddings, metadata, and namespaces.
- **Feature stores** containing training or inference features.
- **Model registry records** showing versions, approvals, and deployment history.
- **Experiment tracking systems** with parameters, metrics, and artifacts.
- **Notebook environments** with code, outputs, and execution history.
- **Feedback and moderation queues** showing human review decisions.
- **RAG** (retrieval-augmented generation) indexes and document chunking pipelines.

When collecting these sources, preserve both the data and the context around it. For example, a vector index without its embedding model version, chunking settings, and source corpus may be difficult to interpret later.

Examples of useful artifacts:

- Export the vector store namespace and metadata.
- Capture the embedding model name and version.
- Preserve the document ingestion pipeline configuration.
- Record the retrieval top-k settings and filters.
- Save the exact prompt template and tool schema used by the agent.

**Acquisition methods and when to use them**

Choose the least disruptive method that still preserves the evidence you need. In general, you prefer read-only acquisition, exports, or snapshots over live modification.

Common acquisition approaches:

- **Logical acquisition**: export selected files, logs, database rows, or SaaS records.
- **Physical acquisition**: capture a full disk or volume image when you need complete low-level data.
- **Memory acquisition**: capture RAM to preserve running processes, tokens, decrypted content, and ephemeral state.
- **Snapshot-based acquisition**: use VM, cloud volume, or storage snapshots when supported and trustworthy.
- **API-based export**: use vendor APIs to export logs, model artifacts, or audit trails.
- **Container/image capture**: preserve container images, manifests, and runtime configuration.

Use logical acquisition when:

- You only need specific logs or records.
- The system is too large for full imaging.
- The platform is SaaS or managed and exposes export APIs.

Use physical or snapshot acquisition when:

- You need deleted data, slack space, or full filesystem context.
- You suspect tampering across multiple files.
- You need a defensible point-in-time copy of a server or volume.

Use memory acquisition when:

- You need active sessions, tokens, decrypted secrets, or in-memory model state.
- The incident is ongoing and shutdown would destroy critical evidence.

> Live acquisition can change the system. If you must collect from a running AI service, document the impact and use the least invasive method available.

**Practical acquisition examples**

Use real tools and commands carefully, and always verify that the method fits the platform and your authorization.

Logical log export on Linux:

```bash
sudo install -d -m 0700 /evidence/case-014
sudo cp --preserve=all /var/log/app/inference.log /evidence/case-014/
sudo sha256sum /evidence/case-014/inference.log > /evidence/case-014/inference.log.sha256
```

Disk imaging with `dd` should be done from a trusted acquisition environment, ideally against a write-blocked source or a snapshot, not a live writable filesystem:

```bash
sudo dd if=/dev/sdb of=/evidence/case-014/host1.img bs=4M conv=noerror,sync status=progress
sudo sha256sum /evidence/case-014/host1.img > /evidence/case-014/host1.img.sha256
```

Memory capture example using a platform-appropriate tool:

```bash
sudo volatility3 -f /evidence/case-014/memory.raw windows.pslist
```

For Linux memory acquisition, the exact method depends on the kernel and approved tooling. Use the vendor or lab-approved module and pass parameters exactly as documented for that build.

Cloud snapshot workflow example:

```bash
aws ec2 create-snapshot --volume-id vol-0123456789abcdef0 --description "Case 014 evidence snapshot"
aws ec2 describe-snapshots --snapshot-ids snap-0123456789abcdef0
```

SaaS export example using a vendor-supported export or eDiscovery function:

```text
Use the platform's admin console or legal hold API to export audit logs, chat history, and file attachments for the case scope.
```

**Integrity verification and hashing**

Hashing proves that a file or image has not changed after collection. Common algorithms include SHA-256 and SHA-512. Use the same algorithm consistently within a case unless policy says otherwise.

Important points:

- Hash the evidence immediately after acquisition.
- Re-hash after transfer or storage if required by policy.
- Store hash values separately from the evidence when possible.
- Protect the hash manifest itself; an untrusted checksum file is not meaningful.

Example:

```bash
sha256sum host1.img > host1.img.sha256
sha256sum -c host1.img.sha256
```

For downloaded artifacts, verify the source of the checksum or signature before trusting it. Prefer signed release manifests or authenticated package repositories over standalone checksum files.

You may also use digital signatures, certificate validation, or platform attestation where available.

**Preserving encryption, secrets, and access tokens**

Encrypted evidence can become useless if you do not preserve the keys, recovery material, or access context needed to interpret it. At the same time, you must handle secrets carefully to avoid expanding exposure.

Collect and document:

- Encryption method and key management system.
- Key identifiers, rotation status, and access policy.
- Recovery keys, escrow references, or HSM (hardware security module) metadata.
- Service account credentials, API tokens, and session identifiers if they are part of the incident.
- Password vault references or sealed secret locations.

Practical guidance:

- Preserve keys and tokens in a separate, access-controlled evidence container.
- Record whether the key was active, rotated, revoked, or expired.
- Avoid copying secrets into unsecured notes or tickets.
- If a secret is compromised, coordinate revocation after preservation is complete.
- For encrypted containers, capture the container file, the password escrow process, and any relevant metadata.

Example artifacts to preserve:

- `vault` audit logs.
- KMS (key management service) key policy changes.
- `.env` files from a compromised container.
- Cloud secret manager access logs.
- SSH keys or API keys found in notebooks or CI/CD pipelines.

**Cloud, SaaS, and immutable storage considerations**

Cloud evidence is often volatile and distributed. You may not control the underlying storage, so you rely on exports, snapshots, audit logs, and provider retention features.

Useful controls and workflows:

- Enable or extend retention on audit logs.
- Use object lock or WORM (write once, read many) storage for exported evidence.
- Snapshot volumes and preserve snapshot IDs.
- Export IAM, policy, and role-change history.
- Preserve serverless function code, environment variables, and invocation logs.
- Capture region, account, tenant, and project identifiers.

Example immutable storage configuration concept:

```bash
aws s3api put-object-lock-configuration \
  --bucket case-014-evidence \
  --object-lock-configuration '{
    "ObjectLockEnabled": "Enabled",
    "Rule": {
      "DefaultRetention": {
        "Mode": "COMPLIANCE",
        "Days": 90
      }
    }
  }'
```

Use immutable storage when policy or law requires evidence to be protected from deletion or alteration. Make sure you understand the retention mode, because compliance mode can prevent even administrators from deleting objects until the retention period ends.

**Anti-forensics and contamination risks**

AI incidents can involve deliberate attempts to hide activity or destroy evidence. You should recognize common anti-forensics behaviors and preserve evidence before they disappear.

Examples include:

- Log deletion or truncation.
- Timestamp manipulation.
- Prompt history clearing.
- Model rollback to hide tampering.
- Rotating secrets to break attribution.
- Poisoning or deleting vector indexes.
- Rebuilding containers to overwrite runtime state.
- Using ephemeral notebooks or short-lived agents to reduce traceability.

Contamination risks also matter when you investigate:

- Opening files on the source system can change metadata.
- Running commands can alter logs or process state.
- Copying evidence without preserving context can break interpretation.
- Mixing original evidence with analyst notes can confuse provenance.

Mitigation steps:

- Work from copies whenever possible.
- Use read-only mounts or snapshots.
- Record every command you run on the source.
- Separate original evidence from derived analysis.
- Preserve timestamps, permissions, and ownership where relevant.

**Retention, disposal, and destruction**

Evidence does not stay forever. After the case closes and all legal, regulatory, and policy requirements are satisfied, you must dispose of evidence in a controlled way.

A defensible retention and disposal process includes:

- Confirming the case is closed and no hold remains.
- Verifying retention periods and contractual obligations.
- Getting approval from legal, compliance, or management as required.
- Documenting what will be destroyed and why.
- Using secure destruction methods appropriate to the media.
- Recording the date, method, and approver for disposal.

Examples of disposal methods:

- Cryptographic erasure for encrypted storage.
- Secure wipe for reusable media.
- Physical destruction for damaged or sensitive drives.
- Vendor-certified destruction for offsite storage.

Do not destroy evidence while a legal hold, audit, or appeal is active. If you are unsure, preserve it longer and escalate.

**Reporting and documentation**

Your final report should let another qualified person understand what you found and how you found it. Keep it factual, concise, and reproducible.

A good report usually includes:

- Case identifier and scope.
- Timeline of collection and analysis.
- Evidence inventory with hashes.
- Tools and versions used.
- Key findings and supporting artifacts.
- Limitations, assumptions, and gaps.
- Custody transfers and storage locations.
- Recommended next steps.

Example report language:

- “We collected the inference gateway logs from the production snapshot at 14:12 UTC.”
- “The SHA-256 hash matched the value recorded at acquisition.”
- “The vector index contained documents from an unauthorized source corpus.”
- “We could not verify the original prompt history because the SaaS retention window had expired before the hold was issued.”

**Practical workflow for an AI incident**

1. Confirm authorization and scope.
2. Issue or confirm a legal hold if needed.
3. Stabilize the environment to prevent further loss.
4. Identify AI-specific and traditional evidence sources.
5. Collect volatile evidence first if it is at risk.
6. Acquire logs, snapshots, exports, and artifacts using approved methods.
7. Hash and inventory each item.
8. Store evidence in controlled, immutable, or access-restricted locations.
9. Maintain chain-of-custody records for every transfer.
10. Analyze copies, not originals, whenever possible.
11. Report findings with clear limitations and supporting evidence.
12. Close the case only after retention and disposal requirements are satisfied.

**Quick comparison of acquisition choices**

| Method | Best for | Strengths | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| Logical export | Logs, SaaS data, selected files | Fast, targeted, low impact | May miss deleted or hidden data |
| Physical image | Full disk evidence | Complete filesystem context | Larger, slower, more invasive |
| Memory capture | Tokens, processes, decrypted data | Captures volatile state | Highly time-sensitive |
| Snapshot | Cloud volumes, VMs | Point-in-time copy | Depends on provider and timing |
| API export | Managed services | Repeatable and auditable | Limited to exposed data |

**Exam focus reminders**

- **Chain of custody** is about documented control from collection to disposition.
- **Legal hold** stops normal deletion and retention workflows.
- **Integrity** depends on hashing, signatures, and controlled storage.
- **AI evidence** includes prompts, embeddings, vector indexes, model artifacts, and telemetry.
- **Volatile data** should be collected early when it matters.
- **Anti-forensics** can include log tampering, timestamp changes, and evidence destruction.
- **Disposal** requires approval, documentation, and policy compliance.
