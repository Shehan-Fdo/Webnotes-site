## **Course:** CompTIA SecAI+ Complete Course (Exam SY0-SAI+)

**Tabletop exercises: purpose and scope**

A tabletop exercise is a discussion-based simulation used to validate how people, processes, and tools respond to a security event. In SecAI contexts, you use it to test incident response for AI-specific risks such as prompt injection, data leakage, model abuse, unsafe outputs, supply chain compromise, and unauthorized model changes.

Tabletop exercises are valuable because they reveal gaps that documentation alone hides:
- **Role confusion** during fast-moving incidents
- **Missing escalation paths** for legal, privacy, or executive review
- **Unclear decision authority** for containment, rollback, or service shutdown
- **Weak evidence handling** for logs, prompts, outputs, and model artifacts
- **Unpracticed communications** with customers, regulators, and internal stakeholders

A good tabletop exercise is realistic but controlled. You are not trying to “win”; you are trying to expose friction before a real incident does.

**Core tabletop design principles**

Design the exercise around a specific scenario, a clear objective, and measurable outcomes. Keep the scenario believable and tied to business impact.

- **Scenario realism** matters more than complexity.
- **Defined objectives** keep the discussion focused.
- **Injects** should force decisions, not just narration.
- **Observers** should capture gaps without steering the team.
- **After-action review** should produce concrete improvements.

Common SecAI tabletop scenarios include:
- A chatbot begins exposing sensitive internal policy text in responses
- A retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) system returns confidential documents to unauthorized users
- A model update causes harmful or biased outputs in a customer-facing workflow
- A third-party model provider reports a compromise affecting API integrity
- A prompt injection campaign manipulates an agent into taking unsafe actions
- A training dataset is found to contain poisoned or unapproved content

**Roles and responsibilities**

Tabletop exercises work best when each participant knows their role before the session starts. In practice, you often include both technical and nontechnical stakeholders.

- **Incident commander** coordinates the response and keeps decisions moving
- **Security analyst** validates indicators, logs, and technical impact
- **AI/ML engineer** assesses model behavior, pipelines, and rollback options
- **Platform or cloud engineer** checks infrastructure, access controls, and deployment state
- **Legal and privacy** evaluate regulatory, contractual, and disclosure obligations
- **Communications or PR** prepares internal and external messaging
- **Business owner** decides on service impact tolerance and customer priorities
- **Executive sponsor** approves high-impact actions when needed

A useful practice is to define a RACI-style ownership model:
- **Responsible** for doing the work
- **Accountable** for the final decision
- **Consulted** for input
- **Informed** for status updates

This reduces delays when the team must decide whether to disable a model, block a feature, or notify customers.

**Escalation workflows: what they are**

An escalation workflow defines how an issue moves from detection to decision-making and resolution. It tells you:
- When to escalate
- Who to notify
- What evidence to collect
- Which approvals are required
- How to communicate status
- When to declare containment, recovery, or closure

In AI security, escalation often needs to move faster than traditional change management because model behavior can affect many users quickly. At the same time, you must avoid over-escalating every anomaly, which creates alert fatigue and slows real response.

**Typical escalation path**

A practical workflow usually follows this pattern:

1. **Detect**
   - A monitoring rule, user report, or analyst identifies suspicious AI behavior.
2. **Triage**
   - You confirm whether the issue is a false positive, a quality issue, or a security incident.
3. **Classify**
   - You determine severity based on impact, scope, sensitivity, and exploitability.
4. **Escalate**
   - You notify the correct responders and decision-makers.
5. **Contain**
   - You limit exposure by disabling features, revoking access, isolating systems, or rolling back changes.
6. **Investigate**
   - You preserve evidence and determine root cause.
7. **Recover**
   - You restore safe service and validate controls.
8. **Review**
   - You document lessons learned and update playbooks.

**Severity and escalation criteria**

Severity should reflect business and security impact, not just technical novelty. A low-severity model quality issue may not need executive escalation, while a small-looking prompt injection can become critical if it exposes regulated data.

Consider these factors:
- **Data sensitivity**: personal data, payment data, secrets, or regulated records
- **User impact**: number of affected users and criticality of the workflow
- **Exploitability**: ease of reproduction and attacker control
- **Persistence**: whether the issue continues after restart or rollback
- **Blast radius**: whether the issue affects one tenant, one model, or the whole platform
- **Compliance impact**: reporting obligations, contractual breach, or policy violation
- **Safety impact**: harmful, deceptive, or unauthorized model actions

A simple severity model can help standardize decisions:

| Severity | Example | Typical response |
|---|---|---|
| Low | Minor hallucination in a noncritical internal assistant | Track, monitor, fix in normal backlog |
| Medium | Repeated unsafe output in a limited pilot | Escalate to product and security, patch quickly |
| High | Sensitive data exposure to unauthorized users | Activate incident response and containment |
| Critical | Active compromise of AI workflow with broad impact | Immediate executive, legal, and security escalation |

**Building the workflow**

A strong escalation workflow is documented, tested, and easy to follow under pressure. It should include contact paths, decision thresholds, and fallback options.

- **Trigger conditions** define what starts the workflow
- **Notification matrix** maps incident types to responders
- **Decision authority** identifies who can approve shutdowns or rollbacks
- **Communication templates** speed consistent updates
- **Evidence checklist** ensures logs and artifacts are preserved
- **Time targets** set expectations for acknowledgment and escalation

Example notification matrix:

| Event | First contact | Secondary | Decision authority |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prompt injection affecting one workflow | Security analyst | AI engineer | Incident commander |
| Sensitive data exposure | Incident commander | Legal/privacy | Executive sponsor |
| Third-party model outage or compromise | Platform engineer | Vendor management | Business owner |
| Harmful public-facing output | Product owner | Communications | Incident commander |

**Practical evidence collection**

During an AI incident, evidence can disappear quickly if prompts rotate, logs expire, or model versions change. You should preserve enough detail to reconstruct what happened without exposing unnecessary sensitive data.

Collect:
- **Prompt and response logs**
- **Conversation identifiers**
- **Model version and deployment metadata**
- **Retrieval results and document IDs**
- **Agent tool calls and action traces**
- **Access logs and authentication events**
- **Configuration snapshots**
- **Timestamps with time zone context**
- **Hashes of relevant artifacts**

Example CLI commands for preserving evidence on a Linux host:

```bash
# Capture running process and network state
ps auxf > ps-auxf.txt
ss -tulpn > sockets.txt
ip addr show > ip-addr.txt

# Preserve logs
journalctl --since "2026-04-26 08:00:00" --until "2026-04-26 10:00:00" > journal-window.txt

# Hash collected artifacts
sha256sum *.txt > hashes.sha256
```

If you work with containers or Kubernetes, capture deployment state before making changes:

```bash
kubectl get pods -A -o wide > pods.txt
kubectl get deploy -A -o yaml > deployments.yaml
kubectl get cm,secret -A > config-inventory.txt
kubectl logs deployment/ai-service --since=2h > ai-service-logs.txt
```

> Preserve evidence before rollback when possible. A fast fix that destroys the only useful logs can make root cause analysis much harder.

**Containment actions**

Containment should reduce risk while keeping the business running as safely as possible. In AI environments, containment may be more nuanced than simply taking a server offline.

Possible actions include:
- **Disable a feature flag** for a risky model capability
- **Route traffic to a safe fallback** or human review queue
- **Revoke API keys** for a compromised provider integration
- **Block specific prompts or tool actions** temporarily
- **Roll back to a known-good model version**
- **Restrict access** to sensitive retrieval sources
- **Pause fine-tuning or ingestion pipelines**
- **Quarantine poisoned datasets or artifacts**

Example feature-flag rollback:

```bash
export AI_CHATBOT_ENABLED=false
export AI_TOOL_CALLS_ENABLED=false
```

Example Kubernetes rollout rollback:

```bash
kubectl rollout undo deployment/ai-assistant
kubectl rollout status deployment/ai-assistant
```

Containment decisions should balance speed and precision. If you disable too much, you may create unnecessary downtime. If you disable too little, the incident continues.

**Communication workflow**

Communication is part of incident response, not an afterthought. In a tabletop exercise, you should practice who says what, to whom, and when.

Your workflow should define:
- **Internal updates** to security, engineering, and leadership
- **Customer notifications** when service or data impact exists
- **Vendor escalation** for third-party model or infrastructure issues
- **Regulatory review** for privacy, safety, or breach obligations
- **Status cadence** such as every 30 or 60 minutes during active response

A concise status update format helps:
- What happened
- What is affected
- What you have done
- What you are doing next
- What users should expect

Example update template:

```text
Incident: AI assistant returned unauthorized internal content
Impact: Limited to one tenant; no confirmed external exfiltration
Action: Disabled retrieval from affected index and preserved logs
Next: Validate scope, review access controls, and confirm containment
ETA: Next update in 30 minutes
```

**Tabletop injects and decision points**

Injects are scripted events introduced during the exercise to force the team to make decisions. Good injects change the situation in a realistic way.

Examples:
- A user reports that the assistant revealed a confidential policy document
- Logs show the model called an external tool without approval
- The vendor says a model endpoint may have been exposed
- Legal asks whether the event triggers notification obligations
- A business unit requests immediate restoration despite unresolved risk

Decision points should test:
- Whether to escalate severity
- Whether to shut down the feature
- Whether to notify customers
- Whether to involve legal or privacy
- Whether to preserve or rotate credentials
- Whether to continue service with compensating controls

**After-action review and improvement**

The exercise is only useful if you convert findings into action. After-action review should happen soon after the tabletop while details are fresh.

Document:
- What happened during the exercise
- Which decisions were delayed or unclear
- Which controls worked as intended
- Which logs, alerts, or runbooks were missing
- Which owners need follow-up actions
- Which policies or playbooks need revision

Track improvements as concrete tasks:
- Update the incident response plan
- Add AI-specific escalation criteria
- Improve logging for prompts, tool calls, and retrieval events
- Define rollback authority for model deployments
- Train staff on legal and communications escalation
- Schedule a follow-up tabletop to validate fixes

**Practical tabletop agenda**

A simple 60- to 90-minute agenda keeps the exercise focused:

1. **Briefing**
   - State objectives, scope, and rules.
2. **Scenario introduction**
   - Present the baseline incident.
3. **Inject sequence**
   - Add new facts and decision points.
4. **Escalation discussion**
   - Walk through notifications, approvals, and containment.
5. **Recovery planning**
   - Decide how to restore service safely.
6. **Debrief**
   - Capture lessons learned and action items.

**Example escalation workflow in practice**

A customer reports that an AI support assistant exposed a document snippet containing internal pricing guidance.

1. The analyst confirms the output and opens an incident ticket.
2. The incident commander classifies the event as high severity because sensitive business data may be exposed.
3. The platform engineer disables retrieval from the affected knowledge base.
4. The AI engineer checks whether the issue came from indexing, permissions, or prompt construction.
5. Legal and privacy review whether the content includes regulated or contractual data.
6. Communications prepares a holding statement in case customer notification is required.
7. The team preserves logs, model version data, and retrieval traces.
8. The team restores service only after validating access controls and testing the fix.

This workflow shows why escalation is not just “telling more people.” It is a controlled path to faster decisions, safer containment, and better evidence.

**Common mistakes to avoid**

- **Treating tabletop exercises as presentations** instead of decision drills
- **Using unrealistic scenarios** that do not map to actual AI risk
- **Leaving out legal, privacy, or communications** until too late
- **Failing to define severity thresholds** before the exercise
- **Not capturing evidence** during the simulated incident
- **Skipping the after-action review** or failing to assign owners
- **Overlooking third-party dependencies** such as model APIs or vector databases

**Quick reference: what good looks like**

A mature tabletop and escalation program has:
- Documented scenarios tied to business risk
- Clear roles and decision authority
- AI-specific severity criteria
- Tested notification and approval paths
- Evidence preservation steps
- Containment and rollback options
- Communication templates
- Action tracking after the exercise

**Study takeaway**

Tabletop exercises validate whether your incident response plan works under pressure, while escalation workflows ensure the right people make the right decisions at the right time. In SecAI environments, both must account for model behavior, data exposure, third-party dependencies, and fast-changing system state.
