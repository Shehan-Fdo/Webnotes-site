**Business continuity fundamentals**

Business continuity planning keeps critical AI services available during disruptions such as outages, data corruption, model failures, supply-chain issues, or security incidents. In AI environments, continuity is not only about keeping servers online; it also includes preserving model availability, data pipelines, inference quality, and the ability to make safe decisions when automation is degraded.

A strong continuity strategy identifies what must stay running, what can be delayed, and what can fail over to a manual or reduced-capability mode. You should think in terms of business impact, not just infrastructure uptime.

- **Business impact analysis (BIA)** ranks systems by operational importance and downtime cost.
- **Recovery time objective (RTO)** defines how quickly a service must be restored.
- **Recovery point objective (RPO)** defines how much data loss is acceptable.
- **Maximum tolerable downtime (MTD)** sets the outer limit for outage duration before unacceptable harm occurs.
- **Critical dependencies** include identity systems, feature stores, vector databases, object storage, and model registries.

A practical AI continuity plan often includes:
- redundant inference endpoints,
- replicated data stores,
- versioned model artifacts,
- fallback prompts or rules-based logic,
- manual approval paths for high-risk decisions.

**Continuity planning for AI systems**

AI systems fail in different ways than traditional applications. A model may still be “up” while producing unsafe, biased, stale, or hallucinated outputs. Continuity planning should therefore cover both service availability and decision quality.

Common continuity concerns include:
- **Data pipeline interruption** that prevents fresh features or retraining data from arriving.
- **Model drift** that degrades output quality over time.
- **Dependency outage** such as a third-party API, embedding service, or GPU cluster failure.
- **Prompt or policy corruption** that changes system behavior unexpectedly.
- **Credential or key compromise** that forces rotation and service interruption.

You should define degraded modes before an incident occurs. Examples include:
- serving a previous known-good model version,
- disabling autonomous actions and requiring human review,
- switching to cached responses for low-risk queries,
- routing traffic to a secondary region,
- pausing nonessential batch jobs to preserve capacity.

**Rollback strategy**

Rollback is the process of returning to a previous known-good state after a failed deployment, configuration change, or model update. In AI operations, rollback may involve code, infrastructure, prompts, policies, feature definitions, or the model itself.

Rollback works best when you design for reversibility:
- **Version everything**: code, prompts, datasets, model weights, and configuration.
- **Keep immutable artifacts** so you can redeploy the exact prior state.
- **Use canary or blue-green releases** to limit blast radius.
- **Record deployment metadata** such as timestamps, checksums, and approval history.
- **Test rollback paths** regularly, not only forward deployments.

A rollback plan should answer:
- What is the last known-good version?
- How do you verify integrity before restoring it?
- What data changes must be preserved or replayed?
- What user-facing behavior changes during rollback?
- Who approves the rollback and who executes it?

**Rollback patterns**

| Pattern | Best use | Strengths | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Blue-green deployment** | Fast service cutover | Simple rollback by switching traffic back | Requires duplicate environments |
| **Canary release** | Gradual validation | Limits exposure to bad changes | Slower to fully deploy |
| **Feature flags** | Isolating risky behavior | Disable features without redeploying | Flag sprawl can create complexity |
| **Version pinning** | Stable dependencies | Prevents surprise updates | Can delay security fixes |
| **Snapshot restore** | Data or state corruption | Restores a known point in time | May lose recent changes |

A rollback is not always enough if the change corrupted data or trained the model on bad inputs. In those cases, you may need a full recovery workflow that includes data restoration, reindexing, retraining, and validation.

**Recovery strategies**

Recovery restores service after an incident and may go beyond rollback. Recovery can include rebuilding systems, restoring backups, rehydrating data, revalidating models, and re-enabling automation in stages.

Key recovery methods include:
- **Backups** for configuration, data, and artifacts.
- **Snapshots** for fast point-in-time restoration of volumes or databases.
- **Replication** for high availability and regional failover.
- **Disaster recovery (DR)** sites for major outages.
- **Rebuild from infrastructure as code (IaC)** for clean redeployment.
- **Model revalidation** to confirm outputs are safe and accurate after restoration.

Recovery should be sequenced to avoid reintroducing the problem. For example, restoring a database before fixing the application bug that caused corruption can immediately damage the restored data again.

**Recovery planning steps**

1. Identify the incident scope and stop further damage.
2. Preserve evidence if the event may involve security or compliance issues.
3. Restore the most critical dependencies first.
4. Validate data integrity and model behavior before full release.
5. Re-enable automation gradually and monitor closely.
6. Document lessons learned and update controls.

**Backup and restore considerations**

Backups are essential, but they must be usable. For AI systems, you should back up more than just application data.

Include:
- model artifacts and weights,
- prompt templates and policy files,
- feature store contents,
- training datasets and labels,
- configuration files and secrets references,
- infrastructure definitions,
- audit logs and approval records.

Important backup practices:
- **Encrypt backups** at rest and in transit.
- **Test restores** on a schedule.
- **Separate backup storage** from production credentials.
- **Use retention policies** that match legal and operational needs.
- **Protect against ransomware** with immutable or write-once storage where possible.

Example backup workflow:
```bash
# Example: archive model artifacts and configuration
tar -czf backup-$(date +%F).tgz /opt/models /etc/ai-app /var/lib/feature-store

# Example: verify checksum
sha256sum backup-$(date +%F).tgz > backup-$(date +%F).tgz.sha256

# Example: restore
tar -xzf backup-2026-04-26.tgz -C /
```

**High availability and failover**

High availability (HA) reduces downtime by eliminating single points of failure. In AI deployments, HA often requires multiple inference nodes, load balancing, replicated storage, and health checks that detect both service and model-level failures.

Common HA design elements:
- **Load balancers** distribute requests across healthy nodes.
- **Health checks** verify endpoint responsiveness and readiness.
- **Auto-scaling** adds capacity during spikes or partial failures.
- **Multi-region failover** protects against regional outages.
- **State synchronization** keeps critical data consistent across sites.

A simple failover topology might look like this:
- primary region serves live inference,
- secondary region receives replicated model artifacts and data,
- health checks detect primary failure,
- traffic manager shifts requests to secondary,
- monitoring confirms service and output quality.

> Failover restores availability, but it does not automatically restore correctness. You still need validation after traffic shifts.

**Validation after recovery**

After rollback or recovery, you must confirm that the system is not only running but also behaving correctly. AI validation should include both technical and business checks.

Validate:
- endpoint availability and latency,
- model version and checksum,
- prompt and policy integrity,
- data freshness,
- output quality on known test cases,
- access controls and logging,
- downstream workflow behavior.

A practical validation command set might include:
```bash
# Check service health
curl -fsS https://ai.example.com/health

# Confirm deployed version
kubectl get deploy ai-inference -o jsonpath='{.spec.template.spec.containers[0].image}'

# Inspect recent logs
kubectl logs deploy/ai-inference --tail=50

# Run a smoke test
python smoke_test.py --endpoint https://ai.example.com/infer
```

If validation fails, do not re-enable full automation. Keep the system in a safe degraded mode until the issue is resolved.

**Manual fallback and safe mode**

Manual fallback is a controlled reduction in automation when AI output cannot be trusted. This is especially important for high-impact decisions such as access approvals, fraud detection, medical triage, or security response.

Safe mode may include:
- requiring human approval for all actions,
- limiting the system to read-only recommendations,
- disabling external side effects,
- using a simpler rules engine,
- restricting the model to low-risk tasks.

You should define:
- which triggers activate safe mode,
- who can authorize exit from safe mode,
- what logs are required during the period,
- how to communicate the degraded state to users.

**Incident response integration**

Continuity, rollback, and recovery should align with incident response. If an incident is caused by compromise, you may need containment, eradication, and recovery in that order. If the issue is operational, rollback may be the fastest path. If the issue is data corruption, recovery may require restoration and reprocessing.

Useful operational distinctions:
- **Rollback** reverses a recent change.
- **Recovery** restores service and data after disruption.
- **Remediation** fixes the root cause.
- **Resilience** is the system’s ability to absorb and adapt to failure.

A mature program includes post-incident review:
- what failed,
- why detection was delayed or effective,
- whether RTO and RPO were met,
- whether the rollback path worked,
- what controls need improvement.

**Practical configuration example**

A Kubernetes-based AI service can use readiness probes, multiple replicas, and a rollback-friendly deployment strategy:

```yaml
apiVersion: apps/v1
kind: Deployment
metadata:
  name: ai-inference
spec:
  replicas: 3
  strategy:
    type: RollingUpdate
    rollingUpdate:
      maxUnavailable: 1
      maxSurge: 1
  template:
    spec:
      containers:
        - name: inference
          image: registry.example.com/ai-inference:v2.4.1
          readinessProbe:
            httpGet:
              path: /ready
              port: 8080
            initialDelaySeconds: 10
            periodSeconds: 5
```

This setup supports controlled updates and quick rollback to a prior image tag if the new version fails validation.

**Common exam takeaways**

- **Continuity** focuses on keeping essential services available during disruption.
- **Rollback** returns the environment to a previous known-good state.
- **Recovery** restores service, data, and trust after an incident.
- **RTO** and **RPO** drive design choices for backups, replication, and failover.
- **AI systems** require validation of both infrastructure and model behavior.
- **Safe mode** reduces risk when automation cannot be trusted.
- **Testing** rollback and restore procedures is as important as creating them.

**Quick comparison**

| Concept | Primary goal | Typical trigger |
|---|---|---|
| **Business continuity** | Keep critical operations running | Outage, disruption, dependency failure |
| **Rollback** | Undo a bad change | Failed deployment, bad model update |
| **Recovery** | Restore service and data | Disaster, corruption, compromise |
| **Failover** | Shift traffic to a healthy system | Site or node failure |
| **Safe mode** | Reduce risk while degraded | Untrusted model behavior |

**Study checklist**

- Define RTO, RPO, and MTD for each critical AI service.
- Version prompts, models, data, and infrastructure definitions.
- Test rollback and restore procedures on a schedule.
- Include manual fallback for high-risk decisions.
- Validate model behavior after every recovery.
- Keep backups encrypted, isolated, and restorable.
- Document who approves failover, rollback, and re-enablement.
