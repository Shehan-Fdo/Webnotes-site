## **Course:** CompTIA SecAI+ Complete Course (Exam SY0-SAI+)

**ISO/IEC AI governance standards overview**

ISO/IEC AI governance standards give you a structured way to manage AI systems across their lifecycle, from design and deployment to monitoring, change control, and retirement. For exam purposes, the most important distinction is between **requirements** standards and **guidance** standards:

- **ISO/IEC 42001** defines requirements for an **AI management system (AIMS)** and is the primary certifiable management-system standard.
- **ISO/IEC 42005** provides guidance for **AI impact assessment**, helping you evaluate potential effects on people, organizations, and society.
- **ISO/IEC 23894** provides guidance for **AI risk management**, focusing on identifying, analyzing, evaluating, and treating AI-related risk.

A useful exam shortcut is to remember this relationship:

- **42001 = what you must have** in the management system.
- **42005 = how to assess impact** in a structured way.
- **23894 = how to manage AI risk** using a risk-management process.

These standards are often used together. In practice, 42001 sets the governance framework, while 42005 and 23894 support the evidence and methods used inside that framework.

**ISO/IEC 42001: AI management system fundamentals**

ISO/IEC 42001 is modeled after other management-system standards, so it uses familiar concepts such as context, leadership, planning, support, operation, performance evaluation, and improvement. That structure matters because exam questions often ask you to identify where a control belongs in the lifecycle.

The standard expects you to establish, implement, maintain, and continually improve an AIMS. The AIMS should be proportionate to the organization’s AI use cases, risk profile, and regulatory obligations.

Key management-system expectations include:

- **Scope**: define which AI systems, business units, products, services, and locations are covered.
- **Policy**: publish an AI governance policy that states intent, principles, and accountability.
- **Objectives**: set measurable AI governance objectives, such as bias reduction targets or review SLAs.
- **Roles and responsibilities**: assign ownership for risk, approval, monitoring, and escalation.
- **Competence**: ensure people operating or governing AI are trained for their responsibilities.
- **Communication**: define what is communicated, to whom, when, and by what method.
- **Documented information**: maintain records, procedures, evidence, and retained outputs needed to prove control operation.

AIMS documentation is a common exam theme because auditors look for evidence, not just intent. Typical documented information includes:

- AIMS scope statement
- AI governance policy
- Risk assessment and treatment records
- Impact assessment reports
- Model approval and exception records
- Competence and training records
- Internal audit reports
- Management review minutes and action items
- Corrective action records
- Supplier and third-party due diligence records

A practical documentation pattern looks like this:

```yaml
aims:
  scope: "Customer support chatbot, fraud detection model, and HR screening assistant"
  owner: "AI Governance Committee"
  review_cadence: "Quarterly"
  approval_required_for:
    - "New model deployment"
    - "Material model retraining"
    - "New data source"
    - "New user population"
  records:
    - "risk-register"
    - "impact-assessment"
    - "audit-log"
    - "training-completion"
```

**How the management-system lifecycle is tested**

ISO management-system questions often focus on the control loop: plan, do, check, act. In AI governance, that loop becomes especially important because models drift, data changes, and deployment contexts evolve.

Typical lifecycle elements include:

- **Planning**
  - define scope and governance objectives
  - identify AI use cases and classify them by criticality or risk
  - establish risk criteria and approval thresholds
- **Operation**
  - perform assessments before deployment
  - approve changes through formal review
  - monitor model behavior, incidents, and drift
- **Performance evaluation**
  - run internal audits
  - review metrics, incidents, and compliance status
  - assess whether controls are effective
- **Improvement**
  - handle nonconformities
  - implement corrective actions
  - update controls after lessons learned

A common exam trap is confusing a one-time assessment with ongoing governance. In reality, AI governance is continuous because the system can change after deployment.

**AI system inventory and use-case categorization**

AIMS governance starts with knowing what AI you have. An **AI system inventory** is the authoritative list of AI assets, models, services, and use cases in scope. Without it, you cannot reliably assign controls, owners, or review frequency.

Inventory records usually include:

- system name and business purpose
- model type and version
- data sources and data sensitivity
- deployment environment
- business owner and technical owner
- user population and affected stakeholders
- decision impact level
- third-party dependencies
- monitoring and review cadence

Use-case categorization helps you decide how deep governance should go. For example:

- **Low-impact use cases** may need lighter review and standard monitoring.
- **High-impact use cases** may require formal impact assessment, human oversight, stronger testing, and executive approval.
- **Regulated or safety-sensitive use cases** often need stricter evidence, traceability, and change control.

A simple categorization example:

```text
Use case: Resume screening assistant
Category: High impact / employment decision support
Controls:
- Mandatory impact assessment
- Bias and fairness testing
- Human review before final decision
- Quarterly governance review
- Supplier contract review
```

**Annex A control themes and how they are organized**

For exam prep, you should know that Annex A in ISO/IEC 42001 groups AI management controls into themes rather than isolated technical settings. The exact wording can vary by edition and summary source, but the control themes are commonly organized around the following governance areas:

- **Policies and governance**
  - AI policy direction
  - leadership accountability
  - governance roles and oversight
- **AI system inventory and classification**
  - identification of AI systems and use cases
  - categorization by risk, impact, or criticality
- **Impact assessment**
  - assessment of potential effects on individuals, groups, and organizations
  - review of intended and unintended consequences
- **Risk treatment and control selection**
  - selecting controls based on risk
  - documenting residual risk and acceptance
- **Data governance**
  - data quality, provenance, suitability, and lifecycle controls
- **Model development and validation**
  - design, testing, verification, and performance evaluation
- **Deployment and operation**
  - release approval, monitoring, logging, and rollback readiness
- **Human oversight**
  - review, intervention, escalation, and override mechanisms
- **Supplier and third-party governance**
  - outsourced AI, external models, and shared responsibility
- **Incident and nonconformity handling**
  - reporting, investigation, correction, and lessons learned
- **Documentation and traceability**
  - records, evidence, and auditability
- **Continual improvement**
  - corrective action and governance updates

The exam-relevant point is that Annex A is not just a checklist of technical safeguards. It is a governance control set that supports accountability, traceability, and lifecycle management.

**AI risk management with ISO/IEC 23894**

ISO/IEC 23894 is guidance for AI risk management, so it helps you build a process rather than imposing mandatory requirements. It aligns well with general risk-management thinking, but it emphasizes AI-specific concerns such as model behavior, data dependence, and human interaction.

A typical AI risk management process includes:

1. Establish the context and risk criteria.
2. Identify AI risks across the lifecycle.
3. Analyze likelihood and impact.
4. Evaluate whether the risk is acceptable.
5. Treat the risk using controls, transfer, avoidance, or acceptance.
6. Monitor and review continuously.

AI-specific risk categories commonly tested include:

- **Robustness**: the model fails under edge cases, adversarial inputs, or distribution shift.
- **Explainability**: stakeholders cannot understand why the system produced an output.
- **Accountability**: no clear owner exists for decisions, exceptions, or incidents.
- **Security**: prompt injection, model extraction, data poisoning, or adversarial manipulation.
- **Privacy**: personal data leakage, excessive collection, or inference of sensitive attributes.
- **Bias and fairness**: disparate impact across protected or vulnerable groups.
- **Human oversight**: operators over-trust the model or cannot intervene effectively.
- **Safety**: outputs create physical, financial, legal, or reputational harm.
- **Reliability**: inconsistent outputs, hallucinations, or unstable performance.
- **Compliance**: the system violates legal, contractual, or policy obligations.

A practical risk register entry might look like this:

```text
Risk ID: AI-R-014
Use case: Customer support chatbot
Risk: Hallucinated policy advice causes customer harm
Category: Reliability / compliance
Likelihood: Medium
Impact: High
Treatment:
- Retrieval-augmented generation
- Approved knowledge base only
- Confidence threshold and escalation to human agent
- Logging and sampling review
Owner: Product Security and Support Operations
Review cadence: Monthly
```

**Impact assessment under ISO/IEC 42005**

ISO/IEC 42005 is guidance for AI impact assessment. It helps you evaluate how an AI system may affect people, organizations, and society before and during use. This is broader than technical risk because it includes social and organizational consequences.

Impact assessment usually asks:

- Who can be affected?
- What decisions does the AI influence?
- What harms could occur?
- Are the effects direct or indirect?
- Are vulnerable groups disproportionately affected?
- What safeguards and human oversight exist?
- Is the residual impact acceptable?

Common triggers for repeating or updating an impact assessment include:

- material model changes
- new training or fine-tuning data sources
- new deployment context or business process
- new user population or affected population
- new jurisdiction or regulatory environment
- significant performance degradation
- incident, complaint, or adverse event
- change in supplier, foundation model, or hosting architecture

A strong exam answer distinguishes impact from risk:

- **Impact assessment** focuses on consequences and affected stakeholders.
- **Risk management** focuses on likelihood, controls, and residual risk.

They overlap, but they are not identical.

**Supplier and third-party governance**

AI systems often depend on external models, APIs, data providers, cloud platforms, and integrators. That creates shared responsibility, which means you cannot outsource accountability just because you outsource the technology.

Supplier governance should cover:

- due diligence before onboarding
- security, privacy, and legal review
- model transparency and documentation
- service-level expectations and incident notification
- data usage restrictions
- audit rights and evidence access
- subcontractor controls
- exit and portability planning

For outsourced or shared AI services, you should define who owns:

- model performance monitoring
- prompt and output logging
- incident response
- retraining or updates
- regulatory disclosures
- customer communications
- approval for material changes

A contract control example:

```text
Supplier clause:
- Provider must notify customer within 24 hours of any material model change.
- Provider must disclose training-data restrictions and known limitations.
- Customer retains approval authority for production use in regulated workflows.
- Provider must support audit evidence requests within 10 business days.
```

A common exam point is that third-party use does not remove the need for internal governance. You still need oversight, acceptance criteria, and documented accountability.

**Documentation, records, and audit evidence**

ISO management-system standards are evidence-driven. Auditors typically evaluate whether you can show that controls exist, are implemented, and are effective.

You should expect to maintain documented information for:

- scope and applicability decisions
- policy and objectives
- risk and impact assessments
- approval and exception workflows
- competence and training
- monitoring results and incident logs
- internal audit findings
- management review outputs
- corrective actions and closure evidence
- supplier assessments and contracts

A practical evidence set for an AI use case might include:

- model card or system description
- data sheet or dataset provenance record
- validation report
- bias and fairness test results
- human oversight procedure
- rollback plan
- monitoring dashboard screenshots or exports
- change approval ticket
- incident postmortem

**Nonconformity and corrective action**

A **nonconformity** is a failure to meet a requirement, policy, or expected control. In AI governance, examples include missing approvals, unreviewed model changes, incomplete logs, or a failed bias test that was ignored.

A corrective action workflow usually follows this pattern:

1. Detect the nonconformity.
2. Contain the issue if needed.
3. Record the finding and assign ownership.
4. Analyze root cause.
5. Implement corrective action.
6. Verify effectiveness.
7. Close the record.

Example workflow:

```text
Finding: Production model updated without governance approval
Containment: Roll back to approved version
Root cause: CI/CD pipeline lacked release gate
Corrective action:
- Add mandatory approval step
- Require evidence attachment before deployment
- Train release engineers
Effectiveness check: No unapproved releases for 90 days
```

The exam may ask you to distinguish corrective action from correction:

- **Correction** fixes the immediate issue.
- **Corrective action** removes the cause so it does not recur.

**Internal audit program and management review**

An internal audit program checks whether the AIMS conforms to requirements and works as intended. It should be risk-based and cover the full scope over time, not just one model or one team.

A good internal audit program includes:

- audit scope and criteria
- audit frequency based on risk
- auditor independence
- evidence collection methods
- reporting and follow-up
- tracking of findings to closure

Management review is the leadership checkpoint. It evaluates whether the AIMS remains suitable, adequate, and effective.

Typical management review inputs include:

- audit results
- nonconformities and corrective actions
- monitoring and measurement results
- incident trends
- supplier performance
- status of objectives
- changes in context, risks, or obligations
- stakeholder feedback

Typical outputs include:

- decisions on improvements
- resource allocation
- policy or objective updates
- risk acceptance or escalation decisions
- action items with owners and deadlines

A simple review cadence example:

```text
AI Governance Committee
- Monthly: operational risk review
- Quarterly: management review
- Annually: full AIMS effectiveness review and audit plan refresh
```

**Practical governance topology**

A common enterprise AI governance topology separates strategic oversight, operational control, and technical execution.

```text
Board / Executive Leadership
        |
        v
AI Governance Committee
        |
        +--> Risk & Compliance
        +--> Security & Privacy
        +--> Legal & Procurement
        +--> Product / Business Owner
        +--> ML / Data Engineering
        |
        v
Model Approval + Monitoring + Incident Response
```

This structure helps you assign accountability without overloading a single team. It also supports segregation of duties, which auditors often value.

**Certification and audit implications**

Because ISO/IEC 42001 is a management-system standard, organizations may seek external certification readiness. That means they need more than a good policy; they need repeatable evidence that the system operates consistently.

External auditors commonly look for:

- a defined scope
- leadership commitment
- documented objectives and responsibilities
- evidence of risk and impact assessments
- operational controls and monitoring
- internal audit results
- management review records
- corrective action closure
- continual improvement evidence

Surveillance audits, where applicable, typically check whether the organization continues to operate the AIMS effectively after initial certification. A common failure pattern is strong documentation but weak operational evidence, such as missing logs, stale risk reviews, or untracked exceptions.

**Exam differentiation tips**

Use these distinctions to answer scenario questions accurately:

- **42001** is the management-system requirement standard.
- **42005** is guidance for impact assessment.
- **23894** is guidance for AI risk management.
- **Impact assessment** asks what could happen and who is affected.
- **Risk management** asks how likely it is, how severe it is, and what controls reduce it.
- **Inventory and classification** determine governance depth.
- **Supplier governance** still requires internal accountability.
- **Corrective action** addresses root cause, not just the immediate symptom.
- **Management review** is leadership oversight, not technical testing.

**Quick reference checklist**

- Define the AIMS scope and inventory all AI systems.
- Classify use cases by impact and governance depth.
- Publish policy, objectives, roles, and communication paths.
- Maintain documented information and audit-ready records.
- Perform impact assessments and repeat them when material changes occur.
- Run AI risk management with AI-specific categories such as bias, privacy, security, robustness, and explainability.
- Control suppliers through contracts, oversight, and evidence requirements.
- Operate internal audits, management reviews, and corrective actions on a recurring cadence.
- Treat certification readiness as an evidence problem, not just a policy problem.
