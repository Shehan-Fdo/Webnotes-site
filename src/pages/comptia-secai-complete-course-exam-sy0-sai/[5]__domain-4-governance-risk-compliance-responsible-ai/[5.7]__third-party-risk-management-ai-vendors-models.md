## **Course:** CompTIA SecAI+ Complete Course (Exam SY0-SAI+)

**Third-party risk management overview**

AI vendors expand capability quickly, but they also expand your attack surface, data exposure, and compliance burden. Third-party risk management (TPRM) for AI is the process of evaluating, contracting, monitoring, and periodically reassessing external providers that supply models, APIs, datasets, hosting, labeling, or managed AI services.

The key difference from traditional vendor risk is that AI vendors can change behavior without a classic software release cycle. Model updates, prompt-routing changes, retraining, new subprocessors, and policy changes can all alter risk. You need to assess not only the vendor’s security posture, but also the model lifecycle, data handling, output safety, and operational resilience.

- **Vendor scope** includes model providers, API aggregators, data brokers, annotation firms, MLOps platforms, and managed AI service providers.
- **Model risk** includes hallucinations, unsafe outputs, bias, drift, prompt injection exposure, and unauthorized data retention.
- **Third-party exposure** often includes customer data, prompts, embeddings, logs, fine-tuning datasets, and telemetry.
- **Control ownership** must be explicit so you know which risks are handled by the vendor and which remain your responsibility.

**AI vendor due diligence intake**

A strong intake process starts with a structured questionnaire and supporting evidence. You should ask for both policy-level answers and proof artifacts, because AI vendors often describe controls in marketing terms that need validation.

A practical intake packet usually includes:
- Security questionnaire responses
- Privacy and data processing addendum
- AI governance or model risk management policy
- Incident response summary and breach history
- Subprocessor list and data flow diagram
- Architecture overview for the AI service
- Model lifecycle documentation
- Business continuity and disaster recovery summary
- Independent assurance reports, if available, such as SOC 2 or ISO 27001

The questionnaire should be specific to AI, not just generic cloud security. Focus on how the vendor trains, hosts, updates, and monitors models, and how they protect customer data across the full lifecycle.

**Core questionnaire areas**

- **Security controls**
  - Authentication methods, MFA (multifactor authentication), encryption, logging, vulnerability management, and secure SDLC (software development life cycle).
  - Segmentation between customer tenants and between training, inference, and support environments.
- **Privacy and data use**
  - Whether prompts, outputs, embeddings, and uploaded files are used for training or product improvement.
  - Retention periods, deletion workflows, and data residency options.
  - Whether the vendor acts as a processor, subprocessor, or controller under applicable privacy law.
- **AI governance**
  - Human review requirements for high-impact outputs.
  - Model approval gates before production release.
  - Testing for bias, toxicity, jailbreak resistance, and unsafe content.
  - Rollback procedures if a model update causes harmful behavior.
- **Incident history**
  - Prior security incidents, model safety incidents, privacy complaints, and regulatory inquiries.
  - Root cause analysis quality and remediation timelines.
- **Subcontractors and subprocessors**
  - Which third parties can access data, logs, or model artifacts.
  - Whether subprocessors are approved, monitored, and contractually bound.
- **Model lifecycle**
  - Training data provenance, retraining cadence, evaluation methods, and deprecation policy.
  - Versioning, change control, and release notes for model updates.
- **Business continuity**
  - Failover design, recovery objectives, dependency mapping, and outage handling for model/API downtime.

A useful intake question set is shown below.

```text
Security
- Do you enforce MFA for all administrative access?
- How are customer environments segmented from internal support systems?
- Are logs immutable or protected from tampering?

Privacy and data handling
- Are prompts, outputs, and uploaded files used for training by default?
- What is the retention period for customer content and telemetry?
- How do customers request deletion, export, or restriction of data use?

AI governance
- What approval is required before a model update reaches production?
- Do you perform human review for high-risk outputs?
- How do you test for hallucinations, bias, and prompt injection?

Incident and resilience
- What AI-specific incidents occurred in the last 12 months?
- What are your RTO and RPO targets for the service?
- Have you tested failover for model/API outages?

Subprocessors and access
- Which subprocessors can access customer data or logs?
- Do support engineers use privileged accounts with just-in-time access?
- Is vendor support separated from production administration?
```

**Third-party tiering and classification**

You should classify AI vendors by business criticality and data sensitivity so the depth of review matches the risk. A tiering framework helps you decide how much evidence to collect, how often to reassess, and what contractual controls to require.

Common tiers include:
- **Critical**
  - The AI service is essential to core operations, customer-facing decisions, or regulated workflows.
  - Failure, compromise, or unsafe behavior would cause major operational, legal, or safety impact.
- **High**
  - The service handles sensitive data or materially influences business decisions, but there is some fallback capability.
- **Medium**
  - The service supports internal productivity or low-risk use cases with limited data exposure.
- **Low**
  - The service has minimal data access and limited operational impact if unavailable.

Tiering should drive review depth and cadence:
- **Critical vendors**
  - Full due diligence, executive approval, contract review, security architecture review, and frequent monitoring.
  - Reassess at least annually, and often quarterly for key metrics.
- **High vendors**
  - Detailed questionnaire, evidence review, and periodic control validation.
  - Reassess semiannually or on major change.
- **Medium vendors**
  - Standard questionnaire and baseline evidence.
  - Reassess annually or on trigger events.
- **Low vendors**
  - Lightweight review with focused privacy and security checks.
  - Reassess on renewal or material change.

A simple classification model can be expressed like this:

| Tier | Typical data | Business impact | Review depth | Monitoring cadence |
|---|---|---:|---|---|
| Critical | Regulated, confidential, or customer-impacting | Very high | Full due diligence | Quarterly or continuous |
| High | Sensitive internal or customer data | High | Detailed review | Semiannual |
| Medium | Limited internal data | Moderate | Standard review | Annual |
| Low | Public or non-sensitive data | Low | Basic review | Renewal-based |

**Model governance controls**

AI vendors should not be treated as static software suppliers. Their models can change behavior after retraining, fine-tuning, safety-tuning, or prompt-routing updates. You need governance controls that make those changes visible and reversible.

Important controls include:
- **Human-in-the-loop** review for high-risk decisions, especially where outputs affect hiring, credit, healthcare, legal, or safety outcomes.
- **Approval gates** before production deployment of new model versions, prompt templates, or safety policies.
- **Testing gates** for bias, toxicity, prompt injection resistance, and regression against known failure cases.
- **Rollback procedures** that let you revert to a prior model version or disable a feature quickly after unsafe behavior.
- **Version control** for prompts, system instructions, guardrails, and model endpoints.
- **Change notifications** so the vendor must notify you before material model changes, not after.

A practical governance workflow often looks like this:
1. Vendor submits a change request for a new model version or safety policy.
2. Security, privacy, legal, and business owners review the impact.
3. The vendor provides test results, release notes, and known limitations.
4. You validate the change in a staging or sandbox environment.
5. You approve production release only after acceptance criteria are met.
6. You retain a rollback path and incident escalation plan.

> If a vendor cannot explain how to revert a harmful model update, treat that as a material governance gap.

**Access management for vendor personnel**

Vendor access is a major risk because support engineers, ML engineers, and platform administrators may have broad visibility into customer data, logs, or model artifacts. You should verify that access is tightly controlled and reviewed.

Key access controls:
- **Privileged access management** for admin and support accounts.
- **MFA** for all privileged users.
- **Just-in-time access** or time-bound elevation for support tasks.
- **Separation of duties** so the same person cannot approve, deploy, and audit a change without oversight.
- **Named accounts** instead of shared admin credentials.
- **Periodic access reviews** for vendor personnel with production access.
- **Session logging** and command auditing for privileged actions.

You should ask whether vendor support can:
- View customer prompts, outputs, or files
- Access production logs containing sensitive data
- Modify model configurations or safety filters
- Export datasets or embeddings
- Bypass normal approval workflows during incident response

A strong contract should require:
- Least privilege access
- Background checks where appropriate
- Logging and monitoring of privileged activity
- Immediate revocation when access is no longer needed
- Notification when privileged access is used for customer support

**Data protection and privacy controls**

AI services often process more than the obvious input. Prompts, attachments, embeddings, conversation history, telemetry, and feedback data can all contain sensitive information. You need to understand where that data goes and how long it stays there.

Focus areas:
- **Data minimization** so the vendor collects only what is needed.
- **Retention limits** for prompts, logs, and training artifacts.
- **Deletion workflows** that actually remove data from active systems and backups according to policy.
- **Encryption** in transit and at rest.
- **Tenant isolation** to prevent cross-customer leakage.
- **Data residency** if regulatory or contractual requirements apply.
- **Training opt-out** if customer content must not be used to improve the vendor’s models.

Practical questions:
- Are customer prompts excluded from training by default?
- Are embeddings stored separately from raw content?
- Can you delete a conversation thread and its derived artifacts?
- Are logs redacted before support access?
- Does the vendor retain data in backup systems beyond the stated retention period?

**Monitoring metrics and KRIs/KPIs**

Ongoing monitoring should use key risk indicators (KRIs) and key performance indicators (KPIs) that reflect both security and model behavior. Traditional uptime metrics are not enough.

Useful metrics include:
- **Incident rate** for security, privacy, and model safety events
- **Model drift indicators** showing output quality degradation over time
- **SLA breach trends** for latency, availability, and support response
- **Subprocessor churn** when the vendor adds or replaces subprocessors frequently
- **Access review exceptions** for overdue or unresolved privileged access findings
- **Patch and vulnerability remediation time**
- **False positive and false negative rates** for safety filters or moderation controls
- **Rollback frequency** after model releases
- **Complaint volume** related to harmful, biased, or incorrect outputs

A simple monitoring dashboard might track:
- Monthly uptime and latency
- Number of critical incidents
- Number of model changes released
- Number of failed regression tests
- Number of unresolved access review findings
- Number of subprocessors added in the last quarter

```text
Example KRI thresholds
- Critical incident rate > 1 per quarter: escalate to risk committee
- SLA breaches > 3 in a month: require vendor remediation plan
- Subprocessor changes without notice: block new data transfers
- Model drift score above threshold: suspend production use until retested
```

**Business continuity and disaster recovery**

AI services depend on more than the model itself. They may rely on vector databases, object storage, identity providers, content filters, telemetry pipelines, and external APIs. Your continuity review should test the whole dependency chain.

You should confirm:
- **RTO** (Recovery Time Objective): how quickly the service must be restored
- **RPO** (Recovery Point Objective): how much data loss is acceptable
- **Failover validation** for regional or provider outages
- **Recovery testing** for model/API outages and degraded modes
- **Dependency resilience** for upstream and downstream services
- **Manual fallback** procedures if the AI service is unavailable

Good questions to ask:
- Has the vendor tested failover for the model endpoint?
- Can the service degrade gracefully if moderation or retrieval components fail?
- Are backups encrypted and tested for restore success?
- Does the vendor have a documented process for switching to a previous stable model?
- Are customers notified when the service enters a reduced-capability mode?

A resilient design often includes:
- Primary and secondary regions
- Cached or fallback responses for non-critical workflows
- Circuit breakers to stop repeated failed calls
- Queueing for delayed processing
- Manual review paths when AI output is unavailable or untrusted

**Practical assessment workflow**

A complete AI vendor assessment combines intake, validation, tiering, and monitoring. The goal is to make a repeatable process rather than a one-time checklist.

1. Identify the use case and data classification.
2. Assign a vendor tier based on criticality and sensitivity.
3. Send the AI-specific questionnaire and request evidence.
4. Review security, privacy, governance, access, and continuity controls.
5. Validate contractual terms, including data use, subprocessors, and incident notification.
6. Approve, conditionally approve, or reject the vendor.
7. Set monitoring metrics and reassessment dates.
8. Reevaluate after incidents, major model changes, or subprocessor changes.

Example of a completed assessment summary:
- Vendor: Managed LLM API provider
- Tier: High
- Data: Internal support tickets with limited personal data
- Findings:
  - MFA and encryption are in place
  - Prompts are not used for training by default
  - Privileged access review is quarterly, but support access logs are retained only 30 days
  - No documented rollback procedure for model updates
  - DR testing exists, but failover for moderation service has not been validated
- Decision:
  - Conditional approval
  - Require longer log retention, documented rollback, and failover test evidence before production expansion

**Sample questionnaire response interpretation**

A good answer is specific, measurable, and supported by evidence. A weak answer is vague or policy-only.

- **Strong response**
  - “Customer prompts are excluded from training by default, retained for 30 days for abuse detection, and deleted within 7 days after customer request. Evidence: data retention policy and deletion workflow screenshot.”
- **Weak response**
  - “We take privacy seriously and follow industry best practices.”
- **Strong response**
  - “All production model changes require security, privacy, and product approval, and rollback to the prior version is available within 15 minutes.”
- **Weak response**
  - “We have a robust change management process.”

**Contractual and legal considerations**

Contracts should translate risk findings into enforceable obligations. For AI vendors, the agreement should cover data use, incident notification, audit rights, subprocessors, and service continuity.

Common clauses include:
- Data processing terms and confidentiality
- Restrictions on training with customer data
- Breach notification timelines
- Subprocessor approval or notice requirements
- Right to audit or receive independent assurance reports
- Service levels and support commitments
- Termination assistance and data deletion obligations
- Liability allocation for privacy, security, and model misuse where negotiable

You should also confirm whether the vendor’s terms allow:
- Broad reuse of customer content
- Unilateral model changes without notice
- Limited liability for AI-generated harm
- Cross-border data transfers without safeguards

**Exam focus and practical takeaways**

For exam purposes, remember that AI third-party risk management is broader than standard vendor review. You are evaluating the vendor’s security posture, but also the behavior and lifecycle of the model itself.

High-value concepts to remember:
- **Tiering** determines review depth, approval authority, and monitoring cadence.
- **Questionnaires** must include AI-specific topics such as model lifecycle, training data use, and rollback.
- **Access management** must cover vendor admins, support staff, and privileged access reviews.
- **Monitoring** should include KRIs and KPIs tied to incidents, drift, SLA breaches, and subprocessor churn.
- **Continuity** must validate failover, recovery, and dependency resilience for AI services.
- **Governance** must include human review, approval gates, and rollback for unsafe model changes.
