## **Course:** CompTIA SecAI+ Complete Course (Exam SY0-SAI+)

**Lab purpose and exam focus**

This lab teaches you how to classify AI use cases under the **EU AI Act** and map them to the **NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF)**. In exam scenarios, you are usually not asked to memorize every legal clause. You are asked to identify the right risk posture, assign the right role, and document the decision in a repeatable way.

The key skill is to separate:
- **Legal classification** under the EU AI Act
- **Operational risk management** using NIST AI RMF
- **Security and privacy risks** that may exist even when a system is not legally high-risk

A useful mental model is:
- The EU AI Act tells you what category the system falls into and what obligations follow.
- NIST AI RMF tells you how to manage the system responsibly across its lifecycle.
- Your internal lab artifact should connect both views in one traceable record.

**Core workflow for the lab**

Use a repeatable process so your answer is defensible and easy to audit.

1. Identify the AI system, its purpose, users, and deployment context.
2. Determine the role of each party: provider, deployer, importer, distributor, authorized representative, or product manufacturer where relevant.
3. Check whether the use case is prohibited, high-risk, transparency-obligated, or otherwise minimal risk under the EU AI Act.
4. Map the system to NIST AI RMF functions and subcategories.
5. Record residual risks, controls, owners, and reassessment triggers.
6. Escalate borderline cases for legal or compliance review instead of overclassifying them.

> If you are uncertain, do not guess a legal category. Document the uncertainty, note the facts that matter, and escalate.

**EU AI Act classification model**

The EU AI Act is not best understood as a simple four-bucket model. For exam purposes, the most important distinctions are:
- **Prohibited practices**: certain AI uses are banned outright.
- **High-risk systems**: allowed only if strict requirements are met.
- **Transparency obligations**: certain systems must disclose that AI is being used or that content is synthetic/manipulated.
- **Minimal risk**: the residual category for systems that do not fall into the above groups.

A common study shorthand is “limited risk,” but the Act’s core legal structure is better remembered as prohibited, high-risk, transparency obligations, and minimal risk.

**Prohibited practices: what to recognize**

Prohibited practices are narrow and specific. Do not overgeneralize them.

Typical exam-relevant examples include:
- **Subliminal or deceptive manipulation** that materially distorts behavior and is likely to cause significant harm
- **Exploitation of vulnerabilities** of a person or group due to age, disability, or social/economic situation when it materially distorts behavior and causes or is likely to cause significant harm
- **Social scoring** by public authorities or on their behalf that leads to unjustified or disproportionate treatment
- **Certain forms of biometric categorization or inference** that are specifically restricted by the Act
- **Untargeted scraping** of facial images from the internet or CCTV for facial recognition databases
- **Emotion recognition** in certain sensitive contexts, especially where the law specifically restricts the use case
- **Biometric identification** in certain public or law-enforcement contexts where the Act imposes strict limits or exceptions

Do not treat every personalization feature, nudging system, or vulnerability-aware design as prohibited. The legal test is narrower: the behavior must meet the Act’s specific conditions, not just be persuasive or adaptive.

**High-risk systems: how to classify them**

High-risk status is not only about business function examples. Some systems are high-risk because they are:
- A safety component of a regulated product
- A product covered by sectoral legislation
- A system used in a listed high-impact area

Common high-risk use cases include:
- **Employment and worker management**
- **Education and vocational training**
- **Access to essential private or public services**
- **Law enforcement**
- **Migration, asylum, and border control**
- **Administration of justice and democratic processes**
- **Biometric identification and certain biometric categorization uses**
- **Safety components in regulated products** such as machinery, medical devices, vehicles, or other product categories covered by sectoral rules

For exam questions, ask two separate questions:
- Is the use case listed as high-risk?
- Is the AI system part of a regulated product or safety component that makes it high-risk even if the business use sounds ordinary?

A hiring model used to rank candidates is a classic high-risk scenario. A model embedded in a regulated medical device may also be high-risk even if the user story sounds like “decision support.”

**Transparency obligations**

Some systems are not high-risk but still require disclosure. These obligations often apply when users might reasonably believe they are interacting with a human or when content is synthetic or manipulated.

Examples:
- Chatbots that interact with people should disclose that the user is interacting with AI when the context could be misleading.
- Deepfakes or synthetic media may require labeling or disclosure.
- Emotion recognition or biometric categorization may trigger notice obligations depending on the exact use and context.

A customer-support chatbot is not automatically “limited risk.” It may be minimal risk, transparency-obligated, or something more sensitive depending on:
- Whether it interacts directly with users
- Whether it generates synthetic content
- Whether it is used in a regulated domain
- Whether it influences rights, access, or decisions

**Minimal risk**

Minimal risk is the residual category. Many everyday AI tools fall here if they do not trigger prohibited, high-risk, or transparency obligations.

Examples often include:
- Spam filtering
- Basic recommendation engines
- Internal productivity assistants with no regulated decision impact
- Simple content classification tools with no legal or safety consequence

Minimal risk does not mean no risk. It means the EU AI Act does not impose the same formal obligations as for prohibited or high-risk systems.

**Roles and responsibilities**

The EU AI Act uses multiple role terms. For scenario questions, map the actor carefully.

- **Provider**: develops an AI system or has it developed and places it on the market or puts it into service under its own name or trademark
- **Deployer**: uses an AI system under its authority, typically in an operational setting
- **Importer**: brings a system from outside the EU market into the EU market
- **Distributor**: makes a system available without being the provider or importer
- **Authorized representative**: acts on behalf of a provider in the EU when required
- **Product manufacturer**: may be relevant when AI is part of a regulated product

Typical responsibility patterns:
- Providers usually own design-time controls, technical documentation, conformity work, and post-market monitoring.
- Deployers usually own correct use, human oversight in operation, input data quality where applicable, and incident reporting obligations tied to use.
- Importers and distributors usually verify that required documentation, labeling, and compliance status are present before making the system available.
- Authorized representatives support compliance for providers outside the EU.

**Compliance artifacts by role**

Use these artifacts in your lab answer to show traceability.

- **Provider artifacts**
  - Technical documentation
  - Risk management file
  - Data governance records
  - Logging and traceability design
  - Instructions for use
  - Conformity assessment evidence
  - Post-market monitoring plan

- **Deployer artifacts**
  - Use-case assessment
  - Human oversight procedure
  - Local operating procedure
  - Incident reporting workflow
  - Training records for operators
  - Records of intended use and limitations

- **Importer/distributor artifacts**
  - Verification checklist
  - Labeling and documentation review
  - Supply-chain compliance record
  - Escalation log for missing compliance evidence

**NIST AI RMF structure**

The NIST AI RMF has four core functions:
- **Govern**
- **Map**
- **Measure**
- **Manage**

These are not categories under the functions; they are the functions themselves. Each function contains categories and subcategories that help you operationalize the framework.

A practical way to remember them:
- **Govern**: set policy, accountability, and oversight
- **Map**: understand context, intended use, stakeholders, and impacts
- **Measure**: test, evaluate, and quantify risk
- **Manage**: prioritize, respond, and monitor

**NIST AI RMF mapping examples**

Use concrete subcategory-style thinking rather than only naming the functions.

- **Govern**
  - Define AI policy and risk appetite
  - Assign accountable owners
  - Establish review boards or approval gates
  - Require documentation and escalation paths
  - Track third-party and model supply-chain risk

- **Map**
  - Identify intended use and out-of-scope use
  - Identify affected stakeholders
  - Document context, environment, and dependencies
  - Identify potential harms, misuse, and abuse cases
  - Note legal, ethical, and operational constraints

- **Measure**
  - Evaluate model performance and error rates
  - Test robustness, bias, and drift
  - Assess privacy leakage and security exposure
  - Validate human oversight effectiveness
  - Run red-team or adversarial testing where appropriate

- **Manage**
  - Apply mitigations and compensating controls
  - Set thresholds for rollback or suspension
  - Monitor post-deployment behavior
  - Reassess after incidents or material changes
  - Update controls based on measured outcomes

**Worked mapping example**

Scenario: an AI hiring assistant ranks applicants and summarizes interview notes.

- **EU AI Act classification**
  - High-risk because employment and worker management is a listed high-risk area
- **Provider obligations**
  - Technical documentation
  - Risk management
  - Data governance
  - Logging
  - Human oversight design
- **Deployer obligations**
  - Use the system according to instructions
  - Ensure human review of hiring decisions
  - Train recruiters on limitations
  - Monitor for bias or drift in local use
- **NIST AI RMF mapping**
  - Govern: assign HR and compliance owners
  - Map: identify candidates, recruiters, legal exposure, and adverse impact
  - Measure: test ranking bias and summary accuracy
  - Manage: require human approval before final hiring decisions

**Borderline classification decision tree**

When the classification is unclear, use a formal escalation path.

1. Determine whether the system is a prohibited practice.
2. If not prohibited, check whether it is high-risk by use case or by product/safety-component status.
3. If not high-risk, check whether transparency obligations apply.
4. If none apply, classify as minimal risk for EU AI Act purposes.
5. Record any security, privacy, or operational risks separately in the risk register.
6. Escalate to legal/compliance if the facts are incomplete or the use case is changing.

> Do not label a system “high-risk” just because it is technically dangerous, vulnerable to prompt injection, or expensive to operate. Those are operational risks, not legal classification triggers.

**GPAI and foundation model considerations**

**General-purpose AI (GPAI)** models and foundation models can have obligations that differ from downstream high-risk applications.

Key distinction:
- A **GPAI provider** may need to address model-level documentation, transparency, copyright-related policy, and systemic risk controls depending on the model’s scale and capabilities.
- A **downstream provider** that integrates the model into a high-risk system must still meet the high-risk system obligations for the final application.

Do not collapse these into one obligation set. A model provider may satisfy foundation-model duties while a downstream deployer still fails to meet high-risk operational controls.

Scenario logic:
- If you host a general model and expose an API, think about model-level transparency and documentation.
- If you fine-tune or embed that model into hiring, education, or medical workflows, classify the downstream system separately.
- If the downstream use is high-risk, the application inherits high-risk obligations even if the base model is general-purpose.

**Security, privacy, and operational risks**

Not every serious risk changes the EU AI Act classification.

Examples of important but non-classification risks:
- **Prompt injection**
- **Data leakage**
- **Model inversion**
- **Membership inference**
- **Hallucinations**
- **Drift**
- **Availability failures**
- **Unauthorized tool use**

These risks belong in the risk register and NIST AI RMF measurement and management activities. They do not automatically make a system prohibited or high-risk under the Act.

**How to write a strong exam answer**

Use a concise structure:
- State the system and context
- Identify the legal classification
- Name the role(s)
- List the main obligations
- Map to NIST AI RMF functions
- Note residual risk and escalation if needed

A good answer is specific, not generic. For example, “This is high-risk because it is used for employment screening” is stronger than “This is risky because it affects people.”

**Common exam traps**

- Treating “limited risk” as a formal legal bucket instead of a study shorthand
- Assuming all chatbots are the same risk level
- Classifying a system as high-risk only because it is technically complex
- Confusing operational security issues with legal classification
- Forgetting that some systems are high-risk because they are safety components or regulated products
- Ignoring importer, distributor, or authorized representative roles in supply-chain scenarios
- Mixing up provider obligations with deployer obligations
- Using NIST AI RMF as a legal classification framework instead of a risk management framework

**Quick comparison**

| Topic | EU AI Act | NIST AI RMF |
|---|---|---|
| Primary purpose | Legal compliance and obligations | Risk management and governance |
| Main output | Classification and required controls | Lifecycle risk process and mitigation |
| Key question | What category is the system? | How do you identify, measure, and manage risk? |
| Best use in lab | Determine obligations and role duties | Build the control and monitoring plan |

**Reassessment triggers**

Reclassify or review the system when:
- The use case changes
- The model is materially updated or retrained
- New user groups are added
- The system is integrated into a regulated workflow
- A complaint, incident, or audit finding occurs
- The legal or regulatory environment changes
- The deployment crosses a new jurisdiction or market

**Final lab checklist**

- Confirm the AI system purpose and deployment context
- Identify the correct role for each party
- Classify the system under the EU AI Act
- Separate legal classification from operational risk
- Map controls to NIST AI RMF functions
- Record evidence in a risk register and traceability matrix
- Define reassessment triggers and escalation paths
