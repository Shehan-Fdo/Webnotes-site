**AI system lifecycle overview**

An AI system lifecycle covers the full journey from business need and data acquisition through design, training, validation, deployment, monitoring, and retirement. In practice, you treat the model as one component of a broader socio-technical system that includes data pipelines, infrastructure, access controls, human review, logging, and governance.

A useful way to think about the lifecycle is as a series of gates:

- **Define** the use case, risk level, and success criteria.
- **Prepare** data with governance, quality, and legal controls.
- **Build** and train the model in a controlled environment.
- **Validate** performance, safety, and robustness before release.
- **Deploy** with environment-specific controls and rollback plans.
- **Operate** with monitoring, incident response, and periodic review.
- **Retire** the system safely, preserving records when required.

The lifecycle is not linear in the real world. You often loop back to earlier phases when data changes, business requirements shift, or regulations require re-approval.

**Planning and governance**

The planning phase sets the guardrails for everything that follows. You define the business objective, the intended users, the decision impact, and the acceptable risk. This is where you decide whether the system is informational, advisory, or decision-supporting, because that affects the level of human oversight and validation required.

Key planning artifacts include:

- **Use-case statement** describing the problem, scope, and boundaries.
- **Risk assessment** covering privacy, security, bias, safety, and operational impact.
- **Success metrics** tied to business outcomes and technical quality.
- **Approval workflow** identifying owners, reviewers, and sign-off authority.
- **Control requirements** for logging, access, retention, and monitoring.

Governance should also define accountability. A model owner, data owner, security reviewer, and business approver should each have explicit responsibilities. This reduces ambiguity when you need to approve a release, investigate an incident, or retire a model.

**Data governance across the lifecycle**

Data governance is not a one-time checklist. It starts before collection and continues through retention and disposal. You need to know what data you have, where it came from, who can use it, and how long you are allowed to keep it.

Important governance controls include:

- **Data classification** to label sensitive, confidential, regulated, or public data.
- **Consent management** when personal data is collected or reused.
- **Provenance tracking** to record origin, transformations, and lineage.
- **Retention schedules** to define how long training, test, and operational data remain available.
- **Deletion and disposal** procedures for expired or unauthorized data.
- **Quality controls** for completeness, accuracy, timeliness, consistency, and duplication.

A practical data governance workflow often looks like this:

1. Classify the dataset and identify regulated fields such as personal data, health data, or payment data.
2. Verify lawful basis or consent for collection and downstream use.
3. Record provenance, including source system, extraction time, and transformation steps.
4. Apply quality checks such as schema validation, null-rate thresholds, and duplicate detection.
5. Enforce retention and deletion rules for raw data, derived features, and backups.

Example data quality checks in a pipeline:

```python
import pandas as pd

df = pd.read_csv("training_data.csv")

required_columns = ["customer_id", "label", "feature_1", "feature_2"]
missing = [c for c in required_columns if c not in df.columns]
if missing:
    raise ValueError(f"Missing columns: {missing}")

null_rate = df["feature_1"].isna().mean()
if null_rate > 0.05:
    raise ValueError(f"feature_1 null rate too high: {null_rate:.2%}")

duplicate_rows = df.duplicated().sum()
if duplicate_rows > 0:
    print(f"Warning: {duplicate_rows} duplicate rows found")
```

For regulated environments, you also need to document whether data is allowed to leave a region, whether it can be used for model improvement, and whether it must be segregated from other tenants or business units.

**Model risk management and documentation**

Model risk management is the discipline of proving that a model is appropriate, understood, and controlled for its intended use. It is especially important when the model influences decisions with legal, financial, safety, or reputational impact.

Core documentation artifacts include:

- **Model cards** summarizing purpose, intended use, limitations, evaluation results, and ethical considerations.
- **Datasheets for datasets** describing collection methods, labeling process, known gaps, and permitted uses.
- **Decision logs** recording major design choices, tradeoffs, and approvals.
- **Validation reports** showing test results, thresholds, and exceptions.
- **Change records** documenting retraining, tuning, and deployment changes.

These artifacts support auditability and accountability. If a regulator, customer, or internal auditor asks why a model behaved a certain way, you need evidence of what data it used, how it was tested, who approved it, and what controls were in place.

A strong model card usually includes:

- Intended purpose and non-purpose
- Training data summary
- Evaluation metrics and thresholds
- Known limitations and failure modes
- Human oversight requirements
- Monitoring and retraining triggers

A datasheet for a dataset should include:

- Source and collection method
- Labeling instructions and annotator guidance
- Data quality issues and exclusions
- Consent, licensing, and usage restrictions
- Retention and deletion requirements

**Build and training phase**

During build and training, you create the model in a controlled environment that is isolated from production. The goal is reproducibility: if someone reruns the pipeline with the same code, data, and parameters, they should get the same or explainably similar result.

Typical build controls include:

- Version control for code, prompts, and configuration.
- Reproducible environments using containers or pinned dependencies.
- Secrets management for API keys, tokens, and service credentials.
- Restricted access to training data and artifacts.
- Experiment tracking for hyperparameters, metrics, and model versions.

Example containerized training workflow:

```bash
docker build -t ai-train:1.0 .
docker run --rm \
  -e MODEL_REGISTRY_URL="https://registry.example.com" \
  -v "$PWD/data:/data:ro" \
  -v "$PWD/artifacts:/artifacts" \
  ai-train:1.0 python train.py --data /data/train.csv --out /artifacts/model.pkl
```

In many organizations, training data is stored in a separate network segment from production systems. That separation reduces the chance that a compromised training job can reach sensitive operational systems. You should also avoid embedding secrets in notebooks, scripts, or model artifacts.

**Validation criteria and lifecycle gates**

Validation is a release gate, not a formality. Before deployment, you verify that the model meets technical, business, security, and safety requirements. A model that performs well in training can still fail in production due to drift, bias, prompt injection, data leakage, or unsafe outputs.

Common validation criteria include:

- **Accuracy** for overall correctness when classes are balanced and errors have similar cost.
- **Precision** when false positives are expensive.
- **Recall** when false negatives are expensive.
- **F1 score** when you need a balance between precision and recall.
- **Calibration** to confirm predicted probabilities match observed outcomes.
- **Robustness** under noisy, adversarial, or out-of-distribution inputs.
- **Safety testing** for harmful, disallowed, or policy-violating outputs.
- **Fairness checks** to detect disparate performance across groups.
- **Latency and throughput** to ensure operational feasibility.

Use the metric that matches the business risk. For example, a fraud detector may prioritize recall to catch suspicious activity, while a medical triage assistant may require high precision and strong human review to reduce false alarms.

Example metric calculation:

```python
from sklearn.metrics import accuracy_score, precision_score, recall_score, f1_score, brier_score_loss

y_true = [1, 0, 1, 1, 0, 0, 1]
y_pred = [1, 0, 1, 0, 0, 1, 1]
y_prob = [0.92, 0.12, 0.81, 0.44, 0.08, 0.67, 0.88]

print("accuracy:", accuracy_score(y_true, y_pred))
print("precision:", precision_score(y_true, y_pred))
print("recall:", recall_score(y_true, y_pred))
print("f1:", f1_score(y_true, y_pred))
print("brier:", brier_score_loss(y_true, y_prob))
```

Validation should also include scenario-based testing:

- Adversarial prompts or malformed inputs
- Edge cases and rare classes
- Data drift simulations
- Bias and subgroup analysis
- Refusal behavior for unsafe requests
- Output formatting and schema conformance

> A model should not be approved solely because it scores well on a benchmark. You need evidence that it behaves safely and predictably in the environment where it will actually run.

A practical release gate often requires all of the following:

1. Metrics meet minimum thresholds on a locked validation set.
2. Safety tests pass for prohibited content, leakage, and misuse.
3. Calibration and robustness are acceptable for the use case.
4. Human review confirms the model card and risk assessment are complete.
5. Security and privacy reviews approve the deployment plan.

**Deployment environments and configuration**

Development, staging, and production should not be configured the same way. Each environment has a different purpose and therefore different access, data, and network controls.

| Environment | Purpose | Data | Access | Network | Secrets |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dev | Build and test | Synthetic or masked data | Broad for engineers | Least restrictive, but isolated | Local or short-lived |
| Staging | Pre-production validation | Representative, sanitized data | Limited to testers and approvers | Segmented from prod | Managed centrally |
| Prod | Live service | Real operational data | Least privilege | Strict segmentation and egress control | Vaulted, rotated, audited |

Practical environment differences include:

- **Development** uses synthetic or masked data whenever possible.
- **Staging** mirrors production configuration but uses non-production credentials.
- **Production** enforces stricter logging, alerting, and change control.
- **Secrets** should come from a vault or managed secret store, not from source code.
- **Network segmentation** should prevent direct lateral movement between environments.
- **Access control** should use role-based access control (RBAC) or attribute-based access control (ABAC) with approval workflows.

Example Kubernetes-style secret reference:

```yaml
apiVersion: v1
kind: Pod
metadata:
  name: ai-inference
spec:
  containers:
    - name: app
      image: registry.example.com/ai-inference:2.1
      env:
        - name: MODEL_API_KEY
          valueFrom:
            secretKeyRef:
              name: model-api-secret
              key: api_key
```

Example network segmentation concept:

```text
[Dev VPC] ---- no direct route ----> [Prod VPC]
     |                                  |
     +--> [Staging VPC]                 +--> [Inference API]
```

For externally hosted or SaaS-based AI services, you may not control the model internals, but you still control configuration, access, data handling, and monitoring. That means you must review vendor security posture, data processing terms, retention behavior, and incident notification obligations.

**Third-party and SaaS AI services versus internal models**

Internally hosted models and third-party AI services have different operational responsibilities. Internal models give you more control but also more responsibility. SaaS services reduce infrastructure burden but increase dependency on vendor controls and contract terms.

Key differences:

- **Internal models**
  - You manage training, deployment, patching, monitoring, and incident response.
  - You control data locality, logging, and model updates.
  - You need internal MLOps and security operations maturity.

- **Third-party/SaaS AI services**
  - The vendor manages the underlying model and platform.
  - You manage tenant configuration, user access, and data sent to the service.
  - You rely on vendor SLAs, support channels, and transparency about updates.
  - You should monitor vendor notices for model changes, outages, and policy updates.

Shared responsibility is critical. Even if the vendor hosts the model, you remain responsible for whether your users are allowed to submit certain data, whether outputs are reviewed, and whether the service meets your compliance requirements.

Vendor management should include:

- Security review and due diligence
- Data processing agreement review
- SLA and uptime expectations
- Breach notification timelines
- Data retention and deletion commitments
- Subprocessor and cross-border transfer review

**Operational monitoring and incident response**

After deployment, you monitor both the model and the surrounding system. Monitoring should detect performance degradation, data drift, concept drift, abuse, and security events.

Useful operational signals include:

- Input distribution changes
- Output confidence shifts
- Error rates and latency
- Human override rates
- Prompt injection attempts
- Policy violation counts
- Feedback and complaint trends

Example monitoring with a simple threshold check:

```python
import statistics

recent_scores = [0.91, 0.88, 0.86, 0.84, 0.79]
baseline_mean = 0.92

current_mean = statistics.mean(recent_scores)
if baseline_mean - current_mean > 0.05:
    print("Alert: possible performance drift")
```

Incident response for AI systems should include:

- Triage of the model issue and business impact
- Containment, such as disabling a feature or switching to a fallback
- Evidence preservation, including logs and model version identifiers
- Root cause analysis across data, code, prompts, and infrastructure
- Remediation and post-incident review

You should also monitor for abuse patterns such as prompt injection, data exfiltration attempts, and automated scraping. For generative systems, safety monitoring often includes content filters, human review queues, and policy-based refusal logic.

**Continuous improvement and revalidation**

AI systems require scheduled review cycles because the world changes. Data distributions shift, user behavior changes, regulations evolve, and business priorities move. A model that was acceptable at launch may become unreliable or noncompliant later.

Continuous improvement should include:

- **Periodic revalidation** on a fixed schedule, such as quarterly or semiannually.
- **Trigger-based review** after significant business, data, or regulatory changes.
- **Re-approval** before major retraining, feature changes, or scope expansion.
- **Performance trend analysis** to detect gradual degradation.
- **Feedback incorporation** from users, reviewers, and incident reports.

Examples of revalidation triggers:

- New data source or schema change
- Material change in user population
- New regulation or policy requirement
- Significant drift in input or output patterns
- Vendor model update in a SaaS dependency
- Security incident affecting training or inference data

A good practice is to define a review calendar and a change threshold matrix. For example, a minor prompt update may require only limited testing, while a new data source or new use case may require full revalidation and executive approval.

**Retirement and disposal**

Retirement is the controlled end of the lifecycle. You retire a model when it is obsolete, too risky, no longer supported, or replaced by a better system. Retirement should not mean simply deleting a file and moving on.

Retirement steps usually include:

1. Disable production traffic and confirm fallback behavior.
2. Preserve required records, including logs, approvals, and validation evidence.
3. Archive model cards, datasheets, and change history.
4. Dispose of operational assets such as endpoints, containers, keys, and temporary storage.
5. Delete or anonymize data according to retention and legal requirements.
6. Verify that backups, replicas, and caches are handled correctly.

> If a legal hold, litigation hold, or regulatory investigation is active, you must preserve the required records even if you retire the operational system.

That means you may need to keep:

- Audit logs
- Approval records
- Training lineage
- Validation reports
- Incident records
- Retained datasets or snapshots required by policy

At the same time, you should remove access to live services, revoke credentials, and decommission infrastructure that is no longer needed. This reduces attack surface while maintaining compliance.

**Practical lifecycle checklist**

Use this checklist to connect the phases into a repeatable operating model:

- Define the use case, owners, and risk level.
- Classify data and verify consent, provenance, and retention rules.
- Build in isolated environments with version control and secret management.
- Validate with accuracy, precision, recall, F1, calibration, robustness, and safety tests.
- Approve release only after documentation and governance review.
- Deploy with environment-specific access, segmentation, and monitoring.
- Revalidate on a schedule and after major changes.
- Retire safely with record preservation and compliant disposal.

**Exam focus reminders**

For exam purposes, focus on the relationship between lifecycle phases and controls:

- **Governance** starts before data collection and continues through retirement.
- **Validation** is broader than accuracy; it includes safety, robustness, and calibration.
- **Documentation** supports auditability, accountability, and repeatability.
- **Operations** require monitoring, incident response, and periodic review.
- **Third-party services** shift infrastructure responsibility to the vendor, but not accountability.
- **Retirement** must respect retention, legal hold, and evidence preservation requirements.
