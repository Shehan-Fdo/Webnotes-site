## **Course:** CompTIA SecAI+ Complete Course (Exam SY0-SAI+)

**Domain 5 at a glance**

Domain 5 review should help you connect AI security concepts to operational decisions, risk management, and exam-style judgment calls. On the exam, you are rarely asked for a definition alone; you are more often asked to choose the safest, most defensible control, workflow, or response for a given scenario.

A strong review strategy is to group concepts by how they affect confidentiality, integrity, availability, privacy, and governance. That makes it easier to compare controls, identify tradeoffs, and avoid answers that sound technically correct but do not fit the scenario.

- Focus on the *why* behind each control, not just the label.
- Expect questions that mix AI risk, security operations, and compliance.
- Treat vendor claims carefully; verify with logs, policy, and testing.
- Prefer controls that reduce blast radius, improve traceability, and support human oversight.

**Core exam themes**

Domain 5 commonly tests how you secure AI systems across the lifecycle. That includes data collection, model training, deployment, monitoring, and incident response. You should be able to explain how threats change at each stage and which controls are most effective.

Key areas to review include:

- **Data security** for training, fine-tuning, and inference inputs.
- **Model security** against poisoning, evasion, extraction, and prompt injection.
- **Access control** for users, service accounts, APIs, and model endpoints.
- **Monitoring and logging** for detection, auditability, and forensics.
- **Governance and compliance** for acceptable use, privacy, and retention.
- **Incident response** for AI-specific events and downstream business impact.

A useful mental model is to ask: “What is the asset, what is the threat, and what control reduces risk without breaking the workflow?” That framing helps you avoid overengineering and supports better multiple-choice elimination.

**Frequently tested concepts**

**1. Data protection and privacy**

AI systems often process sensitive data in prompts, embeddings, logs, and training corpora. The exam may ask how to reduce exposure without disabling the system.

- **Data minimization** means collecting and retaining only what you need.
- **Masking or redaction** reduces exposure of personal or regulated data.
- **Encryption at rest and in transit** protects data from unauthorized access.
- **Tokenization** replaces sensitive values with non-sensitive substitutes.
- **Retention controls** limit how long prompts, outputs, and telemetry remain available.

Practical example:

```bash
# Example: inspect a storage bucket policy before allowing model logs
aws s3api get-bucket-policy --bucket ai-logs-prod

# Example: verify encryption settings for a storage location
aws s3api get-bucket-encryption --bucket ai-logs-prod
```

Use these controls when the risk is disclosure of sensitive prompts, customer data, or proprietary model artifacts. If the question mentions regulated data, prefer controls that reduce collection and storage first, then add encryption and access restrictions.

**2. Access control and identity**

AI services often rely on humans, applications, and automation. The exam may test whether you can distinguish user access from machine access and choose the right identity control.

- **Least privilege** limits access to only required actions.
- **Role-based access control (RBAC)** assigns permissions by job function.
- **Attribute-based access control (ABAC)** uses attributes such as environment, device, or data sensitivity.
- **Service accounts** should be scoped narrowly and rotated or revoked when no longer needed.
- **Multi-factor authentication (MFA)** protects interactive administrative access.

Practical example:

```bash
# Review attached policies for a role
aws iam list-attached-role-policies --role-name ai-inference-role

# Review inline policies for a role
aws iam list-role-policies --role-name ai-inference-role

# Simulate whether a principal can invoke a model endpoint
aws iam simulate-principal-policy \
  --policy-source-arn arn:aws:iam::123456789012:role/ai-inference-role \
  --action-names bedrock:InvokeModel \
  --resource-arns arn:aws:bedrock:us-east-1::foundation-model/example-model
```

Use policy simulation when you need to validate effective permissions. That is more useful than simply retrieving role metadata, because metadata alone does not prove what the role can do.

**3. Model threats**

Model-specific attacks are a major exam topic because they are unique to AI systems and often misunderstood.

- **Prompt injection** attempts to override system instructions or manipulate model behavior.
- **Data poisoning** corrupts training or fine-tuning data to influence outputs.
- **Model extraction** tries to steal model behavior through repeated queries.
- **Membership inference** attempts to determine whether a record was in the training set.
- **Evasion attacks** craft inputs that cause misclassification at inference time.

Defensive measures include input validation, output filtering, rate limiting, sandboxing, provenance checks, and human review for high-impact decisions. No single control stops all attacks, so the exam often rewards layered defense.

> If a question describes a model following malicious instructions from user content, think **prompt injection** first, not traditional malware.

**4. Logging, monitoring, and detection**

You need visibility into prompts, responses, policy decisions, and administrative actions. The exam may ask what to log, how to detect abuse, or how to preserve evidence.

- Log **who** accessed the system, **what** they requested, and **when**.
- Capture model version, policy version, and configuration changes.
- Monitor for unusual request volume, repeated failures, and suspicious prompt patterns.
- Correlate AI logs with identity, network, and endpoint telemetry.
- Protect logs from tampering with access controls and immutable storage where appropriate.

Practical example:

```bash
# Example: review recent API activity in a cloud audit log
aws cloudtrail lookup-events --lookup-attributes AttributeKey=EventName,AttributeValue=InvokeModel

# Example: inspect container logs for a model service
kubectl logs deployment/ai-gateway -n security-ai
```

Good monitoring supports both detection and accountability. If the scenario involves an incident, prioritize logs that help reconstruct the sequence of events and identify the affected data, model, and users.

**5. Governance and policy**

Governance questions often focus on whether AI use is allowed, restricted, or prohibited in a given context. The best answer usually aligns with business policy, legal requirements, and risk tolerance.

- **Acceptable use policy** defines permitted and prohibited AI activities.
- **Data classification** determines what can be sent to external models.
- **Human-in-the-loop** review is appropriate for high-impact decisions.
- **Model approval** should consider bias, explainability, and business impact.
- **Change management** should cover model updates, prompt changes, and retraining.

A practical governance pattern is to classify use cases by impact:

- Low impact: internal drafting, summarization, or code assistance with non-sensitive data.
- Medium impact: workflow automation with review and logging.
- High impact: decisions affecting employment, finance, healthcare, or legal outcomes.

For high-impact use cases, the exam usually favors stronger oversight, stricter logging, and explicit approval gates.

**6. Incident response for AI systems**

AI incidents can involve data leakage, unsafe outputs, model drift, abuse of APIs, or compromised training pipelines. Your response should contain the issue, preserve evidence, and restore trust in the system.

A typical response sequence is:

1. Identify the affected model, endpoint, dataset, or account.
2. Contain access by disabling keys, limiting traffic, or isolating the service.
3. Preserve logs, prompts, outputs, and configuration snapshots.
4. Eradicate the cause, such as poisoned data, exposed credentials, or unsafe prompts.
5. Recover with validated models, clean data, and updated controls.
6. Review lessons learned and update policy, monitoring, and training.

Practical example:

```bash
# Revoke a compromised API key in a generic workflow
vault token revoke <token-id>

# Rotate a cloud access key after suspected exposure
aws iam update-access-key \
  --user-name ai-service-user \
  --access-key-id AKIAEXAMPLE123456789 \
  --status Inactive
```

Use revocation or rotation when credentials are exposed. Do not confuse that with simply writing a new secret value to a vault path; secret storage and secret rotation are different actions.

**Common exam traps**

The exam often includes answers that sound secure but do not solve the actual problem. Watch for these patterns:

- Choosing encryption when the real issue is excessive data collection.
- Choosing logging when the real issue is missing access control.
- Choosing model retraining when the real issue is prompt injection or bad permissions.
- Choosing a vendor feature without verifying policy, scope, or auditability.
- Choosing a technical fix when the scenario calls for governance or human review.

A good rule is to match the control to the layer where the failure occurs:

- Input layer: validation, sanitization, filtering.
- Identity layer: authentication, authorization, MFA.
- Data layer: classification, encryption, retention.
- Model layer: guardrails, testing, version control.
- Operations layer: logging, monitoring, incident response.

**Practical review scenarios**

**Scenario 1: Sensitive prompts in logs**

If users are entering customer data into prompts and the logs are retained indefinitely, the best response is usually to reduce retention, redact sensitive fields, and restrict log access. Encryption alone does not solve overcollection.

**Scenario 2: Model gives unsafe instructions**

If the model is producing harmful or policy-violating output, add output filtering, tighten system prompts, review training data, and require human approval for risky actions. If the issue is prompt injection, focus on input separation and instruction hierarchy.

**Scenario 3: Unauthorized API usage**

If an AI endpoint is being called by unknown clients, review IAM or API gateway policies, enforce authentication, apply rate limits, and inspect audit logs. If the question asks how to verify effective permissions, use policy simulation or equivalent authorization testing.

**Scenario 4: Training data tampering**

If a dataset may have been poisoned, isolate the pipeline, validate data provenance, compare against trusted baselines, and retrain only after cleaning the source. Do not assume the model itself is the only problem; the pipeline is often the real attack surface.

**High-value commands and checks**

Use these examples as review anchors for cloud and platform operations:

```bash
# Check who can access a secret path in a vault-like system
vault policy read ai-readonly

# Inspect Kubernetes RBAC for a service account
kubectl auth can-i get secrets --as=system:serviceaccount:ai:inference-sa -n ai

# Review network exposure for a service
kubectl get svc ai-gateway -n security-ai -o wide
```

These commands help you reason about permissions, exposure, and service boundaries. On the exam, the exact syntax may differ by platform, but the security principle remains the same: verify access, reduce privilege, and confirm the effective path to the asset.

**FAQ-style reminders**

- **What is the best first step in most incidents?**
  - Identify scope and contain the issue before making broad changes.
- **What matters more, model accuracy or security?**
  - For exam scenarios, security and governance usually take priority when risk is high.
- **Should you trust model output by default?**
  - No. Treat it as untrusted until validated, especially for external actions.
- **Is a secure model enough without secure data?**
  - No. Data, identity, model, and operations all need controls.
- **Do all AI systems need human review?**
  - Not always, but high-impact decisions usually do.

**Final exam strategy**

Read the scenario carefully and identify the primary risk before looking at the answer choices. Then eliminate options that address the wrong layer, overstate certainty, or ignore governance. If two answers seem plausible, choose the one that is more specific to the threat and more aligned with least privilege, traceability, and human oversight.

A strong Domain 5 answer usually does three things:

- Reduces immediate risk.
- Preserves evidence or accountability.
- Fits the business and compliance context.
