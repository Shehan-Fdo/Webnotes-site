## **Course:** CompTIA SecAI+ Complete Course (Exam SY0-SAI+)

**Threat intelligence fundamentals**

Threat intelligence is the process of collecting, analyzing, and operationalizing information about adversaries, their infrastructure, tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs), and the indicators that help you detect or block malicious activity. In practice, you use intelligence to improve detection, prioritize response, reduce noise, and make better risk decisions.

A useful way to think about intelligence is by the decision it supports:

- **Strategic intelligence** supports executives and risk owners.
  - Focuses on trends, business impact, adversary motivation, and long-term risk.
  - Helps answer questions like “Which threat actors target our industry?” and “What is the likely business impact?”
- **Operational intelligence** supports incident response and security operations planning.
  - Focuses on campaigns, infrastructure, TTPs, and active threat activity.
  - Helps answer “What is the current campaign doing?” and “How should we tune detections?”
- **Tactical intelligence** supports defenders building detections and controls.
  - Focuses on IOCs (Indicators of Compromise), signatures, hashes, domains, IPs, URLs, and file paths.
  - Helps answer “What can I block or alert on right now?”
- **Technical intelligence** is often used to describe machine-consumable details that feed tools directly.
  - Includes hashes, YARA rules, Sigma rules, STIX objects, and other structured artifacts.
  - Helps automate detection, enrichment, and response.

> Use the intelligence level that matches the decision you need to make. Strategic data is too broad for a firewall rule, while a single hash is too narrow for executive planning.

**Intelligence lifecycle and requirements**

A mature intelligence program starts with requirements, not collection. You define what matters, then collect and analyze only what supports those needs. This prevents “collect everything” sprawl and keeps intelligence actionable.

Common lifecycle stages include:

1. **Planning and direction**
   - Define the mission, scope, and stakeholders.
   - Identify what decisions intelligence must support.
2. **Collection**
   - Gather data from internal and external sources.
   - Use sensors, logs, feeds, reports, and human input.
3. **Processing**
   - Normalize, deduplicate, and structure the data.
   - Convert raw data into usable records.
4. **Analysis**
   - Correlate data, identify patterns, and assess confidence.
   - Determine relevance to your environment.
5. **Dissemination**
   - Share the finished intelligence with the right audience.
   - Apply handling rules and classification.
6. **Feedback**
   - Measure usefulness and refine requirements.

Two planning concepts are especially important:

- **PIRs** (Priority Intelligence Requirements)
  - The highest-priority questions the organization needs answered.
  - Example: “Are we being targeted by ransomware groups using phishing and remote access tools?”
- **Collection management**
  - The process of mapping requirements to sources, sensors, and collection tasks.
  - Helps ensure you gather the right data at the right time.

A practical collection plan often maps each requirement to a source category:

| Requirement | Likely source | Why it helps |
|---|---|---|
| Detect active phishing | Email gateway, sandbox, threat feed | Captures malicious URLs, attachments, sender patterns |
| Identify exposed assets | CMDB, asset inventory, cloud inventory | Shows what exists and what needs protection |
| Prioritize patching | Vulnerability scanner, exploit intel | Connects known exploits to vulnerable systems |
| Track account abuse | Identity provider logs, IAM telemetry | Reveals suspicious logins and privilege misuse |

**Threat intelligence source categories**

Threat intelligence becomes useful when you combine external intelligence with internal context. External data tells you what is happening in the world; internal data tells you whether it matters to you.

- **Open-source intelligence (OSINT)**
  - Public blogs, reports, social media, code repositories, and community feeds.
  - Good for broad awareness and early indicators.
  - Quality varies, so you should validate before actioning.
- **Commercial intelligence**
  - Vendor-curated feeds, analyst reports, and managed intelligence services.
  - Often includes enrichment, scoring, and analyst context.
  - Useful when you need higher confidence and lower operational overhead.
- **Government and sector sharing**
  - ISACs (Information Sharing and Analysis Centers), CERTs (Computer Emergency Response Teams), and public advisories.
  - Helpful for sector-specific threats and coordinated campaigns.
- **Internal intelligence**
  - Your own logs, incidents, detections, malware analysis, and hunting results.
  - Often the most relevant source because it reflects your environment.
- **Human intelligence and reporting**
  - Analyst notes, incident debriefs, user reports, and partner notifications.
  - Valuable for context that automated sources miss.

**Enrichment source categories**

Enrichment adds context to raw indicators so you can decide whether to alert, block, investigate, or ignore. A single IP address is rarely enough; you need surrounding context.

Important enrichment sources include:

- **Asset inventory and CMDB** (Configuration Management Database)
  - Tells you what systems, applications, owners, and business functions exist.
  - Helps answer whether an indicator touches a critical asset.
  - Example: a malicious domain contacted by a domain controller is more urgent than the same domain contacted by a test VM.
- **Vulnerability scanners**
  - Show missing patches, exposed services, and known weaknesses.
  - Help you prioritize indicators tied to exploitable software.
  - Example: if a feed mentions exploitation of a specific VPN appliance, you can query whether you run that version.
- **Identity providers**
  - Include SSO (Single Sign-On), directory services, MFA (Multi-Factor Authentication), and IAM (Identity and Access Management) logs.
  - Help correlate suspicious IPs, impossible travel, failed logins, and risky accounts.
- **EDR/XDR**
  - EDR (Endpoint Detection and Response) and XDR (Extended Detection and Response) provide endpoint and cross-domain telemetry.
  - Useful for process trees, command lines, parent-child relationships, and host-level sightings.
- **Threat actor and context sources**
  - Include actor profiles, campaign reports, TTP mappings, malware families, and infrastructure histories.
  - Help you understand intent, likely next steps, and expected dwell time.
- **Network and DNS telemetry**
  - DNS logs, proxy logs, firewall logs, and NetFlow can confirm whether an indicator is active in your environment.
- **Cloud and SaaS telemetry**
  - Cloud audit logs, CASB (Cloud Access Security Broker) data, and SaaS admin logs help identify abuse in cloud services.
- **Email and collaboration telemetry**
  - Message traces, attachment detonation results, and link-click logs help validate phishing claims.

A strong enrichment pipeline usually combines multiple sources before making a decision.

**Common enrichment fields and scoring attributes**

Enrichment should produce structured fields that help automation and analysts make consistent decisions.

Common fields include:

- **Confidence**
  - How reliable the indicator or assessment is.
  - Often expressed as low, medium, high, or a numeric score.
- **Severity**
  - How harmful the indicator appears to be if true.
  - Often tied to business impact or threat level.
- **Disposition**
  - The current handling state of the indicator.
  - Examples: malicious, suspicious, benign, unknown, false positive.
- **TTL** (Time to Live) or expiration
  - How long the indicator should remain active before revalidation.
  - Prevents stale indicators from causing noise.
- **Sighting frequency**
  - How often the indicator appears in your telemetry.
  - Repeated sightings may increase priority or indicate persistence.
- **First seen / last seen**
  - Helps determine recency and trend.
- **Source reliability**
  - How trustworthy the source is based on history and context.
- **Tags and labels**
  - Useful for campaign, actor, sector, geography, or control mapping.
- **Owner and workflow state**
  - Helps route items to the right team and track response.

A practical scoring model often combines source confidence and environmental relevance:

```text
overall_priority = source_confidence + internal_sighting_weight + asset_criticality + exploitability
```

You do not need a complex formula to be effective, but you do need consistency. The goal is to avoid treating every indicator as equally urgent.

**Indicator lifecycle management**

Indicators age quickly. Good programs manage indicators through their full lifecycle instead of treating them as permanent truth.

Key lifecycle actions include:

- **Aging**
  - Reduce priority as indicators become stale or lose relevance.
  - Older IPs and domains may be recycled or repurposed.
- **Expiration**
  - Remove or archive indicators when their TTL ends.
  - Revalidate before reusing them in blocking rules.
- **Suppression**
  - Temporarily mute known-benign matches to reduce alert fatigue.
  - Use carefully so you do not hide real activity.
- **False-positive handling**
  - Mark indicators as benign when evidence shows they are not malicious in your environment.
  - Document why the decision was made.
- **Reactivation**
  - Restore an indicator if new evidence appears.
- **Retirement**
  - Remove indicators that are no longer useful or are superseded by better detections.

> Do not keep every indicator in a permanent block list. Recycled IPs, shared hosting, and CDN infrastructure can create long-lived false positives.

**STIX and TAXII in practice**

STIX (Structured Threat Information Expression) and TAXII (Trusted Automated Exchange of Indicator Information) are common standards for sharing threat intelligence in a machine-readable way.

- **STIX**
  - Defines the content and structure of intelligence.
  - Uses objects such as indicators, malware, threat actors, campaigns, identities, relationships, sightings, and reports.
  - Supports relationships between objects, which is critical for context.
- **TAXII**
  - Defines how intelligence is transported and shared.
  - Commonly used to publish and retrieve STIX content between systems.

Practical STIX concepts:

- **Objects**
  - Represent entities such as an IP address, malware family, or report.
- **Relationships**
  - Connect objects, such as “malware uses infrastructure” or “campaign targets sector.”
- **Bundles**
  - Package multiple STIX objects together for exchange.
- **Sightings**
  - Record that an indicator or object was observed in a specific environment.
- **Reports**
  - Group related objects into a human-readable intelligence product.

Practical TAXII concepts:

- **Collections**
  - Logical groupings of intelligence content exposed by a TAXII server.
- **Polling**
  - A client retrieves content from a collection on a schedule.
- **Push or publish workflows**
  - Some deployments support server-side publication or automated ingestion into downstream tools.
- **Filtering and access control**
  - Consumers may only see collections they are authorized to access.

A common workflow is:

1. A threat feed provider publishes STIX objects to a TAXII collection.
2. Your TIP (Threat Intelligence Platform) polls the collection.
3. The TIP normalizes the data and enriches it with internal context.
4. The SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) and SOAR (Security Orchestration, Automation, and Response) tools consume the curated indicators.
5. Analysts review sightings and update disposition.

Example STIX-like structure:

```json
{
  "type": "indicator",
  "id": "indicator--1234",
  "name": "Malicious domain",
  "pattern": "[domain-name:value = 'bad-example.com']",
  "valid_from": "2026-04-26T00:00:00Z",
  "labels": ["malicious-activity"],
  "confidence": 80
}
```

Example TAXII client conceptually polling a collection:

```bash
curl -H "Accept: application/taxii+json;version=2.1" \
  -H "Authorization: Bearer $TAXII_TOKEN" \
  https://taxii.example.org/taxii2/collections/1234/objects/
```

**Sharing governance and handling rules**

Threat intelligence sharing is not just technical; it is also governed by handling rules. The most common concept is TLP (Traffic Light Protocol), which indicates how widely information may be shared.

- **TLP:RED**
  - For named recipients only.
  - Do not redistribute.
- **TLP:AMBER**
  - Limited sharing within the organization or trusted partners.
  - Use when the information is sensitive but actionable.
- **TLP:GREEN**
  - May be shared within the community or organization as allowed by policy.
- **TLP:CLEAR**
  - May be shared publicly.

Handling rules matter because intelligence often contains sensitive details such as victim data, internal detections, or partner information. You should preserve the original marking and apply your organization’s policy before redistribution.

Other governance considerations:

- Respect data classification and privacy requirements.
- Remove unnecessary personally identifiable information (PII) before broad sharing.
- Track provenance so recipients know where the intelligence came from.
- Record whether the item is raw, analyzed, or finished intelligence.

**Operational workflow in a SOC**

A practical SOC workflow ties intelligence to detection and response. The goal is not to collect indicators for their own sake; it is to drive action.

Typical workflow:

1. Ingest external feeds into a TIP or SIEM.
2. Enrich indicators with internal context such as asset ownership and recent sightings.
3. Score and prioritize based on confidence, severity, and relevance.
4. Push high-confidence indicators into detections, watchlists, or block lists.
5. Route suspicious matches to analysts for validation.
6. Suppress or expire indicators that generate repeated false positives.
7. Feed confirmed findings back into the intelligence program.

Example SIEM/SOAR-style workflow:

```yaml
workflow:
  name: "High-confidence malicious domain handling"
  trigger: "new_indicator_ingested"
  conditions:
    confidence: ">= 80"
    disposition: "malicious"
  actions:
    - enrich:
        sources:
          - cmdb
          - dns_logs
          - proxy_logs
          - edr
    - if:
        asset_criticality: "high"
      then:
        - create_case
        - notify: "soc-tier-2"
        - add_to_watchlist: true
    - if:
        sightings_24h: ">= 3"
      then:
        - escalate: true
        - block_domain: true
```

A realistic analyst decision might look like this:

- A malicious domain appears in a commercial feed with high confidence.
- DNS logs show one internal workstation queried it twice.
- The CMDB shows the workstation belongs to finance.
- EDR shows a suspicious PowerShell process shortly after the DNS lookup.
- The indicator is promoted from “feed-only” to “confirmed internal sighting,” and the case is escalated.

**Practical enrichment examples**

Enrichment is most effective when you combine multiple data sources around a single indicator.

Example: suspicious IP address

```bash
# Check whether the IP appears in DNS, proxy, and firewall logs
grep -R "203.0.113.45" /var/log/dns /var/log/proxy /var/log/firewall

# Query a threat intel platform API
curl -H "Authorization: Bearer $TIP_TOKEN" \
  "https://tip.example.org/api/indicators?value=203.0.113.45"
```

Example: asset and vulnerability context

```bash
# Find hosts with a vulnerable package version
nmap --script vuln 10.10.20.0/24

# Query endpoint inventory for a specific software version
curl -H "Authorization: Bearer $CMDB_TOKEN" \
  "https://cmdb.example.org/api/assets?software=VPN-Client&version=5.2.1"
```

Example: identity correlation

```bash
# Search for suspicious sign-ins from a known bad IP
az ad sign-in-log list --filter "ipAddress eq '203.0.113.45'"

# Example with a generic log query
jq '.events[] | select(.src_ip=="203.0.113.45")' auth-logs.json
```

Example: endpoint validation

```powershell
Get-Process | Where-Object { $_.Path -like "*AppData*" }
Get-WinEvent -LogName Microsoft-Windows-PowerShell/Operational -MaxEvents 50
```

These examples show the core pattern: identify the indicator, correlate it with internal telemetry, and decide whether it is actionable.

**Detection engineering and blocking decisions**

Threat intelligence can feed multiple control points, but you should choose the right one based on confidence and business impact.

- **Block**
  - Use for high-confidence, high-risk indicators with low false-positive risk.
  - Examples: known malicious domains, confirmed phishing infrastructure.
- **Alert**
  - Use when confidence is moderate or the indicator needs analyst review.
  - Examples: suspicious IPs, newly registered domains, weakly associated infrastructure.
- **Hunt**
  - Use when the indicator is interesting but not yet strong enough for automated action.
  - Examples: actor TTPs, unusual parent-child process patterns.
- **Monitor**
  - Use when the indicator is low confidence or likely to be noisy.
  - Examples: shared hosting IPs, CDN-hosted content, broad geolocation matches.

A good rule is to avoid hard blocking on indicators that are volatile, shared, or poorly scoped. Instead, use layered detections such as domain reputation plus DNS frequency plus endpoint behavior.

**Common pitfalls**

- Treating all feeds as equally trustworthy.
- Blocking on stale indicators without expiration.
- Ignoring internal context such as asset criticality.
- Failing to document why an indicator was marked benign.
- Over-sharing intelligence without TLP or policy review.
- Using raw indicators without relationships or sightings.
- Collecting data without a clear PIR.

**Exam focus summary**

For exam purposes, remember these high-value distinctions:

- **Strategic** intelligence supports leadership and long-term risk decisions.
- **Operational** intelligence supports campaigns, incidents, and response planning.
- **Tactical** intelligence supports detections and immediate controls.
- **Technical** intelligence is machine-consumable and tool-friendly.
- **PIRs** guide what you collect and why.
- **Collection management** maps requirements to sources.
- **Enrichment** adds context from CMDB, vulnerability scanners, identity systems, EDR/XDR, and threat actor sources.
- **STIX** structures intelligence content; **TAXII** transports it.
- **TLP** controls how intelligence may be shared.
- Indicator management includes aging, expiration, suppression, and false-positive handling.
