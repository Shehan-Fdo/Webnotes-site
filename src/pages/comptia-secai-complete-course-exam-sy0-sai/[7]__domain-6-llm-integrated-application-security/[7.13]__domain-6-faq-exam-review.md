## **Course:** CompTIA SecAI+ Complete Course (Exam SY0-SAI+)

**Domain 6 overview**

Domain 6 typically serves as the review and integration domain for the exam: you are expected to connect AI security concepts, governance, risk, operations, and incident response into practical decisions. In exam terms, this means you should not only recognize definitions, but also choose the safest, most defensible action in a scenario.

Focus on how controls work together:

- **Preventive controls** reduce the chance of misuse or compromise.
- **Detective controls** reveal suspicious behavior, drift, or policy violations.
- **Corrective controls** restore service or reduce impact after an event.
- **Administrative controls** define policy, process, and accountability.
- **Technical controls** enforce rules through systems and tooling.

A strong review strategy is to map each scenario to:
- the asset being protected,
- the threat or failure mode,
- the control type that best fits,
- the operational tradeoff you accept.

**High-yield exam themes**

The exam often tests whether you can distinguish similar concepts under pressure. Pay special attention to these recurring themes:

- **Data governance**: classification, retention, lineage, access control, and approved use.
- **Model governance**: versioning, approval, change control, and reproducibility.
- **Risk management**: likelihood, impact, residual risk, and risk treatment.
- **Security operations**: monitoring, alert triage, logging, and escalation.
- **Incident response**: containment, eradication, recovery, and lessons learned.
- **AI-specific threats**: prompt injection, data poisoning, model inversion, membership inference, and supply chain compromise.
- **Human factors**: social engineering, overreliance on model output, and poor validation.

When you see a question that asks for the “best” answer, look for the option that preserves evidence, limits blast radius, and follows policy. In many cases, the correct choice is not the fastest one, but the one that is most controlled and reversible.

**FAQ: core concepts you should be able to explain**

<dl>
<dt>What is the difference between AI security and traditional cybersecurity?</dt>
<dd>Traditional cybersecurity protects systems, networks, and data from unauthorized access and disruption. AI security adds model-specific concerns such as prompt manipulation, training data integrity, output reliability, and misuse of generated content.</dd>

<dt>Why does model governance matter?</dt>
<dd>Models change over time through retraining, fine-tuning, prompt updates, and dependency changes. Governance ensures you can prove what version was approved, who approved it, what data it used, and whether it still behaves as expected.</dd>

<dt>What is residual risk?</dt>
<dd>Residual risk is the risk that remains after controls are applied. You rarely eliminate risk entirely; instead, you reduce it to an acceptable level or transfer, avoid, or accept it.</dd>

<dt>Why is logging important for AI systems?</dt>
<dd>Logs support detection, investigation, compliance, and reproducibility. For AI systems, logs should capture prompts, responses, model version, policy decisions, and relevant context while respecting privacy and retention requirements.</dd>
</dl>

**Risk management review**

Risk management is one of the most testable areas because it connects policy to action. You should be able to identify the right treatment and explain why it fits the scenario.

- **Avoidance** means stopping the risky activity entirely.
- **Mitigation** means reducing likelihood or impact with controls.
- **Transfer** means shifting financial impact or responsibility, such as through insurance or contracts.
- **Acceptance** means formally acknowledging the risk and proceeding.

A practical example:

```text
Scenario: A team wants to deploy a generative AI assistant that may expose sensitive customer data.
Best first step: classify the data and restrict the assistant to approved sources.
Why: you reduce exposure before deployment and create a defensible control boundary.
```

For exam questions, remember:
- If the risk is unknown, you usually need assessment before deployment.
- If the issue is data exposure, focus on access control, filtering, and least privilege.
- If the issue is unreliable output, focus on validation, human review, and guardrails.
- If the issue is third-party dependency risk, focus on vendor review and supply chain controls.

**AI threat scenarios and responses**

AI systems introduce attack paths that look different from classic web or endpoint attacks, but the response pattern is often familiar: detect, contain, validate, and recover.

- **Prompt injection** attempts to override system instructions or coerce the model into unsafe behavior.
  - Use instruction hierarchy, input filtering, output constraints, and tool permission boundaries.
- **Data poisoning** corrupts training or retrieval data to influence model behavior.
  - Use data provenance, integrity checks, review workflows, and source trust scoring.
- **Model inversion** tries to infer training data from model behavior.
  - Reduce sensitive data exposure, limit memorization, and apply privacy-preserving techniques where appropriate.
- **Membership inference** attempts to determine whether a record was in the training set.
  - Use privacy controls, regularization, and careful data minimization.
- **Supply chain compromise** affects models, libraries, plugins, or hosted services.
  - Validate dependencies, pin versions, review updates, and monitor for anomalous behavior.

A useful mental model is to ask whether the attack targets:
- the **input**,
- the **training data**,
- the **model artifact**,
- the **retrieval layer**,
- the **tooling or plugin layer**,
- or the **human operator**.

**Operational controls and monitoring**

Security operations for AI should be observable. If you cannot see what the system is doing, you cannot confidently trust it.

Typical monitoring signals include:
- unusual prompt volume,
- repeated jailbreak attempts,
- unexpected tool calls,
- abnormal token usage,
- sudden changes in output quality,
- access from unapproved identities or locations,
- retrieval of sensitive documents outside normal patterns.

Useful command-line examples for operational review:

```bash
# Inspect recent authentication events on Linux
journalctl -u ssh --since "24 hours ago"

# Review container logs for an AI service
docker logs --since 1h ai-assistant

# Check active listening ports
ss -tulpn

# View system resource pressure
top
```

If you are reviewing a cloud or containerized deployment, also think about:
- identity and access management,
- secrets handling,
- network segmentation,
- API rate limiting,
- audit logging,
- and alert routing to the right team.

**Incident response review**

Incident response questions usually test sequence and containment logic. The safest answer often starts with preserving evidence and limiting spread.

A standard response flow is:

1. **Identify** the event and confirm scope.
2. **Contain** the issue to stop further impact.
3. **Eradicate** the root cause or malicious artifact.
4. **Recover** services and validate normal operation.
5. **Lessons learned** improve controls and procedures.

For AI incidents, containment may include:
- disabling a compromised plugin,
- rolling back to a known-good model version,
- blocking a malicious prompt source,
- revoking API keys,
- or switching to a manual approval workflow.

> Do not retrain or overwrite evidence before you preserve logs, prompts, model versions, and relevant artifacts. That can destroy forensic value and weaken your investigation.

A practical containment example:

```bash
# Disable a service during investigation
sudo systemctl stop ai-gateway

# Preserve logs before rotation
sudo cp /var/log/ai-gateway.log /secure/evidence/

# Rotate credentials after suspected compromise
aws iam update-access-key --user-name ai-service --access-key-id AKIA... --status Inactive
```

**Governance, compliance, and policy**

Governance is the framework that makes AI use defensible. It defines who can approve, what can be used, where it can be used, and how exceptions are handled.

You should understand these policy elements:
- **Acceptable use**: what users may and may not do with AI tools.
- **Data classification**: how sensitive information is labeled and handled.
- **Retention**: how long prompts, outputs, and logs are stored.
- **Access review**: periodic validation of who still needs access.
- **Change management**: approval and testing before production changes.
- **Exception handling**: documented approval for deviations from policy.

Practical governance controls often include:
- approved model registries,
- prompt templates,
- content filters,
- human review for high-impact decisions,
- and periodic red-team testing.

Example policy snippet:

```yaml
ai_use_policy:
  allowed_data:
    - public
    - internal
  restricted_data:
    - confidential
    - regulated
  requirements:
    - human_review_for_customer_facing_output: true
    - log_prompts_and_responses: true
    - approved_models_only: true
```

**Model lifecycle and change control**

A model is not static. Even if the code stays the same, the behavior can change because of new data, updated prompts, altered retrieval sources, or vendor-side updates.

You should be able to explain:
- **Versioning**: tracking model and prompt versions.
- **Baseline testing**: comparing current behavior to expected behavior.
- **Approval workflow**: ensuring changes are reviewed before release.
- **Rollback**: returning to a known-good state if behavior degrades.
- **Reproducibility**: being able to recreate a result with the same inputs and version.

Good practice is to store:
- model identifier,
- prompt template version,
- retrieval corpus version,
- policy rules in effect,
- and test results from validation runs.

Example deployment metadata:

```json
{
  "model": "llm-prod-3.2",
  "prompt_version": "v14",
  "retrieval_index": "kb-2026-04-01",
  "approved_by": "security-review-board",
  "last_tested": "2026-04-20"
}
```

**Practical exam traps**

Many exam questions are designed to test whether you choose the most appropriate control, not just any plausible one.

Watch for these traps:
- Choosing **detection** when the question asks for prevention.
- Choosing **training** when the issue is actually access control.
- Choosing **model retraining** when the immediate need is containment.
- Choosing **acceptance** when the risk is still unassessed.
- Choosing **automation** when human review is required for high-impact output.
- Confusing **data privacy** with **data security**; privacy concerns lawful and appropriate use, while security concerns protection from unauthorized access and alteration.

If two answers seem close, prefer the one that:
- reduces exposure,
- preserves evidence,
- follows policy,
- and can be audited later.

**CLI and configuration review examples**

You may see scenario-based questions that involve operational commands or configuration patterns. Know what these examples are doing conceptually.

```bash
# Search logs for suspicious prompt patterns
grep -iE "ignore previous|system prompt|jailbreak" /var/log/ai-app.log

# Check file integrity for a model artifact
sha256sum model.bin

# Compare current and expected configuration
diff -u /etc/ai-app/config.yml /secure/baseline/config.yml
```

```yaml
security:
  input_validation: strict
  output_filtering: enabled
  tool_access:
    browser: false
    shell: false
    database: read_only
  audit_logging: enabled
```

These examples reinforce a common exam principle: secure AI systems by constraining inputs, limiting tool access, and logging enough detail to investigate problems without exposing unnecessary sensitive data.

**Quick review checklist**

Before the exam, make sure you can do the following without hesitation:

- Define the major AI-specific threats and the control that addresses each one.
- Distinguish between risk treatment options.
- Explain why logging, versioning, and change control matter.
- Identify the correct incident response phase for a scenario.
- Choose the best control type for a given business requirement.
- Recognize when human review is necessary.
- Explain how governance supports secure and compliant AI use.

**Final exam mindset**

Treat each question as a decision under constraints. The best answer usually balances security, business continuity, and auditability. If the scenario involves sensitive data, untrusted inputs, or high-impact decisions, favor controls that are restrictive, observable, and reversible.
