## **Course:** CompTIA SecAI+ Complete Course (Exam SY0-SAI+)

**Overview**

Model poisoning and training data manipulation are integrity attacks against machine learning (ML) systems. The attacker changes training inputs, labels, gradients, checkpoints, or metadata so the resulting model behaves incorrectly, unfairly, or maliciously. In exam terms, the key idea is that the attack targets the **training pipeline**, not just the deployed model.

These attacks matter because ML systems often trust large, distributed, and partially unverified data sources. If you cannot trust the data lifecycle, you cannot fully trust the model output.

**Core terminology**

- **Data poisoning** usually refers to corrupting the training data itself.
- **Model poisoning** usually refers to corrupting the model update process, such as malicious gradients, weights, or checkpoints.
- On many exams and in real-world security discussions, the terms overlap because both aim to influence model behavior through the training process.
- Treat them as **distinct but related**:
  - Data poisoning focuses on the dataset.
  - Model poisoning focuses on the model parameters or updates.
  - Both are forms of training-time integrity compromise.

> In practice, the distinction is often contextual rather than absolute. A poisoned dataset can produce a poisoned model, and a poisoned update can be delivered through a data-like pipeline in federated or distributed training.

**Threat model and attacker access**

Poisoning is most feasible where the attacker can influence data before or during training. Common access levels include:

- **External contributor**
  - Uploads samples to a public dataset, feedback system, or crowdsourcing platform.
  - Submits malicious labels, prompts, or examples through a web form or API.
- **Insider**
  - Has access to data preparation, labeling, feature engineering, or training jobs.
  - Can alter preprocessing scripts, label maps, or training manifests.
- **Supply-chain attacker**
  - Compromises a third-party dataset, model artifact, dependency, or data vendor.
  - Inserts malicious content upstream so downstream teams ingest it as trusted input.

Poisoning is most feasible at these ML lifecycle stages:

- **Data collection**
  - Public scraping, user-generated content, telemetry, and partner feeds are common entry points.
- **Labeling and annotation**
  - Crowdsourced labeling is vulnerable to malicious or low-quality annotators.
- **Preprocessing and feature engineering**
  - Attackers may alter normalization, filtering, deduplication, or tokenization logic.
- **Training and fine-tuning**
  - Malicious samples can be injected directly into the training set or batch stream.
- **Federated aggregation**
  - Malicious clients can submit poisoned updates during distributed learning.
- **Model registry and artifact handling**
  - Tampered checkpoints or metadata can propagate a compromised model.

**Attack phases**

A poisoning campaign often follows a predictable sequence:

1. **Reconnaissance**
   - The attacker learns the model objective, data sources, labeling rules, and retraining cadence.
2. **Injection**
   - The attacker inserts poisoned samples, labels, gradients, or artifacts.
3. **Stealth**
   - The attacker tries to keep the poison statistically plausible to avoid detection.
4. **Activation**
   - The model learns the malicious association or backdoor during training.
5. **Persistence**
   - The attacker aims for the behavior to survive retraining, validation, or partial cleanup.

**Common poisoning variants**

- **Indiscriminate poisoning**
  - Degrades overall model performance.
  - Goal: reduce accuracy, increase error rates, or create instability.
  - Example: flood the dataset with mislabeled samples so the classifier becomes unreliable.
- **Targeted poisoning**
  - Causes a specific input or class to be misclassified.
  - Goal: create a backdoor or trigger-based behavior.
  - Example: any image containing a small sticker is classified as a chosen label.
- **Backdoor poisoning**
  - A targeted form where a trigger causes a hidden malicious behavior.
  - Often designed to pass normal validation while remaining exploitable later.
- **Availability attack**
  - Aims to make the model broadly unusable.
  - Often overlaps with indiscriminate poisoning.
- **Integrity attack**
  - Aims to change specific decisions while preserving general performance.

**Source-specific poisoning scenarios**

- **Federated learning poisoning**
  - Malicious clients send crafted gradients or model updates.
  - The attacker may use sign-flipping, scaling, or stealthy update shaping.
  - Defenses often rely on robust aggregation, client reputation, and anomaly detection.
- **Crowdsourced labeling abuse**
  - Attackers submit incorrect labels at scale or coordinate label manipulation.
  - This is common in human-in-the-loop pipelines and annotation marketplaces.
- **Data vendor compromise**
  - A third-party dataset is altered before ingestion.
  - This is especially dangerous when the dataset is treated as authoritative.
- **Prompt or feedback poisoning**
  - In systems that retrain from user feedback, malicious prompts, ratings, or conversations can bias future behavior.

**How poisoning differs from other ML attacks**

- **Evasion attacks** happen at inference time and try to fool a trained model with crafted inputs.
- **Poisoning attacks** happen before or during training and try to shape the model itself.
- **Model extraction** steals model behavior.
- **Poisoning** changes model behavior.

**Detection and monitoring**

Detection is useful, but it is not perfect. Exam questions often expect you to understand both the method and its limitations.

- **Statistical outlier detection**
  - Looks for unusual feature distributions, label patterns, or embedding clusters.
  - Limitation: sophisticated poisons can mimic normal data and evade thresholds.
  - Risk: false positives on rare but legitimate edge cases.
- **Influence functions and sample attribution**
  - Estimate which training points most affect a prediction.
  - Limitation: computationally expensive and less reliable on large deep models.
  - Risk: false negatives when poison influence is distributed across many samples.
- **Gradient anomaly detection**
  - Flags suspicious client updates in federated learning.
  - Limitation: attackers can craft low-magnitude or colluding updates to blend in.
  - Risk: robust thresholds may reject honest but noisy clients.
- **Data provenance checks**
  - Compare hashes, signatures, and lineage records.
  - Limitation: provenance proves origin and integrity, not semantic correctness.
  - Risk: signed malicious data is still malicious if the signer is compromised.
- **Validation set monitoring**
  - Tracks sudden changes in accuracy, class confusion, or trigger-like behavior.
  - Limitation: targeted backdoors may not affect standard validation metrics.
  - Risk: false negatives when the trigger is absent from the test set.

A practical exam takeaway is that detection methods often trade off sensitivity and specificity. If you tune them aggressively, you reduce false negatives but increase false positives. If you tune them loosely, you miss stealthy poisoning.

**Secure MLOps controls**

Secure MLOps means applying security controls across the ML pipeline, not only at deployment.

- **Signed datasets**
  - Sign dataset releases or manifests so tampering is detectable.
  - Use cryptographic hashes for files and manifests.
- **Lineage tracking**
  - Record where data came from, who changed it, and which pipeline consumed it.
  - Common tooling patterns include dataset versioning and artifact lineage graphs.
- **Approval gates**
  - Require human or policy approval before a dataset, label set, or model enters training.
  - Use separate approval for high-risk sources or production retraining.
- **Immutable artifact repositories**
  - Store datasets, feature snapshots, and model checkpoints in write-once or append-only repositories.
  - This reduces silent replacement of trusted artifacts.
- **Least privilege**
  - Restrict who can upload data, modify labels, trigger training, or publish models.
- **Separation of duties**
  - The person who approves data should not be the same person who can silently replace it.
- **Reproducible builds and training**
  - Pin dependencies, container images, and preprocessing code.
  - Reproducibility helps you detect unexpected changes.
- **Policy-as-code**
  - Enforce checks automatically in CI/CD and ML pipelines.

Example pipeline controls in a common MLOps style:

```yaml
# Example CI/CD gate for an ML training pipeline
stages:
  - validate
  - approve
  - train
  - register

validate_dataset:
  stage: validate
  script:
    - sha256sum -c dataset.sha256
    - python validate_schema.py --input data/train.parquet
    - python check_label_distribution.py --input data/train.parquet
  allow_failure: false

approve_training:
  stage: approve
  when: manual
  script:
    - echo "Approved by data steward"

train_model:
  stage: train
  script:
    - python train.py --data data/train.parquet --output artifacts/model.pkl
    - python evaluate.py --model artifacts/model.pkl --threshold 0.92

register_model:
  stage: register
  script:
    - python register_model.py --artifact artifacts/model.pkl --sign
```

Example of a simple integrity check before training:

```bash
sha256sum -c manifests/train.sha256
python -m ml_pipeline.validate --dataset s3://ml-bucket/datasets/train_v12/
```

Example of a signed artifact workflow:

```bash
cosign sign-blob --key cosign.key model.pkl > model.pkl.sig
cosign verify-blob --key cosign.pub --signature model.pkl.sig model.pkl
```

**Data sanitization and robust training**

Sanitization and robust training reduce the impact of poisoned samples, but they do not guarantee safety.

- **Trimming**
  - Remove extreme values or suspicious samples before training.
  - Useful when poison creates obvious statistical anomalies.
- **Robust loss functions**
  - Reduce the influence of outliers during optimization.
  - Helpful when a small number of samples are malicious or noisy.
- **Differential privacy**
  - Adds noise and limits the influence of any single record.
  - Can reduce memorization and make some poisoning harder, though it is not a complete defense.
- **Outlier-resistant training**
  - Uses robust estimators, median-based aggregation, or trimmed means.
  - Common in federated learning and noisy-label settings.
- **Label smoothing and noise-aware training**
  - Can reduce overconfidence in mislabeled data.
  - Not a substitute for source validation.
- **Robust aggregation**
  - In distributed learning, methods such as trimmed mean, coordinate-wise median, or Krum-like approaches can reduce the effect of malicious clients.
  - These methods can still fail under adaptive or colluding attackers.

**Practical example: robust preprocessing**

```python
import pandas as pd
from sklearn.ensemble import IsolationForest

df = pd.read_parquet("train.parquet")

# Basic feature outlier screening
iso = IsolationForest(contamination=0.01, random_state=42)
mask = iso.fit_predict(df[["feature1", "feature2", "feature3"]])

clean_df = df[mask == 1].copy()
clean_df.to_parquet("train_clean.parquet")
```

This kind of filtering can help, but it may also remove legitimate rare cases. For exam purposes, remember that sanitization is a risk-reduction control, not a guarantee.

**Federated learning controls**

Federated learning increases exposure because the server aggregates updates from many clients.

- Use **secure aggregation** where possible to reduce visibility into individual updates.
- Apply **client authentication** and device attestation when feasible.
- Rate-limit or quarantine new clients with unusual update patterns.
- Compare update norms, cosine similarity, and loss impact across clients.
- Use robust aggregation rather than plain averaging when threat models justify it.

Example of a conceptual aggregation policy:

```python
# Pseudocode for robust federated aggregation
updates = collect_client_updates()
filtered = [u for u in updates if norm(u) < threshold and is_consistent(u)]
global_update = trimmed_mean(filtered)
apply(global_update)
```

**Incident response for suspected poisoning**

A poisoning incident response plan should be explicit and fast. Retraining alone is not enough.

1. **Containment**
   - Pause retraining jobs and freeze model promotion.
   - Quarantine suspicious datasets, clients, or annotation sources.
   - Revoke access tokens or API keys if abuse is ongoing.
2. **Preservation and forensics**
   - Snapshot datasets, manifests, logs, checkpoints, and pipeline metadata.
   - Preserve hashes, timestamps, and lineage records.
   - Identify the first bad version and the earliest suspicious source.
3. **Impact assessment**
   - Determine whether the issue is indiscriminate degradation or a targeted backdoor.
   - Test for trigger-based behavior, class-specific drift, and unusual error patterns.
4. **Eradication**
   - Remove poisoned records, revoke compromised sources, and patch pipeline weaknesses.
   - Rebuild from trusted artifacts and verified dependencies.
5. **Recovery**
   - Retrain from clean data, validate against holdout sets, and re-sign artifacts.
   - Reintroduce the model through approval gates and staged rollout.
6. **Notification**
   - Inform stakeholders, data owners, security teams, and affected business owners.
   - If regulated data or customer impact is involved, follow legal and compliance procedures.
7. **Lessons learned**
   - Update controls, detection rules, and supplier requirements.
   - Add tests for the attack pattern that succeeded.

**Practical response checklist**

- Freeze the model registry entry for the suspected version.
- Block automatic retraining from the affected source.
- Compare training manifests across recent runs.
- Recompute dataset hashes and verify signatures.
- Review annotation logs for unusual label distributions.
- Run backdoor and trigger tests on candidate models.
- Document the root cause and the exact poisoned entry point.

**Exam focus points**

- Know the difference between **data poisoning** and **model poisoning**, and remember that they often overlap in practice.
- Be able to identify attacker access levels: external contributor, insider, and supply-chain compromise.
- Understand where poisoning is easiest in the ML lifecycle: collection, labeling, preprocessing, training, federated aggregation, and registry handling.
- Distinguish **indiscriminate** from **targeted** poisoning.
- Recognize source-specific attacks such as **federated learning poisoning** and **crowdsourced labeling abuse**.
- Know that detection methods have limitations, especially false positives and false negatives.
- Remember secure MLOps controls such as signed datasets, lineage tracking, approval gates, and immutable artifact repositories.
- Be able to name robust training defenses such as trimming, robust loss functions, differential privacy, and outlier-resistant aggregation.
- Know the incident response sequence: contain, preserve, assess, eradicate, recover, notify, and improve.

**Quick comparison**

| Topic | What it means | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Data poisoning | Corrupting training data | Changes what the model learns |
| Model poisoning | Corrupting updates or parameters | Alters the model directly |
| Indiscriminate poisoning | Broad performance degradation | Causes general unreliability |
| Targeted poisoning | Specific misclassification or backdoor | Enables stealthy abuse |
| Detection | Statistical, provenance, or attribution checks | Helps but is not foolproof |
| Secure MLOps | Signed data, lineage, gates, immutability | Reduces tampering risk |

**Summary**

Poisoning attacks exploit trust in the ML supply chain. The strongest defenses combine provenance, access control, approval workflows, robust training, and continuous monitoring. For the exam, focus on where the attacker can intervene, how the attack changes model behavior, and why no single detection method is sufficient on its own.
