## **Course:** CompTIA SecAI+ Complete Course (Exam SY0-SAI+)

**Overview**

Deepfakes and synthetic media are AI-generated or AI-altered audio, video, images, and text that can convincingly imitate real people, places, or events. In security operations, the main risk is not just misinformation; it is **impersonation at scale**. Attackers use these tools to bypass trust, pressure employees, and manipulate business processes such as payments, password resets, and executive approvals.

AI-enabled social engineering combines traditional persuasion with machine-generated realism. The attacker may clone a voice, generate a believable video call, or draft highly tailored messages using public data from social media, company websites, and leaked records. The result is a faster, more convincing attack chain with less manual effort.

**Core concepts**

- **Deepfake**: synthetic audio, video, or image content that imitates a real person or scene.
- **Synthetic media**: a broader category that includes AI-generated content, not only impersonation.
- **AI-enabled social engineering**: manipulation that uses AI to improve targeting, realism, scale, or timing.
- **Impersonation risk**: the chance that a user accepts a fake identity as legitimate.

A useful mental model is that AI does not replace social engineering; it amplifies it. The attacker still needs a human decision, but AI reduces the clues that defenders traditionally relied on, such as awkward phrasing, poor audio quality, or obvious visual artifacts.

**How attackers use synthetic media**

Attackers use synthetic media in several common ways:

- **Voice cloning** to imitate executives, help desk callers, or family members in urgent scenarios.
- **Video deepfakes** to create fake meetings, interviews, or recorded messages.
- **Synthetic text** to produce polished phishing emails, chat messages, and SMS lures.
- **Face swapping and image generation** to create fake IDs, profile photos, or supporting documents.
- **Conversation simulation** to maintain a believable back-and-forth during a scam.

These techniques are especially effective when the attacker has context. Public conference videos, voicemail greetings, LinkedIn posts, and corporate org charts can provide enough material to mimic tone, vocabulary, and relationships.

**Common attack patterns**

- **Business Email Compromise (BEC)**: an attacker impersonates an executive or vendor to redirect funds or change payment details.
- **Help desk fraud**: a caller uses a cloned voice or scripted details to reset credentials or MFA (multi-factor authentication) factors.
- **Vendor impersonation**: synthetic media supports fake invoices, fake onboarding, or fake contract changes.
- **Credential harvesting**: AI-generated lures increase click-through rates on phishing pages.
- **Disinformation campaigns**: fake audio or video is used to damage trust, influence decisions, or create confusion during incidents.

A deepfake does not need to be perfect to succeed. It only needs to be believable long enough for the target to act. That is why urgency, authority, and secrecy remain central to the attack.

**Why these attacks work**

AI-enabled social engineering succeeds because it exploits human shortcuts:

- **Authority bias**: people comply when a message appears to come from a leader or trusted partner.
- **Urgency**: short deadlines reduce verification.
- **Familiarity**: a recognizable voice or face lowers suspicion.
- **Fear of consequences**: users act quickly to avoid missing a payment, blocking a deal, or upsetting management.
- **Cognitive overload**: busy staff may not inspect subtle inconsistencies.

Attackers also benefit from the fact that many organizations still rely on weak identity proofing. If a help desk accepts a voice call as sufficient proof, a cloned voice can become a credential reset tool.

**Indicators of synthetic media**

No single sign proves a deepfake, but defenders should look for patterns:

- **Audio anomalies**
  - unnatural cadence or pauses
  - inconsistent breathing or background noise
  - robotic transitions between words
  - mismatched emotion and content
- **Video anomalies**
  - irregular blinking or facial movement
  - lip-sync drift
  - lighting inconsistencies
  - distorted hands, teeth, or edges
- **Text anomalies**
  - overly polished but generic language
  - subtle tone shifts from the real sender
  - unusual urgency or secrecy
  - requests that bypass normal process
- **Context anomalies**
  - unusual timing
  - unexpected payment changes
  - requests outside normal channels
  - contact from a new number or account

> A realistic deepfake may not show obvious visual defects. Verification should rely on process controls, not only on human intuition.

**Verification controls**

The strongest defense is to verify identity through a channel that the attacker is unlikely to control.

- **Out-of-band verification**: confirm a request using a separate trusted method, such as a known phone number or internal ticketing workflow.
- **Call-back procedures**: hang up and call the person back using a number already on file.
- **Code words or shared secrets**: use carefully managed phrases for high-risk scenarios, though these must be protected and rotated if exposed.
- **Approval thresholds**: require secondary approval for payments, account changes, or sensitive access.
- **Identity proofing**: use stronger checks for password resets and MFA enrollment.
- **Least privilege**: limit what a single help desk agent or manager can approve.

A practical workflow for a suspicious executive request might look like this:

1. Pause the request and avoid confirming any sensitive detail.
2. Verify the sender using a known-good contact method.
3. Escalate to a second approver for financial or access changes.
4. Document the event in the ticketing system.
5. Report the attempt to security operations if fraud is suspected.

**Technical controls and tooling**

Organizations can reduce risk with layered controls across identity, email, endpoint, and monitoring systems.

- **Email authentication**
  - `SPF` (Sender Policy Framework)
  - `DKIM` (DomainKeys Identified Mail)
  - `DMARC` (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting, and Conformance)
- **MFA hardening**
  - phishing-resistant methods such as FIDO2/WebAuthn
  - number matching or challenge-based prompts
  - device binding where supported
- **Logging and detection**
  - SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) correlation for unusual approvals
  - UEBA (User and Entity Behavior Analytics) for abnormal communication patterns
  - call center logging for repeated reset attempts
- **Content provenance**
  - signed media workflows where available
  - metadata preservation
  - watermarking or authenticity verification when supported by the platform

Example email authentication checks:

```bash
dig txt example.com
dig txt _dmarc.example.com
dig txt selector1._domainkey.example.com
```

Example of a DMARC policy record:

```txt
_dmarc.example.com. IN TXT "v=DMARC1; p=quarantine; rua=mailto:dmarc-reports@example.com; adkim=s; aspf=s"
```

Example of a help desk verification script:

```text
1. Confirm the caller's identity using a known callback number.
2. Ask for a ticket number or pre-established verification step.
3. Refuse password reset or MFA changes without policy-approved proof.
4. Escalate any urgent executive request that bypasses normal controls.
```

**Detection and response workflow**

When you suspect a deepfake or synthetic-media attack, treat it as both a fraud issue and a security incident.

1. Preserve the evidence.
   - Save the message, headers, call logs, screenshots, and timestamps.
   - Export chat transcripts and voicemail files if policy allows.
2. Validate the source.
   - Check sender domains, phone numbers, account history, and recent changes.
   - Compare the request against known business processes.
3. Contain the impact.
   - Freeze suspicious payments or account changes.
   - Disable compromised accounts if compromise is confirmed.
4. Notify stakeholders.
   - Inform finance, legal, HR, and leadership as needed.
   - Use a controlled communication path to avoid further impersonation.
5. Hunt for related activity.
   - Search for similar lures, repeated targets, or linked infrastructure.
   - Review authentication logs and mailbox rules.
6. Document lessons learned.
   - Update playbooks, training, and approval workflows.

**Real-world examples**

- A finance employee receives a voicemail that sounds like the CFO requesting an urgent wire transfer. The correct response is to verify through a known internal number and require dual approval.
- A help desk agent receives a video call from a “remote executive” asking for MFA reset. The agent should not rely on the video alone and should follow identity proofing policy.
- A recruiter receives a polished AI-generated candidate profile with a synthetic headshot and fabricated work history. The organization should validate references and identity through trusted channels.

**Defensive policy design**

Policies should assume that voice, video, and text can all be forged.

- Require **two-person approval** for high-value transfers and privileged access changes.
- Prohibit password resets based only on caller recognition.
- Define escalation paths for suspected impersonation.
- Train staff to challenge urgency and secrecy.
- Limit public exposure of executive voice samples, direct numbers, and internal process details.
- Review social media and public web content for unnecessary identity clues.

A strong policy is specific enough to be actionable. “Be careful” is not a control; “verify all payment changes using a known callback number and second approver” is.

**User awareness and training**

Training should focus on behavior, not just examples of fake media.

- Teach users to slow down when a request is urgent.
- Encourage verification of unusual requests through a second channel.
- Show how attackers combine public information with AI-generated content.
- Reinforce that a familiar voice or face is not proof of identity.
- Include reporting steps so users know what to do immediately.

Short simulations work well when they reflect real workflows:

- fake invoice approval
- executive wire transfer request
- password reset escalation
- vendor bank account change
- urgent gift-card or payroll diversion request

**Exam focus points**

- Deepfakes are a subset of synthetic media focused on realistic impersonation.
- AI increases the scale, speed, and realism of social engineering.
- The best defenses are process controls, not visual inspection alone.
- Out-of-band verification and callback procedures are critical.
- Email authentication and phishing-resistant MFA reduce common delivery paths.
- Incident response should preserve evidence and coordinate across business teams.

**Quick comparison**

| Term | Meaning | Security relevance |
|---|---|---|
| Deepfake | AI-generated impersonation of a real person | Used for fraud, impersonation, and deception |
| Synthetic media | AI-generated or AI-altered content | Broader category that includes deepfakes |
| Social engineering | Manipulating people into unsafe actions | Primary attack method enhanced by AI |
| Out-of-band verification | Confirming through a separate trusted channel | Strong defense against impersonation |

**Practical command examples**

Use DNS and mail checks to validate sender authenticity and domain posture:

```bash
nslookup -type=txt example.com
nslookup -type=txt _dmarc.example.com
```

Inspect message headers for authentication results:

```text
Authentication-Results: spf=pass smtp.mailfrom=example.com;
 dkim=pass header.d=example.com;
 dmarc=pass action=none header.from=example.com
```

If your organization uses a SIEM, search for unusual approval patterns or repeated reset requests. The exact query depends on the platform, but the goal is to correlate identity events, communication events, and financial actions.

**Key takeaway**

Deepfakes and synthetic media are most dangerous when they are paired with urgency, authority, and weak verification. Your defensive strategy should assume that any voice, face, or message can be forged and should rely on layered controls, strong identity proofing, and disciplined escalation procedures.
