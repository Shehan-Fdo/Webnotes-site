**Data governance in AI systems**

Data governance is the set of policies, controls, and responsibilities that determine how data is collected, classified, used, protected, retained, and audited across the AI lifecycle. In AI environments, governance matters because model quality, privacy, legal exposure, and security all depend on the trustworthiness of the underlying data.

A strong governance program answers four questions:

- What data are you allowed to use?
- Where did the data come from?
- How do you know the data is accurate and unmodified?
- Who can access, change, approve, or export it?

AI systems amplify governance failures. Poorly governed training data can introduce bias, leak sensitive information, violate licensing terms, or create models that are impossible to audit later. Governance should therefore cover both the data itself and the processes around ingestion, labeling, storage, sharing, and deletion.

**Core governance principles**

Two privacy and compliance principles appear often in exam scenarios:

- **Data minimization** means you collect and retain only the data needed for the stated purpose.
- **Purpose limitation** means you use the data only for the purpose disclosed and approved at collection time.

These principles reduce legal risk and limit blast radius if a dataset is exposed or misused. They also improve model hygiene by discouraging unnecessary collection of personal, regulated, or low-value data.

Other common governance principles include:

- **Least privilege** for access to datasets, labels, and model artifacts.
- **Retention limits** so stale or unnecessary data is removed on schedule.
- **Accountability** through ownership, approvals, and audit trails.
- **Transparency** so users and reviewers can understand what data was used and why.

**Data classification and handling requirements**

Classification drives how you store, share, process, and delete data. In AI programs, classification should apply to raw data, derived features, labels, prompts, embeddings, logs, and model outputs when they contain sensitive content.

A practical classification scheme often looks like this:

- **Public**
  - Approved for open distribution.
  - Can usually be used in low-risk demos or public benchmarks.
  - Still needs integrity controls to prevent tampering.
- **Internal**
  - Intended for employees or trusted contractors.
  - May include operational data, internal docs, or non-public telemetry.
  - Requires access control and logging.
- **Confidential**
  - Sensitive business, customer, or regulated data.
  - Requires stronger encryption, tighter access, and approval workflows.
  - Often restricted from external model training unless explicitly reviewed.
- **Restricted**
  - Highest sensitivity, such as secrets, credentials, highly regulated records, or data with severe legal impact.
  - Usually prohibited from broad AI training use.
  - May require dedicated enclaves, tokenization, or complete exclusion.

Classification determines handling requirements such as:

- Encryption at rest and in transit
- Approved storage locations
- Masking or tokenization
- DLP (Data Loss Prevention) inspection
- Export restrictions
- Retention and deletion rules
- Human review before external sharing or model training

> If a dataset is classified as confidential or restricted, do not assume it is safe to use just because it is technically accessible. Access and authorization are separate controls.

**Sourcing data for AI**

Data sourcing is the process of identifying, acquiring, and approving data for training, fine-tuning, evaluation, or retrieval-augmented generation. The source matters because it affects trust, legality, bias, and reproducibility.

Common source types include:

- Internal enterprise systems
- Customer-submitted data
- Public datasets
- Licensed commercial datasets
- Open-source corpora
- Synthetic data
- Third-party APIs and data brokers

Each source type has different risks:

- **Internal data** may contain sensitive business or personal information.
- **Customer data** may require consent, contract review, and privacy controls.
- **Public data** may still be copyrighted, biased, stale, or poisoned.
- **Licensed data** may have usage limits, attribution requirements, or model-training restrictions.
- **Synthetic data** can reduce privacy exposure but may amplify bias or fail to reflect real-world edge cases.

When sourcing data, verify:

- Ownership or usage rights
- Consent and notice requirements
- Licensing terms and downstream restrictions
- Geographic or regulatory constraints
- Data freshness and relevance
- Known quality issues or sampling bias

**Third-party model and training data risks**

Third-party datasets and pretrained models introduce supply-chain style risk. You should treat them as untrusted until validated.

Key risks include:

- **Data poisoning** where malicious or manipulated records influence model behavior
- **Copyright and licensing conflicts** when training data usage exceeds license terms
- **Consent limitations** when personal data was collected for a different purpose
- **Hidden provenance gaps** where the original source cannot be verified
- **Backdoor triggers** embedded in data or model artifacts

Practical controls include:

- Vendor due diligence and contract review
- Dataset documentation and usage attestations
- Hash verification for delivered artifacts
- Restricted ingestion pipelines
- Quarantine and validation before promotion to production

**Provenance, lineage, and chain of custody**

These terms are related but tested separately.

| Term | Meaning | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Provenance** | The origin and history of a dataset or artifact | Helps you understand where data came from and whether it is trustworthy |
| **Lineage** | The path data follows through transformations, joins, filters, labeling, and feature engineering | Helps you reproduce results and trace errors back to a source step |
| **Chain of custody** | The documented control and transfer history of an item from creation to current holder | Helps prove integrity and accountability, especially for sensitive or legal evidence |

Exam-style distinctions:

- Provenance answers “Where did this come from?”
- Lineage answers “What happened to it along the way?”
- Chain of custody answers “Who had it, when, and under what control?”

In AI governance, you want all three:

- Provenance for source trust
- Lineage for reproducibility
- Chain of custody for accountability and tamper evidence

**Validation before training or deployment**

Validation checks whether data is suitable for the intended AI task. It is not just about schema correctness; it also includes security, privacy, and model-risk checks.

Typical validation steps include:

1. Verify schema, types, ranges, and required fields.
2. Check for duplicates, missing values, and malformed records.
3. Review label quality and inter-annotator consistency.
4. Detect class imbalance, skew, and sampling bias.
5. Scan for outliers, anomalies, and poisoning indicators.
6. Confirm sensitive data handling and redaction.
7. Validate that the dataset matches the approved use case.

AI-specific validation checks often include:

- **Bias and skew detection** across classes, demographics, time periods, or sources
- **Label quality review** to catch noisy, inconsistent, or adversarial labels
- **Poisoning detection** for suspicious patterns, repeated triggers, or abnormal clusters
- **Outlier detection** to identify records that may be corrupted or intentionally crafted
- **Leakage checks** to ensure training and test sets are properly separated
- **Prompt injection review** for text corpora used in retrieval or fine-tuning

A dataset can be syntactically valid and still be operationally unsafe. For example, a CSV may parse correctly but contain mislabeled examples, duplicated records, or hidden sensitive fields that make it unsuitable for training.

**Practical validation workflow**

A simple enterprise workflow often looks like this:

1. Ingest data into a quarantine zone.
2. Run automated schema and quality checks.
3. Apply DLP and sensitive-data scanning.
4. Review source documentation, licensing, and consent.
5. Perform sampling and human review for labels and edge cases.
6. Approve, reject, or remediate the dataset.
7. Promote only validated data into the training or feature store.

Example checks with common tools:

```bash
# Basic file integrity and inventory
sha256sum dataset.csv
wc -l dataset.csv
head -n 5 dataset.csv

# Search for obvious secrets or sensitive patterns
grep -Ei 'password|secret|ssn|credit card|api[_-]?key' dataset.csv

# Compare file hashes after transfer
sha256sum -c dataset.csv.sha256
```

Example Python validation snippet:

```python
import pandas as pd

df = pd.read_csv("dataset.csv")

# Schema and completeness checks
required = ["text", "label"]
missing_cols = [c for c in required if c not in df.columns]
if missing_cols:
    raise ValueError(f"Missing columns: {missing_cols}")

if df["text"].isna().mean() > 0.01:
    raise ValueError("Too many missing text values")

# Simple label distribution check
print(df["label"].value_counts(normalize=True))

# Duplicate detection
dupes = df.duplicated(subset=["text"]).sum()
print(f"Duplicate rows: {dupes}")
```

**Integrity controls**

Integrity means data has not been altered in an unauthorized or undetected way. In AI pipelines, integrity applies to source files, labels, feature sets, embeddings, checkpoints, and logs.

Common integrity controls include:

- **Cryptographic hashes** such as SHA-256 for file verification
- **Digital signatures** for signed datasets or manifests
- **Immutable storage** for approved artifacts
- **Object lock** or write-once-read-many controls for retention and tamper resistance
- **Versioning** for datasets and model artifacts
- **Checksums** during transfer and storage validation

Example object storage controls:

```bash
# AWS S3 example: enable versioning and object lock on a bucket
aws s3api put-bucket-versioning \
  --bucket ai-training-data \
  --versioning-configuration Status=Enabled

aws s3api put-object-lock-configuration \
  --bucket ai-training-data \
  --object-lock-configuration '{
    "ObjectLockEnabled": "Enabled",
    "Rule": {
      "DefaultRetention": {
        "Mode": "GOVERNANCE",
        "Days": 30
      }
    }
  }'
```

Example Linux file immutability control:

```bash
sudo chattr +i /data/approved/dataset.csv
lsattr /data/approved/dataset.csv
```

Use immutable or locked storage when you need strong evidence that approved data was not silently changed. This is especially useful for regulated datasets, audit evidence, and model release artifacts.

**Enterprise controls: DLP, IAM, and segmentation**

Operational governance depends on controls that enforce policy.

- **DLP** inspects data in motion, at rest, or in use to detect sensitive content and block unauthorized transfer.
- **IAM** (Identity and Access Management) restricts who can read, write, approve, or export datasets.
- **Network segmentation** isolates training environments, labeling tools, and production systems.
- **Secrets management** keeps API keys and credentials out of datasets and prompts.

Example DLP-style policy logic:

```yaml
rules:
  - name: block_restricted_export
    match:
      classification: restricted
      destination: external
    action: deny

  - name: alert_on_pii_in_training_set
    match:
      content_contains:
        - email
        - ssn
        - credit_card
    action: quarantine
```

Example IAM policy concept:

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "ReadApprovedTrainingDataOnly",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Action": ["s3:GetObject"],
      "Resource": "arn:aws:s3:::ai-training-data/approved/*"
    },
    {
      "Sid": "DenyDeleteOnLockedData",
      "Effect": "Deny",
      "Action": ["s3:DeleteObject"],
      "Resource": "arn:aws:s3:::ai-training-data/approved/*"
    }
  ]
}
```

These controls should align with classification. For example, restricted data may require a separate account, private endpoint, or isolated enclave, while internal data may only need role-based access and logging.

**Labeling and annotation governance**

Labels are part of the data product, not just metadata. Poor label governance can degrade model performance as much as bad source data.

Good labeling practices include:

- Clear label definitions and examples
- Annotator training and calibration
- Double review for ambiguous cases
- Inter-annotator agreement checks
- Versioned label schemas
- Audit trails for label changes

Common issues:

- Label drift when definitions change over time
- Inconsistent human judgment across annotators
- Adversarial labeling in outsourced workflows
- Leakage of target labels into features

For exam scenarios, remember that label quality is both a data quality issue and a security issue when labels can be manipulated to influence model behavior.

**Privacy, compliance, and legal considerations**

AI governance must account for privacy and legal constraints, especially when personal or regulated data is involved.

Watch for:

- Consent scope and revocation
- Data subject rights and deletion requests
- Cross-border transfer restrictions
- Retention and disposal obligations
- Copyright and database rights
- Contractual limits on reuse or redistribution

Practical privacy controls include:

- Pseudonymization or tokenization
- Redaction of direct identifiers
- Aggregation where possible
- Purpose-specific access approvals
- Deletion workflows for expired or withdrawn data

> Do not assume anonymized data is risk-free. Re-identification can still be possible when datasets are combined or when rare attributes remain.

**Operational examples**

A secure AI data pipeline often uses a quarantine-to-approved flow:

- Raw data lands in a restricted ingestion bucket.
- Automated scanners check for PII (personally identifiable information), secrets, malware, and schema issues.
- A data steward reviews source documentation and classification.
- Approved data is copied into a versioned training store.
- Hashes and manifests are recorded for reproducibility.
- Access is granted only to the minimum set of roles needed.

Example pipeline layout:

```text
source systems -> ingestion quarantine -> validation -> approved dataset store -> feature store -> training job
```

Example manifest file:

```json
{
  "dataset_name": "customer_support_tickets_v3",
  "classification": "confidential",
  "source": "crm_export",
  "sha256": "9f2c1d...e7a4",
  "approved_by": "data_governance",
  "approved_date": "2026-04-26",
  "retention_days": 90
}
```

**Exam focus points**

- Governance defines who can use data, for what purpose, and under what controls.
- Classification drives handling requirements; higher sensitivity means tighter access, logging, and retention.
- Provenance, lineage, and chain of custody are distinct and often tested separately.
- Validation should include quality, bias, skew, label review, and poisoning detection, not just schema checks.
- Integrity relies on hashes, signatures, versioning, and immutable storage.
- Third-party data and pretrained assets can introduce licensing, consent, and poisoning risk.
- Data minimization and purpose limitation are core privacy principles in AI governance.

**Quick comparison summary**

| Concept | Primary question | Typical control |
|---|---|---|
| Governance | Who may use the data and why? | Policy, approvals, audit |
| Classification | How sensitive is the data? | Access control, DLP, encryption |
| Sourcing | Where did the data come from? | Vendor review, licensing checks |
| Validation | Is the data fit for use? | Quality checks, bias review |
| Provenance | What is the origin? | Metadata, source records |
| Lineage | What transformations occurred? | Pipeline logs, versioning |
| Chain of custody | Who controlled it over time? | Signatures, custody logs |
| Integrity | Has it been altered? | Hashes, signatures, immutability |
