## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**Host isolation and containment**

Containment assumes a breach may already be in progress and focuses on limiting damage quickly. In practice, you isolate affected endpoints, stop lateral movement, and keep the business running as safely as possible.

- **Isolation** separates a suspected host from the rest of the environment.
- **Containment** limits spread across devices, accounts, and network segments.
- **EDR** (Endpoint Detection and Response) supports rapid detection, investigation, and response on endpoints.
- Network segmentation helps reduce the blast radius when a device is compromised.
- Some devices, especially medical and IoMT (Internet of Medical Things) systems, may need special handling because you cannot always power them down or patch them like standard IT assets.

A practical containment plan uses clear governance, escalation paths, and playbooks for common threats such as ransomware or phishing. The goal is to act fast without causing unnecessary operational disruption.

**How containment is executed**

You usually combine technical controls with coordinated response actions. The response should be deliberate, not improvised.

1. Identify the affected host or hosts.
2. Isolate the endpoint or segment the network path.
3. Lock out compromised accounts and revoke active sessions when needed.
4. Stop lateral movement before it reaches additional systems.
5. Validate that the containment action does not break critical operations.

> In critical incidents, speed matters, but so does control. Containment should stop impact while preserving the ability to investigate and recover.

**Evidence preservation**

Preserving evidence means keeping enough trustworthy data to reconstruct what happened later. This matters because attackers may delete dropper files, remove event log entries, or clean up prefetch files on infected hosts.

- Preserve artifacts before they are overwritten or removed.
- Capture data that supports timeline reconstruction and root cause analysis.
- Keep a clear record of actions taken during isolation and response.
- Use evidence preservation to support forensic review and board-ready reporting.

A key distinction is between seeing that alerts stopped and proving the threat is gone. Evidence of absence requires a complete, searchable record across endpoints, plus continuous reanalysis as new threat intelligence arrives. Without that, you may only have absence of evidence, which is weaker in a regulatory or leadership review.

**Useful evidence sources**

Different data sources help you reconstruct the incident when local artifacts are missing or altered.

- **EDR telemetry** can show post-compromise activity and response actions.
- **Registry artifacts** may reveal persistence or configuration changes.
- **File system metadata** can help identify file creation and modification history.
- **Creation time** of a dropper may help establish an initial timeline.
- **Prefetch files** can be useful when they are still available, but attackers may delete them.

When a malware sample cleans up after itself, rely on multiple sources instead of a single host artifact. Cross-check endpoint telemetry with file metadata and other forensic data to reduce blind spots.

**Containment and recovery priorities**

Containment is not the end of the incident response process. After isolation, you still need to confirm eradication, validate recovery, and reduce the chance of recurrence.

- Reassess the environment as new indicators emerge.
- Confirm that malicious files and variants are not present elsewhere.
- Harden systems after recovery.
- Document the sequence of actions for audit and lessons learned.

For exam purposes, remember the relationship between the phases:

- **Containment** limits spread.
- **Forensics** explains what happened.
- **Recovery** restores safe operations.
- **Evidence preservation** makes the investigation defensible.

**Quick comparison**

| Focus | Primary goal | Typical outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Isolation | Separate the affected host | Reduced exposure to the rest of the network |
| Containment | Stop spread and impact | Lateral movement is blocked or slowed |
| Evidence preservation | Keep usable proof | Investigators can reconstruct timeline and root cause |

**Exam reminders**

- Use isolation when you need to protect the environment from a suspected compromise.
- Preserve evidence before cleanup removes artifacts.
- Treat endpoint telemetry as a major source of truth when local logs are tampered with.
- Do not confuse “alerts stopped” with “the threat is gone.”
