## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**EDR workflow overview**

<abbr title="Endpoint Detection and Response">EDR</abbr> continuously monitors endpoints for evidence of threats and can take automatic actions to help contain them. In practice, EDR workflows focus on turning noisy endpoint telemetry into a smaller set of actionable alerts that analysts can investigate quickly.

Endpoints include devices such as desktops, laptops, mobile phones, virtual machines, and <abbr title="Internet of Things">IoT</abbr> devices. Because these systems are common entry points, EDR helps you detect and remediate threats before they spread across the network.

- EDR logs endpoint behavior around the clock.
- It analyzes activity to surface suspicious patterns such as ransomware-like behavior.
- It can alert security staff and support containment actions.
- It helps you investigate what happened, what was affected, and what response is needed.

**Why alert triage matters**

EDR tools often produce a high signal-to-noise ratio. Large environments can generate hundreds or thousands of alerts, and many of them are false positives. Manual triage is slow, and delays can let incidents progress before you confirm whether an alert is real.

A practical triage workflow starts by classifying the alert, checking legitimacy, and scoping impact. That early scoping step determines how far you need to expand the investigation beyond local endpoint data into related cloud or security context.

> EDR alert triage is time-sensitive: the goal is to separate real threats from noise before the incident escalates.

**Core triage activities**

During triage, you review the alert and gather enough context to decide whether to escalate, contain, or dismiss it. EDR investigations commonly span files, processes, and network activity, so you need a disciplined approach.

- Validate the alert source and event details.
- Check whether the activity matches expected user or system behavior.
- Identify indicators of compromise, or <abbr title="Indicators of Compromise">IOCs</abbr>, when available.
- Correlate related alerts or suspicious activity across endpoints.
- Determine whether other endpoints may be affected.

**Automation and orchestration**

Automation helps reduce the time analysts spend on repetitive triage steps. Security orchestration, automation, and response (<abbr title="Security Orchestration, Automation, and Response">SOAR</abbr>) tools can analyze EDR events, enrich alerts with threat intelligence, and trigger response actions at machine speed.

Examples of automated triage actions include:

- Extracting a source IP address from an alert.
- Sending the IP to threat intelligence platforms.
- Adding reputation data to the case.
- Correlating multiple suspicious events into a single incident.
- Retrieving user or host data to support investigation.

Automation is especially useful when you need to address alerts faster than a human-only workflow allows. It can also reduce missed alerts and help you stop attacks earlier in the kill chain.

**Alert queue handling**

Many EDR platforms present alerts in a queue so you can review recent events first. A grouped queue view helps you organize alerts by time and focus on the newest activity. Automated investigation and remediation can also reduce the number of alerts that require manual review, allowing you to focus on higher-value threats.

A typical queue review process is:

1. Open the alert queue and sort by recency or severity.
2. Review the alert summary and affected entity.
3. Decide whether automated investigation is available.
4. Triage the alert using endpoint, process, file, and network context.
5. Escalate, contain, or close the alert based on evidence.

**Response and containment**

Once you confirm suspicious activity, response actions should limit spread and preserve evidence. EDR platforms can support containment and remediation, while additional tools may collect endpoint artifacts for deeper analysis.

- Contain the affected endpoint when the risk is credible.
- Preserve relevant telemetry for later investigation.
- Collect additional host artifacts when EDR data alone is not enough.
- Expand scope to identify related systems and users.

**Practical exam takeaways**

For exam prep, focus on the relationship between detection, triage, and response. EDR gives you endpoint visibility and automated containment options, while triage determines whether an alert is noise, a localized issue, or part of a broader incident.

- EDR is endpoint-focused and behavior-driven.
- Triage is the decision point for legitimacy and impact.
- Automation improves speed and consistency.
- Correlation helps you find related endpoints and incidents.
- Response should be fast, evidence-based, and scoped to the threat.
