## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**Threat hunting planning basics**

Threat hunting is a proactive security practice. You search for undetected threats, attacker behavior, and vulnerabilities before damage occurs. For CySA+ (Cybersecurity Analyst), the planning step matters because effective hunting depends on clear objectives and the right data, not ad hoc searching.

A strong hunt plan keeps you focused on what you want to prove or disprove. It also helps you avoid wasting time on broad, unfocused analysis. In practice, you define the hunt scope first, then identify the telemetry and evidence you need to support the hunt.

- **Proactive** hunting looks for hidden activity instead of waiting for alerts.
- **Structured** planning reduces wasted effort and supports repeatable analysis.
- **Specialized** analysts often lead hunts because the work requires skill and experience.

**Define the hunt scope and objective**

Start by deciding what you are hunting for and why. A hunt should have a clear purpose, such as validating a suspected behavior, checking for a known adversary pattern, or looking for anomalies that suggest compromise. Without a defined scope, you risk collecting too much data and losing direction.

You should also keep the hunt practical. A crawl, walk, run approach helps you start small and mature the process over time. That means you can begin with focused hunts and expand as your team gains experience.

- Define the target behavior, threat, or question.
- Limit the environment or time window when possible.
- Keep the objective measurable so you can tell whether the hunt succeeded.

**Gather intelligence before collecting data**

Research comes before data collection. Use what you know about the threat, the environment, and the likely attack behavior to decide which sources matter. This step helps you avoid collecting irrelevant telemetry and improves the quality of your analysis.

Threat hunting often uses indicators of compromise (IoCs), indicators of attack (IoAs), and tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs). These terms help you connect observed activity to attacker behavior.

- **IoC**: evidence that may indicate compromise, such as suspicious artifacts or patterns.
- **IoA**: behavior that suggests an attack is in progress or likely.
- **TTPs**: the methods and patterns attackers use to operate.

**Select the right telemetry and evidence**

Threat hunting relies on data from multiple sources. Common sources include logs, network traffic, cloud telemetry, and endpoint data. The best source depends on the question you are trying to answer.

Telemetry selection should also reflect operational needs. In some environments, teams retain network metadata for long periods so they can support threat hunting or legal evidence needs. In others, telemetry may also support compliance or monitoring goals.

| Data source | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Logs | Help you trace activity and correlate events |
| Network traffic | Shows communication patterns and suspicious connections |
| Cloud telemetry | Supports hunts in cloud-native environments |
| Endpoint data | Helps you inspect host-level behavior |

> Retain data long enough to support the hunt. If the relevant telemetry is missing or too short-lived, you may not be able to confirm suspicious activity.

**Plan the hunt workflow**

A practical hunt plan follows a sequence. First, define the scope and objectives. Next, gather intelligence and research the behavior you want to investigate. Then collect the data you need and analyze it for patterns, anomalies, or known malicious indicators.

This workflow works best when it is repeatable. A repeatable process helps you improve each hunt and makes it easier to compare results across environments or time periods.

1. Define the hunt question and scope.
2. Research the threat or behavior.
3. Identify the required telemetry.
4. Collect and review the data.
5. Analyze findings and refine the hunt.

**Use telemetry with operational context**

Data requirements are not only about volume. They are also about context. For example, network metadata can support both security analysis and legal evidence, while telemetry from operations teams may help you understand normal behavior and reduce false positives.

When you coordinate with other teams, you improve the quality of your hunt. Security monitoring, network operations, and compliance needs can all influence what data you collect and how long you keep it.

- Align hunt data with the question you are asking.
- Coordinate with teams that already collect useful telemetry.
- Keep enough context to distinguish normal behavior from suspicious activity.

**Key takeaways**

Threat hunting is most effective when you plan it before you start collecting data. Clear scope, relevant intelligence, and the right telemetry make the hunt more efficient and more defensible. For exam prep, remember that hunt planning is a structured process: define the objective, choose the data, and analyze with context.
