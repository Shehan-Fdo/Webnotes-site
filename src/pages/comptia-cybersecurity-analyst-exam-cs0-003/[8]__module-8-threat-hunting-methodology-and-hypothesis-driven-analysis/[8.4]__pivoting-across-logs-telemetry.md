## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**Why pivoting matters**

Pivoting across logs and telemetry means you use one clue to find the next most useful source of evidence. In threat hunting, alerts, logs, endpoint signals, and network telemetry each reveal part of the story, but rarely the whole picture. The goal is to move from a hypothesis to proof with enough context to confirm what happened.

Modern environments make this harder because signals are spread across tools and data types. Logs can show activity, endpoint data can show host behavior, and network telemetry can provide the packet-level or traffic-level context needed to validate suspicious behavior. When you connect these sources, you reduce guesswork and improve confidence in your findings.

- **Logs** often provide the first signal, but they may not prove intent or full scope.
- **Endpoint data** helps you inspect process, host, and user activity on a device.
- **Network telemetry** adds context for east-west traffic, historical review, and validation.
- **Correlation** across sources helps you confirm whether a weak signal is meaningful.

**Threat hunting workflow**

Threat hunting is proactive. Instead of waiting for an alert, you assume an adversary may already be present and use available evidence to test that assumption. A practical hunt often starts with a suspicious event, then expands outward across related logs and telemetry until you can explain the activity.

1. Start with the strongest available clue, such as an alert, log entry, or endpoint signal.
2. Identify related entities, such as hosts, users, IP addresses, domains, or processes.
3. Pivot into other data sources to look for supporting or contradicting evidence.
4. Compare the activity against known behavior baselines and expected patterns.
5. Decide whether the signal is noise, suspicious, or strong enough to investigate further.

This workflow is especially useful when signals are weak or incomplete. If one tool only shows part of the attack, another source may supply the missing context. The key is to keep moving from one verified detail to the next instead of jumping to conclusions.

**SIEM, EDR, and network visibility**

A <abbr title="Security Information and Event Management">SIEM</abbr> platform aggregates and correlates security data from across the enterprise, including logs from applications, devices, and networks. That makes it useful for broad visibility and real-time correlation. <abbr title="Endpoint Detection and Response">EDR</abbr> focuses on endpoint activity and helps you inspect what happened on a host. Network telemetry fills in gaps when endpoint and log data do not fully explain the event.

- **SIEM** is useful for collecting and correlating many log sources in one place.
- **EDR** is useful for endpoint-level investigation and response.
- **Network telemetry** is useful when you need proof, historical context, or visibility into traffic that other tools miss.
- **Integrated views** help close telemetry gaps when each tool sees only part of an attack.

> Manual pivots across disconnected products do not scale well. If the evidence is fragmented, you may miss weak signals or spend too much time chasing incomplete leads.

**How to pivot effectively**

You pivot by following identifiers and relationships. A single event can lead you to a host, then to a user, then to a process, then to related network activity. Each step should answer a specific question and produce a new clue.

- Use timestamps to align events across tools.
- Match on hostnames, IP addresses, usernames, process names, and domains.
- Look for repeated patterns that connect separate alerts into one activity chain.
- Check whether the evidence supports the same story across logs, endpoint data, and network telemetry.

When the environment is cloud-heavy or highly dynamic, manual review becomes harder because resources may be short-lived and log volume may be high. In those cases, automated correlation and guided investigation can help surface weak signals faster, but human judgment still decides what matters.

**Practical investigation focus**

The most useful pivots are the ones that answer a concrete question. For example, you may want to know whether an alert reflects a real compromise, whether activity spread laterally, or whether the same actor touched multiple systems. Network-level evidence is especially valuable when you need to validate suspicious behavior across hybrid environments or east-west traffic.

- Confirm whether the activity is isolated or part of a broader chain.
- Look for evidence that supports or refutes the initial hypothesis.
- Use historical context to investigate before, during, and after the suspected event.
- Treat anomalies as leads, not conclusions, until you can connect them to stronger evidence.

**Key takeaway**

Pivoting across logs and telemetry is about building confidence through correlation. You start with a signal, move through related data sources, and use each pivot to narrow uncertainty. The better you connect SIEM data, endpoint evidence, and network telemetry, the faster you can move from suspicion to proof.
