## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**Threat hunting approach**

Threat hunting is a proactive discipline that looks for attacker activity that automated tools may miss. In CySA+ terms, you use hunting to detect and analyze malicious behavior, improve visibility, and strengthen continuous monitoring.

Hypothesis-driven hunting starts with a testable assumption about what an adversary may be doing in your environment. Intelligence-driven hunting starts with threat intelligence and uses that context to focus the search. Both approaches aim to move beyond random searching and toward targeted investigation.

- **Hypothesis-driven** hunting begins with a supposition you can confirm or deny with data.
- **Intelligence-driven** hunting uses current threat intelligence to shape what you look for.
- Hunting is most useful when you want to find activity that does not trigger obvious alerts.
- Behavioral analysis matters because attacker infrastructure can change quickly.

**Hypothesis-driven hunting**

A hypothesis is a testable statement about possible adversary behavior. The goal is not to guess blindly; it is to form a reasoned theory, search for evidence, and then refine your conclusion based on what you find.

This method is often described as the classic hunting approach. It encourages critical thinking and a structured investigation process. A strong hypothesis is grounded in what you know about your environment, recent trends, and relevant intelligence.

- Build the hypothesis from observable conditions, trends, or intelligence.
- Focus on adversary behavior rather than only on static indicators.
- Use data and analysis to confirm or deny the assumption.
- Refine the hypothesis when findings change your understanding.

> Avoid relying only on indicators of compromise (IOCs) such as file hashes, IP addresses, or domains. Those values can change quickly, so behavior-based hunting is more durable.

**Intelligence-driven hunting**

Intelligence-driven hunting uses threat intelligence as the starting point for the hunt. Instead of beginning with a broad question, you begin with known or suspected adversary activity and then look for evidence that matches that context.

This approach is practical when you want to prioritize likely threats and reduce wasted effort. It helps you focus on tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs), which are more stable than individual indicators.

- Use threat intelligence to identify likely attacker behavior.
- Map the hunt to TTPs rather than only to IOCs.
- Prioritize searches that match current or relevant threat activity.
- Treat intelligence as a guide, not as proof by itself.

**Why behavior matters**

Attackers can rotate infrastructure quickly. A hash can change in minutes, a command-and-control server can move in hours, and a domain can be replaced the same day. Because of that, hunting based only on static indicators is fragile.

Behavioral hunting is stronger because it looks for patterns that require the attacker to change how they operate, not just what they use. That makes it better suited for finding sophisticated activity that evades signature-based detection.

- **TTPs** are more durable than single indicators.
- Behavioral evidence can reveal activity even when infrastructure changes.
- Signature-based detection alone may miss advanced threats.
- Hunting programs become more effective when they test behavior systematically.

**Framework context**

The PEAK threat hunting framework identifies three primary hunt types:

- Hypothesis-driven hunts
- Baseline hunts
- Model-assisted threat hunts

PEAK is commonly described as a structure for organizing hunting work into stages of preparation, execution, and action with knowledge. In practice, it gives you a repeatable way to move from planning to investigation to improvement.

**How to generate a useful hunt hypothesis**

A good hypothesis is specific enough to test and broad enough to uncover meaningful activity. You should base it on what you know about your environment, current intelligence, and the kinds of attacks that automated tools may miss.

1. Identify a behavior or threat pattern you want to test.
2. Tie it to evidence from your environment or current intelligence.
3. Define what data you need to confirm or deny the idea.
4. Run the hunt and review the results.
5. Refine the hypothesis if the evidence suggests a different path.

**Practical exam takeaways**

For CySA+, expect questions that ask how to choose a hunting approach or what makes a hunt effective. The safest answer usually emphasizes behavior, intelligence, and structured analysis rather than random searching.

- Use hypothesis-driven hunting when you have a testable theory.
- Use intelligence-driven hunting when threat intelligence provides the lead.
- Prefer behavioral evidence over static IOCs when possible.
- Look for methods that support proactive detection and continuous improvement.
