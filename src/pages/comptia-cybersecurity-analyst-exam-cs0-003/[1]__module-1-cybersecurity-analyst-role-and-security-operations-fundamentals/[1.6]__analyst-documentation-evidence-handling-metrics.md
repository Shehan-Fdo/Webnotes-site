## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**Analyst documentation and evidence integrity**

Analyst documentation supports investigations by recording what happened, what you observed, and how you handled evidence. In digital forensics, documentation is not just administrative work; it helps preserve the integrity and reliability of evidence so it can support legal proceedings if needed.

Chain of custody (<abbr title="chain of custody">CoC</abbr>) is the recorded history of evidence from collection through storage, transfer, and review. A complete CoC helps show that evidence remained authentic and untampered with.

- Document evidence collection, handling, transfer, and storage.
- Keep the evidence history complete and traceable.
- Preserve evidence so it remains reliable in court proceedings.
- Treat evidence integrity as a core part of incident response readiness.

> A broken or incomplete chain of custody can make evidence inadmissible or weaken its value in an investigation.

**Chain of custody essentials**

The chain of custody should be maintained at all times by everyone who handles the evidence. It verifies where evidence has traveled and who handled it before trial. The practical goal is to prevent substitution, tampering, misidentification, damage, alteration, contamination, misplacement, or falsification.

A strong CoC supports both legal integrity and authenticity. In cyber investigations, that means you handle digital evidence carefully from the moment you collect it until it is archived or presented.

- Identify evidence clearly at collection time.
- Record every transfer and every handler.
- Store evidence securely to reduce the risk of alteration or loss.
- Use consistent procedures so the evidence record stays durable.

**Evidence handling workflow**

Good evidence handling starts with proper collection and continues through packaging, documentation, and secure storage. The evidence record should stay accurate enough that another reviewer can understand what was collected, how it moved, and where it is now.

1. Collect the evidence carefully and identify it clearly.
2. Document who handled it, when, and for what purpose.
3. Package and store it securely to protect it from damage or contamination.
4. Maintain the record through transfer, analysis, and archiving.

**Reports and analyst records**

Analyst documentation often includes reports that summarize findings and actions taken during an investigation. The evidence context emphasizes analysis reports and investigator’s reports as part of organized evidence handling.

Keep reports factual, clear, and tied to the evidence record. Use them to explain what you found, what you did, and how the evidence supports your conclusions.

- Write reports that are accurate and organized.
- Tie findings back to documented evidence.
- Keep language factual and avoid unsupported conclusions.
- Use consistent terminology across notes and reports.

**Metrics for security operations**

Security operations center (<abbr title="Security Operations Center">SOC</abbr>) metrics provide measurable insight into security posture and operational efficiency. They help you see whether detection and response are effective, where workflows slow down, and whether the team can scale with business needs.

The evidence highlights three useful areas:
- Threat detection and response effectiveness
- Analyst team cognitive load
- Business growth preparedness

Metrics are most useful when they support decisions. They should show whether you detect threats quickly, respond effectively, and manage workload without losing visibility.

**Key SOC metrics**

Mean time to detect (<abbr title="Mean Time to Detect">MTTD</abbr>) measures how long it takes to discover a threat or suspicious activity. Lower MTTD helps reduce attacker dwell time, which is the period an adversary remains undetected in an environment.

Mean time to respond (<abbr title="Mean Time to Respond">MTTR</abbr>) measures how long it takes to respond after detection. Together, MTTD and MTTR help you evaluate whether your team can contain threats before they achieve their objectives.

- **MTTD** shows how quickly you notice compromise.
- **MTTR** shows how quickly you act after detection.
- False negatives show missed detections that can hide real threats.
- Metrics should reveal process gaps, blind spots, and workflow delays.

| Metric | What it tells you | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| MTTD | Time from malicious activity to awareness | Lower values reduce dwell time |
| MTTR | Time from detection to response | Lower values improve containment |
| False negatives | Missed detections | High values indicate visibility or tuning issues |

**Using metrics in practice**

Use metrics to identify where your security process needs improvement. If detection is slow, look for poor log management, excessive noise, blind spots, or detections that trigger too late to matter. If response is slow, review workflow, triage, and playbooks.

You can also use metrics to support reporting and planning. Consistent measurement helps you compare performance over time and justify changes to tools, staffing, or process.

- Track metrics consistently over time.
- Focus on outcome-based measures, not just alert volume.
- Use metrics to guide tuning, workflow changes, and training.
- Report results in a way that supports decision-making.

**Exam focus reminders**

For CS0-003, connect documentation, evidence handling, and metrics as parts of the same analyst workflow. Evidence handling protects admissibility, documentation preserves context, and metrics show whether your security operations are effective.

- Chain of custody protects evidence integrity and authenticity.
- Proper documentation supports legal and investigative use.
- MTTD and MTTR help measure detection and response performance.
- Metrics should drive practical improvements in the SOC.
