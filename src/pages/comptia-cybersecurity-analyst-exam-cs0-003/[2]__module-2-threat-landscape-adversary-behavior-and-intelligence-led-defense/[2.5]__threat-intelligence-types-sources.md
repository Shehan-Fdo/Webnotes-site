## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**Threat intelligence overview**

Cyber threat intelligence (CTI) is the collection, analysis, and dissemination of information about potential or current attacks that threaten digital assets. Its purpose is to help decision-makers prevent, detect, and respond to threats with better context than raw data alone.

CTI becomes useful when you match the right intelligence to the right audience. A board member needs risk and investment context, while a security operations center (SOC) analyst needs indicators and details that support immediate action.

- CTI supports **prevention**, **detection**, and **response**.
- It helps you understand adversary tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs).
- It turns scattered observations into information you can act on.

**Types of threat intelligence**

The main intelligence types are strategic, operational, tactical, and technical. Each type serves a different purpose and moves at a different speed.

| Type | Primary audience | Main focus | Typical use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Strategic | Leadership | Business risk and long-term trends | Budgeting, planning, and risk decisions |
| Operational | Security teams | Campaign context and adversary activity | Hunting and response planning |
| Tactical | SOC and defenders | Indicators of compromise (IOCs) | Blocking and detection |
| Technical | Security tools and analysts | Signatures and machine-readable details | Feeds for detection systems |

Strategic intelligence gives high-level context, such as where risk is increasing and why it matters to the organization. Operational intelligence explains who is behind activity and how campaigns unfold. Tactical intelligence provides the indicators you can use right away. Technical intelligence supports tools with signatures and other precise detection content.

- **Strategic** intelligence supports executive decisions.
- **Operational** intelligence adds campaign and actor context.
- **Tactical** intelligence focuses on actionable indicators.
- **Technical** intelligence feeds detection logic and automation.

> Sending the wrong intelligence type to the wrong audience reduces value and can turn CTI into data without context.

**Sources of threat intelligence**

Threat intelligence comes from a variety of open and closed sources. Using multiple sources gives you a broader view of the threat landscape and improves your ability to anticipate attacks.

Common source categories include:

- **Open sources** such as public reporting, blogs, and shared advisories.
- **Closed sources** such as private feeds, vendor intelligence, and restricted communities.
- **Internal sources** such as logs, alerts, and incident data from your own environment.

A strong program combines these sources so you can compare external threat activity with what is happening inside your organization. That combination helps you identify patterns, validate alerts, and prioritize response actions.

**How the types and sources work together**

Threat intelligence is most effective when you connect source data to a specific use case. For example, a tactical feed may provide indicators for blocking, while operational context helps you understand whether those indicators are part of a larger campaign.

- Use source data to collect raw observations.
- Analyze the data to identify relevance and context.
- Disseminate the result in the format the audience can use.

This workflow keeps intelligence practical. If you only collect data without analysis, you do not get the context needed for decisions.

**Practical exam focus**

For exam purposes, focus on matching the intelligence type to the audience and the outcome you want.

1. Identify the audience.
2. Determine whether the need is strategic, operational, tactical, or technical.
3. Choose sources that support that need.
4. Deliver the intelligence in a form the recipient can use.

A small team may start with tactical intelligence and credential monitoring, then add operational context as hunting matures, and strategic reporting when leadership needs planning input.

**Key takeaways**

CTI is not just threat data. It is processed information that supports action. The most effective programs use multiple source types and deliver the right intelligence level to the right consumer.

- **Strategic** intelligence informs leadership.
- **Operational** intelligence explains campaigns and actors.
- **Tactical** intelligence supports immediate defense.
- **Technical** intelligence powers tools and detections.
- Multiple sources improve coverage and confidence.
