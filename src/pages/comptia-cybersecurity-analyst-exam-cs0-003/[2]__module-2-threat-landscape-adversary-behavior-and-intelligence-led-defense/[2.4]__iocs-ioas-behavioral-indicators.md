## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**Indicator types at a glance**

Security teams use indicators to detect, confirm, and respond to malicious activity. The three common categories are indicators of compromise (IOCs), indicators of attack (IOAs), and behavioral indicators. They differ mainly by timing and what they reveal about the adversary.

- **IOCs** are evidence that a breach may already have occurred.
- **IOAs** point to an attack in progress or imminent.
- **Behavioral indicators** focus on patterns of activity that reveal tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs).

<dl>
<dt>IOC</dt>
<dd>Forensic evidence that suggests compromise has already happened.</dd>
<dt>IOA</dt>
<dd>Activity or pattern that suggests an attack is underway or about to happen.</dd>
<dt>Behavioral indicator</dt>
<dd>Observable behavior that helps identify adversary methods rather than a single artifact.</dd>

**Indicators of compromise**

An IOC is a digital clue that supports the conclusion that an endpoint or network has been breached. It is forensic in nature, so you usually find it after malicious activity has occurred. Common sources include event logs, extended detection and response (XDR) tools, and security information and event management (SIEM) systems.

- **Network traffic anomalies** can show unusual communication patterns.
- **Compromised credentials** can indicate unauthorized access.
- **Data exfiltration** can reveal that information has already left the environment.
- **Malware artifacts** such as file names or hashes can help identify known malicious files.
- **Command-and-control (C2) details** such as IP addresses or domains can connect infected systems to attacker infrastructure.

IOCs help you contain an incident, remove the threat, and understand what happened after recovery. They are useful for hunting known bad artifacts, but they are strongest when the attack has already left evidence behind.

> IOC-based detection is often easier to evade than behavior-based detection because many low-level artifacts can be changed or replaced.

**Indicators of attack**

IOAs describe activity that suggests an attack is happening or is about to happen. They are more forward-looking than IOCs and help you spot malicious intent before full compromise is confirmed.

- **Suspicious account activity** can indicate an attacker probing access.
- **Unauthorized network scans** can suggest reconnaissance.
- **Abnormal file modifications** can point to active tampering.
- **Unusual outbound traffic** can indicate data movement or beaconing.
- **Phishing or social engineering attempts** can be early signs of an attack chain.

IOAs are valuable when you want to interrupt an intrusion early. They focus on the sequence of actions and the attacker’s intent, not just the final artifact left behind.

**Behavioral indicators**

Behavioral indicators describe patterns of activity that reveal how an adversary operates. They are tied to behavior rather than a single hash, IP address, or domain. This makes them useful for detecting threats even when the attacker changes tools or infrastructure.

- **Repeated privilege escalation attempts** can show an effort to gain more control.
- **Abnormal account behavior** can reveal misuse of legitimate credentials.
- **Unusual system behavior** can show that a process is acting outside normal expectations.
- **Communication with known malicious infrastructure** can support a broader behavioral pattern.
- **Consistent attacker TTPs** can help you recognize the same threat actor across incidents.

Behavior-based detection is often more resilient than IOC-only detection because it focuses on what the attacker does, not just what the attacker uses.

**How the indicators differ**

| Type | What it shows | Typical timing | Practical use |
|---|---|---|---|
| IOC | Evidence of compromise | After compromise | Confirm breach, scope impact, support forensics |
| IOA | Signs of an attack | During or before compromise | Interrupt malicious activity early |
| Behavioral indicator | Adversary patterns and TTPs | Across activity over time | Detect recurring methods and reduce reliance on single artifacts |

A useful way to remember the difference is that IOCs answer, “What evidence is left behind?”, IOAs answer, “What attack is happening now?”, and behavioral indicators answer, “How is the adversary operating?”

**Where you look for indicators**

You usually correlate indicators across multiple telemetry sources instead of relying on one alert. That gives you better confidence and helps reduce false positives.

- **Event logs** provide host and authentication evidence.
- **XDR** platforms correlate endpoint and network activity.
- **SIEM** platforms centralize logs and support investigation.
- **Network monitoring** can reveal traffic anomalies and suspicious destinations.
- **Endpoint telemetry** can expose process, file, and account activity.

**Practical response workflow**

1. Identify the indicator and classify it as IOC, IOA, or behavioral.
2. Correlate it with logs, endpoint data, and network telemetry.
3. Confirm whether the activity matches known malicious patterns.
4. Contain the affected system or account if compromise is likely.
5. Remove malicious artifacts and close the attack path.
6. Use the findings to improve detection and reduce repeat risk.

**Exam focus points**

- **IOCs** are evidence of compromise, not just suspicion.
- **IOAs** help you detect an attack while it is unfolding.
- **Behavioral indicators** emphasize patterns, intent, and TTPs.
- **SIEM** and **XDR** are common places to search for indicator data.
- Strong analysis combines multiple indicators instead of depending on one artifact.
