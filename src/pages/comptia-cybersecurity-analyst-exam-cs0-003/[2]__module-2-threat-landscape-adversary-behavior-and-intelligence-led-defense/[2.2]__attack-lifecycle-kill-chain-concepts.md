## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**Attack lifecycle overview**

The cyber attack lifecycle describes an intrusion as a sequence of stages rather than a single event. In practice, defenders often see fragments such as a suspicious email, an unusual login, or an odd outbound connection. Putting those fragments in order helps you understand what the adversary is trying to accomplish and where you can interrupt the attack.

A useful way to think about this model is that attacks usually take time and planning. Evidence from industry sources describes cyberattacks as following a prescriptive process over weeks or months, which makes stage-based analysis valuable for detection and response.

- **Sequence** matters because isolated alerts rarely show the full picture.
- **Fragments** of activity become more meaningful when you correlate them over time.
- **Defense** improves when you identify the stage an attacker is in.

**Cyber Kill Chain concept**

The Cyber Kill Chain is a framework developed by Lockheed Martin as part of an intelligence-driven defense model. It identifies what adversaries must complete to achieve their objective and breaks an intrusion into seven steps. This structure helps you increase visibility into an attack and better understand adversary tactics, techniques, and procedures.

The seven stages are commonly listed as:

1. Reconnaissance
2. Weaponization
3. Delivery
4. Exploitation
5. Installation
6. Command and control (`C2`)
7. Actions on objectives

Each stage gives defenders a chance to detect, delay, or stop the intrusion before the attacker reaches the final objective.

- **Reconnaissance** is target research before the attack.
- **Weaponization** prepares the malicious payload or exploit.
- **Delivery** gets the payload to the target.
- **Exploitation** triggers the vulnerability or user action.
- **Installation** places the attacker’s tools on the system.
- **Command and control** establishes remote communication.
- **Actions on objectives** are the attacker’s end goals, such as theft or disruption.

**How the stages help defenders**

The value of the model is not just naming steps. It gives you a practical way to map observed activity to likely attacker intent. If you can identify where the intrusion is unfolding, you can choose controls that fit that stage instead of reacting only after damage occurs.

For example, early-stage activity may be easier to block with awareness, filtering, or monitoring, while later-stage activity may require containment and incident response. The model also supports better communication between analysts because it gives a shared vocabulary for describing what is happening.

> Use conservative wording when you are unsure of the exact stage. A partial indicator may suggest a phase, but it does not always prove it.

**Attack lifecycle vs. kill chain**

Both terms describe attacker progression, but they are used slightly differently in practice. The attack lifecycle is the broader idea of an intrusion unfolding over time. The Cyber Kill Chain is a specific seven-step framework that breaks that progression into named phases.

| Concept | Focus | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Attack lifecycle | Broad sequence of intrusion activity | Helps you think about attacks as a process |
| Cyber Kill Chain | Seven-stage framework | Helps you map and disrupt specific adversary actions |

**Analyst takeaways**

When you study these concepts for CS0-003, focus on how they support detection and response. You do not need to treat every incident as a perfect linear path, but the model gives you a strong mental framework for organizing evidence and prioritizing action.

- Look for patterns across multiple alerts instead of one event at a time.
- Map observed behavior to the most likely stage.
- Use the stage to guide your next defensive action.
- Remember that the model is meant to improve visibility and prevention, not just documentation.
