## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**Incident classification basics**

Incident classification gives you a consistent way to sort security events so you can respond in the right order. In practice, you use classification to reduce ambiguity, assign the right resources, and speed up restoration when services are disrupted.

A security incident is an adverse event that can compromise the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of systems and data. Classification helps you decide whether an event is routine noise or something that needs immediate escalation.

- **Confidentiality** impact includes unauthorized disclosure of data.
- **Integrity** impact includes unauthorized change or corruption of data.
- **Availability** impact includes service interruption or loss of access.
- Classification supports faster communication across teams and stakeholders.

**Severity assessment**

Severity measures how much an incident affects users and the business. Many organizations use a severity scale such as `SEV-1` through `SEV-4`, where a lower number means a more severe incident.

Severity is not just a label. It is a practical decision point that helps you prioritize work, allocate responders, and focus on the incidents with the greatest business impact.

- `SEV-1` usually indicates the most critical condition.
- Lower-priority incidents still matter, but they do not require the same urgency.
- Severity should reflect business impact, not just technical complexity.

**Impact and likelihood**

A useful severity model considers both impact and likelihood. Impact describes how damaging the incident could be to systems, data, customers, or operations. Likelihood describes how probable it is that the threat will occur or escalate.

This balance helps you avoid overreacting to low-risk events and underreacting to incidents that can spread quickly. In a structured incident response plan, these factors make the classification process more consistent.

- **Impact** focuses on damage to confidentiality, integrity, or availability.
- **Likelihood** focuses on the chance of escalation or realization.
- High impact with high likelihood usually justifies higher severity.
- Low impact with low likelihood usually supports lower severity.

**Common severity levels**

Many incident response plans use a simple Low, Medium, High, Critical model. This gives responders a shared language for triage and escalation.

| Severity | Practical meaning |
|---|---|
| Low | Minimal impact to systems, customers, or business operations |
| Medium | Noticeable impact that needs attention but is not immediately disruptive |
| High | Significant impact that affects operations or important services |
| Critical | Severe impact that threatens major business functions or broad availability |

Use the organization’s own criteria when available, but keep the scale consistent. The goal is to make decisions repeatable, not subjective.

**How classification supports response**

A standardized severity matrix helps the Security Incident Response Team, or SIRT, respond appropriately and communicate consistently. It also supports resource allocation and escalation decisions.

When you classify an incident well, you improve the entire response flow:

- You identify who needs to be engaged.
- You decide how quickly to escalate.
- You direct effort toward the most business-critical issues.
- You reduce confusion during high-pressure events.

**Practical assessment approach**

1. Identify the event and confirm whether it is a security incident.
2. Determine the affected systems, services, users, and data.
3. Estimate the impact to confidentiality, integrity, and availability.
4. Judge the likelihood of escalation or wider disruption.
5. Assign the severity level that best matches the combined risk.
6. Communicate the classification clearly to the response team.

> Use conservative, practical phrasing when evidence is incomplete. If you cannot confirm the full scope yet, classify based on the best verified impact and update the severity as more information becomes available.

**Exam focus**

For CySA+ style questions, expect incident classification to center on business impact, response priority, and escalation. You should be able to distinguish between a security event and a security incident, and explain why severity levels help teams act faster and more consistently.

- Classification is a decision-support process.
- Severity is based on impact and likelihood.
- Standardized levels improve response speed and communication.
- Lower numbers in a `SEV` scale usually mean higher severity.
