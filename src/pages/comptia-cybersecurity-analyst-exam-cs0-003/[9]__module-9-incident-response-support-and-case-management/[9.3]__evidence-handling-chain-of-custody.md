## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**Evidence handling fundamentals**

Digital evidence is fragile and can be altered, deleted, or corrupted more easily than physical evidence. Because of that, you need to preserve integrity from the moment of collection through presentation.

The core goal is to keep evidence legally defensible. Any unauthorized or accidental modification can undermine its value in an investigation or courtroom setting.

- **Digital evidence** includes items such as emails, documents, mobile data, text messages, network traffic, cloud data, logs, metadata, and social media content.
- Preserve the original state as much as possible during collection and storage.
- Treat every interaction with evidence as something that may need to be explained later.

> Any attempt to modify evidence, even unintentionally, can make it unusable in court.

**Chain of custody**

Chain of custody is the documented process that tracks who handled evidence, when it changed hands, how it was stored, and what actions were taken. It supports integrity by creating a clear, auditable history.

This documentation matters because investigators, attorneys, and courts need confidence that the evidence presented is the same evidence collected.

- Record the **collector** of the evidence.
- Record each **transfer** between people or systems.
- Record the **time** and date of handling.
- Record the **storage** location and conditions.
- Record any **actions** performed on the evidence.

**Preservation and documentation practices**

Good evidence handling focuses on minimizing change and maximizing traceability. You should be able to show what happened to the evidence at every stage.

A practical approach is to collect, document, store, and present evidence in a way that leaves as little ambiguity as possible.

1. Collect the evidence using a controlled process.
2. Document who handled it and when.
3. Store it securely to reduce the risk of alteration.
4. Track any movement or access.
5. Present it with a complete history of handling.

- Use consistent documentation so the evidence trail is easy to follow.
- Keep storage and transfer details complete enough to support later review.
- Assume that missing handling details weaken the evidence record.

**Why this matters in investigations**

Digital evidence often supports computer crime, fraud, and corporate investigations. Its value depends on whether you can prove it remained intact and properly controlled.

Chain of custody is the practice most directly associated with controlling evidence handling to protect integrity during an investigation. In exam terms, this is the concept to associate with documented evidence control.

**Exam focus**

- **Chain of custody** = documented control of evidence handling.
- **Evidence integrity** = evidence remains unchanged or explainably changed.
- **Digital evidence** = fragile data that can be easily altered or corrupted.
- **Legal validity** depends on careful preservation and complete documentation.
