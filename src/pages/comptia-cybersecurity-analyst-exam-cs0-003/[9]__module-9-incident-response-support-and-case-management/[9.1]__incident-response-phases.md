## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**Incident response lifecycle overview**

The incident response (IR) lifecycle is a structured workflow for handling security incidents. It helps you identify, contain, eradicate, and recover from an incident while reducing damage and restoring normal operations faster.

Different frameworks describe the lifecycle with slightly different phase counts, but the core flow stays consistent. NIST commonly groups the work into four phases, while other models expand the middle steps into six.

- **Preparation** builds the people, process, and tools you need before an incident happens.
- **Detection and analysis** focuses on recognizing suspicious activity and confirming whether an incident exists.
- **Containment** limits spread and buys time for response actions.
- **Eradication** removes the cause of the incident.
- **Recovery** returns systems and services to normal operation.
- **Post-incident activity** captures lessons learned and improves future response.

**NIST incident response phases**

NIST is one of the best-known frameworks for incident response. In the four-phase model, the middle response work is grouped together so you can think about the lifecycle more efficiently during an exam or in practice.

1. **Preparation**
   - Define policies, roles, and procedures.
   - Train responders and keep tools ready.
   - Build the foundation for faster action when an alert occurs.

2. **Detection and analysis**
   - Identify indicators of compromise and validate the event.
   - Determine scope, impact, and likely cause.
   - Decide whether the event is a true incident and how urgent it is.

3. **Containment, eradication, and recovery**
   - Contain the incident to prevent further damage.
   - Remove the attacker’s foothold or the malicious cause.
   - Restore affected systems and services carefully.

4. **Post-incident activity**
   - Review what happened and what worked.
   - Update plans, detections, and runbooks based on lessons learned.
   - Use the incident to improve future readiness.

**Six-step model and how it maps**

Some sources describe the lifecycle as six phases instead of four. This is usually the same process, just split into more detailed steps. The practical difference is that containment, eradication, and recovery are separated so teams can manage each action more clearly.

| Six-step model | NIST grouping |
|---|---|
| Preparation | Preparation |
| Detection and analysis | Detection and analysis |
| Containment | Containment, eradication, and recovery |
| Eradication | Containment, eradication, and recovery |
| Recovery | Containment, eradication, and recovery |
| Post-incident activity | Post-incident activity |

**What each phase is for**

The phases are not just a checklist. They form a loop that improves over time. What you learn after an incident should feed back into preparation, detections, and response procedures.

- **Preparation** reduces response time because you are not building the process during the crisis.
- **Detection and analysis** helps you separate noise from a real security event.
- **Containment** gives you time to investigate without letting the incident spread.
- **Eradication** ensures the root cause is removed, not just hidden.
- **Recovery** brings systems back online in a controlled way.
- **Post-incident activity** strengthens the next response cycle.

> **Important:** Do not treat containment and eradication as the same action. Containment limits damage; eradication removes the cause.

**Practical exam focus**

For exam prep, focus on the purpose of each phase and the order of the lifecycle. You should be able to match a task to the correct phase and explain why the phase matters.

- If the team is writing policies, training staff, and staging tools, that is **preparation**.
- If the team is confirming an alert and assessing impact, that is **detection and analysis**.
- If the team is isolating a host or segment, that is **containment**.
- If the team is removing malware or attacker access, that is **eradication**.
- If the team is restoring services, that is **recovery**.
- If the team is updating procedures after the event, that is **post-incident activity**.

**Why the lifecycle matters**

A documented incident response plan gives your organization a repeatable way to handle breaches and cyberattacks. It supports faster decision-making, better coordination, and more consistent recovery. Regular updates and training keep the plan useful when a real incident occurs.
