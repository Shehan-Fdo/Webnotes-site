## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**Post-incident review purpose**

A post-incident review is a detailed retrospective that helps you understand an incident from start to finish. It is part of the incident response process and works best as a cross-functional effort across the people, processes, and technologies involved.

The main goal is to learn from the event and reduce the chance of recurrence. You use the review to identify what happened, why it happened, and what should change afterward.

- Capture the full sequence of events.
- Identify the root cause and contributing factors.
- Review the scope of the attack and affected systems.
- Document lessons learned for future response.

**What you should look for**

A strong review goes beyond the obvious alert or visible damage. It should uncover weaknesses that the threat actor exploited and reveal gaps in detection, response, or communication.

Focus on both technical and operational findings:

- **Indicators of compromise**: artifacts that show malicious activity.
- **Tactics, techniques, and procedures**: attacker behavior patterns that explain how the incident unfolded.
- **Vulnerabilities**: exposed weaknesses that were compromised or left unaddressed.
- **Process flaws**: manual errors, procedural failures, or communication gaps.
- **Unknown attack vectors**: paths or methods the response team did not previously recognize.

> Use conservative wording when evidence is incomplete. If you cannot prove a cause, record it as a hypothesis and validate it before changing controls.

**Postmortem outputs**

Some incidents, especially those that affect customers or involve a data breach, require a full postmortem. In practice, the postmortem becomes the record of what happened and what the team learned.

A useful postmortem includes:

- A documented sequence of events.
- A technical summary supported by evidence.
- The actors involved, if known.
- How the response was executed.
- Key takeaways and follow-up actions.

These outputs help you turn an incident into actionable improvement instead of a one-time report.

**Control improvement and follow-up**

The review should lead to concrete changes that prevent recurrence. The evidence emphasizes follow-up action, continuous improvement, and better alignment with the current threat environment.

Typical improvement areas include:

- Remediating vulnerabilities faster.
- Strengthening controls around the weaknesses the incident exposed.
- Updating procedures where manual or communication failures occurred.
- Improving detection and response coverage for similar threats.
- Adjusting risk mitigation controls based on what the incident revealed.

A practical review does not stop at documentation. It feeds lessons learned back into the security program so future incidents are less likely and easier to contain.

**How to conduct the review**

1. Gather the incident response team and other relevant stakeholders.
2. Reconstruct the incident timeline from evidence.
3. Identify root cause, contributing factors, and affected assets.
4. Record indicators, attacker behavior, and any process breakdowns.
5. Define follow-up actions that close the gaps you found.
6. Track remediation until the control changes are complete.

**Exam focus**

For CySA+ exam prep, remember the relationship between review and improvement:

- The review explains what happened.
- The postmortem documents evidence and lessons learned.
- The follow-up actions improve controls and reduce recurrence.
- The process is cross-functional, not limited to the security team.

If a question asks what to do after containment and recovery, look for answers that emphasize retrospective analysis, lessons learned, and control enhancement rather than simply closing the ticket.
