## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**Why uncertainty matters in CTI**

Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI) information is often incomplete. It can include intelligence gaps, assumptions, assessments, and judgments, so you should avoid presenting analysis as if it were certain when the evidence is limited.

Standardized language helps reduce miscommunication. Terms like “may,” “we believe,” and “we assess” can be interpreted differently, so use consistent wording when you communicate likelihood and confidence.

- **Uncertainty** appears when evidence is partial, conflicting, or indirect.
- **Judgments** should be qualified so readers understand the limits of the analysis.
- **Standardized language** improves consistency across reports and teams.

**Words of Estimative Probability and analytic confidence**

Intelligence analysis often uses Words of Estimative Probability (WEP) and Levels of Confidence in Assessment (LCA) to communicate uncertainty. WEP expresses likelihood, while confidence describes how strong the supporting evidence is.

A practical approach is to pair a probability statement with a confidence statement. That gives the consumer both the expected outcome and the analyst’s certainty about the assessment.

| Concept | What it communicates | Practical use |
|---|---|---|
| WEP | Likelihood or probability | Describes how likely an event is |
| LCA | Confidence in the assessment | Describes how strong the evidence is |

- **WEP** helps you express likelihood in a controlled way.
- **LCA** helps you show how much trust to place in the assessment.
- **Analytic confidence** is not the same as certainty; it still allows for error.

**How confidence is grounded in evidence**

Confidence levels are based on the extent and quality of information available and on how well different lines of evidence corroborate one another. In practice, stronger corroboration supports stronger confidence.

The CDC describes confidence levels as low, moderate, and high. That three-level model is useful because it keeps the assessment simple while still reflecting the quality of the evidence.

- **Low** confidence means the evidence is limited or weakly supported.
- **Moderate** confidence means the evidence is credible but not fully conclusive.
- **High** confidence means the evidence is strong and well corroborated, but still not absolute.

> A high-confidence judgment is still not a fact or a certainty.

**Communicating uncertainty clearly**

Use language that matches the evidence and avoid overstating conclusions. If the evidence is thin, say so directly instead of implying certainty.

The FIRST Standard recommends a streamlined approach for communicating uncertainty and specifically notes the value of using only LCA in some reporting contexts. That approach can help CTI teams communicate clearly without overwhelming consumers with multiple overlapping standards.

- State the assessment and the basis for it.
- Qualify the statement when evidence is incomplete.
- Keep the language consistent across reports.
- Tailor probability and confidence language to organizational needs when required.

**Practical reporting guidance**

When you write an assessment, make the relationship between evidence and confidence obvious. Readers should be able to see what you know, what you infer, and how sure you are.

1. Identify the evidence you have.
2. Note any gaps, assumptions, or conflicting indicators.
3. State the assessment using standardized probability language.
4. Add a confidence level that reflects the quality and corroboration of the evidence.
5. Revise the confidence level if new evidence changes the assessment.

**Exam focus**

For CySA+ reporting questions, focus on the communication goal: help decision makers understand both the likely outcome and the strength of the supporting evidence. The key is not to sound certain when the analysis is still probabilistic.

- Use **clear qualifiers** instead of vague hedging.
- Separate **likelihood** from **confidence**.
- Base confidence on evidence quality and corroboration.
- Keep the message concise enough for operational consumers.

**Takeaway**

Uncertainty is normal in CTI. Your job is to communicate it in a way that is accurate, consistent, and useful for decision making. Standardized probability language and confidence levels make your reporting more reliable and easier to interpret.
