## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**Service accounts as non-human identities**

Service accounts are non-human identities used by applications, services, and automation. They often run continuously, may hold elevated privileges, and do not follow the normal human patterns that many detection systems expect.

Because they are designed for machine use, service accounts can be overlooked during routine identity reviews. That makes them a high-value target when they are over-permissioned, stale, or poorly monitored.

- They can operate 24/7 with persistent access.
- They may have admin-level rights if teams choose convenience over least privilege.
- Their activity can resemble legitimate automation even when an attacker uses them.

> Service accounts do not behave like users, so human-focused alerting can miss compromise.

**Why service accounts are risky**

A compromised service account can provide broad access without raising obvious suspicion. If the account has not changed credentials for a long time or is tied to forgotten automation, it can become a durable foothold.

Security posture improves when you treat these accounts as a separate identity class rather than as ordinary user accounts.

- Apply **least privilege** to every service account.
- Rotate credentials on a defined schedule.
- Review whether the account is still needed and still in use.
- Monitor for unusual behavior instead of relying only on user-centric baselines.

**Delegated permissions and Kerberos delegation**

Delegation lets one identity act on behalf of another. In Active Directory, <abbr title="Kerberos delegation">Kerberos delegation</abbr> is a common area of concern because it can be abused to extend access beyond the original account.

The main delegation types you should recognize are:

- **Unconstrained delegation**
- **Constrained delegation**
- **Resource-based constrained delegation**

Delegation risk is not limited to human users. It can also apply to computer accounts, which means a trusted service may act on behalf of sensitive machine identities as well.

- Delegation can become an elevation-of-privilege path.
- A trusted service can be used to impersonate other identities.
- Sensitive machine accounts increase the impact when delegation is abused.

**Abuse patterns to recognize**

Attackers often look for identities that already have broad access or special trust relationships. Service principals in Microsoft Entra ID can be especially dangerous when they receive excessive permissions for convenience.

One example is abuse of `User.DeleteRestore.All`, where an attacker can delete many objects, disrupt operations, and make recovery harder while covering tracks.

Common abuse patterns include:

- **Over-permissioned service accounts** used for lateral movement or data access.
- **Delegation abuse** to impersonate other users or machine accounts.
- **Service principal abuse** in Entra ID to perform destructive actions.
- **Stale accounts** that remain valid targets even when nobody actively monitors them.

**Stale and forgotten accounts**

Stale accounts are a security risk because they may still be enabled even though nobody uses them. Microsoft Defender for Identity identifies stale Active Directory user accounts as those that have not logged in during the past 90 days, while excluding service accounts and disabled or deleted accounts.

That distinction matters: service accounts are often intentionally long-lived, so you should not treat them the same way as ordinary user accounts. Instead, verify ownership, purpose, and current usage.

- Confirm the business need for each account.
- Check whether the account is still tied to an active service.
- Remove or disable accounts that no longer have a valid purpose.
- Track where the account is used across the environment.

**Practical review checklist**

Use a simple review process to reduce exposure from service accounts and delegated permissions.

1. Identify service accounts, service principals, and delegated trust relationships.
2. Verify whether each identity still has a valid business purpose.
3. Compare granted permissions with the minimum required access.
4. Look for stale, forgotten, or orphaned accounts.
5. Monitor for unusual access patterns, destructive actions, or impersonation behavior.

**Detection and monitoring focus**

Because service accounts do not follow normal human schedules, detection should focus on behavior and privilege rather than time of day alone. Layered monitoring helps you find where the account is used and whether its activity matches its intended function.

Useful monitoring goals include:

- Tracking where the account authenticates.
- Reviewing privilege changes and delegation settings.
- Watching for unexpected object deletion or access expansion.
- Correlating service account activity with the service it supports.

**Key takeaways**

Service accounts, delegated permissions, and service principals all expand the attack surface when they are over-trusted. The safest approach is to keep permissions narrow, validate ongoing need, and monitor for abuse patterns that blend in with legitimate automation.
