## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**Conditional access as a policy engine**

Microsoft Entra Conditional Access is a Zero Trust policy engine that uses identity-driven signals to make access decisions. In practice, it works as an `if-then` control: if a user tries to access a resource, then the policy can require an action such as multifactor authentication (MFA).

The key idea is that access decisions are not based only on location or network perimeter. They are based on signals about the user and the sign-in attempt.

- **Identity-driven signals** help determine whether access should be allowed.
- **Policy enforcement** happens when the configured conditions are satisfied.
- **MFA** is a common response when risk is elevated.

**Risk-based access policies**

Microsoft Entra ID Protection provides two user-specific risk conditions that Conditional Access can use:

- **Sign-in risk**
- **User risk**

These risk-based access policies apply when ID Protection detects a risk level during sign-in and sends that risk level to Conditional Access. You can then choose an access control method based on the policy outcome.

A practical example is requiring MFA only when the sign-in risk level is medium or high. That approach reduces friction for low-risk access while still adding protection when the risk signal increases.

> Do not combine sign-in risk and user risk conditions in the same Conditional Access policy. Create separate policies for each risk condition.

**How the risk signals are used**

During each sign-in, ID Protection evaluates the event and passes the detected risk level to Conditional Access. Conditional Access then checks the policy conditions and enforces the configured control if the conditions match.

This makes the response adaptive:

- Low-risk sign-ins can proceed with normal access.
- Medium- or high-risk sign-ins can trigger extra verification.
- Risky behavior can also lead to blocking or remediation actions.

The main benefit is that users are prompted only when the risk level justifies it, which supports both security and usability.

**Policy types and responses**

There are two risk policy types you should distinguish:

- **User risk policy**: responds to risk associated with the user account.
- **Sign-in risk policy**: responds to risk associated with a specific sign-in attempt.

These policies can automate the response to risk and allow users to self-remediate when appropriate. Depending on the policy, the response can include:

- Blocking the sign-in attempt
- Requiring MFA
- Requiring a secure password change

**Configuration and prerequisites**

To create or edit Conditional Access policies, you need the **Conditional Access Administrator** role, which is the least privileged role required for that task.

For full access to Microsoft Entra ID Protection features, you need one of these licenses:

- Microsoft Entra ID P2
- Microsoft Entra Suite

**Practical policy design**

When you design risk-based access controls, keep the policy scope clear and separate. Use one policy for sign-in risk and another for user risk so the controls remain easy to understand and troubleshoot.

1. Identify whether the risk is tied to the sign-in attempt or the user account.
2. Create a separate Conditional Access policy for that risk condition.
3. Choose the access control action, such as MFA, blocking, or password change.
4. Test the policy behavior against expected risk levels.
5. Review whether the response is strict enough without creating unnecessary friction.

**Exam focus**

For exam purposes, remember these relationships:

- Conditional Access enforces access decisions using identity signals.
- ID Protection supplies risk signals to Conditional Access.
- Sign-in risk and user risk are separate conditions.
- Risk-based policies can require MFA, block access, or trigger remediation.
- Separate policies are recommended for separate risk conditions.

**Quick comparison**

| Item | What it applies to | Typical response |
|---|---|---|
| Sign-in risk | A specific sign-in attempt | MFA, block, or other access control |
| User risk | The user account | Password change or other remediation |

**Key takeaway**

Conditional Access and ID Protection work together to turn identity risk into an access decision. You use the risk signal to decide whether the user should proceed normally, prove identity with MFA, or take a remediation step before access continues.
