## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**Baselines and why they matter**

A baseline is your reference point for what “normal” looks like in an environment. In security operations, you use it to compare current behavior against expected behavior for systems and users. That comparison helps you spot suspicious changes that may otherwise blend into routine activity.

Baselining matters because modern attacks often try to look ordinary. If you do not know the normal pattern, you cannot reliably identify abnormal behavior. This is why baselines are closely tied to continuous monitoring and SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) workflows.

- **Normal behavior** includes expected activity patterns for hosts, accounts, and services.
- **Abnormal behavior** is any meaningful deviation from that expected pattern.
- **Visibility** must be complete and accurate for the baseline to be useful.
- **Configuration** quality affects whether monitoring tools produce useful detections.

**Anomaly detection in practice**

Anomaly detection, also called outlier detection, looks for deviations from the baseline. In cybersecurity, it helps identify activity that does not fit the established pattern of a user, endpoint, or network segment. Some platforms use machine learning (ML) and artificial intelligence (AI) to improve this process, but the core idea remains the same: detect what stands out.

Detection rules are the operational layer that turns this idea into action. In a Security Operations Center (SOC), rules help automatically identify malicious activity from large volumes of security data. Without effective rules, even a strong SIEM can become little more than log storage.

- **Detection rules** translate suspicious patterns into alerts.
- **SIEM** platforms depend on good data and good tuning.
- **Threats** may evade technical defenses and remain hidden if monitoring is weak.
- **Context** matters, because a deviation is only meaningful relative to the environment.

> Anomaly detection is only as strong as the baseline behind it and the data feeding it.

**Building and using baselines**

You build baselines by observing behavior over time and identifying stable patterns. The goal is not to eliminate all variation, but to understand what is expected enough to recognize meaningful change. This applies to both systems and users.

A practical baseline should reflect the environment you actually monitor. If the data is incomplete, the baseline will be misleading. If the environment changes and the baseline is not updated, you may create false positives or miss real threats.

1. Collect representative activity data from the systems and users you monitor.
2. Identify recurring patterns that define normal behavior.
3. Compare new activity against the established reference.
4. Adjust the baseline when legitimate changes alter normal operations.
5. Review alerts to separate true anomalies from expected variation.

**Coverage gaps and their impact**

Coverage gaps are blind spots in monitoring. They appear when tools, logs, sensors, or rules do not fully observe the environment. A gap can come from missing log sources, poor configuration, incomplete asset visibility, or rules that do not cover a relevant behavior.

These gaps weaken anomaly detection because the system cannot compare against what it cannot see. They also reduce the value of SIEM analysis, since the platform depends on complete and accurate visibility to detect suspicious activity effectively.

- **Missing telemetry** creates blind spots in detection.
- **Incomplete asset coverage** leaves parts of the environment unmonitored.
- **Poor rule coverage** allows suspicious behavior to pass without alerting.
- **Weak tuning** can hide important signals inside noise.

**How to reduce coverage gaps**

You reduce coverage gaps by checking what your monitoring stack can actually see and by validating that the right sources feed your detections. The objective is not just more data, but better data coverage for the assets and behaviors that matter.

- Confirm that critical systems send logs to the monitoring platform.
- Verify that detection rules cover the behaviors you care about.
- Review whether the SIEM has visibility into the full environment.
- Tune rules so they remain useful as the environment changes.
- Reassess coverage after major changes, incidents, or new deployments.

**Key relationships to remember**

Baselines, anomaly detection, and coverage gaps work together. A baseline defines normal behavior, anomaly detection finds deviations, and coverage gaps limit how well you can see either one. If any part is weak, detection quality drops.

| Concept | Purpose | Risk if weak |
|---|---|---|
| Baseline | Defines expected behavior | False alerts or missed deviations |
| Anomaly detection | Flags unusual activity | Suspicious activity blends in |
| Coverage | Provides visibility | Blind spots in monitoring |

**Exam focus**

For exam purposes, connect the concepts in a practical way:

- Baselines support detection by defining normal activity.
- Anomaly detection identifies behavior that deviates from that normal.
- SIEM and detection rules only work well when visibility is complete and accurate.
- Coverage gaps reduce confidence in alerts and can hide malicious activity.
