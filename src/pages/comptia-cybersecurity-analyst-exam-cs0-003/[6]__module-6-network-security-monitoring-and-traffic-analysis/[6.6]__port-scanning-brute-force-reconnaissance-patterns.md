## **Course:** CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst (Exam CS0-003)

**Reconnaissance patterns**

Reconnaissance is the intelligence-gathering phase that helps an attacker map systems, identities, services, and trust relationships before exploitation. In network environments, this often shows up as service discovery, port scans, and other probing activity that tries to identify what is exposed and what may be vulnerable.

Modern reconnaissance is often low-and-slow. Attackers may blend into normal traffic, use legitimate protocols, and spread activity across time to reduce obvious spikes. That means you should look for behavior patterns, not just single noisy events.

- **Network service discovery** can include port scans, vulnerability scans, and wordlist scans against remote hosts.
- **Service enumeration** often aims to list running services and identify software versions or operating systems.
- **Behavioral detection** matters because reconnaissance may use legitimate tools and protocols.
- **Early detection** reduces attacker precision and limits downstream impact.

**Port scanning signals**

Port scanning is a common way to discover which services are reachable on a host or network device. The goal is usually to find open ports, identify exposed services, and determine whether a target is worth further probing.

A practical detection approach is to baseline normal network activity first, then look for deviations. Sudden increases in connection attempts, repeated access to many ports, or a single source touching many hosts in a short window can all indicate scanning behavior.

- **Many ports, one host** suggests a host-focused scan.
- **One port, many hosts** suggests a sweep for a specific service.
- **Repeated short connections** can indicate probing rather than normal application use.
- **Unexpected service access** may reveal reconnaissance against internal infrastructure.

> Use conservative interpretation: a scan-like pattern is suspicious, but it is not proof of malicious intent without context.

**Brute force patterns**

Brute force activity usually appears as repeated authentication attempts rather than direct network discovery. It often targets accounts, services, or login portals and may be paired with reconnaissance to identify the best target first.

You should watch for bursts of failed logons, repeated attempts from the same source, or attempts distributed across many accounts. Low-and-slow brute force can be harder to spot because it avoids obvious lockout thresholds and noisy spikes.

- **Failed logon bursts** can indicate password guessing.
- **Distributed attempts** across accounts may suggest credential stuffing or broad guessing.
- **Timing patterns** matter when attempts occur at regular intervals.
- **Source concentration** helps you separate a single noisy client from coordinated activity.

**Detection and investigation approach**

1. Establish a baseline for normal host, user, and network activity.
2. Compare current activity against expected volume, timing, and destination patterns.
3. Correlate network events with authentication logs and endpoint activity.
4. Look for repeated probing, unusual service access, or clustered login failures.
5. Confirm whether the activity matches authorized scanning, testing, or administration.

When you investigate, focus on the sequence of events. Reconnaissance often comes before exploitation, and brute force may follow service discovery if an attacker finds a reachable login surface.

**Nmap and network discovery**

Nmap (Network Mapper) is a common tool for network reconnaissance and service discovery. It is used to find live hosts, open ports, running services, software versions, and sometimes operating system details. In security work, it supports methodical discovery and enumeration.

Nmap can also support scripting for automated checks and service enumeration. That makes it useful in authorized assessments, but the same capabilities can also be used by adversaries during network service discovery.

```bash
nmap -sV 192.0.2.10
nmap -Pn 192.0.2.0/24
```

- `-sV` helps identify service versions.
- `-Pn` skips host discovery and treats targets as online.
- Version and host discovery results can help you understand what an attacker may have learned.

**What to remember for analysis**

Reconnaissance, scanning, and brute force often appear as patterns rather than isolated alerts. The most useful clues are repetition, breadth, timing, and correlation across logs.

- **Breadth**: many ports, many hosts, or many accounts.
- **Timing**: repeated attempts over short or extended periods.
- **Correlation**: network probes followed by authentication failures or service access.
- **Context**: authorized testing, admin activity, and maintenance windows can explain some scans.

If you can identify the pattern early, you can reduce the attacker’s ability to map the environment and choose the next step.
