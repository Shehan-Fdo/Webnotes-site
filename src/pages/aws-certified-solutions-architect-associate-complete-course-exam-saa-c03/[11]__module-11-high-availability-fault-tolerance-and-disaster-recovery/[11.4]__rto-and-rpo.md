## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Associate Complete Course (Exam SAA-C03)

**Core concepts**

<dl>
<dt><strong>RTO</strong> (Recovery Time Objective)</dt>
<dd>The maximum acceptable time to restore a service after an outage. It answers: <em>How long can the application be down?</em></dd>
<dt><strong>RPO</strong> (Recovery Point Objective)</dt>
<dd>The maximum acceptable amount of data loss measured in time. It answers: <em>How much data can you afford to lose?</em></dd>
</dl>

These two objectives drive disaster recovery, backup, replication, and failover design. In AWS, you choose services and architectures based on business tolerance for downtime and data loss, not just on technical preference.

- **RTO** is about service restoration speed.
- **RPO** is about data freshness at the time of recovery.
- Lower values usually mean higher cost and more operational complexity.
- They are business requirements first, technical implementations second.

**How to think about them**

A simple way to remember the difference is:

- If a database fails at 10:00 and you restore it at 10:20, your **RTO** is 20 minutes.
- If the last recoverable backup is from 09:55, your **RPO** is 5 minutes.

That means the business lost 20 minutes of service and 5 minutes of data. A design can meet one objective and fail the other, so you must evaluate both independently.

**Typical AWS disaster recovery patterns**

AWS disaster recovery strategies are often described in increasing order of resilience and cost:

| Strategy | RTO | RPO | Typical use |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Backup and restore | High | High | Non-critical workloads, lowest cost |
| Pilot light | Medium | Low to medium | Core services kept minimal in AWS |
| Warm standby | Low | Low | Faster recovery with scaled-down duplicate environment |
| Multi-site active/active | Very low | Very low | Mission-critical systems, highest cost and complexity |

- **Backup and restore** means you keep backups and rebuild infrastructure after failure.
- **Pilot light** keeps essential components running, such as databases or core services, while the rest is provisioned during recovery.
- **Warm standby** keeps a scaled-down but functional copy of the environment ready to scale up.
- **Active/active** serves traffic from multiple sites or Regions at the same time.

> Lower RTO and RPO usually require more automation, more replication, and more always-on infrastructure.

**AWS services that help meet RTO and RPO**

Different services contribute to different parts of the recovery plan:

- **Amazon S3**
  - Stores backups, logs, and static recovery artifacts.
  - Supports versioning, lifecycle policies, and cross-Region replication.
- **AWS Backup**
  - Centralizes backup policies across supported AWS services.
  - Helps automate retention and restore workflows.
- **Amazon EBS snapshots**
  - Provide point-in-time backups for EC2 instance volumes.
  - Useful for restoring servers and application data.
- **Amazon RDS automated backups**
  - Support point-in-time recovery for relational databases.
  - Reduce RPO by allowing recovery to a specific time.
- **Amazon Aurora Global Database**
  - Replicates across Regions with low-latency replication for fast disaster recovery.
- **Amazon Route 53**
  - Supports DNS-based failover and health checks.
  - Helps reduce RTO by redirecting users to healthy endpoints.
- **Elastic Load Balancing**
  - Distributes traffic across healthy targets and Availability Zones.
- **Amazon EC2 Auto Scaling**
  - Replaces failed instances and scales capacity during recovery.
- **AWS Elastic Disaster Recovery**
  - Replicates servers into AWS for low-RTO recovery scenarios.

**Backup, replication, and failover**

RTO and RPO are influenced by three major mechanisms:

- **Backup**
  - Creates recoverable copies at intervals.
  - Best for reducing cost and meeting moderate recovery needs.
  - Usually improves RPO only as often as backups run.
- **Replication**
  - Copies data continuously or near-continuously.
  - Better for low RPO because data loss is minimized.
  - Can be synchronous or asynchronous.
- **Failover**
  - Switches traffic to a standby system or Region.
  - Best for reducing RTO because users are redirected quickly.

A common design mistake is assuming replication alone solves disaster recovery. Replication may protect data, but if the application stack is not ready to serve traffic, the RTO can still be poor.

**Synchronous vs asynchronous replication**

- **Synchronous replication**
  - Writes must complete on both primary and secondary before success is returned.
  - Provides very low RPO, often near zero.
  - Adds latency and is usually limited to shorter distances or specific managed services.
- **Asynchronous replication**
  - Writes are acknowledged on the primary first, then copied to the secondary.
  - Usually has a small non-zero RPO.
  - More common across Regions because it scales better over distance.

For exam scenarios, remember:

- Use **synchronous** replication when data loss must be minimized and latency is acceptable.
- Use **asynchronous** replication when cross-Region resilience matters and some data loss is tolerable.

**Practical examples**

A few realistic scenarios help anchor the concepts:

- A static marketing site can tolerate a 4-hour RTO and 24-hour RPO.
  - Use S3, versioning, and periodic backups.
  - Route 53 can fail over to a secondary site if needed.
- An internal reporting app may need a 1-hour RTO and 15-minute RPO.
  - Use automated database backups, snapshots, and scripted infrastructure rebuilds.
  - Keep infrastructure as code ready for rapid redeployment.
- A payment processing system may require near-zero RPO and very low RTO.
  - Use multi-AZ architecture, replication, automated failover, and possibly multi-Region design.
  - Consider Aurora Global Database or other low-latency replication patterns.

**How to reduce RTO**

To reduce recovery time, focus on how quickly you can restore service:

- Automate infrastructure creation with **AWS CloudFormation** or similar infrastructure as code.
- Keep AMIs, container images, and configuration artifacts ready.
- Use health checks and DNS failover with Route 53.
- Pre-provision standby capacity when the business requires fast recovery.
- Minimize manual steps in the recovery runbook.
- Test failover regularly so the process is reliable under pressure.

Example CloudFormation-oriented recovery approach:

```yaml
Resources:
  AppServer:
    Type: AWS::EC2::Instance
    Properties:
      ImageId: ami-0123456789abcdef0
      InstanceType: t3.micro
      SubnetId: subnet-abc123
      SecurityGroupIds:
        - sg-0123456789abcdef0
```

If the primary environment fails, you can redeploy the stack in another Region or account and then restore data from backups or replicas.

**How to reduce RPO**

To reduce data loss, focus on how often data is captured or replicated:

- Increase backup frequency.
- Use point-in-time recovery where available.
- Replicate data across Availability Zones or Regions.
- Store transaction logs and enable versioning for object storage.
- Design applications to write critical state to durable managed services.

Example RDS backup and restore workflow:

```bash
aws rds create-db-snapshot \
  --db-instance-identifier mydb \
  --db-snapshot-identifier mydb-prechange-20260426

aws rds restore-db-instance-from-db-snapshot \
  --db-instance-identifier mydb-restored \
  --db-snapshot-identifier mydb-prechange-20260426
```

This approach improves recoverability, but the RPO depends on how recent the snapshot is and whether automated backups or logs are available for point-in-time recovery.

**Multi-AZ and Multi-Region considerations**

- **Multi-AZ**
  - Protects against Availability Zone failure.
  - Usually improves availability and can reduce RTO.
  - Often does not provide the same disaster recovery scope as a separate Region.
- **Multi-Region**
  - Protects against Region-wide disruption.
  - Usually improves both RTO and RPO when designed well.
  - Increases cost, complexity, and operational overhead.

Use Multi-AZ for high availability within a Region. Use Multi-Region when the business impact of a full-Region outage is unacceptable.

**Exam decision cues**

When you see a question, identify the business goal first:

- If the question asks how quickly service must return, think **RTO**.
- If the question asks how much data loss is acceptable, think **RPO**.
- If the requirement is “no more than 5 minutes of data loss,” choose a design with low-latency replication or very frequent backups.
- If the requirement is “restore service within 15 minutes,” choose automation, standby capacity, or failover.
- If both are extremely strict, expect a more expensive architecture such as active/active or highly automated warm standby.

**Common pitfalls**

- Confusing **backup frequency** with RTO; frequent backups help RPO more than RTO.
- Assuming **Multi-AZ** equals disaster recovery for every scenario.
- Ignoring application dependencies such as DNS, secrets, queues, and identity systems.
- Designing only for data recovery without planning how traffic returns to the application.
- Forgetting to test the recovery process; an untested plan often fails during an actual incident.

**Quick reference**

| Term | Focus | Question it answers | Lower is better for |
|---|---|---|---|
| RTO | Time to restore service | How long can we be down? | Availability and user impact |
| RPO | Amount of data loss | How much data can we lose? | Data protection and consistency |

**Practical recovery checklist**

1. Identify the application’s RTO and RPO requirements.
2. Classify the workload by business criticality.
3. Choose a recovery pattern that matches the targets.
4. Automate infrastructure and data restoration.
5. Add monitoring, health checks, and failover logic.
6. Test the plan and measure actual recovery time and data loss.
7. Update the design if the measured results do not meet the target.

**Key takeaway**

RTO and RPO are business-defined recovery targets that shape every disaster recovery decision in AWS. RTO measures how fast you restore service; RPO measures how much data you can afford to lose. The best architecture is the one that meets both targets at the lowest acceptable cost and complexity.
