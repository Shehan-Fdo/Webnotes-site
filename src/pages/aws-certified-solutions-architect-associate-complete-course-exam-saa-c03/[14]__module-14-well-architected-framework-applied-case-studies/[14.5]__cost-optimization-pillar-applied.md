## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Associate Complete Course (Exam SAA-C03)

**Cost Optimization pillar overview**

Cost optimization is about getting the required business outcome at the lowest total cost, not simply choosing the cheapest service. In SAA-C03 scenarios, you usually balance price, performance, operational effort, and flexibility. The exam often tests whether you can reduce spend without harming availability, durability, or latency.

The main levers are:

- **Right-size** resources so you do not pay for unused capacity.
- **Choose the right pricing model** for steady or variable workloads.
- **Match storage class and retention policy** to access patterns.
- **Use managed services and automation** to reduce operational overhead.
- **Measure and allocate spend** so teams can see and control usage.

A good answer usually considers both immediate savings and long-term governance. For example, a workload may be cheaper on Spot Instances, but only if it can tolerate interruption. A database may be cheaper on provisioned capacity, but only if traffic is predictable enough to benefit from reservations or auto scaling.

**AWS billing, visibility, and allocation**

AWS Billing and Cost Management is the primary place to inspect, allocate, and control spend. In AWS Organizations, the management account or payer account can see consolidated billing across linked accounts, which is important for enterprise cost visibility and chargeback.

Key capabilities include:

- **Consolidated billing** combines usage across accounts for one bill and enables shared pricing benefits such as volume discounts.
- **Payer and linked account visibility** lets you view costs by account, service, region, and usage type.
- **Cost allocation** helps you distribute spend across business dimensions such as application, environment, team, or project.
- **AWS Cost Categories** let you group costs using rules, such as mapping linked accounts to `Prod`, `Dev`, or `Shared Services`.
- **Cost allocation tags** support chargeback and showback when you tag resources consistently.

Use Cost Categories when account structure alone does not match business reporting. For example, one account may host multiple applications, or one application may span several accounts. Cost Categories can map those resources into business-friendly buckets for reporting and budgeting.

A practical governance pattern is:

- Tag resources with keys like `Application`, `Owner`, `Environment`, and `CostCenter`.
- Activate cost allocation tags in Billing.
- Build Cost Categories for business reporting.
- Use AWS Organizations to centralize billing and policy control.

**Pricing models and when to use them**

AWS pricing models are a frequent exam topic because the best answer depends on workload predictability and interruption tolerance.

| Model | Best for | Key tradeoff |
|---|---|---|
| On-Demand | Unpredictable or short-lived workloads | Highest unit cost, no commitment |
| Reserved Instances (RIs) | Steady usage with predictable baseline | Commitment required, less flexibility |
| Savings Plans | Flexible compute commitment | Scope and service limitations apply |
| Spot Instances | Fault-tolerant, interruptible workloads | Can be reclaimed by AWS |
| Dedicated Hosts / Instances | Compliance or licensing needs | Higher cost, specialized use |

**Reserved Instances** come in different forms:

- **Standard RIs** provide the deepest discount for steady-state usage.
- **Convertible RIs** offer more flexibility to change instance families, operating systems, or tenancy, but usually with a smaller discount.
- **Payment options** include All Upfront, Partial Upfront, and No Upfront, which trade cash flow for discount level.

**Savings Plans** are a commitment to spend a fixed amount per hour for a term, usually 1 or 3 years.

- **Compute Savings Plans** are the most flexible and can apply across EC2, Fargate, and Lambda.
- **EC2 Instance Savings Plans** are narrower and apply to a specific instance family in a region.
- Savings Plans generally do not cover every AWS service, so do not assume they replace all reservations.

For exam scenarios:

- Choose **On-Demand** for spiky, unknown, or temporary demand.
- Choose **Spot** for batch, CI/CD, container, analytics, or stateless processing that can restart.
- Choose **RIs** or **Savings Plans** for a stable baseline.
- Use **Convertible RIs** when you expect architecture changes but still want commitment savings.

> Be careful: a commitment only saves money if you actually use it. Overcommitting can increase waste.

**Compute cost optimization**

EC2 is often the first place to optimize because it is easy to overprovision. The exam expects you to know both instance selection and purchasing strategy.

Important tactics:

- **Right-size instances** by reviewing CPU, memory, network, and disk utilization.
- **Use Graviton-based instances** when compatible, since they often provide better price-performance.
- **Use Auto Scaling** to match capacity to demand.
- **Use Spot capacity** for interruption-tolerant workloads.
- **Use mixed purchasing strategies** for resilience and savings.

For cost-optimized architectures, **EC2 Auto Scaling with Mixed Instances** is a common pattern. You define a launch template and let the group use multiple instance types, sizes, and purchase options. This improves flexibility and can reduce cost by allowing the group to choose cheaper capacity when available.

A typical strategy is:

- Keep a small On-Demand baseline for stability.
- Add Spot Instances for burst capacity.
- Use multiple instance families and sizes to improve Spot availability.
- Spread across Availability Zones for resilience.

Example Auto Scaling group configuration concept:

```json
{
  "AutoScalingGroupName": "web-asg",
  "MixedInstancesPolicy": {
    "LaunchTemplate": {
      "LaunchTemplateSpecification": {
        "LaunchTemplateId": "lt-0123456789abcdef0",
        "Version": "$Latest"
      },
      "Overrides": [
        { "InstanceType": "m7g.large" },
        { "InstanceType": "m6i.large" },
        { "InstanceType": "t4g.large" }
      ]
    },
    "InstancesDistribution": {
      "OnDemandBaseCapacity": 2,
      "OnDemandPercentageAboveBaseCapacity": 20,
      "SpotAllocationStrategy": "capacity-optimized"
    }
  }
}
```

The `capacity-optimized` strategy is often a strong choice for Spot because it prefers pools with more available capacity, which can reduce interruption risk.

**Spot Fleet** is another way to request Spot capacity across multiple instance types and pools. In modern architectures, Auto Scaling with Mixed Instances is often the more common exam answer for EC2 fleets, but Spot Fleet may still appear in older or specialized scenarios. Use Spot when the workload can be interrupted and resumed, such as:

- Batch processing
- Stateless web tiers behind a load balancer
- CI runners
- Big data jobs
- Container workloads with retry logic

**Lambda cost optimization**

Lambda is usually cost-effective for event-driven workloads, but the exam expects you to understand the tradeoffs.

Cost drivers include:

- **Invocations**
- **Duration**
- **Memory size**
- **Provisioned concurrency**
- **Data transfer and downstream service usage**

Memory sizing matters because Lambda allocates CPU proportionally with memory. A function with too little memory may run longer and cost more overall. A function with too much memory may waste money if the runtime does not benefit from the extra CPU.

Practical guidance:

- Benchmark different memory settings with real workloads.
- Use the shortest practical timeout.
- Keep functions small and focused.
- Avoid unnecessary cold-start overhead where latency matters.
- Use **Provisioned Concurrency** only when you need predictable low latency, because it adds cost even when idle.

Example CLI to tune memory:

```bash
aws lambda update-function-configuration \
  --function-name image-resize \
  --memory-size 1024 \
  --timeout 15
```

A common exam tradeoff is:

- Use **on-demand Lambda** for irregular traffic and event processing.
- Use **Provisioned Concurrency** for latency-sensitive APIs or user-facing functions with strict response-time requirements.
- Avoid provisioned concurrency for low-traffic functions where cold starts are acceptable.

**Storage cost optimization**

Storage questions often test whether you can match durability, access frequency, and retention requirements to the right service or class.

**Amazon S3** is the main object storage service and offers multiple storage classes:

- **S3 Standard** for frequent access.
- **S3 Intelligent-Tiering** for unknown or changing access patterns.
- **S3 Standard-IA** and **One Zone-IA** for infrequent access.
- **S3 Glacier Instant Retrieval**, **Flexible Retrieval**, and **Deep Archive** for archival data.

Important nuances:

- **Intelligent-Tiering** adds monitoring and automation fees for objects that are eligible for tiering. It is useful when access patterns are unknown or change over time.
- **Glacier retrieval options** differ in speed and cost:
  - **Instant Retrieval** for archive data that still needs millisecond access.
  - **Flexible Retrieval** for archives that can tolerate minutes to hours.
  - **Deep Archive** for the lowest storage cost with the slowest retrieval.
- **Lifecycle policies** automate transitions and expirations, which is often the best answer for long-term cost control.
- **Object Lock** is a retention feature for compliance or backup protection, not a cost-saving feature by itself. However, it can be important when you need immutable backups and want to prevent accidental deletion.

Example lifecycle configuration:

```json
{
  "Rules": [
    {
      "ID": "archive-old-logs",
      "Status": "Enabled",
      "Filter": { "Prefix": "logs/" },
      "Transitions": [
        { "Days": 30, "StorageClass": "STANDARD_IA" },
        { "Days": 90, "StorageClass": "GLACIER" }
      ],
      "Expiration": { "Days": 365 }
    }
  ]
}
```

Use S3 lifecycle policies when data has a predictable aging pattern, such as logs, backups, and media archives. Use Intelligent-Tiering when you cannot predict access frequency and want AWS to move objects automatically.

**EBS, EFS, and FSx cost optimization**

Block and file storage choices can have a large cost impact.

**Amazon EBS** optimization usually means:

- Choosing the right volume type, such as `gp3` instead of older `gp2` when appropriate.
- Matching IOPS and throughput to actual needs.
- Deleting unattached volumes and old snapshots.
- Using snapshot lifecycle management for retention control.

**Amazon EFS** is shared file storage and is often more expensive than EBS for the same raw capacity, but it provides elastic, multi-AZ access. Use it when multiple instances or containers need shared POSIX file storage.

Cost optimization ideas for EFS:

- Use **EFS Standard** for frequent access and **EFS Infrequent Access** for files that are rarely read.
- Apply lifecycle policies to move cold files to IA automatically.
- Avoid using EFS when a simpler object store or block volume is sufficient.

**Amazon FSx** has multiple variants, and the right choice depends on workload:

- **FSx for Windows File Server** for Windows-based shared file systems.
- **FSx for Lustre** for high-performance compute and HPC workloads.
- **FSx for NetApp ONTAP** for advanced storage features and hybrid use cases.
- **FSx for OpenZFS** for Linux-based workloads needing ZFS features.

Cost guidance:

- Use FSx only when the workload needs its specific file-system features.
- For HPC or analytics, FSx for Lustre can be cost-effective when high throughput is required for short periods.
- For general shared file storage, compare EFS and FSx based on protocol, performance, and operational requirements.

**Database cost optimization**

Database cost questions often focus on matching capacity model and access pattern.

**Amazon DynamoDB** has several cost levers:

- **On-demand capacity mode** is best for unpredictable or spiky traffic.
- **Provisioned capacity mode** is cheaper when traffic is predictable.
- **Auto scaling** helps provisioned tables adjust capacity automatically.
- **DAX (DynamoDB Accelerator)** can reduce read pressure and latency for read-heavy workloads, but it adds cost and is only useful when caching improves overall efficiency.
- **Table design** affects cost because poor partition key design can create hot partitions and force overprovisioning.

Practical guidance:

- Use on-demand for new applications with unknown traffic.
- Switch to provisioned when usage becomes stable and predictable.
- Use auto scaling to avoid manual capacity tuning.
- Design keys to distribute traffic evenly.
- Use sparse indexes and avoid unnecessary global secondary indexes because each index adds storage and write cost.

Example CLI for provisioned capacity:

```bash
aws dynamodb update-table \
  --table-name Orders \
  --provisioned-throughput ReadCapacityUnits=50,WriteCapacityUnits=20
```

For read-heavy workloads, DAX can reduce repeated reads from DynamoDB, but it is not a universal cost saver. If the workload is write-heavy or has low cache hit rates, DAX may add cost without enough benefit.

**RDS and Aurora cost considerations**

Relational databases are often optimized by choosing the right deployment model and storage.

Common tactics:

- Use the smallest instance class that meets performance needs.
- Turn off nonproduction databases when not in use.
- Use **Reserved Instances** for steady-state database workloads.
- Choose storage and IOPS carefully for performance-sensitive workloads.
- Consider **Aurora Serverless** for variable or intermittent workloads when supported by the scenario.

For exam purposes, remember that managed databases reduce operational overhead, but they are not automatically the cheapest option. The right answer depends on availability, maintenance, and scaling requirements.

**Data transfer and architecture choices**

Data transfer can become a hidden cost. The exam may ask you to reduce spend by changing architecture rather than just changing instance size.

Watch for:

- Cross-AZ traffic charges
- Cross-Region replication costs
- Internet egress charges
- NAT Gateway data processing charges
- Unnecessary data movement between services

Optimization patterns:

- Keep chatty components in the same Availability Zone when resilience requirements allow.
- Use VPC endpoints for private access to AWS services instead of routing through NAT Gateway.
- Cache frequently accessed content with CloudFront.
- Compress and batch data where appropriate.

Example VPC endpoint use case:

```bash
aws ec2 create-vpc-endpoint \
  --vpc-id vpc-0123456789abcdef0 \
  --service-name com.amazonaws.us-east-1.s3 \
  --route-table-ids rtb-0123456789abcdef0
```

This can reduce NAT Gateway usage for S3 access and improve both cost and security.

**Monitoring, budgets, and anomaly detection**

Cost control is not complete without alerting. AWS provides several tools that work together.

**AWS Cost Explorer** helps you analyze historical spend and trends.

**AWS Budgets** lets you set thresholds for cost, usage, RI coverage, and Savings Plans coverage.

**Cost Anomaly Detection** uses machine learning to identify unusual spend patterns and notify you when costs deviate from expected behavior.

A practical workflow is:

- Use Cost Explorer to identify the main cost drivers.
- Use Budgets to enforce thresholds and trigger actions.
- Use Cost Anomaly Detection to catch unexpected spikes early.
- Route alerts to email, SNS, or operational workflows.

Example budget with notifications:

```json
{
  "BudgetName": "monthly-prod-budget",
  "BudgetLimit": {
    "Amount": "5000",
    "Unit": "USD"
  },
  "TimeUnit": "MONTHLY",
  "BudgetType": "COST",
  "CostFilters": {
    "TagKeyValue": ["Environment$Prod"]
  },
  "NotificationsWithSubscribers": [
    {
      "Notification": {
        "NotificationType": "ACTUAL",
        "ComparisonOperator": "GREATER_THAN",
        "Threshold": 80,
        "ThresholdType": "PERCENTAGE"
      },
      "Subscribers": [
        {
          "SubscriptionType": "EMAIL",
          "Address": "finops@example.com"
        }
      ]
    }
  ]
}
```

Budgets can also trigger actions, such as applying an IAM policy or stopping resources through automation. In exam scenarios, this is useful when you need to prevent runaway spend rather than only report it after the fact.

Example SNS-based alerting pattern:

```bash
aws sns create-topic --name cost-alerts
aws budgets create-budget --account-id 123456789012 --budget file://budget.json
```

A common operational pattern is to send budget alerts to SNS, then fan out to email, chat, or a ticketing system. For more advanced governance, pair alerts with Lambda automation that tags, notifies, or stops noncritical resources.

**Practical decision patterns**

When you see a cost optimization question, ask:

1. Is the workload steady or variable?
2. Can it tolerate interruption?
3. Is the cost problem compute, storage, database, or data transfer?
4. Can automation reduce waste?
5. Do you need governance, reporting, or chargeback?

Typical exam mappings:

- **Steady EC2 baseline** → Reserved Instances or Savings Plans.
- **Burst capacity** → Spot Instances with Auto Scaling.
- **Unknown S3 access** → Intelligent-Tiering.
- **Long-term archive** → Glacier classes with lifecycle transitions.
- **Shared file storage** → EFS or FSx only when required by the workload.
- **Predictable DynamoDB traffic** → Provisioned capacity with auto scaling.
- **Latency-sensitive Lambda** → Provisioned concurrency only when justified.
- **Unexpected spend spike** → Cost Anomaly Detection plus Budgets.

**Common exam traps**

- Choosing the cheapest service without checking availability or performance requirements.
- Using Spot for workloads that cannot restart safely.
- Overlooking NAT Gateway, cross-AZ, or cross-Region transfer costs.
- Assuming Savings Plans cover every service.
- Using Intelligent-Tiering for very small objects or workloads where monitoring fees outweigh savings.
- Keeping old snapshots, unattached EBS volumes, or unused databases.
- Selecting DAX when the workload is not read-heavy enough to benefit.
- Ignoring business reporting needs when account structure does not match chargeback requirements.

**Quick reference**

- **Cost Categories** organize spend by business dimension.
- **Consolidated billing** centralizes charges across AWS Organizations.
- **Savings Plans** are flexible commitments, but scope matters.
- **RIs** are best for stable baselines, with Standard and Convertible tradeoffs.
- **Spot** is for interruption-tolerant workloads.
- **Mixed Instances** improve EC2 flexibility and Spot usage.
- **Lifecycle policies** are essential for S3 cost control.
- **EFS IA** and FSx selection depend on file access and protocol needs.
- **DynamoDB** cost depends heavily on capacity mode and key design.
- **Lambda** cost is shaped by memory, duration, and concurrency choices.
- **Budgets** and **Cost Anomaly Detection** provide alerting and governance.
