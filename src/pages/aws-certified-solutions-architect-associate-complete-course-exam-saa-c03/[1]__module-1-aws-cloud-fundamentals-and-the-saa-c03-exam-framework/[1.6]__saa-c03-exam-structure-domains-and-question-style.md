## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Associate Complete Course (Exam SAA-C03)

**Exam overview**

The AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Associate exam validates your ability to design secure, resilient, high-performing, and cost-effective architectures on AWS. SAA-C03 is a scenario-based exam: you are rarely asked for isolated facts, and you are usually asked to choose the best architecture for a business requirement.

The exam emphasizes tradeoffs. You must compare services, understand managed vs. self-managed options, and choose the solution that best fits constraints such as availability, latency, operational overhead, security, and cost.

**Current exam structure**

AWS exam details can change over time, so always verify the latest registration page before scheduling. For study planning, the commonly published structure is:

- **Format:** multiple choice and multiple response
- **Question count:** 65 total questions
- **Time limit:** 130 minutes
- **Delivery:** testing center or online proctoring
- **Scoring:** scaled score, not a raw percentage
- **Passing result:** AWS reports pass/fail with a scaled score; it does not publish a simple percentage threshold

A practical pacing target is about 2 minutes per question on average, with a little time reserved for review. That means you should not spend too long on any single scenario early in the exam.

> The exam may include unscored items, but AWS does not always publish a fixed number for every version. Do not memorize an outdated pilot-question count as a guaranteed fact.

**Official domain breakdown**

SAA-C03 is organized into four domains. The exact weights are important because they tell you where to spend your study time.

| Domain | Focus | Weight |
|---|---|---:|
| Domain 1 | Design secure architectures | 30% |
| Domain 2 | Design resilient architectures | 26% |
| Domain 3 | Design high-performing architectures | 24% |
| Domain 4 | Design cost-optimized architectures | 20% |

These weights are a study guide, not a guarantee of exact question counts. Still, they are useful for prioritizing review time. Security and resilience together make up more than half the exam, so they deserve extra attention.

**What each domain really tests**

**Domain 1: Design secure architectures**

This domain checks whether you can protect data, identities, networks, and workloads using AWS-native controls. Expect questions about least privilege, encryption, key management, secrets handling, network segmentation, and logging.

Common topics include:

- IAM (Identity and Access Management) roles, policies, and permission boundaries
- KMS (Key Management Service) for encryption keys
- Secrets Manager vs. Parameter Store for secret storage
- Security groups, NACLs (network ACLs), and private subnets
- WAF (Web Application Firewall), Shield, and edge protection
- CloudTrail, Config, and centralized logging

**Domain 2: Design resilient architectures**

This domain focuses on fault tolerance, recovery, and high availability. You are expected to choose designs that survive failures without excessive manual intervention.

Common topics include:

- Multi-AZ deployments
- Auto Scaling groups and load balancers
- S3 durability and replication patterns
- RDS Multi-AZ vs. read replicas
- Backup and restore strategies
- Route 53 health checks and failover routing
- Decoupling with SQS, SNS, and EventBridge

**Domain 3: Design high-performing architectures**

This domain tests whether you can meet latency, throughput, and scaling requirements. The key is to match the workload to the right storage, compute, and networking service.

Common topics include:

- EC2 instance families and placement considerations
- EBS volume types and performance tradeoffs
- S3 performance patterns and multipart upload
- CloudFront and edge caching
- ElastiCache for read-heavy workloads
- DynamoDB partitioning and access patterns
- Aurora and RDS performance features

**Domain 4: Design cost-optimized architectures**

This domain asks you to reduce cost without breaking requirements. The best answer is not always the cheapest service; it is the cheapest service that still satisfies the business need.

Common topics include:

- Right-sizing compute
- Savings Plans and Reserved Instances
- S3 storage classes and lifecycle policies
- Spot Instances for interruptible workloads
- Managed services that reduce operational overhead
- Data transfer and cross-AZ cost awareness
- Choosing serverless only when it fits the workload

**Question style you should expect**

SAA-C03 questions are usually written as short business scenarios. They often include one or more of the following:

- A business goal, such as reducing latency or improving availability
- A constraint, such as minimal operational overhead or limited budget
- A workload description, such as batch processing, web hosting, or analytics
- A failure mode, such as an AZ outage or database bottleneck
- A security requirement, such as encryption or private connectivity

The wording often includes distractors that are technically valid but do not satisfy the full requirement. Your job is to identify the best fit, not just a possible fit.

**Common prompt patterns**

- **Architecture selection:** “Which solution best meets the requirements?”
- **Troubleshooting:** “What should the architect do to resolve the issue?”
- **Optimization:** “How can the company reduce cost while maintaining performance?”
- **Migration:** “What is the best approach to move the workload to AWS?”
- **Security hardening:** “How should the team protect sensitive data?”

You may also see references to diagrams, even if the diagram is described in text. Read those prompts as topology questions: where traffic enters, where data is stored, and which components are public or private.

**How multiple-response questions work**

Multiple-response questions ask you to choose more than one correct answer. Treat them carefully because partial correctness is not enough.

A good strategy is:

- Identify the exact requirement first.
- Eliminate answers that violate any hard constraint.
- Prefer answers that solve the problem with the least operational complexity.
- Check whether the selected options work together as a complete design.

For example, if a question asks for a highly available web application, selecting only an Auto Scaling group is incomplete if there is no load balancer or multi-AZ design. The answer set must satisfy the full scenario.

**How to approach elimination**

Use a disciplined elimination process instead of guessing from keywords.

1. Read the last sentence first to find the actual ask.
2. Highlight constraints such as “least operational overhead,” “lowest cost,” or “no public internet access.”
3. Remove answers that solve the wrong problem.
4. Remove answers that are technically possible but operationally heavy.
5. Compare the remaining options against the business requirement.

A useful mental rule is: the exam usually rewards the simplest architecture that fully meets the requirement.

**Practical examples of distractor elimination**

**Example 1: Secrets management**

A company wants to store application secrets securely and rotate them automatically.

- **Best fit:** AWS Secrets Manager
- Why: it is designed for secret storage and supports rotation workflows.
- Distractor: Systems Manager Parameter Store can store secure strings, but automatic rotation is not its primary feature.

If the question emphasizes rotation, Secrets Manager is usually the stronger answer.

**Example 2: Web application hosting**

A startup needs to host a traditional web application with unpredictable traffic and minimal administration.

- **Possible answers:** Elastic Beanstalk, ECS on Fargate, EC2 Auto Scaling, Lambda
- **Best fit depends on the app**
  - If it is a standard web app with long-running processes or a conventional runtime, Elastic Beanstalk or ECS/Fargate is often more appropriate.
  - If it is a simple event-driven API or lightweight backend, Lambda may fit.
- Why: Lambda is not always the default best answer for a web app, especially when the workload needs persistent processes, specific runtime behavior, or more conventional deployment patterns.

**Example 3: Database availability**

A company needs a relational database with automatic failover in a single region.

- **Best fit:** RDS Multi-AZ
- Why: Multi-AZ provides synchronous standby replication and automatic failover.
- Distractor: read replicas improve read scaling, but they are not the primary high-availability solution.

**Example 4: Static content delivery**

A global audience needs faster access to static assets.

- **Best fit:** S3 with CloudFront
- Why: CloudFront caches content at edge locations and reduces latency.
- Distractor: S3 alone is durable and scalable, but it does not provide edge caching.

**Exam logistics that affect pacing**

The 130-minute time limit means you must manage time actively. If you average too long per question, you will run out of time on the harder scenarios.

A practical pacing plan:

- First pass: answer easy and medium questions quickly.
- Mark difficult questions and move on.
- Second pass: return to marked questions with fresh context.
- Final pass: review only if time remains.

Do not let one long scenario consume several minutes unless you are close to a confident answer. The exam is designed to reward broad competence, not perfection on every item.

**Scenario language and clue words**

Certain words often point toward a service family or design pattern. Use them as clues, but do not rely on them alone.

- **High availability** often suggests Multi-AZ, load balancing, and failover
- **Low latency** often suggests caching, edge delivery, or in-memory data stores
- **Burst traffic** often suggests Auto Scaling, serverless, or managed elasticity
- **Private access** often suggests VPC endpoints, private subnets, or Direct Connect
- **Automatic rotation** often suggests Secrets Manager rather than Parameter Store
- **Batch processing** often suggests decoupling, queues, or Spot Instances
- **Durability** often suggests S3, backups, or replication
- **Cost reduction** often suggests right-sizing, lifecycle policies, or managed services

**CLI and configuration examples for exam context**

You do not need to memorize every command for the exam, but practical familiarity helps you understand how AWS services are actually configured.

```bash
# Inspect an EC2 instance profile and attached IAM role
aws ec2 describe-instances --instance-ids i-0123456789abcdef0 \
  --query 'Reservations[0].Instances[0].IamInstanceProfile'

# Check S3 bucket versioning
aws s3api get-bucket-versioning --bucket my-app-bucket

# View CloudTrail trails
aws cloudtrail describe-trails

# List KMS keys
aws kms list-keys
```

A simple CloudFormation example for a highly available web tier might look like this:

```yaml
Resources:
  WebAsg:
    Type: AWS::AutoScaling::AutoScalingGroup
    Properties:
      MinSize: "2"
      MaxSize: "6"
      VPCZoneIdentifier:
        - subnet-aaa
        - subnet-bbb
      TargetGroupARNs:
        - !Ref WebTargetGroup

  WebAlb:
    Type: AWS::ElasticLoadBalancingV2::LoadBalancer
    Properties:
      Scheme: internet-facing
      Subnets:
        - subnet-aaa
        - subnet-bbb
```

This kind of topology is common in exam questions because it combines resilience, scaling, and traffic distribution.

**How to think about architecture tradeoffs**

The exam often compares services that overlap. Your answer should reflect the workload’s real needs.

- **EC2** gives you the most control, but you manage more.
- **ECS/Fargate** reduces container management overhead.
- **Lambda** removes server management, but it is best for event-driven or short-lived execution patterns.
- **RDS** is a managed relational database, while **DynamoDB** is a managed NoSQL database with different access patterns.
- **S3** is object storage, not a file system or block device.
- **EBS** is block storage attached to EC2, not shared object storage.
- **CloudFront** improves global delivery, but it is not a database cache.
- **ElastiCache** improves read performance for supported caching use cases, but it is not a primary data store.

**A simple decision framework**

When you face a scenario, ask yourself:

1. What is the workload type: web, batch, analytics, transactional, or event-driven?
2. What is the primary constraint: cost, availability, performance, or security?
3. What is the simplest AWS service that satisfies the requirement?
4. Does the answer preserve the required level of operational simplicity?
5. Does it fail gracefully under the stated failure scenario?

This framework helps you avoid answers that are technically correct but architecturally incomplete.

**Study priorities by domain**

If you are planning your prep, focus on the following order:

- **Security:** IAM, KMS, Secrets Manager, VPC security, logging
- **Resilience:** Multi-AZ, backups, replication, failover, decoupling
- **Performance:** caching, storage performance, scaling, content delivery
- **Cost:** storage classes, compute pricing models, right-sizing, managed services

That order reflects both the exam weighting and the fact that security and resilience often appear inside other domains as hidden constraints.

**Quick exam-day reminders**

- Read the final line of the question carefully.
- Eliminate answers that violate a hard requirement.
- Prefer managed services when they meet the need.
- Do not assume the cheapest option is the best answer.
- Do not assume Lambda is always the best default for application hosting.
- Do not confuse secret storage with secret rotation.
- Use your first pass to secure easy points and preserve time.

**Mini practice set**

**Question 1**

A company needs a highly available relational database with automatic failover in one region.

- A. RDS Multi-AZ
- B. RDS read replica
- C. DynamoDB global table
- D. S3 versioning

**Answer:** A

**Why:** Multi-AZ is the standard HA choice for a relational database in one region. Read replicas help reads, DynamoDB is not relational, and S3 is not a database.

**Question 2**

A global website serves static images and CSS files to users worldwide.

- A. EC2 Auto Scaling
- B. CloudFront with S3 origin
- C. RDS Multi-AZ
- D. ElastiCache only

**Answer:** B

**Why:** CloudFront caches content at edge locations and S3 stores the static objects durably.

**Question 3**

A team wants to store application secrets and rotate them automatically.

- A. S3
- B. Systems Manager Parameter Store
- C. AWS Secrets Manager
- D. EBS

**Answer:** C

**Why:** Secrets Manager is built for secrets and rotation. Parameter Store can store secure values, but automatic rotation is not its core feature.

**Question 4**

A workload has unpredictable traffic and needs minimal server management. It is a conventional web app with long-running processes.

- A. Lambda
- B. Elastic Beanstalk or ECS/Fargate
- C. S3 only
- D. Glacier

**Answer:** B

**Why:** Traditional web apps often fit managed application platforms or containers better than Lambda.

**Final exam mindset**

SAA-C03 rewards architectural judgment. You are not just memorizing services; you are learning how AWS services fit together under real constraints. If you can identify the workload, the constraint, and the simplest valid design, you will answer many questions correctly even when the wording is unfamiliar.
