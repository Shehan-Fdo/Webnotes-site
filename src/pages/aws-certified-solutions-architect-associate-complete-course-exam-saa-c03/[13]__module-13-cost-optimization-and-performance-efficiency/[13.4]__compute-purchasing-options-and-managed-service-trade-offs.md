## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Associate Complete Course (Exam SAA-C03)

**Compute purchasing options: the exam decision lens**

AWS compute questions usually test whether you can match workload constraints to the right pricing model, tenancy model, or managed service. The key is to separate three ideas:

- **Cost optimization**: reduce spend for steady or flexible usage.
- **Capacity assurance**: make sure compute is available when you need it.
- **Operational burden**: decide how much infrastructure you want to manage.

A common exam trap is assuming the cheapest option is always the best. In practice, you choose based on duration, predictability, interruption tolerance, licensing, and how much control you need.

**EC2 On-Demand, Reserved Instances, and Savings Plans**

<dl>
<dt><strong>On-Demand</strong></dt>
<dd>Pay for compute by the second for many Linux and Windows EC2 instances, with a 60-second minimum. Use it for short-term, unpredictable, or spiky workloads.</dd>
<dt><strong>Reserved Instances (RIs)</strong></dt>
<dd>Commit to a specific EC2 configuration for a discount. Standard RIs offer the deepest discount but limited flexibility; Convertible RIs trade some discount for exchange flexibility.</dd>
<dt><strong>Savings Plans</strong></dt>
<dd>Commit to a spend amount per hour for a billing discount. They are not capacity reservations and are more flexible than RIs.</dd>
</dl>

On-Demand is the default choice when you need no commitment. It is also the safest answer when the workload is new, uncertain, or expected to change quickly. For exam purposes, remember that On-Demand pricing is not a reservation mechanism; it only affects billing.

Reserved Instances and Savings Plans both reduce cost through commitment, but they behave differently:

- **Reserved Instances** are tied to instance attributes or family scope, depending on type.
- **Savings Plans** are tied to spend commitment, not a specific instance reservation.
- **Neither** is a substitute for capacity planning unless you are specifically using a Zonal RI for capacity reservation.

> **Critical nuance:** Regional Reserved Instances provide billing discounts and size flexibility, but they do not reserve capacity in an Availability Zone. Only Zonal Reserved Instances provide capacity reservation.

**Reserved Instances in detail**

Reserved Instances are best when you have steady-state usage and can predict the instance family, platform, and region. They are commonly used for production systems with long-lived baseline demand.

Key exam points:

- **Standard RIs**
  - Offer the highest discount potential.
  - Are best for stable workloads.
  - Have limited modification and exchange flexibility.
- **Convertible RIs**
  - Offer less discount than Standard RIs in exchange for more flexibility.
  - Can be exchanged for other Convertible RIs with different attributes.
  - Suit environments that may change instance family, OS, or tenancy over time.
- **Regional RIs**
  - Apply discounts across a region.
  - Provide size flexibility within the same instance family.
  - Do not reserve capacity.
- **Zonal RIs**
  - Apply to a specific Availability Zone.
  - Provide capacity reservation in that AZ.
  - Are useful when you must guarantee capacity in a particular zone.

A practical way to think about RIs is:

- Choose **Standard RI** when the workload is stable and you want maximum savings.
- Choose **Convertible RI** when you expect the workload to evolve.
- Choose **Zonal RI** when you need both discounting and capacity reservation in a specific AZ.
- Choose **Regional RI** when you want discounting with more flexibility but no capacity guarantee.

Example scenario:

- A company runs a fixed fleet of `m6i.large` instances for a customer portal 24/7.
- The architecture is stable and the region is unlikely to change.
- A **Standard Regional RI** is often a strong fit if capacity reservation is not required.
- If the team must guarantee capacity in `us-east-1a`, a **Zonal RI** is the better exam answer.

**Savings Plans: when flexibility matters**

Savings Plans are a billing discount commitment. You commit to a dollar amount per hour for one or three years, and AWS applies the discount automatically to eligible usage.

Two common types matter for SAA-C03:

- **Compute Savings Plans**
  - Apply to EC2, AWS Fargate, and AWS Lambda.
  - Offer the most flexibility across instance families, sizes, regions, operating systems, and tenancy.
  - Are useful when your compute mix may change.
- **EC2 Instance Savings Plans**
  - Apply only to EC2 usage in a specific instance family within a region.
  - Usually provide stronger savings for that narrower commitment than Compute Savings Plans for the same committed usage, but the exact outcome depends on the workload and commitment.
  - Are a good fit when you know the instance family and region will stay consistent.

Important distinctions:

- Savings Plans are **not** capacity reservations.
- Savings Plans are **billing discounts**, not resource locks.
- Savings Plans are generally easier to manage than RIs because AWS applies them automatically to matching usage.
- Compute Savings Plans are the most flexible option when you expect instance type changes, migrations, or mixed compute services.

A useful exam heuristic:

- If the workload is stable and specific, consider **EC2 Instance Savings Plans** or **Standard RIs**.
- If the workload is stable but you want flexibility, consider **Compute Savings Plans**.
- If you need capacity in a specific AZ, consider a **Zonal RI** instead.

**On-Demand vs RI vs Savings Plans decision table**

| Requirement | Best fit | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Short-lived or unpredictable workload | On-Demand | No commitment, easy to start and stop |
| Stable EC2 baseline, maximum discount | Standard RI | Strong discount for predictable usage |
| Stable EC2 baseline, but future changes likely | Convertible RI | Exchange flexibility |
| Need capacity in a specific AZ | Zonal RI | Provides capacity reservation |
| Want billing discount across EC2, Fargate, and Lambda | Compute Savings Plans | Broadest flexibility |
| Know EC2 family and region, want better discount | EC2 Instance Savings Plans | Narrower commitment, often stronger savings for that scope |

**Spot Instances and interruption-aware design**

Spot Instances let you use spare EC2 capacity at a steep discount, but AWS can reclaim the capacity with short notice. They are ideal for fault-tolerant, flexible, or batch-oriented workloads.

Use Spot for:

- Batch processing
- Distributed data processing
- CI/CD build workers
- Stateless web tiers behind a load balancer
- Big data and rendering jobs
- Queue-based asynchronous processing

Avoid Spot for:

- Stateful databases
- Strictly latency-sensitive single-instance workloads
- Systems that cannot tolerate interruption
- Licensing or compliance scenarios that require guaranteed runtime

Common exam patterns:

- **EC2 Auto Scaling with Spot**
  - Use mixed instances policies.
  - Combine On-Demand and Spot capacity.
  - Use allocation strategies such as `capacity-optimized` or `price-capacity-optimized` to improve availability.
- **Spot Fleet**
  - Useful when you want to request a pool of Spot capacity across instance types and Availability Zones.
  - Helps diversify capacity and reduce interruption risk.
- **Interruption handling**
  - Design for graceful shutdown.
  - Use instance termination notices to drain work.
  - Persist state externally in S3, EBS snapshots, DynamoDB, or SQS depending on the workload.
  - For some workloads, hibernation or stop behavior may be appropriate if supported, but termination is the most common interruption outcome to plan for.

Example Auto Scaling group configuration pattern:

```json
{
  "MixedInstancesPolicy": {
    "LaunchTemplate": {
      "LaunchTemplateSpecification": {
        "LaunchTemplateId": "lt-0123456789abcdef0",
        "Version": "$Latest"
      },
      "Overrides": [
        { "InstanceType": "m6i.large" },
        { "InstanceType": "m6a.large" },
        { "InstanceType": "c6i.large" }
      ]
    },
    "InstancesDistribution": {
      "OnDemandBaseCapacity": 1,
      "OnDemandPercentageAboveBaseCapacity": 20,
      "SpotAllocationStrategy": "capacity-optimized"
    }
  }
}
```

This pattern is useful when you want a small guaranteed baseline and cheaper burst capacity from Spot.

**Dedicated Instances vs Dedicated Hosts**

Dedicated tenancy is about isolation and licensing, not just cost.

- **Dedicated Instances**
  - Run on hardware dedicated to a single customer.
  - Provide instance-level isolation from other AWS customers.
  - Are useful when you need dedicated tenancy but do not need host-level visibility.
- **Dedicated Hosts**
  - Give you an entire physical server dedicated to your account.
  - Expose host-level control and visibility.
  - Are often required for certain BYOL (Bring Your Own License) scenarios, especially when licensing depends on physical cores, sockets, or host affinity.

Exam guidance:

- Choose **Dedicated Instances** when you need dedicated hardware but not host control.
- Choose **Dedicated Hosts** when licensing or compliance requires visibility into the physical server or host affinity.
- Do not confuse dedicated tenancy with capacity reservation or cost optimization; it is primarily about isolation and licensing.

**Container trade-offs: ECS, EKS, and Fargate**

Containers reduce packaging friction, but the right service depends on how much orchestration control you need and how much infrastructure you want to manage.

| Service | Control plane | Node management | Kubernetes compatibility | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ECS | AWS-managed | Optional if using EC2; none with Fargate | No | Simple AWS-native container orchestration |
| EKS | AWS-managed Kubernetes control plane | You manage nodes unless using Fargate or managed node groups | Yes | Teams that need Kubernetes APIs and ecosystem |
| Fargate | AWS-managed | No node management | No direct Kubernetes control plane by itself; can run with ECS or EKS | Serverless containers with minimal ops |

Key trade-offs:

- **ECS**
  - Simpler than EKS.
  - Good when you want AWS-native orchestration without Kubernetes complexity.
  - Supports both EC2 launch type and Fargate launch type.
- **EKS**
  - Best when you need Kubernetes compatibility, portability, or existing Kubernetes tooling.
  - Adds operational complexity compared with ECS.
  - Requires you to think about cluster control plane plus worker nodes or Fargate profiles.
- **Fargate**
  - Removes node management.
  - Works well for many stateless microservices and batch tasks.
  - Is not suitable when you need host-level access, custom kernel modules, privileged workloads, or certain daemon-style patterns that require direct node control.

**ECS launch types and scheduling concepts**

ECS has two main launch types:

- **EC2 launch type**
  - You manage the EC2 instances in the cluster.
  - Good when you want more control over instance selection, daemon tasks, or custom host configuration.
  - Can be cost-effective for steady workloads with good bin packing.
- **Fargate launch type**
  - AWS runs the infrastructure for you.
  - Good when you want to focus on tasks and services rather than servers.
  - Simplifies scaling and operations.

ECS scheduling concepts:

- **Service**
  - Keeps a desired number of tasks running.
  - Best for long-lived web APIs or microservices.
- **Task**
  - A running instance of a task definition.
  - Useful for one-off jobs or batch processing.
- **Task definition**
  - The blueprint for containers, CPU, memory, ports, environment variables, and IAM roles.

Practical ECS example:

```bash
aws ecs create-cluster --cluster-name app-cluster
aws ecs register-task-definition --cli-input-json file://task-def.json
aws ecs create-service \
  --cluster app-cluster \
  --service-name web-service \
  --task-definition web-task \
  --desired-count 2 \
  --launch-type FARGATE
```

This works only if networking, subnets, security groups, and the task definition are already configured. In exam scenarios, remember that creating a service is not the same as creating the entire runtime environment.

**EKS basics and managed node groups**

EKS is the AWS-managed Kubernetes service. The control plane is managed by AWS, but you still need worker capacity.

Typical flow:

1. Create the EKS cluster control plane.
2. Add worker capacity with managed node groups, self-managed nodes, or Fargate profiles.
3. Deploy Kubernetes manifests, Helm charts, or operators.

Example:

```bash
aws eks create-cluster \
  --name prod-cluster \
  --role-arn arn:aws:iam::123456789012:role/EKSClusterRole \
  --resources-vpc-config subnetIds=subnet-aaa,subnet-bbb,securityGroupIds=sg-12345678

aws eks create-nodegroup \
  --cluster-name prod-cluster \
  --nodegroup-name ng-1 \
  --subnets subnet-aaa subnet-bbb \
  --node-role arn:aws:iam::123456789012:role/EKSNodeRole \
  --scaling-config minSize=2,maxSize=6,desiredSize=3
```

Important exam nuance:

- `aws eks create-cluster` creates the control plane, not the worker nodes.
- Managed node groups are created separately.
- EKS is the right answer when Kubernetes compatibility is a requirement, not merely when you want containers.

**Lambda vs containers vs EC2**

A common SAA-C03 question is whether you should use Lambda, containers, or EC2 for a workload.

- **Lambda**
  - Best for event-driven, short-running, stateless functions.
  - Minimal operational overhead.
  - Good for glue code, API backends, stream processing, and automation.
  - Not ideal for long-running processes, custom OS needs, or workloads with tight runtime constraints.
- **Containers**
  - Best when you need packaging consistency, multiple services, or more control over runtime dependencies.
  - Good for microservices, batch jobs, and portable application stacks.
  - More operational overhead than Lambda, less than raw EC2 if using Fargate.
- **EC2**
  - Best when you need full OS control, custom agents, special networking, GPU/accelerator access, or legacy applications.
  - Highest operational burden among the three.
  - Useful when the application cannot be adapted to serverless or container patterns.

Decision cues:

- Choose **Lambda** when the workload is event-driven and fits the execution model.
- Choose **Fargate** when you want containers without managing servers.
- Choose **ECS on EC2** when you need container orchestration plus host control.
- Choose **EKS** when Kubernetes is a requirement.
- Choose **EC2** when you need maximum control or have legacy constraints.

**Practical architecture examples**

A few real-world patterns help anchor the exam logic:

- **E-commerce web tier**
  - Use Auto Scaling EC2 or ECS/Fargate for the front end.
  - Use On-Demand for baseline reliability if traffic is unpredictable.
  - Add Savings Plans if the baseline is steady.
- **Nightly data processing**
  - Use Spot Instances with Auto Scaling or Spot Fleet.
  - Persist checkpoints externally.
  - Use interruption-aware batch workers.
- **Enterprise app with fixed licensing**
  - Use Dedicated Hosts if the license requires physical host affinity.
  - Use Dedicated Instances if dedicated tenancy is enough.
- **Microservices platform**
  - Use ECS/Fargate for simplicity if Kubernetes is not required.
  - Use EKS if the organization already standardizes on Kubernetes.
- **Burstable analytics job**
  - Use a mix of On-Demand and Spot.
  - Consider Compute Savings Plans for the steady baseline if usage spans multiple services.

**Exam-style selection rules**

- If the question emphasizes **lowest cost for interruptible work**, think **Spot**.
- If it emphasizes **predictable long-term usage**, think **Reserved Instances** or **Savings Plans**.
- If it emphasizes **flexibility across services**, think **Compute Savings Plans**.
- If it emphasizes **capacity in a specific AZ**, think **Zonal RI**.
- If it emphasizes **Kubernetes**, think **EKS**.
- If it emphasizes **no server management**, think **Fargate** or **Lambda**.
- If it emphasizes **host-level licensing**, think **Dedicated Hosts**.

**Common pitfalls**

- Confusing **billing discounts** with **capacity reservations**.
- Assuming all Reserved Instances reserve capacity; they do not.
- Assuming Savings Plans are more specific than RIs in a way that guarantees a better discount; the right answer depends on the workload.
- Choosing Spot for stateful or interruption-sensitive systems.
- Using EKS when ECS would be simpler and sufficient.
- Using EC2 when Lambda or Fargate would reduce operational overhead.

**Quick mental model**

Think in this order:

1. Can the workload tolerate interruption?
   - Yes: consider Spot.
2. Is the workload steady and predictable?
   - Yes: consider RI or Savings Plans.
3. Do you need capacity reservation in a specific AZ?
   - Yes: consider Zonal RI.
4. Do you need Kubernetes?
   - Yes: consider EKS.
5. Do you want to avoid server management?
   - Yes: consider Fargate or Lambda.
6. Do you need full OS control or special licensing?
   - Yes: consider EC2 or Dedicated Hosts.
