## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Associate Complete Course (Exam SAA-C03)

**AWS pricing model overview**

AWS uses a consumption-based pricing model: you pay for what you use, when you use it. That sounds simple, but the total bill often depends on several dimensions at once: compute time, storage consumed, requests, data transfer, and managed-service features.

For exam prep, focus on two ideas:

- **Unit pricing**: each service has its own meter, such as instance-hours, GB-months, requests, or GB transferred.
- **Architecture effects**: design choices can change cost more than the service itself, especially for network egress, storage class selection, and managed service features.

A useful mental model is:

1. Identify the service.
2. Identify the billing dimension.
3. Estimate usage volume.
4. Add related costs such as transfer, backup, or support.

**Core AWS billing concepts**

AWS Billing and Cost Management is the set of tools you use to understand, allocate, forecast, and control spend. It is not just a bill viewer; it is part of operational governance.

Key features include:

- **Bills and invoices**: show charges by service and by account.
- **Cost Explorer**: visualizes historical spend and trends.
- **Budgets**: alerts you when actual or forecasted spend crosses thresholds.
- **Cost and Usage Report (CUR)**: the most detailed billing dataset, delivered to Amazon S3 for analysis.
- **Cost Allocation Tags**: tags used to attribute spend to teams, applications, environments, or projects.
- **Cost Categories**: rule-based groupings that let you organize costs across accounts, tags, services, or charge types.

Cost allocation tags are especially important when multiple teams share infrastructure. You activate them in Billing and Cost Management, then use them in reports and dashboards. Cost categories are higher-level business groupings; for example, you might map several accounts and tags into `Production`, `Shared Services`, and `Sandbox`.

A practical example of cost allocation:

```text
Environment=prod
Application=payments
Owner=platform-team
```

With those tags, you can break down EC2, RDS, and EBS spend by application or environment.

> Cost allocation tags only help if they are applied consistently. Untagged resources are harder to attribute and often become “miscellaneous” spend.

**Cost metrics: unblended, blended, and amortized**

AWS billing reports can show different cost views. These are often confused, so learn the differences.

| Metric | Meaning | Best use |
|---|---|---|
| **Unblended cost** | The actual rate charged for each line item before account-level blending | Detailed charge analysis and invoice reconciliation |
| **Blended cost** | A normalized rate averaged across linked accounts in consolidated billing | Comparing spend across accounts in an organization |
| **Amortized cost** | Spreads upfront or recurring commitment charges over the period they apply to | Understanding the true cost of Reserved Instances and Savings Plans |

Unblended cost is the most literal charge view. Blended cost is useful when you want a simpler organization-wide average, but it can hide the real rate paid by a specific account. Amortized cost is the best view for architecture decisions because it reflects the effective cost of commitments over time.

For example, if you buy a Reserved Instance with an upfront fee, the invoice may show a large upfront charge. Amortized cost spreads that fee across the term so you can compare it fairly against On-Demand usage.

**AWS Free Tier**

The AWS Free Tier is a pricing program that lets you try selected services at no cost up to specific usage limits. It is common in exam-adjacent questions because it affects early-stage cost assumptions.

There are generally three Free Tier styles:

- **Always Free**: ongoing free allowances for certain services.
- **12 months free**: limited to the first year after account creation.
- **Trials**: short-term free usage for specific services.

Important exam points:

- Free Tier limits are service-specific and can change over time.
- Exceeding the allowance usually results in standard charges for the extra usage.
- Free Tier does not mean “free forever,” and it does not cover all related costs.
- Data transfer, storage, or requests outside the allowance can still generate charges.

A common trap is assuming a free compute service also includes free storage or network egress. In practice, you must check each meter separately.

**AWS Support plans and pricing impact**

AWS Support is a direct cost factor and can materially change your monthly spend. Support plans are priced differently and include different response times and features.

Common plans include:

- **Basic**: included with all accounts; provides account and billing support, documentation, and service health dashboards.
- **Developer**: intended for non-production or early-stage workloads; lower-cost support with business-hours guidance.
- **Business**: production-oriented support with faster response times and broader technical coverage.
- **Enterprise / Enterprise On-Ramp**: highest-tier support with the most comprehensive guidance and fastest escalation paths.

Pricing impact matters because support is often charged as a percentage of monthly AWS usage, with minimum fees on some plans. That means as your infrastructure spend grows, support cost can scale with it.

When evaluating architecture cost, include support if the workload is production-critical. A low infrastructure estimate can be misleading if the support plan adds a meaningful percentage on top.

**Compute pricing fundamentals**

Compute is usually the largest line item in many workloads, but the exact meter depends on the service.

**EC2 pricing basics**

Amazon EC2 pricing commonly depends on:

- **Instance type and size**: more CPU, memory, or GPU means higher cost.
- **Region**: prices vary by region.
- **Purchase model**: On-Demand, Reserved Instances, Savings Plans, Spot, or Dedicated capacity options.
- **Operating system**: Windows and some commercial software images cost more than Linux.
- **Tenancy**: shared, dedicated instance, or dedicated host.

EC2 is often billed per second with a minimum charge, depending on the instance family and OS. This matters for short-lived workloads, autoscaling, and batch jobs because you may not pay for a full hour if the instance runs briefly.

Additional EC2 cost considerations:

- **EBS-optimized charges** may apply for some older instance types or specific configurations, though many modern instances include EBS optimization by default.
- **Dedicated Instances** and **Dedicated Hosts** increase cost because you pay for isolated hardware capacity.
- **Spot Instances** can reduce cost significantly, but they are interruptible and not suitable for every workload.
- **Elastic IPs** can incur charges when unused or attached in certain ways, so they are not always “free static IPs.”

A simple EC2 cost estimate:

```text
Monthly cost ≈ instance hourly rate × hours used
            + EBS volume cost
            + data transfer out
            + any premium tenancy or licensing charges
```

**Lambda pricing fundamentals**

AWS Lambda is billed based on:

- **Number of requests**
- **Duration** of execution
- **Allocated memory** and CPU proportionality
- **Additional features** such as provisioned concurrency, if used

Lambda is a good example of serverless pricing where compute is not billed as an always-on instance. You pay only when code runs, which can be cost-effective for spiky or event-driven workloads.

Important details:

- Longer execution time increases cost.
- More memory increases cost, but can reduce duration if the function runs faster.
- High request volume can become significant even when each invocation is short.
- Provisioned concurrency adds cost because capacity is kept ready to reduce cold starts.

For exam scenarios, compare Lambda against EC2 or containers by asking whether the workload is intermittent, event-driven, and operationally simple enough to justify serverless pricing.

**Savings Plans and Reserved Instances**

Savings Plans and Reserved Instances are commitment-based pricing models that reduce cost in exchange for predictable usage.

- **Compute Savings Plans**: flexible across EC2, Lambda, and Fargate usage.
- **EC2 Instance Savings Plans**: more restrictive, tied to instance family in a region.
- **Reserved Instances (RIs)**: provide discounts for EC2, RDS, and some other services depending on the offering.

Use these when you have steady or predictable workloads. They are less suitable for highly variable or short-lived environments.

A practical rule:

- Use **On-Demand** for unpredictable or short-term usage.
- Use **Savings Plans** for broad compute commitment.
- Use **Reserved Instances** when you know the exact service and usage pattern.

**Storage pricing fundamentals**

Storage pricing is often more complex than compute because it includes capacity, requests, retrieval, lifecycle transitions, and minimum duration charges.

**Amazon S3 pricing**

S3 pricing typically includes:

- **Storage per GB-month**
- **Requests** such as PUT, GET, LIST, and lifecycle operations
- **Data transfer out**
- **Retrieval fees** for some storage classes
- **Lifecycle transition charges** when moving objects between classes

Different storage classes have different trade-offs:

- **S3 Standard**: higher storage cost, low latency, frequent access.
- **S3 Intelligent-Tiering**: automatic tiering with monitoring fees; useful when access patterns are unknown.
- **S3 Standard-IA** and **One Zone-IA**: lower storage cost, retrieval fees, and minimum storage duration charges.
- **S3 Glacier Instant Retrieval**, **Flexible Retrieval**, and **Deep Archive**: lower storage cost for archival use, with retrieval and minimum duration considerations.

Exam-relevant nuances:

- IA and Glacier classes often have **minimum storage duration charges**. Deleting or moving objects too early can still incur charges as if they were stored for the minimum period.
- Lifecycle transitions are not free; frequent transitions can add cost.
- Request-heavy workloads can make a “cheap” storage class more expensive than expected.
- Small objects can be disproportionately affected by request and transition costs.

A practical S3 trade-off example:

- Use **S3 Standard** for a frequently accessed web asset bucket.
- Use **S3 Standard-IA** for monthly reports that are read occasionally.
- Use **S3 Glacier Deep Archive** for compliance archives that are rarely restored.

**EBS pricing**

Amazon EBS is billed primarily by:

- **Provisioned storage size**
- **Volume type** such as gp3, io1, io2, st1, or sc1
- **Provisioned IOPS** for some volume types
- **Provisioned throughput** where applicable
- **Snapshots** stored in S3-backed snapshot storage

EBS cost is not just about capacity. For performance-oriented volumes, you may pay for IOPS or throughput. Snapshots also add cost over time, especially if you retain many incremental backups.

**EFS and FSx pricing trade-offs**

Amazon EFS and Amazon FSx solve different storage problems, and cost should influence the choice.

- **EFS** is a managed NFS file system for Linux workloads that need shared file access.
- **FSx** provides specialized file systems such as Windows File Server, Lustre, OpenZFS, and NetApp ONTAP.

Cost trade-offs:

- Use **S3** for object storage and static content.
- Use **EFS** when multiple EC2 instances need shared POSIX file access.
- Use **FSx** when you need performance or protocol features specific to the workload, such as Windows integration or high-performance HPC patterns.

A common architecture decision is whether to store shared application files in S3, EFS, or FSx:

- Choose **S3** if the application can use object semantics and does not need file locking or POSIX paths.
- Choose **EFS** if multiple Linux instances need a shared mounted file system.
- Choose **FSx** if you need Windows file sharing, high throughput, or specialized file-system behavior.

**Database pricing fundamentals**

Managed databases have several cost components beyond the instance itself.

**Amazon RDS pricing**

RDS pricing commonly includes:

- **DB instance hours**
- **Storage**
- **I/O** for certain storage and engine combinations
- **Backup storage**
- **Multi-AZ deployment cost**
- **Read replicas**, which add additional instance and storage charges

Multi-AZ increases availability by maintaining a synchronous standby in another Availability Zone, but it also increases cost because you are paying for more infrastructure. Read replicas are separate instances and therefore add cost as well.

Backup storage is often overlooked. Automated backups and manual snapshots can accumulate, especially for large databases or long retention periods.

A rough RDS estimate:

```text
Monthly cost ≈ DB instance hours
            + allocated storage
            + I/O charges
            + backup storage beyond free allocation
            + Multi-AZ standby overhead
            + read replica instances
```

**Amazon Aurora pricing note**

Aurora pricing is similar in spirit to RDS but can differ in storage and I/O behavior depending on the deployment model. For exam purposes, remember that managed database cost is not just “instance price”; storage and replication features matter.

**Networking and data transfer pricing**

Network cost is a major source of surprise in AWS bills.

**Data transfer basics**

Common patterns:

- **Inbound data transfer** is often free, but always verify the service and path.
- **Outbound data transfer to the internet** usually costs money.
- **Inter-AZ data transfer** can be charged when traffic crosses Availability Zones.
- **Inter-region data transfer** is typically more expensive than same-region traffic.

This means architecture placement matters. A design that sends traffic between AZs on every request can cost more than expected, even if compute is cheap.

**CloudFront and edge pricing**

Amazon CloudFront is a content delivery network (CDN) that caches content at edge locations. Pricing usually includes:

- **Data transfer out from edge locations**
- **HTTP/HTTPS requests**
- **Invalidation requests** beyond the free allowance in some cases
- Potential origin fetch costs depending on the origin service

CloudFront can reduce origin load and improve latency, but it is not free. It is often cost-effective when it reduces expensive origin egress or improves user experience enough to justify the edge delivery charges.

**NAT Gateway vs VPC endpoints**

This is a classic cost-optimization trade-off.

- **NAT Gateway** allows private subnets to reach the internet or public AWS endpoints without exposing instances directly.
- **VPC endpoints** and **AWS PrivateLink** let you access supported AWS services privately without traversing a NAT Gateway.

NAT Gateway pricing can include:

- Hourly charge
- Per-GB processed charge

That makes NAT expensive for high-volume traffic to AWS services such as S3 or DynamoDB if you route that traffic through the NAT Gateway unnecessarily.

Use VPC endpoints when:

- You want private access to AWS services.
- You want to reduce NAT data processing charges.
- You want to keep traffic on the AWS network.

Example topology:

```text
Private EC2 -> VPC Endpoint -> S3
Private EC2 -> NAT Gateway -> Internet
```

For S3 access from private subnets, a gateway endpoint is usually the more cost-efficient choice than sending traffic through NAT.

**AWS Marketplace pricing considerations**

AWS Marketplace can affect architecture cost because third-party software often adds licensing or subscription charges on top of infrastructure.

Pricing considerations include:

- **Hourly software charges**
- **Annual subscriptions**
- **Usage-based licensing**
- **Bring Your Own License (BYOL)** models
- **Marketplace metering** for software consumption

When comparing solutions, do not evaluate only EC2 or storage cost. A Marketplace AMI or SaaS product may have a lower infrastructure footprint but a higher total cost due to software licensing.

**Cost optimization trade-offs**

Good architecture decisions balance performance, availability, and cost. The cheapest service is not always the best choice.

Examples:

- **S3 vs EFS vs FSx**
  - Use S3 for object storage and infrequent access.
  - Use EFS for shared file access across Linux instances.
  - Use FSx when you need specialized file-system features or Windows integration.
- **NAT Gateway vs VPC endpoints**
  - Use NAT for general outbound internet access.
  - Use endpoints for private access to AWS services and lower data processing cost.
- **On-Demand vs Savings Plans**
  - Use On-Demand for uncertain demand.
  - Use Savings Plans for steady baseline usage.
- **Multi-AZ vs Single-AZ**
  - Use Multi-AZ for higher availability.
  - Use Single-AZ when cost is more important and downtime is acceptable.

A practical cost comparison workflow:

1. Estimate compute hours.
2. Estimate storage capacity and retention.
3. Estimate request volume and I/O.
4. Estimate data transfer out and cross-AZ traffic.
5. Add backup, support, and licensing.
6. Compare On-Demand against commitment pricing.

**CLI and practical examples**

Use the AWS CLI to inspect cost-related metadata and resource usage patterns.

List tagged resources in EC2:

```bash
aws ec2 describe-instances \
  --query 'Reservations[].Instances[].Tags'
```

Check S3 bucket lifecycle configuration:

```bash
aws s3api get-bucket-lifecycle-configuration \
  --bucket my-archive-bucket
```

View RDS instance class and Multi-AZ setting:

```bash
aws rds describe-db-instances \
  --query 'DBInstances[].{DBInstanceIdentifier:DBInstanceIdentifier,Class:DBInstanceClass,MultiAZ:MultiAZ}'
```

Inspect VPC endpoints:

```bash
aws ec2 describe-vpc-endpoints \
  --filters Name=vpc-id,Values=vpc-1234567890abcdef0
```

These commands help you verify whether a design is using the intended cost model.

**Billing and cost governance best practices**

Strong cost governance starts with visibility.

- Tag resources consistently from day one.
- Use separate accounts for production, development, and sandbox environments.
- Set budgets and alerts before costs grow.
- Review idle resources such as unattached EBS volumes, unused Elastic IPs, and orphaned snapshots.
- Prefer managed services when they reduce operational overhead enough to justify the premium.
- Revisit architecture after traffic patterns stabilize; early design assumptions often overprovision.

**Exam-oriented reminders**

- AWS pricing is service-specific; do not assume one pricing model applies everywhere.
- Data transfer and request charges often matter as much as compute.
- Multi-AZ, backups, and replicas increase cost but improve resilience.
- Free Tier is limited and does not eliminate all related charges.
- Support plans, Marketplace software, and commitment discounts can materially change total cost.
- Amortized cost is the best view for comparing committed pricing against On-Demand.

For SAA-C03, pricing fundamentals are less about memorizing a rate card and more about understanding what drives the bill. If you can identify the billing meter, predict the hidden costs, and choose the right trade-off between performance and spend, you can answer most cost-related architecture questions confidently.
