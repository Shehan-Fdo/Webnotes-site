## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ Hands-On Infrastructure (Exam N10-009)

**Segmented access policy goals**

Segmented access policies divide internal networks into smaller trust zones so you can control which systems talk to each other. The main purpose is to reduce broad east-west access inside the network, which limits lateral movement after a compromise and improves containment.

In Zero Trust-style designs, you do not assume internal traffic is safe just because it stays on the inside. Instead, you verify identity, privilege, and device posture before allowing access to protected resources.

- **Lateral movement** becomes harder when hosts can only reach approved peers and services.
- **Containment** improves because a compromise in one zone does not automatically expose the rest of the environment.
- **Least privilege** applies to internal traffic, not just user logins or perimeter access.

**Why internal segmentation matters**

Older enterprise networks often rely on broad internal trust. That model can make east-west connectivity too permissive, especially when legacy dependencies include directory services, file services, web tiers, and database tiers. A segmented design narrows those paths so each workload only reaches what it needs.

A practical approach is to map how traffic moves between sensitive assets before you define policy. That gives you a clearer view of which flows are required and which are unnecessary.

- Identify the most sensitive assets first.
- Map the traffic paths that support those assets.
- Allow only the flows that are required for business function.
- Restrict privileged management paths more tightly than normal application traffic.

**Micro-segmentation and policy posture**

Micro-segmentation is a finer-grained form of internal segmentation. It can use dependency-based service allow-listing or identity/role-aware controls to limit access more precisely than a flat internal network.

A conservative implementation usually starts with a permissive baseline, then tightens policy in stages. This helps you understand what breaks before you enforce stricter rules.

| Posture | What it does | Practical effect |
|---|---|---|
| Flat baseline | Broad internal trust | Easy connectivity, weak containment |
| Dependency-based allow-listing | Allows only required services | Reduces unnecessary east-west traffic |
| Identity/role-aware policy | Limits privileged management paths | Better control over administrative access |

**Zone-based policy design**

Zone-based policy firewalls and similar controls let you define trust boundaries between internal segments. You can place systems into zones based on function or sensitivity, then apply rules between those zones instead of relying on one large internal network.

This approach works well when you need to separate application tiers or protect management networks from general user traffic. It also helps when you need to enforce different rules for different classes of traffic.

- Group systems by role, sensitivity, or function.
- Define allowed traffic between zones.
- Treat management access as a separate, tightly controlled path.
- Review zone boundaries when applications or dependencies change.

**Implementation workflow**

1. Inventory the internal systems and their dependencies.
2. Identify the zones you need to protect.
3. Map required traffic between zones.
4. Create allow rules for approved services and management paths.
5. Test the policy in a controlled posture before tightening enforcement.
6. Monitor for blocked flows and adjust only when the traffic is legitimate.

**Operational considerations**

Segmentation adds control, but it also adds planning overhead. If you make policies too restrictive too early, you can disrupt legitimate application dependencies. If you leave them too broad, you preserve the same internal exposure you were trying to remove.

A good balance is to start with known dependencies, validate reachability, and then reduce access further where the business does not require it.

> Be careful not to confuse internal segmentation with simple perimeter filtering. The goal here is to control east-west movement inside the network, not just traffic entering or leaving it.

**Key takeaways**

- Internal segmentation reduces trust between internal systems.
- Micro-segmentation narrows access to required services and management paths.
- Zero Trust principles require continuous verification, even inside the network.
- Policy design should follow traffic mapping and asset sensitivity.
- The best results come from least privilege, staged enforcement, and ongoing review.
