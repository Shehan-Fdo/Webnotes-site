## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ Hands-On Infrastructure (Exam N10-009)

**Why packet evidence matters**

Packet evidence helps you verify whether traffic is actually permitted or denied, rather than assuming policy is working. In zero-trust and microsegmentation environments, every connection should be evaluated against explicit rules, and the network should not rely on implicit trust.

This matters because policy intent and real traffic behavior can differ. A rule may exist on paper, but packet-level observation shows whether a flow is truly allowed, blocked, or unexpectedly reaching another workload.

- **Permitted flow**: traffic that matches an explicit allow rule and reaches the destination.
- **Denied flow**: traffic that fails policy evaluation and does not complete the connection.
- **East-West traffic**: internal workload-to-workload traffic that often reveals lateral movement risk.
- **Default deny**: a baseline posture where traffic is blocked unless a rule allows it.

**What you look for in packet evidence**

Packet evidence gives you a practical way to confirm the result of a policy decision. In a zero-trust model, the goal is to prove that only required communication paths work and that everything else is rejected.

Use packet evidence to answer these questions:

- Did the expected service-to-service connection succeed?
- Did the blocked connection fail for the right reason?
- Is there any unexpected lateral movement between workloads?
- Does the observed traffic match the documented policy?

When you validate flows, keep the focus on observable behavior. A packet trace or flow record should support the policy decision you expect to see.

**Validation workflow**

1. Identify the communication path you want to test.
2. Confirm the policy intent for that path, including any explicit allow or default deny behavior.
3. Generate the traffic from the source workload or host.
4. Capture or review packet evidence for the attempt.
5. Compare the observed result with the expected policy outcome.
6. Repeat for both allowed and denied paths so you verify positive and negative cases.

**Reading the evidence**

Packet evidence is most useful when you compare what should happen with what actually happens. A permitted flow should show successful communication along the intended path. A denied flow should show that the connection does not complete or that the traffic is rejected by policy.

<dl>
<dt>Allowed path</dt>
<dd>The packet trace supports the intended communication and shows that the policy permits the flow.</dd>
<dt>Blocked path</dt>
<dd>The packet trace shows the traffic is stopped, which confirms the deny behavior is active.</dd>
<dt>Unexpected path</dt>
<dd>The packet trace reveals traffic that should not exist, which may indicate a missing rule or weak segmentation.</dd>
</dl>

> If your packet evidence shows traffic reaching a destination that should be isolated, treat it as a policy gap until you confirm otherwise.

**Zero-trust and microsegmentation context**

Zero trust means you do not assume trust based on network location. Microsegmentation divides the network into smaller boundaries so you can apply fine-grained access control to specific resources. Packet evidence helps you prove that those boundaries are real.

This is especially important in environments where workloads communicate laterally. Traditional perimeter thinking is not enough when the risk comes from internal movement between services, pods, or servers.

- **Microsegmentation** reduces the blast radius of a compromise.
- **Explicit allow rules** define the only approved communication paths.
- **Logging and packet review** help you validate that policy enforcement matches design.
- **Default deny first** gives you a safer baseline before you open required flows.

**Practical interpretation of results**

Use conservative interpretation when evidence is incomplete. If you cannot clearly prove that a flow was allowed for the right reason, do not assume the policy is correct. Likewise, if a blocked flow appears to succeed, verify whether the traffic bypassed the intended control point.

A good validation process checks both sides of the policy:

- Allowed traffic should work only where required.
- Denied traffic should fail consistently.
- Evidence should match the documented rule set.
- Unexpected connectivity should trigger review.

**Key takeaway**

Packet evidence turns policy validation into something you can observe and defend. For exam scenarios, focus on proving that permitted flows succeed, denied flows fail, and the observed traffic matches the intended zero-trust design.
