## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ Hands-On Infrastructure (Exam N10-009)

**Least-Privilege Connectivity Overview**

Least-privilege connectivity means workloads can communicate only with the specific systems and services they need. This approach reduces unnecessary access and helps limit the impact of compromised hosts, service accounts, or application identities.

In Zero Trust, you do not assume that traffic inside the network is safe. Instead, you verify and restrict communication at a more granular level, closer to the workload itself. This is especially important because lateral movement can spread quickly when internal traffic is broadly trusted.

- **Least privilege** removes excess access that workloads do not need.
- **Zero Trust** treats every connection as untrusted until it is explicitly allowed.
- **Microsegmentation** helps enforce workload-to-workload restrictions with fine-grained policy.
- **Lateral movement** becomes harder when internal paths are tightly controlled.

**Why This Matters**

Modern environments often accumulate broad permissions over time. Service accounts may gain more access than they require, and legacy applications may depend on overly permissive rules just to keep running. That creates a larger attack surface and makes it easier for an attacker to move between workloads after an initial compromise.

Workload identities also need careful handling. Some identities, such as service principals and managed identities, are used by applications or services rather than people. They cannot always use the same controls as human users, so you need policies that fit how workloads actually authenticate and communicate.

> Use the most restrictive policy that still allows the workload to function. If a connection is not required, deny it.

**Core Policy Model**

A practical least-privilege model starts with deny-by-default behavior and then adds only the traffic that is required. This is the same general allowlist idea used by microsegmentation platforms: communication is blocked unless a rule explicitly permits it.

You can think about policy in terms of:
- Which workload is initiating the connection
- Which destination workload or service it needs
- Which ports, protocols, or conditions are required
- Whether the access is temporary, persistent, or tied to a specific condition

**Common Enforcement Approaches**

Different platforms enforce workload policy in different ways, but the goal stays the same: control traffic as close to the workload as possible. In practice, this often means using security groups, segmentation rules, or workload policy engines to define allowed paths.

| Approach | What it does | Practical effect |
|---|---|---|
| Allowlist policy | Denies communication unless explicitly allowed | Reduces unnecessary workload access |
| Microsegmentation | Splits the network into smaller trust zones | Limits blast radius and lateral movement |
| Conditional access for workloads | Adds policy conditions beyond identity alone | Improves control over when access is granted |

**How to Apply Least-Privilege Connectivity**

1. Identify the workloads that need to communicate.
2. Map the exact application flows they require.
3. Remove broad or inherited access that is not needed.
4. Create explicit allow rules for the required traffic only.
5. Validate that the application still functions after tightening policy.
6. Monitor for new dependencies and adjust rules carefully.

**Operational Considerations**

Least-privilege connectivity is not a one-time task. As applications change, new dependencies appear and old ones disappear. If you do not review policy regularly, workloads can drift back into overly permissive states.

Automation helps reduce manual effort in large environments. Tools and workflows such as infrastructure-as-code, policy automation, and centralized rule management make it easier to keep rules consistent without opening unnecessary paths.

- Review rules periodically to catch stale access.
- Prefer short-lived or tightly scoped access where possible.
- Keep policy changes controlled so production traffic is not disrupted.
- Watch for hidden dependencies in legacy applications.

**Security and Compliance Value**

Restricting workload communication supports broader security goals by reducing the chance that one compromised system can reach many others. It also helps organizations demonstrate stronger control over sensitive data and regulated environments.

This matters in environments that must align with security frameworks or compliance requirements. Fine-grained workload policy can support stronger protection for data, applications, and internal services without relying on a broad trusted perimeter.

**Exam Focus**

For Network+ purposes, focus on the relationship between Zero Trust and least privilege:

- Traditional perimeter security trusts too much inside the network.
- Zero Trust assumes internal traffic still needs verification.
- Least-privilege connectivity limits which workloads can talk to each other.
- Microsegmentation is a common way to enforce that model.
- The main benefit is reduced lateral movement and smaller blast radius.
