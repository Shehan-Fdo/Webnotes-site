## **Course:** CompTIA Network+ Hands-On Infrastructure (Exam N10-009)

**Packet evidence as a troubleshooting signal**

Packet captures and network logs help you separate a real network fault from normal background traffic. In practice, you look for patterns that do not match the expected segment, protocol, or device behavior. A capture that shows only DHCP broadcasts and NBNS name queries, for example, can indicate that you are not seeing the traffic you expected on a VLAN or mirrored port.

Packet evidence is especially useful when the problem is not a total outage. You may still see some traffic, but the useful flows are missing, filtered, or malformed. That makes packet-level inspection a strong way to confirm whether the issue is in switching, segmentation, protocol behavior, or sensor visibility.

- **VLAN** issues can hide traffic if the capture point does not see the right tagged frames.
- **Mirroring** problems can produce partial visibility, such as only broadcasts or local discovery traffic.
- **Protocol** clues matter because unexpected DNS, DHCP, or name-service patterns often point to a misconfiguration rather than a failure.

**What to look for in a capture**

Start by checking whether the packets you do see match the network role of the device or segment. If you are capturing on a switch port or span source, confirm whether the traffic includes the expected VLAN headers and whether the observed flows make sense for that location. If only a small subset of traffic appears, the capture path may be incomplete or the filter may be too restrictive.

Zeek can also help by turning traffic into logs that highlight operational problems. Its `weird.log` records unusual or exceptional activity, including malformed connections, traffic that does not conform to a protocol, malfunctioning hardware, or misconfigured devices. Other logs, such as `dns.log`, `dhcp.log`, and `conn.log`, help you compare what the network is doing against what it should be doing.

- `weird.log` flags unusual or exceptional activity.
- `dns.log` helps you spot excessive or repetitive name resolution.
- `dhcp.log` shows lease activity and can confirm whether clients are behaving normally.
- `conn.log` gives a broad view of TCP, UDP, and ICMP connections.

**Common misconfiguration patterns in packet evidence**

Packet evidence often reveals problems that are operational rather than malicious. A management VLAN that carries an unexpectedly high amount of DNS traffic can indicate devices repeatedly “phoning home.” Repeated queries at short intervals suggest a configuration issue, not normal user activity. Likewise, malformed or nonconforming traffic can point to a device or application that is not speaking the protocol correctly.

> If you only see broadcasts, discovery traffic, or a narrow slice of flows, do not assume the network is healthy. Verify the capture point, VLAN visibility, and filters before drawing conclusions.

- **Excessive DNS** may indicate devices or applications repeatedly resolving the same names.
- **Malformed traffic** can suggest a protocol mismatch or broken implementation.
- **Unexpected broadcasts** may show that you are capturing at the wrong point or with limited visibility.
- **Unusual log entries** in Zeek can point to misconfigured hardware or software.

**Practical workflow for evidence-based troubleshooting**

1. Capture traffic from the suspected segment or mirrored port.
2. Check whether the packets include the expected VLAN information.
3. Remove or relax filters if the capture looks too narrow.
4. Compare the observed traffic with the expected role of the device or subnet.
5. Review Zeek logs such as `conn.log`, `dns.log`, `dhcp.log`, and `weird.log` for patterns that confirm the issue.
6. Decide whether the evidence points to a visibility problem, a protocol problem, or a device misconfiguration.

**How to interpret the evidence**

A useful packet trace does more than show packets on the wire. It tells you whether the network is carrying the right traffic, whether the traffic is tagged and segmented correctly, and whether devices are behaving as expected. When the evidence shows only partial traffic, repeated queries, or unusual protocol behavior, treat that as a clue to investigate configuration first.

**Key takeaways**

- Packet evidence helps you confirm whether a problem is in visibility, segmentation, or device behavior.
- VLAN tagging and capture location can determine whether you see the real traffic.
- Zeek logs provide a higher-level view that can expose operational misconfigurations.
- `weird.log` is especially useful when traffic looks abnormal but not obviously malicious.
