## **Course:** AWS Certified Security – Specialty (Exam SCS-C02)

**Prioritization mindset**

Security findings are not all equal. In AWS, you prioritize remediation by combining **risk**, **blast radius**, **exposure**, **data sensitivity**, and **business context**. A low-severity issue on an internet-facing production workload can matter more than a higher-severity issue in an isolated dev account.

The goal is to reduce the most meaningful risk first, not to chase every alert in the order it appears. In practice, you weigh:

- **Exploitability**: how easy it is to abuse the issue.
- **Impact**: what happens if the issue is exploited.
- **Exposure**: whether the resource is public, cross-account, or reachable from sensitive networks.
- **Privilege level**: whether the finding affects an admin path, a trust boundary, or a service role.
- **Data sensitivity**: whether the resource touches regulated or confidential data.
- **Scope**: how many accounts, workloads, or users are affected.
- **Control gaps**: whether compensating controls already reduce the risk.

A useful mental model is: prioritize findings that are **easy to exploit, high impact, broadly exposed, and hard to detect**.

**Common AWS sources of findings**

You often collect findings from multiple AWS services and then normalize them into one remediation queue. Each source has a different signal, so you should understand what it tells you.

- **Amazon Inspector**: identifies software vulnerabilities and network exposure on EC2, container images, and Lambda functions.
- **AWS Security Hub**: aggregates findings from AWS services and partner tools, then maps them to standards and severity.
- **AWS Config**: detects configuration drift and noncompliance against rules and conformance packs.
- **Amazon GuardDuty**: detects suspicious activity, such as credential misuse, reconnaissance, or anomalous API calls.
- **AWS Trusted Advisor**: highlights best-practice issues, including security-related checks for some accounts and support plans.
- **IAM Access Analyzer**: identifies unintended external access to resources and policies.

These tools do not all mean the same thing. For example, a GuardDuty finding may indicate active abuse, while a Config rule may indicate a misconfiguration that increases future risk. You usually treat active indicators of compromise as more urgent than passive posture issues.

**How to rank findings**

A practical prioritization process starts with triage and ends with a remediation plan. You can use a simple scoring approach or a formal risk matrix.

1. **Confirm the finding**
   - Verify whether the alert is valid or a false positive.
   - Check the affected resource, account, region, and time window.
   - Determine whether the issue is still present.

2. **Classify the finding**
   - Identify whether it is a vulnerability, misconfiguration, identity issue, exposure, or suspicious activity.
   - Determine whether it is preventive, detective, or responsive in nature.

3. **Assess impact**
   - Ask what data, systems, or identities are at risk.
   - Consider confidentiality, integrity, and availability.
   - Estimate business consequences such as downtime, data loss, or compliance violations.

4. **Assess likelihood**
   - Evaluate whether the issue is internet-facing, reachable from a trusted network, or limited to a narrow trust boundary.
   - Consider whether exploit code exists, whether the issue is trivial to abuse, and whether the resource is already targeted.

5. **Consider compensating controls**
   - Look for WAF, network segmentation, SCPs, MFA, encryption, or monitoring that reduce risk.
   - Do not let compensating controls hide a serious issue; they only lower priority when they are strong and reliable.

6. **Assign remediation priority**
   - Fix critical, high-impact, high-likelihood issues first.
   - Group related findings when one change resolves multiple issues.
   - Track exceptions with an explicit risk acceptance process.

**A simple risk matrix**

A common approach is to combine likelihood and impact into a priority level.

| Likelihood \ Impact | Low | Medium | High |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Low | P3 | P3 | P2 |
| Medium | P3 | P2 | P1 |
| High | P2 | P1 | P0 |

- **P0**: immediate action, often same day.
- **P1**: urgent, usually within a short SLA.
- **P2**: scheduled remediation.
- **P3**: backlog or monitor, if justified.

This matrix is only a starting point. In AWS, internet exposure, privileged IAM paths, and production data often push a finding higher than the matrix alone suggests.

**High-priority finding patterns**

Some findings deserve fast escalation because they can lead to rapid compromise or broad impact.

- **Public S3 bucket with sensitive data**: exposure is direct and often severe.
- **Overly permissive IAM policy**: `Action: "*"`, `Resource: "*"`, or wildcard trust can enable privilege escalation.
- **Root account misuse**: root credentials or access keys are a critical risk.
- **Security group open to the world**: `0.0.0.0/0` or `::/0` on sensitive ports increases attack surface.
- **Unencrypted data at rest for regulated workloads**: may create compliance and breach risk.
- **Suspicious API activity**: GuardDuty findings suggesting credential compromise should be treated as urgent.
- **Cross-account resource sharing without intent**: unintended access can bypass organizational boundaries.
- **Missing MFA on privileged identities**: increases the chance of account takeover.

**Example: IAM policy review**

When you see a broad IAM policy, prioritize it based on who can use it and what it can reach.

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Action": "*",
      "Resource": "*"
    }
  ]
}
```

This policy is dangerous because it grants full permissions. If attached to a human user, role, or automation path, it can enable privilege escalation, data access, and destructive actions. If the principal is used in production, it should be high priority.

A safer pattern is to scope permissions to specific actions and resources:

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Action": [
        "s3:GetObject",
        "s3:PutObject"
      ],
      "Resource": "arn:aws:s3:::example-bucket/*"
    }
  ]
}
```

**Example: Security group exposure**

A security group that allows SSH from anywhere is often lower priority than a public database port, but it still deserves attention.

```bash
aws ec2 describe-security-groups \
  --group-ids sg-0123456789abcdef0 \
  --query 'SecurityGroups[0].IpPermissions'
```

If you find a rule like this, assess the workload first:

- If it protects a bastion host with MFA and tight logging, the risk is lower.
- If it protects a production instance with weak credentials, the risk is much higher.
- If it exposes a database port, prioritize immediately.

**Example: S3 exposure check**

Use the AWS CLI to inspect bucket policy and public access settings.

```bash
aws s3api get-bucket-policy --bucket example-bucket
aws s3api get-public-access-block --bucket example-bucket
aws s3api get-bucket-acl --bucket example-bucket
```

Prioritize remediation when:

- The bucket contains sensitive or regulated data.
- Public access is intentional but not tightly controlled.
- Bucket policy conditions are missing or too broad.
- Block Public Access is disabled without a strong reason.

**Using AWS Security Hub effectively**

Security Hub is useful because it centralizes findings and normalizes severity. However, you should not rely on severity alone. A medium finding can be more urgent than a high finding if it affects a crown-jewel workload or a trust boundary.

When reviewing Security Hub findings, consider:

- **Severity label**: useful for triage, but not sufficient.
- **Finding type**: misconfiguration, vulnerability, or suspicious activity.
- **Resource type**: IAM, S3, EC2, Lambda, RDS, or organization-level control.
- **Compliance mapping**: whether the issue violates a required standard.
- **Workflow status**: new, suppressed, or resolved.

A good workflow is to sort by severity, then re-rank by business context and exposure.

**Using AWS Config for prioritization**

AWS Config helps you find drift and noncompliance. Its findings are often less urgent than active threat indicators, but they are important because they reveal control failures.

Examples of high-value Config checks include:

- Public read or write access on S3 buckets.
- Security groups allowing unrestricted inbound access.
- EBS volumes without encryption.
- IAM policies that allow overly broad permissions.
- CloudTrail disabled or not multi-region where required.

Config is especially useful for identifying systemic issues. If one bad pattern appears across many accounts, prioritize the root cause rather than each individual resource one by one.

**Using GuardDuty for prioritization**

GuardDuty findings often indicate active or suspicious behavior. Treat them as urgent when they suggest:

- Credential compromise.
- Reconnaissance from unusual IPs.
- Privilege escalation attempts.
- Malware or crypto-mining behavior.
- Data exfiltration patterns.

A GuardDuty finding on a low-value sandbox account may still matter if the same credentials or roles are reused elsewhere. Always check whether the finding reveals a broader identity compromise.

**Using IAM Access Analyzer**

Access Analyzer helps you identify unintended external access. Prioritize findings when:

- A resource policy grants access to `*` or external accounts.
- A role trust policy allows unintended principals.
- A KMS key policy exposes decryption permissions broadly.
- A Lambda, S3, SQS, SNS, or Secrets Manager policy is shared outside the intended boundary.

This is especially important in multi-account environments because a single overly broad resource policy can bypass organizational controls.

**Remediation strategy**

Prioritization should lead to action. The best remediation plan is specific, measurable, and reversible.

- **Fix the root cause** rather than only the symptom.
- **Bundle related changes** when one control closes multiple findings.
- **Use least privilege** to reduce future recurrence.
- **Automate guardrails** with AWS Config rules, SCPs, and IaC policy checks.
- **Validate after change** to confirm the finding is closed.

A practical remediation sequence is:

1. Contain active threats.
2. Remove public or cross-account exposure.
3. Fix privilege escalation paths.
4. Patch known vulnerabilities.
5. Address lower-risk hygiene issues.
6. Add preventive controls to stop recurrence.

**CLI examples for investigation**

Use the AWS CLI to gather evidence before you change anything.

```bash
aws securityhub get-findings \
  --filters '{"WorkflowStatus":[{"Value":"NEW","Comparison":"EQUALS"}]}' \
  --max-results 10
```

```bash
aws guardduty list-findings --detector-id <detector-id>
aws guardduty get-findings --detector-id <detector-id> --finding-ids <id1> <id2>
```

```bash
aws configservice describe-compliance-by-config-rule
aws configservice get-compliance-details-by-config-rule \
  --config-rule-name restricted-ssh
```

```bash
aws accessanalyzer list-findings --analyzer-name <analyzer-name>
```

These commands help you confirm scope, affected resources, and whether the issue is isolated or repeated.

**How to think about business context**

Technical severity is only part of the decision. You should also ask:

- Is the workload production or nonproduction?
- Does it process customer, financial, health, or authentication data?
- Is it internet-facing or internal only?
- Is there a maintenance window or change freeze?
- Does the issue affect a shared service or a single workload?
- Would remediation break critical business functions?

Sometimes you delay a lower-risk fix because a safer, broader change is scheduled soon. That is acceptable if the risk is documented and monitored.

**Common exam cues**

On the exam, look for clues that indicate priority:

- **Internet-facing** plus **sensitive data** usually means high priority.
- **Root credentials**, **admin roles**, or **trust policy issues** usually outrank ordinary misconfigurations.
- **Active compromise indicators** outrank passive compliance drift.
- **Organization-wide controls** can reduce many findings at once, so they are often high leverage.
- **False positives** should be verified before remediation effort is spent.

If two answers seem plausible, choose the one that reduces the most risk with the least delay, especially when the scenario mentions production, public exposure, or privileged access.

**Practical prioritization checklist**

- Confirm the finding is real.
- Identify the affected account, region, and resource.
- Determine whether the issue is active, exposed, or latent.
- Estimate impact on confidentiality, integrity, and availability.
- Check for privilege escalation or lateral movement potential.
- Review compensating controls.
- Rank by business criticality and blast radius.
- Remediate the highest-risk items first.
- Validate closure and add preventive controls.

**Key takeaway**

Prioritizing findings in AWS is a risk-management exercise. You do not simply fix the loudest alert; you fix the issue that combines the highest likelihood, highest impact, and widest exposure. Security Hub, Config, GuardDuty, Inspector, and Access Analyzer each contribute different signals, but your final decision should reflect the workload’s business value, trust boundaries, and potential for compromise.
