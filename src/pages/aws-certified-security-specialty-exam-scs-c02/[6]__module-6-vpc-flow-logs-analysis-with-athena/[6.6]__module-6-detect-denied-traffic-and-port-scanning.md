## **Course:** AWS Certified Security – Specialty (Exam SCS-C02)

**Why denied-traffic detection matters**

Denied traffic is often the earliest signal of reconnaissance, misconfiguration, or an active attack. In AWS, you usually detect it by correlating managed detections such as Amazon GuardDuty with network telemetry from VPC Flow Logs and, when needed, host-based logs from the operating system or firewall.

Port scanning is especially important because it often precedes exploitation. A scan may target a public endpoint, an internal workload, a load balancer, a NAT path, or a peered/VPN-connected network. For exam scenarios, focus on the traffic path and the control point that denied it, not just whether the target is public or private.

**Core AWS services and what they tell you**

- **Amazon GuardDuty** detects suspicious activity using multiple telemetry sources, including VPC Flow Logs, DNS logs, CloudTrail events, and other service-specific signals depending on the finding type.
- **VPC Flow Logs** show network metadata for traffic that traverses a supported network interface, subnet, or VPC path.
- **Security groups** are stateful, so return traffic is automatically allowed when the initiating flow is permitted.
- **Network ACLs (NACLs)** are stateless, so you must allow both directions explicitly, including ephemeral ports where appropriate.
- **CloudWatch Logs Insights** helps you query flow logs quickly when you need to identify source IPs, destination ports, and traffic patterns.
- **Security Hub** can aggregate GuardDuty findings and route them into operational workflows.
- **Amazon EventBridge** can trigger automation such as isolation, ticketing, or notification when a finding arrives.

**GuardDuty findings for port scanning**

GuardDuty commonly surfaces port-scanning activity with findings that indicate reconnaissance against EC2 instances or other resources. In exam questions, distinguish the intent and the target:

- **Recon:EC2/Portscan**
  - Indicates a source is scanning multiple ports on one or more EC2 instances.
  - Often suggests broad reconnaissance rather than a single service probe.
- **Recon:EC2/PortProbe**
  - Indicates a source is probing a specific port or a small set of ports on EC2 instances.
  - Often suggests targeted service discovery or validation of a known service.
- **UnauthorizedAccess**-style findings
  - May appear when a scan is paired with suspicious authentication attempts or follow-on behavior.
  - Treat these as higher urgency if they indicate active exploitation rather than simple reconnaissance.

GuardDuty finding names can vary by resource type and behavior. In the exam, use the finding type, severity, and affected resource to decide whether you are seeing broad scanning, targeted probing, or a separate attack stage.

**How to interpret scan patterns**

A scan is not always malicious by itself, but it is a strong indicator of interest in a target. The context matters:

- A scan against a public endpoint may indicate internet reconnaissance.
- A scan against a private subnet may still be serious if the source is a peered VPC, VPN, AWS Direct Connect, a compromised internal host, or another trusted network path.
- A scan against a load balancer may show up as traffic to the load balancer front end rather than the backend instances.
- A scan against a NAT gateway usually reflects outbound traffic from private instances to external destinations, not inbound probing of the NAT itself.
- A scan against internal east-west targets may indicate lateral movement or post-compromise discovery.

> Do not assume “private” means “safe.” Private networks can still be reached through peering, hybrid connectivity, or compromised internal systems.

**VPC Flow Logs: what they show and what they do not**

VPC Flow Logs are the main low-level network evidence source for denied traffic investigations, but they have important limits:

- They record metadata, not packet payloads.
- They do not show application-layer context such as HTTP paths, usernames, or command content.
- They only capture traffic that traverses the selected logging scope and supported path.
- They do not show traffic that never reaches the VPC, subnet, or ENI path you are logging.
- They may not capture every nuance of a connection failure, especially when the issue occurs above the network layer.

For exam purposes, remember that a flow log can show `ACCEPT` even when the application still fails. That often means the network path is open, but the host, service, or application rejected the connection.

**Choosing the right VPC Flow Logs scope**

You can enable flow logs at different levels depending on the investigation goal:

- **VPC-level**
  - Good for broad visibility across an entire VPC.
  - Useful when you do not yet know which subnet or ENI is involved.
- **Subnet-level**
  - Useful when you want to focus on a specific tier, such as public subnets or application subnets.
  - Reduces noise compared with VPC-wide logging.
- **ENI-level**
  - Best for targeted investigations on a specific instance, NAT gateway, load balancer, or other network interface.
  - Often the most precise choice for incident response.

For near-real-time detection, send logs to a destination that supports fast querying and alerting, such as CloudWatch Logs. For longer retention or large-scale analysis, Amazon S3 is common. Some environments also use Amazon Kinesis Data Firehose for delivery pipelines.

**Practical flow log configuration considerations**

When enabling flow logs for denied-traffic investigations, think about these settings:

- **Aggregation interval**
  - Use a shorter interval when you want faster visibility into scans and denies.
  - Use a longer interval when cost or volume is a concern.
- **Traffic type**
  - Capture `REJECT` when you are specifically looking for denied traffic.
  - Capture `ALL` when you need both accepted and rejected flows for correlation.
- **Destination**
  - Use CloudWatch Logs for operational detection and alerting.
  - Use S3 for archival, batch analysis, or Athena queries.
- **Format**
  - Ensure the fields you need are included and understand the exact field names in your chosen destination and parsing format.

A common exam trap is assuming every flow log query uses the same field names. The exact names depend on the flow log format and how the logs are parsed in the destination.

**Example: enable flow logs for a subnet to CloudWatch Logs**

```bash
aws ec2 create-flow-logs \
  --resource-type Subnet \
  --resource-ids subnet-0123456789abcdef0 \
  --traffic-type ALL \
  --log-destination-type cloud-watch-logs \
  --deliver-logs-permission-arn arn:aws:iam::123456789012:role/FlowLogsDeliveryRole \
  --log-group-name /aws/vpc/flowlogs/subnet-a \
  --max-aggregation-interval 60
```

This is a practical starting point when you need both accepted and rejected traffic for a specific subnet. For a scan investigation, you can later narrow the query to `REJECT` events or a specific source IP.

**CloudWatch Logs Insights examples**

If your flow logs are parsed into fields such as `srcAddr`, `dstAddr`, `srcPort`, `dstPort`, and `action`, you can search for denied traffic like this:

```sql
fields @timestamp, srcAddr, dstAddr, srcPort, dstPort, protocol, action
| filter action = "REJECT"
| sort @timestamp desc
| limit 50
```

To look for repeated probes from one source:

```sql
fields srcAddr, dstAddr, dstPort, action
| filter action = "REJECT"
| stats count(*) as rejects by srcAddr, dstAddr, dstPort
| sort rejects desc
| limit 20
```

If your logs are not in the default parsed format, adjust the field names accordingly. For example, some destinations store the raw log line and require parsing before you can query by address or port.

**How to distinguish security group denies from NACL denies**

This is a common exam nuance.

- **Security group deny**
  - Security groups are stateful and evaluate only allowed traffic.
  - If a connection is blocked by a security group, you often see no successful session establishment.
  - Flow logs may show `REJECT` depending on the logging point and path, but the key clue is that the security group rule set does not permit the flow.
- **NACL deny**
  - NACLs are stateless and evaluate inbound and outbound traffic separately.
  - A deny can affect either direction, including return traffic.
  - Flow logs often show `REJECT` on the specific direction that was blocked.

For return traffic, remember the direction of the ports:

- The client initiates from an ephemeral source port to the server’s destination port.
- The server replies from its service port back to the client’s ephemeral port.
- With a stateless NACL, you must allow both directions explicitly.

For example, if a client connects to SSH on port 22:

- Client to server: source ephemeral port -> destination `22`
- Server to client: source `22` -> destination client ephemeral port

A NACL must allow the relevant ephemeral port range in the correct direction for the return path. Do not memorize a single “return traffic” rule without checking the actual flow.

**Example: NACL rule pattern for SSH**

A simplified pattern might look like this:

```text
Inbound:
- Allow TCP 22 from trusted client CIDRs
- Allow TCP 1024-65535 from trusted client CIDRs only if those clients initiate connections to services on the subnet and you need return traffic to reach instances behind the NACL

Outbound:
- Allow TCP 1024-65535 to trusted client CIDRs for return traffic
- Allow TCP 22 only if the subnet initiates SSH sessions outbound to remote hosts
```

The exact rule set depends on the traffic direction and whether the subnet is acting as a client or server. In exam questions, always map the actual packet flow before choosing the rule.

**How to tell whether the deny is at the network, host, or application layer**

Flow logs alone do not always explain why a connection failed. Correlate with other logs:

- **Network layer**
  - VPC Flow Logs show `REJECT` or repeated connection attempts.
  - NACLs or security groups may be blocking the traffic.
- **Host layer**
  - OS firewall logs, `iptables`, `nftables`, Windows Defender Firewall, or host intrusion prevention logs may show the block.
  - The instance may accept the packet at the network layer but drop it locally.
- **Application layer**
  - The service may be listening but rejecting the request.
  - Logs from Nginx, Apache, SSH, RDP, databases, or custom applications can show authentication or protocol failures.

A useful pattern is: flow logs show `ACCEPT`, but the application still fails. That usually means the network path is open and the problem is inside the host or service.

**Example host-side checks**

```bash
sudo ss -lntp
sudo iptables -S
sudo nft list ruleset
sudo journalctl -u sshd --since "1 hour ago"
```

On Windows, you might inspect:

```powershell
Get-NetFirewallRule | Where-Object {$_.Enabled -eq "True"}
Get-WinEvent -LogName Security -MaxEvents 20
```

**Investigating scans against different AWS targets**

Different targets produce different evidence patterns.

- **EC2 instance**
  - GuardDuty may flag `Recon:EC2/Portscan` or `PortProbe`.
  - Flow logs show repeated attempts to many ports or repeated attempts to one port.
  - Host logs may show connection attempts or service-level rejections.
- **Load balancer**
  - The scan may hit the load balancer listener rather than the backend instance.
  - Check listener ports, target group health, and access logs if enabled.
- **NAT gateway**
  - Outbound traffic from private instances may appear as traffic through the NAT gateway.
  - Investigate the source instance and destination IPs rather than treating the NAT gateway as the target.
- **Peered VPC**
  - The source may be in another AWS account or VPC.
  - Check route tables, peering permissions, and security controls on both sides.
- **VPN or Direct Connect**
  - The source may be on-premises or in a connected network.
  - Correlate with on-prem firewall logs and network monitoring.
- **Internal lateral movement**
  - The source may be another EC2 instance, container host, or compromised workload.
  - Look for east-west traffic and unusual source behavior.

**Practical investigation workflow**

1. Review the GuardDuty finding.
2. Identify the source IP, destination IP, destination port, and affected resource.
3. Check whether the target is public, private, behind a load balancer, or reachable through hybrid connectivity.
4. Query VPC Flow Logs for the source and destination pair.
5. Determine whether the traffic was `ACCEPT` or `REJECT`.
6. Correlate with security group rules, NACL rules, and route tables.
7. Check host logs if the network path was allowed but the service still failed.
8. Decide whether the activity is reconnaissance, misconfiguration, or active compromise.
9. Apply containment if needed.

**Example containment and response actions**

- **Isolate the instance**
  - Move the instance into a quarantine security group with minimal access.
  - Detach or restrict routes if the incident requires stronger containment.
- **Block the source**
  - Update security groups, NACLs, or upstream firewall controls.
  - Use AWS Network Firewall if centralized filtering is in place.
- **Preserve evidence**
  - Keep flow logs, GuardDuty findings, CloudTrail events, and host logs.
  - Avoid destructive changes before you capture what happened.
- **Notify and automate**
  - Send GuardDuty findings to Security Hub.
  - Use EventBridge to trigger Lambda, SNS, or ticketing workflows.
- **Tune detection**
  - Use GuardDuty suppression rules for known benign scanners or approved vulnerability assessment tools.
  - Do not suppress until you confirm the activity is expected and documented.

**Example EventBridge rule for GuardDuty findings**

```json
{
  "source": ["aws.guardduty"],
  "detail-type": ["GuardDuty Finding"],
  "detail": {
    "severity": [{ "numeric": [">=", 4] }],
    "type": [
      { "prefix": "Recon:EC2/" }
    ]
  }
}
```

This pattern can route reconnaissance findings into an automated response pipeline. In practice, you may refine it by account, region, resource type, or severity.

**Security Hub and operational workflow**

Security Hub is useful when you want a single place to aggregate findings from GuardDuty and other services. It helps you:

- Normalize findings across accounts and Regions.
- Prioritize by severity and compliance context.
- Feed findings into incident response or ticketing systems.
- Track remediation status over time.

For exam scenarios, remember that GuardDuty detects, Security Hub aggregates, and EventBridge automates.

**Common exam pitfalls**

- Assuming all port scans are equally dangerous.
- Treating private subnets as unreachable from attackers.
- Forgetting that NACLs are stateless.
- Confusing security group behavior with NACL behavior.
- Ignoring ephemeral ports in return traffic.
- Assuming flow logs show packet payloads or application details.
- Using only network logs when the issue is actually host-based or application-based.
- Overlooking peered VPCs, VPN, Direct Connect, or internal lateral movement paths.
- Choosing a response that blocks evidence collection before you preserve logs.

**Quick comparison: GuardDuty, Flow Logs, and host logs**

| Source | Best for | Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| GuardDuty | Managed detection and prioritization | Finding context may need correlation |
| VPC Flow Logs | Network metadata and deny/accept patterns | No payloads or app context |
| Host logs | Service and OS-level rejection details | Only available if you can access the host |

**Key takeaways**

- Use GuardDuty to identify suspicious scanning and denied traffic patterns quickly.
- Use VPC Flow Logs to confirm source, destination, ports, and accept/reject behavior.
- Use security group and NACL logic to determine where the block occurs.
- Remember that NACLs are stateless and require explicit return-path rules.
- Correlate with host logs when the network path is allowed but the connection still fails.
- Investigate internal and hybrid paths, not just internet-facing targets.
- Use Security Hub and EventBridge to operationalize detection and response.
