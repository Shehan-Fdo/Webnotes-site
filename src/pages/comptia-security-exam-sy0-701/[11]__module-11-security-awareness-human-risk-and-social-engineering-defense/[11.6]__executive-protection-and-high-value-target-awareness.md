## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Why executive protection matters**

High-value targets attract attackers because they can unlock money, sensitive data, and trusted access. In practice, executives and other senior personnel are attractive targets for phishing variants such as **whaling** and business email compromise (BEC), because their authority can speed up fraudulent approvals and data access.

Attackers do not rely on generic spam when they target these people. They use tailored social engineering, urgency, and impersonation to increase the chance of success. Executive-focused awareness training matters because standard awareness programs may not fully address the unique risk profile of people who handle the organization’s most sensitive assets.

- High-value targets are often pursued for financial gain and access to valuable information.
- **Whaling** focuses on senior leaders such as CEOs and CFOs.
- **BEC** uses deception to trick people into transferring funds or exposing data.
- Sophisticated attacks may include deepfake-enabled impersonation and other advanced social engineering.

**Common attack patterns**

Whaling is a targeted phishing method that impersonates executives or trusted contacts. The attacker usually wants the victim to take a fast action, such as sending money, opening a malicious attachment, or revealing confidential information. CEO fraud is closely related, but the emphasis differs: whaling targets the executive directly, while CEO fraud uses the executive’s identity to target others.

These attacks work because they exploit authority, urgency, and trust. The message often looks legitimate and may reference real business context, which makes it harder to spot than broad phishing campaigns.

- **Authority abuse** is central to whaling and CEO fraud.
- Urgent language pushes victims to act before verifying.
- Trusted identities can be copied through email spoofing or impersonation.
- Multi-channel impersonation increases risk when attackers move beyond email.

**Defensive controls**

A layered defense works best for executive protection. No single control stops every attempt, so you combine technical safeguards, process controls, and targeted training. Email authentication helps reduce spoofing, while behavioral detection and risk simulations improve detection and response.

> Executive protection is not only about the executive’s inbox. It also includes the people, processes, and devices that can approve payments or expose sensitive data.

Key controls include:

- **MFA**: Multi-factor authentication reduces the value of stolen credentials.
- **DMARC**, **SPF**, and **DKIM**: These email authentication controls help validate sender identity.
- Dual-authorization workflows: Require more than one approval for sensitive transactions.
- Executive-specific training: Teach leaders and assistants to verify unusual requests.
- Risk simulations: Practice response to phishing, impersonation, and fraud scenarios.

**Practical awareness checklist**

Use a simple verification habit whenever a request is unusual, urgent, or financial. The goal is to slow down the decision and confirm the request through a trusted channel.

1. Verify the sender through a separate channel before acting.
2. Check whether the request matches normal business process.
3. Confirm payment or data requests with a second approver.
4. Report suspicious messages to the security team immediately.
5. Review whether the request uses urgency, secrecy, or authority pressure.

**What to remember for the exam**

Security+ expects you to recognize that executives are high-value targets and that their exposure extends beyond the office. Personal data exposure, home network risk, and public visibility can all increase the attack surface. The best answer usually combines awareness, authentication, and process controls rather than relying on user caution alone.

- Executives are targeted because they have access and influence.
- Whaling and BEC are social engineering threats with financial impact.
- Layered defenses reduce risk better than a single control.
- Training should reflect the target’s role and the attacker’s tactics.
