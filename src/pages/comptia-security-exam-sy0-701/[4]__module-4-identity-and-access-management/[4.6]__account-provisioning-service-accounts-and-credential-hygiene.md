## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Account provisioning and deprovisioning**

Account provisioning is the process of creating and granting access to users, while deprovisioning removes access when it is no longer needed. Good identity and access management (IAM) keeps access aligned with job role and business need.

Provisioning and deprovisioning matter because forgotten accounts and lingering credentials increase risk. When access is not removed promptly, former employees, vendors, or role-changed users may retain active logins that no longer match their responsibilities.

- Grant only the access required for the current role.
- Remove access when a user leaves, changes roles, or no longer needs a resource.
- Review accounts regularly to find stale or risky access.
- Treat provisioning and deprovisioning as part of the full account lifecycle, not one-time tasks.

**Service accounts and non-human identities**

Service accounts are non-human identities used by applications, jobs, and automation. They often run continuously and may have persistent access, which makes them a distinct attack surface from human users.

Because service accounts can operate without normal human patterns, they may not trigger the same behavioral alerts as interactive logins. If an attacker compromises a service account, the activity can look like legitimate automation.

- Use service accounts only when a workload needs machine-to-machine access.
- Apply **least privilege** so the account can do only what the service requires.
- Avoid broad administrative access unless it is truly necessary.
- Monitor service account activity because it may blend in with normal system behavior.

> Service accounts are especially risky when they are old, overprivileged, or forgotten. A long-lived machine identity can become a quiet path to persistent access.

**Credential hygiene**

Credential hygiene is the ongoing practice of managing, securing, and auditing credentials to reduce unauthorized access. It includes strong password practices, rotation, reuse prevention, and access review.

Poor credential hygiene is a common cause of identity-related breaches. Stale passwords, reused secrets, and standing privileges expand the attack surface and make compromise easier to exploit.

- Use strong passwords and avoid reuse across accounts.
- Rotate passwords and secrets regularly.
- Audit accounts to find stale, unused, or overprivileged credentials.
- Reduce standing privileges where possible.
- Detect compromised credentials and respond quickly.

**Secret rotation and credential lifecycle**

Secret rotation means replacing sensitive credentials on a regular or policy-driven basis. This applies to passwords, access keys, tokens, and cryptographic keys.

Rotation reduces the value of stolen credentials and limits how long a compromised secret remains useful. In practice, rotation usually means generating a new secret, validating it, updating dependent systems, and retiring the old secret.

- Rotate access keys and passwords frequently.
- Prefer automated rotation when the environment supports it.
- Validate the new credential before retiring the old one.
- Update all dependent systems so rotation does not break service.

**Practical patterns for automation**

Some environments support automated rotation for secrets tied to resources with two sets of credentials. This approach helps you update one credential set while the other remains available, reducing downtime risk.

For Kubernetes workloads, service account token rotation can reduce the risk of long-lived credentials. Short-lived tokens and automated replacement lower exposure if a token is compromised.

```bash
# Example pattern for credential rotation planning
1. Create a new credential.
2. Validate the new credential with the application or service.
3. Update dependent systems to use the new value.
4. Revoke or retire the old credential after the grace period.
```

**What to remember for the exam**

- **Provisioning** adds access; **deprovisioning** removes it.
- Service accounts are machine identities and need separate oversight from human users.
- Credential hygiene focuses on strong, unique, monitored, and rotated credentials.
- Secret rotation shortens the useful life of compromised passwords, keys, and tokens.
- Least privilege and regular review reduce the impact of stale or excessive access.
