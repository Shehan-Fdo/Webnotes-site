## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Privileged Access Management overview**

Privileged Access Management (PAM) focuses on controlling elevated access to sensitive systems, accounts, and resources. The goal is to give users and non-human identities only the access they need, when they need it, and no more.

PAM matters because standing access creates a larger attack window. If privileged credentials are always available, attackers and malicious insiders have more time to abuse them, move laterally, and reach sensitive data.

- **Least privilege** is the core idea behind PAM.
- **Standing access** means privileges remain available all the time.
- **Granular access** limits permissions to a specific resource or task.

**Just-in-Time access**

Just-in-Time (JIT) access grants elevated privileges only for a predetermined period and only for a specific task. Instead of keeping admin rights active, you activate them when needed and remove them when the task ends.

This approach reduces exposure by shrinking the time window in which privileged credentials can be misused. It also helps enforce least privilege by limiting access by time, location, and action.

- **JIT** reduces standing privileges.
- **Time-limited access** lowers the chance of credential abuse.
- **Task-specific access** keeps elevation tied to a real need.

> JIT is most useful when you want to minimize the amount of time a user or service can operate with elevated rights.

**Just Enough Administration**

Just Enough Administration (JEA) is a model for delegating administrative work with only the minimum permissions required. It is commonly described as a way to perform admin tasks without granting broad administrative control.

JEA aligns with least privilege by narrowing what an account can do. In practice, it helps reduce attack surface and limits the damage if an account is misused.

- **JEA** focuses on minimum required permissions.
- It supports administrative delegation without full admin rights.
- It helps reduce lateral movement opportunities.

**JIT vs. JEA**

JIT and JEA solve related but different problems. JIT controls *when* elevated access exists, while JEA controls *how much* privilege is available for the task.

| Control | Primary focus | Practical effect |
|---|---|---|
| JIT | Timing | Grants access only for a limited period |
| JEA | Scope | Grants only the permissions needed for the task |

Use JIT when you want to avoid always-on elevation. Use JEA when you want to restrict what an administrator can do even during an approved session.

**Why these controls matter**

These models reduce risk from both external attackers and insider threats. They are especially valuable when broad, static, long-term access would otherwise expose critical systems.

They also fit modern environments where access needs change quickly. Dynamic work patterns and cloud-based systems make permanent privilege harder to justify, so time-bound and task-bound access becomes more practical.

- **Reduced exposure** means fewer opportunities for misuse.
- **Smaller attack surface** means fewer privileged paths to defend.
- **Better control** means easier monitoring of who has access and why.

**Practical study points**

When you see a scenario in an exam question, look for the control that matches the problem:

1. If the issue is users having admin rights all the time, think JIT or PAM.
2. If the issue is users needing only a narrow set of admin actions, think JEA.
3. If the issue is limiting access to a specific timeframe, choose JIT.
4. If the issue is limiting permissions to only what the task requires, choose JEA.

**Key takeaway**

PAM is the broader discipline, while JIT and JEA are techniques that make privileged access safer. Together, they support least privilege by reducing both the duration and scope of elevated access.
