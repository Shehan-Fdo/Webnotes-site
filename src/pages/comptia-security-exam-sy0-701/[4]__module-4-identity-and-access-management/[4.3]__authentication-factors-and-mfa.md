## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Authentication factors**

Authentication factors are the traits or evidence you use to verify identity before granting access. In practice, they answer a simple question: does this user prove they are who they claim to be?

The common factor categories are:

- **Knowledge**: something you know, such as a password or PIN.
- **Possession**: something you have, such as a physical token or trusted device.
- **Inherence**: something you are, such as a biometric trait.

These factors are the building blocks for stronger authentication designs. Passwords alone are widely targeted, so combining factors reduces the chance that a single stolen secret leads to account compromise.

**Multi-factor authentication**

<abbr title="Multi-Factor Authentication">MFA</abbr> requires more than one factor to authenticate. It adds an extra layer on top of a password or other primary method, which makes unauthorized access harder even if one factor is exposed.

Common MFA patterns include:

- A password plus a one-time code.
- A password plus a device-based approval.
- A password plus a biometric check.

One-time codes are often delivered by text, email, or an authenticator app. They can prove access to a trusted device or account, but they are not equally strong in every delivery method. Text and email are commonly treated as less secure channels than phishing-resistant methods.

> Use conservative language on the exam: MFA improves security, but not every MFA method offers the same resistance to phishing or interception.

**Passwordless authentication**

Passwordless authentication removes the need to enter a password during sign-in. Instead, you authenticate with a secure alternative such as a biometric or device-based verification.

This approach matters because passwords remain a primary attack vector and a source of user friction. Moving away from passwords can improve the user experience and reduce help desk burden while also supporting a stronger security posture.

Key points to remember:

- Passwordless authentication simplifies login by removing the password step.
- It can reduce reliance on password reuse and password-related attacks.
- It fits well with a Zero Trust approach that verifies access more explicitly.

**Phishing-resistant authentication**

Phishing-resistant methods are designed to resist credential theft and replay attacks. Microsoft guidance recommends moving toward phishing-resistant passwordless authentication as part of a broader Zero Trust strategy.

Use this idea to separate stronger methods from basic MFA:

- **Less secure MFA** may still rely on passwords plus a text message.
- **Stronger authentication** can require phishing-resistant combinations for sensitive resources.
- **Authentication strength** policies can define which combinations are allowed for a resource.

An authentication strength is a Conditional Access control that specifies which authentication method combinations users can use. You can require stronger methods for sensitive resources and allow less strict combinations for lower-risk access.

**How to think about the options**

Use the method that matches the risk and the user experience you need. MFA is a baseline improvement over passwords alone, while passwordless and phishing-resistant methods raise the bar further.

| Method | Core idea | Practical takeaway |
|---|---|---|
| Password only | One knowledge factor | Weakest common option; highly exposed to theft and reuse |
| MFA | Two or more factors | Better than passwords alone, but strength depends on the factors used |
| Passwordless | No password entry | Reduces password risk and user friction |
| Phishing-resistant authentication | Resistant to interception and replay | Best fit for sensitive access and Zero Trust alignment |

**Exam focus**

For Security+ purposes, focus on the relationship between the terms rather than memorizing vendor-specific features.

- Authentication factors are the categories used to prove identity.
- MFA combines multiple factors.
- Passwordless removes the password from the sign-in flow.
- Phishing-resistant methods are a stronger subset of authentication approaches.
- Conditional Access can enforce different authentication strengths for different resources.

**Practical study cues**

When you see a scenario, ask what problem the organization is trying to solve:

- If the issue is password theft, look for MFA or passwordless options.
- If the issue is phishing, prefer phishing-resistant methods.
- If the issue is user friction, passwordless may be the best fit.
- If the issue is protecting sensitive data, use stronger authentication requirements.
