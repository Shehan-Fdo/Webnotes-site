## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**AAA and access control overview**

AAA stands for **authentication**, **authorization**, and **accounting**. In Security+ terms, these three functions work together to verify identity, decide what a user can do, and record what happened. Access control is the broader practice of restricting access to systems, data, and resources based on defined security policies.

A practical way to think about the flow is:

- **Authentication** answers “who are you?”
- **Authorization** answers “what can you do?”
- **Accounting** answers “what did you do?”

Access control is a core part of identity and access management (IAM). It helps ensure users get the access they need for their duties while reducing unauthorized access to sensitive information.

**Access control fundamentals**

Access control applies to both physical and logical environments.

- **Physical access control** protects buildings, devices, and documents.
- **Logical access control** protects digital assets such as systems, applications, and data.

Controls can also vary by scope:

- **Fine-grained control** makes precise access decisions.
- **Coarse-grained control** is simpler and broader.

Modern environments often use more than one model because different resources and risk levels need different enforcement styles. In practice, access control is most effective when it supports least privilege, regular auditing, policy-based decisions, and integration with IAM.

> Access control is only useful when it is consistently enforced and reviewed. Misconfiguration can create access that looks valid on paper but is too broad in practice.

**Core access control models**

The main models you should recognize for SY0-701 are DAC, MAC, RBAC, and ABAC. Other models may appear in broader industry discussions, but these four are the most important for exam prep.

| Model | How access is decided | Typical strength |
|---|---|---|
| **DAC** | The resource owner decides who gets access | Flexible and user-driven |
| **MAC** | A central authority enforces access rules | Strict and highly controlled |
| **RBAC** | Access is based on job role | Simple to manage at scale |
| **ABAC** | Access is based on attributes and conditions | Highly precise and policy-driven |

**Discretionary Access Control (DAC)**

DAC gives the owner of a resource control over who can access it. If you create the resource, you can usually decide who else can use it.

- Works well for collaboration.
- Gives users more control over their own resources.
- Is less suitable for highly sensitive data because control is decentralized.

**Mandatory Access Control (MAC)**

MAC uses centrally enforced rules. Users and resources are assigned security labels, and access is determined by policy rather than by the resource owner.

- Supports strict enforcement.
- Reduces user discretion.
- Fits environments where access must be tightly controlled.

**Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)**

RBAC assigns permissions to roles instead of individual users. A user receives access by being placed into a role.

- Simplifies administration.
- Matches access to job function.
- Works well when many users need the same permissions.

**Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC)**

ABAC evaluates attributes before granting access. Attributes can describe the user, the resource, the environment, or the action.

- Supports more detailed decisions than role-only models.
- Works well when access depends on context.
- Is useful when policy needs to consider multiple conditions at once.

**Other models you may see**

Some sources mention additional models such as rule-based, relationship-based, policy-based, and history-based access control. These are useful to recognize, but they are less central than DAC, MAC, RBAC, and ABAC for Security+ study.

- **Rule-based access control** uses predefined rules to make decisions.
- **Relationship-based access control** considers relationships between entities.
- **Policy-based access control** applies organizational policy to access decisions.
- **History-based access control** can factor in prior behavior or access history.

**How to choose the right model**

When you compare models, focus on who controls access and how decisions are made.

1. Identify whether the environment needs user-driven flexibility or centralized enforcement.
2. Determine whether access should follow job function, labels, attributes, or explicit rules.
3. Match the model to the sensitivity of the data and the need for auditability.
4. Use the simplest model that still meets the security requirement.

A quick exam clue:

- If the owner decides, think **DAC**.
- If a central authority decides, think **MAC**.
- If the job role decides, think **RBAC**.
- If multiple conditions decide, think **ABAC**.

**Best-practice themes**

Access control works best when it is part of a larger security program rather than a standalone setting.

- Enforce **least privilege** so users only get the access they need.
- Review permissions regularly through auditing.
- Use policy-based access decisions where appropriate.
- Integrate access control with IAM processes such as provisioning and removal of access.
- Consider both digital and physical access when designing controls.

**Exam focus reminders**

Security+ questions often test your ability to match a scenario to the correct model or AAA function.

- Authentication verifies identity.
- Authorization determines permissions.
- Accounting records activity.
- DAC emphasizes owner control.
- MAC emphasizes centralized control.
- RBAC emphasizes job roles.
- ABAC emphasizes attributes and conditions.

If a scenario mentions users, vendors, contractors, or customers needing different access at different times, think about how IAM and access control work together to give the right permissions to the right people at the right time.
