## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Threat actor fundamentals**

A **threat actor** is an individual or group that intentionally causes harm in the digital sphere. In practice, you use the term to describe the entity behind an attack so you can better understand motive, capability, and likely next steps.

Threat actors target devices, networks, and systems by exploiting weaknesses. Their actions can include disruptive attacks, malware, phishing, extortion, espionage, and data theft.

- Threat actors can be **individuals** or **groups**.
- They may be inside your organization or outside it.
- Their behavior is shaped by their goals, resources, and tactics.
- Knowing the actor helps you interpret why an incident happened.

**Why adversary categories matter**

Adversary categories help you group attackers by common traits instead of treating every incident as isolated. This makes it easier to predict behavior and choose defenses that fit the threat.

A practical profile usually includes:
- **Motivation**: why the actor attacks
- **Capability**: how skilled and well-resourced the actor is
- **Tactics**: the methods used to reach the goal
- **Targeting**: who or what the actor focuses on

> Use conservative labeling when evidence is limited. If you cannot identify the actor with confidence, describe the observed behavior and likely motive instead of overcommitting to a category.

**Common threat actor perspectives**

Threat actors are often discussed by where they operate from or what drives them. The evidence highlights several useful viewpoints:

- **Nation-state actors** act on behalf of a government or nation-state.
- They are often highly sophisticated and well-resourced.
- Their goals can include political, military, or economic advantage.
- They may conduct cyber espionage, data theft, or advanced persistent threats (APTs).

- **Insider threat actors** work within the organization.
- They can create risk because they already have legitimate access or knowledge.
- Their actions may be intentional or harmful to the organization’s security.

- **Cybercriminals** and organized groups focus on financial gain or other harmful outcomes.
- They commonly exploit weaknesses in networks and software.
- Their attacks may be disruptive, deceptive, or extortion-based.

- **Cyber terrorists** are another example of a distinct threat actor type.
- They pose unique security challenges because their objectives can extend beyond simple theft or disruption.

**Location and relationship to the target**

Another useful way to think about adversaries is whether they are internal or external to the organization. This distinction affects access, visibility, and response.

- **Internal actors** may already have access to systems or data.
- **External actors** usually need to gain access through exposed services, social engineering, or other attack paths.
- Both can exploit weaknesses, but their opportunities and detection patterns differ.

**Motives you should recognize**

Threat actors are not defined only by skill. Their motives often explain the type of attack you see.

- **Financial gain** is a common driver.
- **Political leverage** can motivate government-linked or ideologically driven activity.
- **Revenge** may drive harmful insider behavior or targeted attacks.
- **Espionage** focuses on stealing information for strategic advantage.

**How to use actor context during analysis**

When you investigate an incident, start by asking who benefits and what the attacker likely wanted. That context helps you narrow the category and prioritize response.

1. Identify the observable behavior.
2. Note whether the actor appears internal or external.
3. Look for motive indicators such as theft, disruption, or espionage.
4. Match the behavior to the most conservative category that fits the evidence.
5. Use that category to guide containment and future monitoring.

**Exam focus**

For SY0-701, be ready to recognize that:
- A threat actor is the entity causing the security event.
- Categories are useful because they connect motive, capability, and tactics.
- Nation-state, insider, criminal, and terrorist-style actors are all relevant examples.
- The same attack technique can be used by different adversaries for different reasons.

**Quick comparison**

| Category | Typical relationship | Common motive | Notable trait |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nation-state | External, government-linked | Political, military, economic | Highly resourced |
| Insider | Internal | Revenge, misuse, sabotage, convenience | Legitimate access |
| Cybercriminal | External or organized group | Financial gain | Exploits weaknesses |
| Cyber terrorist | External or ideologically driven | Disruption, fear, broader impact | Unique security challenge |

**Key takeaway**

You do not classify threat actors just to name them. You classify them to improve your understanding of intent, capability, and likely attack paths. That context makes your response more accurate and your defenses more targeted.
