## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Overview**

Common attack vectors often rely on trust, urgency, and familiar communication channels. In the evidence, phishing appears as a broad category that includes email, SMS, voice calls, and targeted website-based lures. Watering hole attacks use a different approach: attackers compromise websites that a target group already visits and wait for victims to arrive.

These attacks matter because they can lead to credential theft, malware delivery, or broader network compromise. A practical exam focus is recognizing the delivery method and the attacker’s target selection.

**Phishing family**

Phishing is a social engineering attack that usually uses email to trick users into taking an action that benefits the attacker. The goal is often to get the victim to open an attachment, download a file, or enter credentials.

- **Phishing** is broad and often sent to many recipients with limited personalization.
- **Spear phishing** targets a specific person or group and uses details that make the message more convincing.
- **Whaling** targets high-value individuals, such as executives or other prominent roles.
- **Smishing** uses SMS text messages as the delivery channel.
- **Vishing** uses voice calls to pressure the target into revealing information.
- Phishing can also include website-based or search-engine-based lures, not just email.

A useful exam distinction is scope. Generic phishing casts a wide net, while spear phishing and whaling are more targeted and tailored.

**How phishing works**

Phishing messages try to push you into acting before you verify the source. The attacker may impersonate a legitimate organization or a trusted contact and use urgency, curiosity, or fear to lower your guard.

- The message may ask you to click a link, open an attachment, or provide credentials.
- The attacker may personalize the message to increase credibility.
- The attack can be the initial step in a larger compromise, including ransomware delivery.

> Phishing is often effective because it targets people, not just systems.

**Watering hole attacks**

A watering hole attack targets a group by infecting websites they commonly visit. Instead of sending a direct message to the victim, the attacker compromises a trusted site and waits for the target to browse there.

- The attacker focuses on a specific audience that shares a common website or portal.
- The compromised site can deliver malware or malicious code to visitors.
- The attack may aim to infect a user’s device and then reach a connected corporate network.
- The technique depends on trust in familiar websites and can be difficult to detect quickly.

This attack differs from phishing because the victim is not necessarily lured by a direct message. The compromise happens through a site the target already trusts.

**Comparison of common vectors**

| Vector | Typical channel | Targeting style | Common goal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Phishing | Email | Broad | Steal credentials or trigger malicious action |
| Spear phishing | Email or similar message | Specific person or group | Increase success with personalization |
| Whaling | Email or similar message | High-value individual | Gain access through a privileged target |
| Smishing | SMS | Broad or targeted | Trick the user into responding or clicking |
| Vishing | Voice call | Broad or targeted | Extract information through conversation |
| Watering hole | Trusted website | Specific group | Deliver malware or gain network access |

**Recognition cues**

When you see an attack scenario, identify the delivery path first. Then look for the level of targeting and the attacker’s objective.

- If the attack arrives by email and is widely distributed, think **phishing**.
- If the message is tailored to one person or role, think **spear phishing** or **whaling**.
- If the attack uses text messages, think **smishing**.
- If it uses a phone call, think **vishing**.
- If a trusted website is compromised to infect visitors, think **watering hole**.

**Practical defense mindset**

The evidence emphasizes awareness and layered protection. User education helps reduce success rates, but technical controls still matter because attackers adapt their methods.

- Train users to verify unexpected requests before acting.
- Treat urgent requests for credentials or file downloads with caution.
- Keep websites and applications patched to reduce watering hole exposure.
- Use layered defenses because no single control stops every vector.

**Key takeaway**

Common attack vectors are less about the tool and more about the path into the target. Phishing variants exploit communication channels, while watering hole attacks exploit trusted websites. For exam purposes, match the channel, the target scope, and the attacker’s goal.
