## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Security control fundamentals**

Security controls are the safeguards you use to reduce risk to data, systems, people, and physical assets. A strong control strategy uses multiple control types together so one control can prevent an issue, another can detect it, and another can help you recover.

Security controls support confidentiality, integrity, and availability by limiting unauthorized access, spotting suspicious activity, and reducing impact when an event occurs. They also help protect people and facilities, not just information.

- **Preventive** controls try to stop an event before it happens.
- **Detective** controls identify events or suspicious activity after they occur or while they are happening.
- **Corrective** controls reduce impact and restore normal operations after an incident.
- **Compensatory** controls provide an alternate way to meet a security need when the preferred control is not available.
- **Deterrent** controls discourage unwanted behavior by increasing the perceived cost or risk.

**Major control categories**

A common way to group controls is by how they are implemented. The three broad categories are technical, administrative, and physical controls. These categories help you map a control to the right layer of defense.

| Category | What it focuses on | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| Technical | Technology-based enforcement and monitoring | Access control, alerting, and recovery support |
| Administrative | Policies, procedures, and governance | Security rules, workforce guidance, and oversight |
| Physical | Protection of facilities, equipment, and people | Barriers, locks, and environmental protection |

Technical controls use systems or software to enforce security. Administrative controls shape how people work and how the organization manages security. Physical controls protect tangible assets from unauthorized access and other physical threats.

**Control goals and how they work together**

Controls are most effective when they are layered. One control may prevent an attack, another may detect it, and a third may limit damage. This layered approach is important because no single control stops every threat.

- Preventive controls reduce the chance of compromise.
- Detective controls give you visibility into events and anomalies.
- Corrective controls help you respond and recover.
- Deterrent controls influence attacker behavior before an incident starts.
- Compensatory controls fill gaps when a primary control is not practical.

A practical security strategy usually combines several control goals rather than relying on one type alone.

**Administrative controls**

Administrative controls are the policies, procedures, and management actions that direct security behavior. They define expectations for the workforce and guide how security measures are selected, implemented, and maintained.

Common administrative examples include:
- Security policies and procedures
- Risk assessments
- Security awareness training
- Incident response planning
- Workforce guidance such as password policies and data handling rules

Administrative controls are especially important because they shape consistent behavior across the organization. They also support oversight, accountability, and repeatable security practices.

**Physical controls**

Physical controls protect buildings, rooms, devices, and people from unauthorized access or harm. They are part of a broader security strategy because physical threats can lead to theft, disruption, or compromise of systems and data.

Examples of physical controls include:
- Locks and barriers
- Badges or access cards
- Security guards
- Cameras and monitoring
- Environmental protections for facilities and equipment

Physical security is not separate from cybersecurity. If an attacker can reach a device, server room, or workspace, they may bypass logical protections entirely.

**Technical controls**

Technical controls are implemented through technology. They help enforce access, monitor activity, and support response and recovery.

Examples include:
- Authentication and access control systems
- Logging and alerting tools
- Endpoint protections
- Backup and recovery mechanisms
- Security monitoring systems

Technical controls are often the most visible controls in day-to-day operations, but they work best when supported by administrative policy and physical protection.

**How to think about exam questions**

When you see a control in a question, identify both its category and its purpose. The same control can fit more than one description depending on the context, so focus on the primary function described in the scenario.

1. Identify whether the control is technical, administrative, or physical.
2. Determine whether it prevents, detects, corrects, deters, or compensates.
3. Match the control to the risk it addresses.
4. Choose the answer that best fits the scenario, not just the label.

> If a question describes a policy, procedure, or training activity, it usually points to an administrative control. If it describes a device, software feature, or monitoring tool, it usually points to a technical control. If it describes barriers, locks, or facility protection, it usually points to a physical control.

**Quick comparison**

| Control type | Primary purpose | Example focus |
|---|---|---|
| Preventive | Stop an event | Reduce the chance of unauthorized access |
| Detective | Find an event | Alert on suspicious activity |
| Corrective | Limit damage and restore | Recover after an incident |
| Compensatory | Replace a missing control | Alternate safeguard when the preferred one is unavailable |
| Deterrent | Discourage action | Increase the perceived risk to an attacker |

**Key takeaway**

Security controls work as a system, not as isolated items. For Security+ exam prep, be ready to classify controls by category and by goal, then explain how they support layered defense across technology, process, and physical security.
