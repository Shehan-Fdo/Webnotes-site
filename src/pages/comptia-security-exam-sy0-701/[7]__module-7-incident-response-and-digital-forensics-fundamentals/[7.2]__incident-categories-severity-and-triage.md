## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Incident categories and why they matter**

Security incidents are not all equal. A useful response process starts by classifying the event so you can decide what it affects, how urgent it is, and who needs to act. In practice, incident categories and severity levels give you a shared language for response, communication, and resource allocation.

A standardized severity framework helps you triage incidents based on business impact and urgency. It also improves communication across teams, streamlines response, and supports service objectives such as faster restoration and better mean time to repair (MTTR).

- **Classification** groups incidents by type or impact so you can compare them consistently.
- **Severity** reflects how much business damage or urgency the incident creates.
- **Triage** is the quick assessment step that determines what needs immediate attention and what can wait.

**Severity levels**

Severity levels are commonly expressed on a scale, such as 1 through 5, with the highest level representing the most critical outage or impact. The exact labels can vary by organization, but the goal stays the same: match response effort to risk.

A practical severity model should be clear enough for responders to use quickly and stable enough for stakeholders to trust. Regular review matters because business needs change, systems change, and incident patterns change.

| Severity focus | Typical meaning |
|---|---|
| Critical | Major outage or severe business impact |
| High | Significant disruption that needs fast response |
| Medium | Noticeable issue with limited business impact |
| Low | Minor issue that can be handled with lower urgency |
| Informational | Event worth recording but not requiring immediate action |

- Use the same severity definitions across teams to reduce confusion.
- Revisit severity criteria regularly so they stay aligned with current operations.
- Keep the scale practical; responders should be able to apply it quickly during an active event.

> If severity definitions are vague or inconsistent, triage becomes slower and response decisions become harder to defend.

**Triage in incident response**

Triage is the process of sorting alerts and incidents so you focus on the most important threats first. It is similar to emergency-room triage: you quickly identify what is most urgent, what is likely false positive noise, and what can safely wait.

This step matters because security teams often face alert overload from sources such as endpoint detection and response (EDR), security information and event management (SIEM), threat intelligence feeds, and internal logs. Without structured triage, teams can waste time on low-value alerts while real threats escalate.

- **Goal:** identify true risk quickly.
- **Benefit:** reduce delay in incident response.
- **Outcome:** lower breach impact and better use of analyst time.

**A practical triage workflow**

1. Collect the alert or incident details from available tools and logs.
2. Determine whether the event is likely real or a false positive.
3. Assess the affected systems, users, data, and business process.
4. Assign a severity level based on impact and urgency.
5. Escalate or route the incident to the right response path.

Triage is not just about speed. It is about making a defensible decision with limited time and incomplete information. The better your triage process, the more consistently your team can separate urgent incidents from background noise.

**How severity and triage work together**

Triage feeds severity assignment, and severity drives response priority. You first decide what the event appears to be, then you decide how serious it is, and then you act accordingly. This relationship keeps response organized and helps teams allocate resources where they matter most.

- Triage answers, “What is this and how urgent is it?”
- Severity answers, “How much does this affect the business?”
- Response planning answers, “What do we do next?”

**Operational best practices**

Clear severity definitions and repeatable triage steps improve incident management. They also help teams communicate with stakeholders in a consistent way, which is especially important when an incident affects service availability or business operations.

- Define severity criteria in advance.
- Train responders to apply the same criteria consistently.
- Update the framework when business priorities or systems change.
- Use triage to reduce false positives before escalation.
- Document decisions so later review is easier.

**Exam focus**

For Security+ SY0-701, focus on the relationship between incident classification, severity, and triage. You should be able to explain why organizations use severity levels, how triage reduces noise, and how both support faster and more effective incident response.

- Severity levels help prioritize incidents by impact and urgency.
- Triage helps identify which alerts deserve immediate attention.
- Standardized processes improve communication and response efficiency.
