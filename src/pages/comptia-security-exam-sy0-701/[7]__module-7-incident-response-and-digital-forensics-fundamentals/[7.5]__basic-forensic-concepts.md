## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Digital forensics overview**

Digital forensics is the process of identifying, preserving, analyzing, and presenting digital evidence. In Security+ terms, the focus is not just on finding data, but on handling it in a way that supports incident response and legal use.

The core goal is to preserve evidence without changing it. That means you work carefully, document actions, and prefer analysis on copies rather than originals.

- **Digital evidence** can support cybersecurity investigations and criminal cases.
- **Integrity** matters because altered evidence may be unreliable in court or during an investigation.
- **Chain of custody** helps show who handled evidence and when.

**Evidence types**

A practical forensic workflow starts by recognizing whether data is volatile or non-volatile. Volatile data disappears when a system powers off, so you collect it first when possible. Non-volatile data remains stored on media such as drives and removable storage.

<dl>
<dt>Volatile data</dt>
<dd>Temporary information such as RAM contents, CPU cache data, and running processes.</dd>
<dt>Non-volatile data</dt>
<dd>Persistent information stored on HDDs, SSDs, NVM, and similar media.</dd>
</dl>

- **Volatile data** is time-sensitive and often collected early.
- **Non-volatile data** is useful for longer-term storage analysis.
- **Memory** can contain signs of malware or other activity that never touched disk.

**Disk imaging**

Disk imaging creates an exact bit-for-bit copy of a storage device. This is a foundational forensic task because it lets you analyze a copy while protecting the original media from modification or damage.

Forensic imaging is more than normal file copying. It captures the full device state, including sectors that may contain deleted data.

> <mark>Important:</mark> If you skip proper imaging, you risk compromising evidence integrity and weakening the investigation.

- **Bit-for-bit copy** preserves the original storage structure.
- **Deleted data** may still be present in unallocated areas or captured sectors.
- **Original media** should remain untouched whenever possible.

**Memory forensics**

Memory forensics examines volatile memory to uncover evidence of malware infections, fileless malware, and other activity that may only exist in RAM. This is especially useful after a breach or during active incident response.

Because some threats execute directly in memory without writing files to disk, memory analysis can reveal artifacts that traditional disk analysis misses.

- **RAM** may contain active processes and malicious code.
- **Fileless malware** can avoid disk-based detection.
- **Incident response** often benefits from memory collection early in the investigation.

**Forensic workflow**

A basic forensic workflow follows a preservation-first approach. You collect evidence carefully, analyze copies, and produce findings that can be explained clearly.

1. Identify the evidence source and determine whether it is volatile or non-volatile.
2. Preserve the original evidence and collect a forensic copy when appropriate.
3. Analyze the copy for artifacts, timelines, malware indicators, or deleted data.
4. Document findings so they remain useful for legal or operational review.

**Tools and reporting**

Forensic tools support acquisition, analysis, and reporting. The evidence emphasizes disk imaging tools and broader forensic platforms used to collect and examine digital artifacts.

Good reporting matters as much as technical collection. Your findings should be clear, repeatable, and suitable for legal or regulatory review.

- **Acquisition tools** help create forensic images and collect data.
- **Analysis tools** help inspect memory, files, logs, and artifacts.
- **Reports** should explain what you found and how you preserved it.

**Exam focus**

For Security+ SY0-701, focus on the relationship between evidence type, collection method, and integrity. The exam expects you to recognize why volatile data is collected quickly, why disk images are used, and why preserving the original evidence is essential.

- **Preservation** comes before analysis.
- **Volatile data** is collected first when possible.
- **Forensic imaging** supports reliable, repeatable analysis.
- **Memory analysis** helps detect threats that may not exist on disk.
