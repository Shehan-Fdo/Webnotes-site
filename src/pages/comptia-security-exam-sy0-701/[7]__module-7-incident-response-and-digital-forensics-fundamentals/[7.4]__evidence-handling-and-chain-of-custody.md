## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Evidence handling fundamentals**

Digital forensics depends on preserving evidence from the moment you collect it until final disposition. The goal is to keep the evidence reliable, traceable, and usable in legal or investigative settings. Chain of custody is the documented history of who handled the evidence, when they handled it, and what happened to it.

A strong process protects both **integrity** and **admissibility**. If you cannot show that evidence stayed controlled and unchanged, its value in court or in an investigation can be challenged.

- Treat evidence as sensitive from the first contact.
- Record each transfer, access event, and storage change.
- Keep the evidence path continuous and explainable.
- Use conservative handling when the evidence may be volatile.

**Chain of custody**

Chain of custody is the chronological documentation and physical control of evidence. It acts as a paper trail that shows the evidence was not altered, tampered with, or mishandled. In digital investigations, this record supports both forensic credibility and legal review.

The process matters because digital data can be volatile and because every contact can affect preservation. The Locard Exchange Principle applies here: every contact leaves a trace. That means you should limit unnecessary handling and control who touches the evidence.

- Document collection details as soon as possible.
- Maintain accountability for every person who handles the item.
- Preserve continuity from collection through presentation.
- Avoid gaps in the record that could weaken trust in the evidence.

**Preservation and collection practices**

Evidence preservation starts before the forensic team finishes its analysis. When an incident occurs, you should control access and reduce the chance of accidental change. Practical preservation steps focus on isolation, documentation, and controlled storage.

Volatile data can disappear when a system powers off, so you usually prioritize it first during digital forensics. Non-volatile data remains stored on media such as hard drives and SSDs, but it still needs careful handling to avoid alteration.

1. Secure the device or data source immediately.
2. Document the condition of the evidence and the collection context.
3. Limit access to authorized personnel only.
4. Store the evidence in a controlled environment.
5. Continue integrity checks and standardized handling throughout analysis.

> Do not assume evidence is safe just because it is digital. Poor handling, weak documentation, or uncontrolled access can make it unusable in court.

**Tools and controls**

Some tools help prevent evidence contamination during collection and analysis. Write-blocking technology reduces the risk of modifying source data during acquisition. Faraday bags and analysis enclosures help isolate devices and support controlled examination of digital evidence.

These controls are useful when you need to preserve the original state of the evidence while still allowing investigation. The key idea is to separate collection from alteration.

- Use **write-blocking** when acquiring data from storage media.
- Use **Faraday** solutions when you need to isolate devices from external signals.
- Use controlled storage to reduce tampering risk.
- Apply standardized procedures so handling stays consistent.

**Documentation and legal reliability**

Documentation is not optional; it is part of the evidence itself. Good records show what was collected, who handled it, when it changed hands, and how it was preserved. This supports reliability in court and helps investigators reconstruct events.

Common failures include incomplete notes, untracked transfers, and inconsistent handling. These mistakes can undermine admissibility even when the underlying data is valuable. A disciplined process reduces that risk.

- Record each action in a clear, chronological way.
- Keep custody records aligned with the physical evidence.
- Use consistent procedures across the investigation.
- Review records for gaps before presenting evidence.

**Exam focus**

For Security+ purposes, focus on the relationship between evidence handling, preservation, and chain of custody. You should recognize that digital evidence must remain traceable and protected from alteration. You should also know that volatile data requires prompt attention and that documentation supports legal defensibility.
