## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Risk analysis overview**

Risk analysis helps you identify potential threats, estimate their likelihood and impact, and decide how to respond. In cybersecurity, you use it to understand which assets matter most and whether current controls are adequate.

Both qualitative and quantitative approaches are useful. Qualitative methods help you compare risks when data is limited or hard to measure. Quantitative methods help you express risk in numeric terms when you have enough data to support calculations.

- **Risk** is the chance that a threat will exploit a vulnerability and cause loss.
- **Likelihood** describes how probable an event is.
- **Impact** describes the effect on the organization if the event occurs.
- **Risk-oriented thinking** supports better decisions about accept, mitigate, transfer, or avoid responses.

**Qualitative risk analysis**

Qualitative risk analysis uses descriptive or ordinal scales to rank risks. Common examples include `low`, `medium`, and `high`, or color-based scales such as green, yellow, and red. This approach is useful when you need a fast, practical view of relative severity.

It often relies on expert judgment and experience. That makes it especially helpful when data is incomplete, inconsistent, or difficult to quantify. A risk map or impact matrix is a common way to visualize the results.

- Uses **ordinal scales** instead of exact numbers.
- Helps you compare risks across assets or scenarios.
- Works well when you need speed and broad prioritization.
- Can be less consistent if the scale is not clearly defined.

> Qualitative analysis is useful for ranking, but it can be subjective if people interpret the scale differently.

**Quantitative risk analysis**

Quantitative risk analysis expresses risk in numeric terms. It is useful when you can measure or estimate financial or operational loss with enough confidence to support calculations. This method helps you compare risks more precisely and focus remediation on the highest-value issues.

A common model is:

```text
Risk = Likelihood × Impact
```

In practice, the value comes from turning exposure into measurable terms that support business decisions. Quantitative analysis is especially helpful for financial risk, but it can also support broader operational planning when the data is strong enough.

- Uses **numeric values** to estimate exposure.
- Supports more precise comparisons between risks.
- Helps align security work with business impact.
- Depends on better data quality than qualitative analysis.

**Likelihood and impact**

Likelihood and impact are the core inputs in both approaches. You first estimate how likely a threat is to occur, then estimate how severe the result would be if it does.

Impact can be viewed across multiple business dimensions:

- **Financial** impact, such as direct loss.
- **Operational** impact, such as disruption to services.
- **Reputational** impact, such as media attention or loss of trust.
- **Health and safety** impact, where people may be harmed.
- **Compliance/legal** impact, where obligations or penalties may apply.

A practical assessment asks two questions:

1. How likely is the event?
2. How bad is the outcome if it happens?

**Choosing the right method**

You do not need to treat qualitative and quantitative analysis as mutually exclusive. In practice, many organizations use both to build a more accurate picture of risk. Qualitative analysis gives you a fast way to sort and prioritize. Quantitative analysis gives you stronger numeric support when the data allows it.

Use qualitative analysis when:

- You need a quick assessment.
- Data is limited or hard to measure.
- You want a simple risk ranking for discussion.

Use quantitative analysis when:

- You have enough reliable data.
- You need financial justification.
- You want to compare exposure more precisely.

**Exam focus**

For Security+ purposes, focus on the purpose of each method and the difference in output.

- **Qualitative** = descriptive ranking of risk.
- **Quantitative** = numeric measurement of risk.
- Both depend on **likelihood** and **impact**.
- Both support risk response decisions and prioritization.

A strong answer usually connects the method to the situation. If the scenario emphasizes limited data and fast prioritization, qualitative analysis fits best. If it emphasizes measurable loss and business justification, quantitative analysis fits best.
