## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Risk management lifecycle overview**

Risk management is a continuous cycle that helps you identify threats, assess their impact, choose a response, and review the outcome. The process is practical rather than one-time: you repeat it as risks, systems, and business conditions change.

A common lifecycle includes these stages:

- **Identify** risks by finding what can go wrong.
- **Assess** risks to understand how severe they are.
- **Treat** or respond to the risks.
- **Monitor** the results and review whether the response worked.

The goal is to keep exposure within an organization’s risk appetite, which is the level of risk it is willing to accept.

**Risk identification**

Risk identification starts with listing assets, threats, and vulnerabilities. You use sources such as asset inventories, past incidents, audits, and assessments to build a risk list.

Typical identification output includes:

- A list of risks tied to specific assets or processes
- A clear chain from vulnerability to possible impact
- Early visibility into internal and external threats

For example, an unpatched server can create a vulnerability that leads to malware infection and data loss. This kind of cause-and-effect view helps you connect technical weaknesses to business impact.

**Risk analysis and assessment**

After you identify risks, you analyze them to understand how bad they are. Assessment helps you prioritize what needs attention first.

Two common approaches are used:

- **Qualitative analysis**
  - Uses labels such as high, medium, and low
  - Often relies on a risk matrix or heat map
  - Is more subjective
- **Quantitative analysis**
  - Uses numbers and formulas
  - Can include single loss expectancy (SLE), annual rate of occurrence (ARO), and annualized loss expectancy (ALE)
  - Is more objective

A practical assessment often combines both methods. Qualitative analysis is faster for broad prioritization, while quantitative analysis gives you a more measurable view of financial exposure.

**Risk treatment options**

Once you know the risk level, you decide what to do about it. The standard treatment options are:

- **Mitigate** the risk by reducing likelihood or impact
- **Transfer** the risk to another party
- **Accept** the risk and live with the exposure
- **Avoid** the risk by eliminating the risky activity

Mitigation is the most common response in cybersecurity. It includes actions such as patching systems, deploying firewalls, enforcing multi-factor authentication (MFA), and segmenting networks.

| Option | Meaning | Typical example |
|---|---|---|
| Mitigate | Reduce the risk | Patching, firewall rules, MFA |
| Transfer | Shift the risk | Cyber insurance |
| Accept | Live with the risk | Low-impact risk |
| Avoid | Eliminate the risk | Stop the risky activity |

> Use acceptance only when the remaining exposure is within tolerance and the cost of treatment is not justified.

**Monitoring and review**

Risk management does not end after treatment. You monitor the outcome to confirm that the chosen response actually reduced exposure or shifted it as intended.

Monitoring helps you:

- Verify that controls still work
- Detect changes in the threat landscape
- Reassess risks after incidents, audits, or system changes
- Keep the process aligned with business priorities

This review step is important because risk is dynamic. A control that was effective earlier may become less useful as systems, attackers, or business operations change.

**Exam focus points**

For Security+ purposes, focus on the relationship between the lifecycle stages and the treatment choices.

- Identify risks from assets, threats, and vulnerabilities
- Assess risks using qualitative or quantitative methods
- Choose a treatment based on business need and risk level
- Monitor the result and repeat the cycle as conditions change

A good way to remember the flow is: identify, assess, treat, monitor. If a question asks what happens next, look for the step that matches the lifecycle rather than a one-time fix.
