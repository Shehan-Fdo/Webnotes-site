## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Business impact analysis overview**

A **business impact analysis** (BIA) predicts the consequences of a disruption to your business and gathers information needed to develop recovery strategies. It is a core part of continuity planning because it helps you understand what breaks first, what the impact looks like over time, and what you must restore first.

A BIA is not just a list of assets. It connects business functions, systems, people, and dependencies to the effects of interruption. That makes it useful for planning around cyber incidents, natural disasters, power outages, supplier failures, and other events that can interrupt operations.

- Identify critical business functions before a disruption occurs.
- Evaluate the impact of downtime on operations, finances, reputation, and legal obligations.
- Use the results to guide recovery priorities and continuity planning.
- Pair the BIA with a risk assessment, which identifies potential loss scenarios.

**Why criticality matters**

Criticality is the idea that some functions, systems, and resources matter more than others during a disruption. The BIA helps you determine which mission or business processes are most important and which resources support them.

When you understand criticality, you can focus recovery efforts where they matter most. This reduces wasted effort and helps you restore the services that keep the organization operating.

- **Critical functions** are the processes that must continue or be restored quickly.
- **Resource requirements** include the systems, data, personnel, and dependencies needed to support those functions.
- **Recovery priorities** define the order in which you restore services after an interruption.

**What a BIA evaluates**

A BIA systematically examines how a disruption affects the organization over time. It looks at the consequences of losing a process, system, or service and helps you characterize the damage.

Common impact areas include:

- **Financial impact** such as lost revenue or added recovery cost.
- **Operational impact** such as halted workflows or reduced service delivery.
- **Reputational impact** such as loss of customer trust.
- **Legal or compliance impact** such as missed obligations or regulatory exposure.

A practical BIA also reveals dependencies that may not be obvious at first glance. For example, a service may appear important on its own, but its true criticality becomes clearer when you see how many other processes depend on it.

**How BIA supports continuity and recovery**

The BIA is a foundation for business continuity management and disaster recovery planning. It helps you build recovery strategies that match the actual needs of the organization instead of using a generic plan.

It supports planning by helping you:

- Determine which processes are mission-critical.
- Identify the resources needed to keep those processes running.
- Set recovery priorities for systems and services.
- Inform prevention and mitigation investments.

This is why BIA is often described as the bridge between risk awareness and practical recovery planning. You first identify what could go wrong, then you determine what the disruption would cost, and finally you decide what to restore first.

**Typical BIA workflow**

1. Identify business functions and supporting systems.
2. Determine how disruption affects each function over time.
3. Document the financial, operational, reputational, and legal consequences.
4. Identify resource requirements and dependencies.
5. Rank recovery priorities based on criticality.

This process gives you a structured view of what matters most during an outage or incident.

**Exam focus points**

- A BIA predicts the consequences of disruption.
- A BIA helps determine recovery priorities.
- Criticality is based on business impact and dependency, not just technical importance.
- The BIA supports continuity planning, disaster recovery, and mitigation decisions.
- Risk assessment identifies potential loss scenarios; the BIA evaluates the impact of those scenarios.

**Practical takeaway**

If a system fails, the question is not only whether it is broken. The more important question is what business function stops, what the consequences are, and how quickly you need to recover it. That is the value of business impact analysis and criticality.
