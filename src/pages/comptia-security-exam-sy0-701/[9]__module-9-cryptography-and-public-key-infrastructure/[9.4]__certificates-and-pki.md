## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Certificates and PKI overview**

Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) is the set of policies, procedures, hardware, and software used to create, distribute, manage, store, revoke, and otherwise handle digital certificates. It supports trust in encrypted communications by tying identities to cryptographic keys.

X.509 is the standard format for public key certificates. These certificates are used widely in Internet protocols such as Transport Layer Security (TLS) and Secure Sockets Layer (SSL), which underpin HTTPS. They also appear in offline uses such as electronic signatures.

- **PKI** governs certificate lifecycle and trust relationships.
- **X.509** defines the certificate format used across many systems.
- Certificates help verify that a user, device, organization, or service is really the entity it claims to be.

**How certificates establish trust**

An X.509 certificate binds an identity to a public key using a digital signature. The certificate usually contains an identity field, such as a hostname, organization, or individual, plus the associated public key. The certificate is then signed by a certificate authority (CA) or self-signed.

PKI relies on asymmetric cryptography. One key encrypts or verifies, while the paired private key decrypts or signs. This separation supports confidentiality, integrity, and authentication in networked systems.

- **Public key** is shared and used for encryption or verification.
- **Private key** stays secret and is used for decryption or signing.
- **Digital signature** helps prove the certificate has not been altered.
- **CA** issues and signs certificates to extend trust.

**Certificate roles in the trust chain**

Certificates are often discussed as part of a chain of trust. A root CA sits at the top of the hierarchy and is self-signed. Lower-level certificates derive trust from that root through intermediate signing relationships.

This hierarchy matters because the security of the entire PKI system depends on the trust anchor at the top and on correct certificate management throughout the chain.

| Role | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Root CA | Top-level trust anchor; self-signed |
| Intermediate CA | Extends trust and helps manage issuance |
| End-entity certificate | Identifies a user, device, service, or application |

**Where PKI is used**

PKI is common in web security, device identity, and secure communications. It helps manage identity for websites, mobile apps, online documents, connected devices, and network services.

It is also used to secure TLS and mutual TLS (mTLS) connections. In these protocols, certificates authenticate identities and enable encrypted communication between endpoints.

- **TLS** protects data in transit between clients and servers.
- **mTLS** adds mutual authentication, so both sides present certificates.
- **Digital certificates** can support people, devices, and applications.

**Lifecycle and management concerns**

Certificate management can be cumbersome, error-prone, and difficult to debug. Because certificates have a lifecycle, you need to account for issuance, distribution, storage, revocation, and replacement.

Practical PKI work often focuses on keeping trust valid over time. If a certificate is expired, revoked, or misissued, the trust relationship can fail even if the cryptography itself is sound.

> Certificate lifecycle issues are a common operational risk: trust depends on both correct issuance and ongoing management.

**Key exam takeaways**

- X.509 is the standard certificate format you should associate with PKI.
- PKI uses asymmetric cryptography to support confidentiality, integrity, and authentication.
- A certificate binds an identity to a public key.
- CAs sign certificates and help establish trust.
- Root CAs anchor the trust chain, while intermediate and end-entity certificates extend it.
- TLS and mTLS are major real-world uses of certificates.
