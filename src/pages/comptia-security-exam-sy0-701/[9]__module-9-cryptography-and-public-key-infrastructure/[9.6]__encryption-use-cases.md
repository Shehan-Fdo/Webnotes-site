## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Encryption use cases**

Encryption protects data in different states, and the right choice depends on where the data lives and who must access it. For Security+ purposes, focus on the practical distinction between protecting stored data, protecting network traffic, and protecting content so only the intended recipient can read it.

- **Data at rest** is stored data on disks, backups, or other media.
- **Data in transit** is data moving across a network.
- **Data in use** is data being processed by an application or system.
- **Key management** determines who can decrypt data and under what conditions.

> Encryption at rest helps with lost hardware or stolen files, but it does not stop a privileged insider or attacker with valid credentials from reading decrypted data.

**Data at rest**

Encryption at rest protects information stored on endpoints, servers, databases, and backups. Common examples include full disk encryption (FDE), transparent data encryption (TDE), and server-side encryption (SSE). These controls reduce exposure if a device is lost or storage media is stolen.

Use this model when you need to protect stored data without changing how users interact with the application.

- **FDE** protects an entire disk or volume.
- **TDE** protects database files while the database remains usable.
- **SSE** encrypts data on the server side before it is written to storage.
- File-level encryption can provide more granular control than drive-level encryption.

A practical limitation is that at-rest encryption does not automatically protect data after a system decrypts it for an authorized process. That is why access control, least privilege, and auditing still matter.

**Data in transit**

Encryption in transit protects data as it crosses a network. Transport Layer Security (TLS) is the common protocol used to secure communication between systems and applications. It helps prevent interception, alteration, and forgery while data moves between endpoints.

Use this model for web traffic, remote access, API calls, and other network communications where confidentiality and authenticity matter.

- **TLS** secures communication between two systems.
- It protects login credentials, financial transactions, and other sensitive traffic.
- It supports privacy by reducing the risk of eavesdropping on network links.
- It complements network and identity controls rather than replacing them.

**End-to-end encryption**

End-to-end encryption (E2EE) encrypts data on the sender’s device and decrypts it only on the intended recipient’s device. This limits exposure to intermediaries, including service providers, and is common in messaging, online transactions, and some storage services.

Use E2EE when you want the strongest privacy boundary between sender and recipient.

- The provider cannot read the protected content in normal operation.
- Key handling becomes more important because users or organizations must protect the keys.
- Monitoring, troubleshooting, and compliance workflows can become harder.
- It is useful when third-party access should be minimized.

**File-level encryption and granular access**

File-level encryption protects individual files instead of entire disks or folders. This gives you more precise control over who can open specific content and can support audit trails and least-privilege access.

This approach is useful when you need to share selected files while keeping the rest of the data protected under your own keys.

- It reduces the impact of ransomware and exfiltration.
- It supports secure third-party sharing.
- It can fit zero trust designs by keeping data encrypted under customer-controlled keys.
- It helps simplify compliance requirements where access tracking matters.

**Key management and planning**

Encryption is only as effective as the process that protects the keys. Public key infrastructure (PKI) supports the creation, distribution, storage, revocation, and use of digital certificates, and it often works alongside certificate authorities (CAs) to establish trust.

When you plan encryption, think about how keys are generated, stored, rotated, revoked, and recovered.

- **PKI** provides the policies and procedures around certificates and trust.
- **CAs** issue and manage certificates that bind identities to keys.
- **Key escrow** may matter when you need recovery options for encrypted systems.
- **Trusted Platform Module (TPM)** presence can be relevant when planning full disk encryption on laptops.

A good encryption design balances confidentiality with operational needs. If you cannot recover keys, you may lose access to the data; if too many people can access the keys, you weaken the protection.

**Common exam takeaways**

- Choose **at rest** encryption for stored data and backups.
- Choose **in transit** encryption for network communication.
- Choose **E2EE** when only the sender and recipient should read the content.
- Remember that privileged users can still expose data after decryption.
- Treat encryption as an additional control that works with identity, resource, and network protections.

**Quick comparison**

| Use case | Protects | Typical benefit | Main limitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Data at rest | Stored files, disks, backups | Reduces exposure from theft or loss | Does not stop access after decryption |
| Data in transit | Network traffic | Prevents interception and tampering | Does not secure data once received |
| End-to-end encryption | Sender-to-recipient content | Limits provider visibility | Harder monitoring and key recovery |
| File-level encryption | Individual files | Granular sharing and access control | Requires careful key handling |

**Exam focus reminders**

1. Identify the data state first: at rest, in transit, or in use.
2. Match the encryption method to the use case.
3. Consider who controls the keys and who can recover them.
4. Do not assume encryption replaces access control or monitoring.
5. For laptop encryption scenarios, think about recovery and hardware support such as TPM.
