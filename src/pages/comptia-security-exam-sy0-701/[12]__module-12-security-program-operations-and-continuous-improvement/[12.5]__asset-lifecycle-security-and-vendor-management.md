## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Asset lifecycle security and vendor management**

Asset lifecycle security extends beyond your own systems. Third-party vendors, suppliers, and service providers can introduce cybersecurity, compliance, financial, and reputational risk into your environment. A practical vendor risk management (VRM) or third-party risk management (TPRM) program helps you identify, assess, mitigate, monitor, and eventually offboard those relationships in a controlled way.

TPRM is not a one-time assessment. It is a lifecycle process that tracks risk before the relationship starts and throughout the contract term. That approach matters because vendor exposure changes over time, especially in interconnected digital ecosystems where organizations rely on many external services.

- **VRM** focuses on identifying, evaluating, and controlling vendor-related risk.
- **TPRM** emphasizes the broader third-party relationship and its full lifecycle.
- Vendor risk can affect business continuity, data protection, regulatory alignment, and reputation.

**Why lifecycle thinking matters**

A vendor may look acceptable during procurement but become a higher risk later if its controls weaken, its access expands, or its business changes. Lifecycle management helps you avoid treating security as a single approval step. Instead, you keep checking whether the vendor still meets your expectations.

Common risk areas include:
- Access to sensitive systems, data, or infrastructure
- Security control gaps in external partners
- Compliance misalignment with internal requirements
- Supply chain exposure from connected services
- Reputational damage after a vendor incident

**Typical TPRM lifecycle phases**

A structured lifecycle gives you repeatable checkpoints for decision-making. The exact labels vary by source, but the practical flow is consistent.

1. **Vendor sourcing and selection**
   - Identify business need and candidate vendors.
   - Compare vendors using security and business criteria, not cost alone.
   - Screen for obvious risk before deeper engagement.

2. **Due diligence and onboarding**
   - Gather security, compliance, and operational information.
   - Confirm the vendor can meet your baseline requirements.
   - Define access, responsibilities, and contract expectations before go-live.

3. **Risk assessment**
   - Evaluate the vendor’s risk posture against your requirements.
   - Consider the sensitivity of data, systems, and business processes involved.
   - Use the assessment to support a risk-based decision.

4. **Remediation and mitigation**
   - Address identified gaps with corrective actions.
   - Reduce exposure through controls, restrictions, or contract terms.
   - Track whether the vendor completes required fixes.

5. **Continuous monitoring**
   - Reassess the vendor over time instead of relying on a single review.
   - Watch for changes in posture, service scope, or business impact.
   - Keep the program adaptable as the relationship evolves.

6. **Offboarding**
   - End access and close the relationship in a controlled manner.
   - Ensure data handling, return, or disposal expectations are met.
   - Confirm the vendor no longer retains unnecessary access.

**Vendor management controls to focus on**

Strong vendor management is about control and verification. You want to know what the vendor can access, what standards they follow, and how you will respond if their risk changes.

- Require security expectations in contracts and service terms.
- Limit vendor access to only what is needed.
- Verify that external partners follow security and compliance standards.
- Monitor for changes that affect risk posture.
- Keep records of assessments, decisions, and remediation status.

**Practical exam takeaways**

Security+ expects you to recognize that third-party risk is ongoing and programmatic. If a question describes a vendor relationship, think about the full lifecycle rather than only initial approval.

- A one-time questionnaire is not enough for long-term risk control.
- Monitoring matters because vendor risk can change after onboarding.
- Offboarding is part of security because access and data exposure must end cleanly.
- Risk-based decisions are more effective than blanket approval or rejection.

**Quick comparison: VRM and TPRM**

| Term | Focus | Practical use |
|---|---|---|
| VRM | Vendor-specific risk | Evaluate and control risk from suppliers and service providers |
| TPRM | Broader third-party risk | Manage risk across the full external relationship lifecycle |

**How to study this topic**

When you review a scenario, ask three questions:
- What stage of the vendor lifecycle is this?
- What risk is being introduced or reduced?
- What control best fits the situation: assessment, mitigation, monitoring, or offboarding?

If the scenario mentions a breach, service change, or new access, the correct response often involves reassessment or monitoring rather than a one-time approval.
