## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Business continuity and disaster recovery overview**

Business continuity and disaster recovery (BCDR) work together, but they solve different problems. Business continuity focuses on keeping critical operations running during a disruption, while disaster recovery focuses on restoring IT systems and data after an incident. In practice, you use both to reduce downtime, limit business impact, and improve resilience.

- **Business continuity** keeps essential functions operating during a crisis.
- **Disaster recovery** restores systems, applications, and data after disruption.
- **BCDR** is the combined strategy for staying operational and recovering quickly.

Common disruptions include cyberattacks, equipment failures, natural disasters, power outages, and supply chain interruptions. These events can halt operations unexpectedly, so planning before an incident is essential.

**How the two plans fit together**

A business continuity plan (BCP) and a disaster recovery plan (DRP) are related, but they are not interchangeable. The BCP is broader because it addresses how the organization continues delivering critical services. The DRP is narrower because it concentrates on technical restoration.

| Area | Business Continuity Plan | Disaster Recovery Plan |
|---|---|---|
| Primary focus | Maintain operations | Restore IT systems and data |
| Scope | Organization-wide | Technology and recovery processes |
| Goal | Keep critical functions available | Return systems to working state |

When you integrate them, you create a more complete response to disruption. Cybersecurity is part of that integration because cyber threats can interrupt operations, compromise data, and damage reputation.

**Core recovery metrics**

Two metrics guide recovery planning: Recovery Time Objective (RTO) and Recovery Point Objective (RPO). These help you define acceptable downtime and data loss.

<dl>
<dt><strong>RTO</strong></dt>
<dd>The maximum acceptable time to restore a system or service after disruption.</dd>
<dt><strong>RPO</strong></dt>
<dd>The maximum acceptable amount of data loss, measured by time, that the organization can tolerate.</dd>
</dl>

Use these metrics to prioritize systems and recovery actions. Critical services usually need shorter RTO and RPO values than less essential services.

**Essential planning components**

A practical BCDR program includes both operational and technical elements. The exact details vary by organization, but the plan should cover the most important functions and recovery steps.

- Identify critical business processes and supporting systems.
- Define recovery priorities based on business impact.
- Document roles, responsibilities, and communication paths.
- Include data protection and restoration procedures.
- Account for external dependencies such as vendors and supply chains.

> If you do not test and maintain the plan, it can fail when you need it most.

**Building and maintaining BCDR**

You build BCDR by aligning business needs with technical recovery capabilities. Start with the services that matter most, then map the systems and data that support them. After that, define how you will continue operations during a disruption and how you will restore technology afterward.

1. Identify critical operations and the systems that support them.
2. Set recovery targets using RTO and RPO.
3. Document continuity and recovery procedures.
4. Test the plan under realistic conditions.
5. Update the plan after changes, incidents, or test results.

Testing and maintenance are ongoing tasks, not one-time events. Regular review helps you keep the plan aligned with current systems, staffing, and risks.

**Cybersecurity integration**

Cybersecurity strengthens BCDR by reducing the chance that a threat becomes a full outage. Because modern organizations rely heavily on digital infrastructure, cyber controls belong in continuity planning from the start.

- Protect critical assets so disruptions are less likely to spread.
- Plan for cyber incidents as well as physical events.
- Preserve the ability to recover even when data or systems are compromised.
- Treat resilience as both an operational and security requirement.

**Exam focus points**

For Security+ purposes, remember the distinction between continuity and recovery. The exam may ask you to identify which plan keeps the business running and which plan restores technology. It may also ask you to interpret RTO and RPO in a recovery scenario.

- BCP = keep the business operating.
- DRP = restore systems and data.
- RTO = how fast you must recover.
- RPO = how much data loss you can accept.
