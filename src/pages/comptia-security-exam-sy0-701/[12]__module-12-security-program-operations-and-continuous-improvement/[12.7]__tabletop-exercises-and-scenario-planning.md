## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Tabletop exercise purpose**

Tabletop exercises are discussion-based planning exercises that help you evaluate how your organization responds to a threat scenario before a real incident occurs. They give you a safe way to test readiness, identify gaps, and improve coordination across the response team.

CISA describes tabletop exercise packages as customizable resources that help stakeholders start internal discussions about their ability to address a range of threat scenarios. These packages typically include template objectives, scenarios, and discussion questions.

- **Readiness** improves when you practice response decisions before an incident.
- **Customization** matters because scenarios should reflect your organization’s environment.
- **Discussion** is the core activity, not live system execution.

**What tabletop exercises test**

Tabletop exercises help you evaluate more than technical controls. They also test whether your documented response plans are usable under pressure and whether the right people know their roles.

Common areas to assess include:

- **Processes** for detection, response, and recovery
- **Procedures** for escalation and coordination
- **Technical skills** needed during an incident
- **Communication** between teams and decision makers

A practical exercise should stay focused on planning and validation. Use test systems if you need to simulate technical actions, and avoid affecting production systems during the exercise.

> Do not use tabletop exercises to make changes on live systems. Keep the activity safe, controlled, and separate from production.

**Scenario planning approach**

Scenario planning works best when you tailor the exercise to your business context. Generic scenarios can help you start, but the most useful exercises reflect your actual risks, assets, and response responsibilities.

A strong scenario usually identifies:

- The **threat scenario** being discussed
- The **processes** under test
- The **threat actors** involved
- The **assets** that could be impacted

Some exercise sets are intentionally organized from simpler to more challenging scenarios, which helps you build confidence and expand the discussion over time. Short exercises can still be useful; some tabletop scenarios are designed to be completed in as little as 15 minutes.

**How to run a tabletop exercise**

1. Define the objective of the exercise.
2. Choose a scenario that matches your risk profile.
3. Prepare discussion questions and expected decision points.
4. Bring in the people who would actually participate in a real response.
5. Run the discussion and capture observations.
6. Review what worked and what needs improvement.

The goal is to validate planning, not to “win” the scenario. After the exercise, review the results and refine your incident response documentation, communication paths, and escalation steps.

**What to look for during review**

After the exercise, focus on whether the team could act quickly and consistently. Look for gaps in documentation, unclear responsibilities, and missing coordination points.

Useful review questions include:

- Did the team understand the scenario and its likely impact?
- Were the response steps clear and realistic?
- Did the exercise reveal missing procedures or weak handoffs?
- Were the right stakeholders involved at the right time?

**Exam focus**

For Security+ purposes, remember that tabletop exercises are a planning and validation tool. They support incident response readiness by letting you practice decisions, test procedures, and improve your response plan without waiting for a real event.

Key takeaways:

- Tabletop exercises are **simulated discussions**, not live attacks.
- Scenario planning should be **customized** to your organization.
- Exercises help you identify **gaps** before an incident happens.
- Review and follow-up are essential for improving readiness.
