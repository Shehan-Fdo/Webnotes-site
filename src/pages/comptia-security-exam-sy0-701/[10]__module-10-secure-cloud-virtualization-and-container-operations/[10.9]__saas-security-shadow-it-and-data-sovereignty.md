## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**SaaS security overview**

Software as a Service (SaaS) changes how you secure users and data because the traditional network boundary is weaker. Employees use sanctioned and unsanctioned apps across managed and unmanaged devices, so security teams need visibility into what is actually in use.

SaaS sprawl is a practical risk. As more cloud apps enter the environment, it becomes harder to track which apps are approved, who uses them, and where data moves.

- **Visibility** matters because you cannot protect what you do not know is in use.
- **App sprawl** increases the chance of inconsistent controls and governance gaps.
- **Data egress** to unsanctioned services can expose sensitive information.

**Shadow IT in SaaS**

Shadow IT is the use of unauthorized or unsanctioned SaaS applications without approval or oversight from IT or security teams. It is often driven by speed, convenience, or the need for tools that official services do not meet quickly enough.

Shadow IT is not usually malicious, but it still creates risk. It can expand the attack surface, complicate compliance, and weaken governance when users move data into tools the organization does not monitor.

- **Unauthorized apps** bypass normal review and control processes.
- **Productivity pressure** often explains why users adopt these tools.
- **Governance gaps** appear when security teams lack app-level visibility.
- **AI integrations** can increase the chance of data leaving approved boundaries.

> Shadow IT may improve agility, but you still need controls that identify, assess, and manage unsanctioned SaaS use.

**SaaS security priorities**

A SaaS security strategy focuses on securing data and users across cloud applications rather than relying on perimeter defenses alone. The key challenge is to understand which apps are sanctioned, how they are used, and what data they handle.

Practical security work centers on discovery, policy, and monitoring. You want to reduce blind spots and make sure users do not move sensitive data into services that have not been reviewed.

1. Discover the SaaS applications in use.
2. Determine which apps are sanctioned and which are not.
3. Monitor data movement and user activity.
4. Apply controls that reduce exposure from unsanctioned use.

**Data sovereignty and data residency**

Data sovereignty is the legal authority a nation has over data within its borders. Data residency is the physical or geographical location where data is stored or processed. These concepts are related, but they are not the same.

If data is collected in one country and stored or processed in another, the organization may need to follow the laws of both jurisdictions. The location of cloud servers can influence sovereignty decisions, but legal obligations may also depend on where data was collected and what it contains.

| Concept | Meaning | Security impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Data sovereignty** | Legal and regulatory authority over data | Determines which laws and regulations apply |
| **Data residency** | Physical location of stored or processed data | Influences jurisdiction, latency, and compliance planning |

- **Sovereignty** is a legal concept.
- **Residency** is a geographical concept.
- **Cross-border data** can trigger multiple legal requirements.
- **Sensitive data** such as medical or payment information needs careful handling.

**Compliance and resilience considerations**

SaaS data sovereignty is more than choosing a region. A practical approach includes legal, operational, and technical controls that support compliance and recovery across borders.

Encryption, immutability, and verified backup and recovery help strengthen control over cloud-based data. This matters when you need to protect sensitive content while still using SaaS for business agility.

- **Encryption** helps protect data in transit and at rest.
- **Immutability** supports stronger protection against unwanted changes.
- **Backup and recovery** help you maintain resilience when incidents occur.
- **Compliance-centric planning** turns sovereignty requirements into a governance strategy.

**Exam focus**

For Security+, focus on the distinction between legal authority and physical location, and on the operational risk created by unsanctioned SaaS use. The exam may expect you to recognize that cloud adoption increases the need for visibility, policy enforcement, and jurisdiction-aware data handling.

- **Shadow IT** is an unsanctioned SaaS usage problem.
- **SaaS security** requires discovery and governance, not just perimeter controls.
- **Data sovereignty** is about law and jurisdiction.
- **Data residency** is about where data lives.
