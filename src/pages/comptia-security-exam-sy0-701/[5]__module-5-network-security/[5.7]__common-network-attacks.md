## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Common network attack categories**

Network attacks target the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of traffic and services. For Security+ purposes, focus on how an attacker interferes with communication, redirects traffic, or overwhelms a target. Some attacks are passive at first, but many become active once the attacker manipulates packets, names, or sessions.

- **On-path attack** is the Security+ term for what is commonly called a man-in-the-middle (MitM) attack.
- **Spoofing** attacks impersonate a trusted system or service to redirect traffic or gain trust.
- **Denial-of-service (DoS)** attacks reduce availability by exhausting resources or disrupting service.
- **Botnets** often amplify network attacks by coordinating many compromised systems.

**On-path and man-in-the-middle attacks**

An on-path attack places the attacker between two communicating parties so traffic can be intercepted, altered, or relayed without the victims realizing it. The evidence highlights that MitM attacks exploit weak network configuration and missing security controls, especially when communication integrity and confidentiality are not protected.

Common MitM-related techniques include:

- **ARP spoofing/poisoning** on local networks to redirect traffic through the attacker.
- **DNS spoofing** to send users to a malicious or incorrect destination.
- **DHCP spoofing** to hand out attacker-controlled network settings.
- **NDP spoofing** in IPv6 environments, where neighbor discovery can be abused.
- **STP spoofing** in switched networks, where spanning tree behavior can be manipulated.

> Use encryption such as TLS (Transport Layer Security) to protect data in transit, but remember that encryption does not stop every form of traffic redirection or endpoint compromise.

**Spoofing and redirection attacks**

Spoofing works by forging identity-related network information so a system trusts the attacker’s traffic. In practice, this often supports a larger attack chain, such as credential capture, traffic interception, or malicious site redirection.

- **ARP spoofing** targets address resolution on a local segment.
- **DNS spoofing** changes name resolution so a domain points to the wrong host.
- **DHCP spoofing** can alter how clients learn gateway, DNS, or addressing details.
- **NDP spoofing** affects IPv6 neighbor relationships and can redirect local traffic.

A useful exam distinction is that spoofing does not always mean full interception. Sometimes the attacker only needs to mislead a client long enough to reroute traffic or break trust.

**Denial-of-service and availability attacks**

DoS attacks focus on making a service unavailable or unreliable. The evidence describes network attacks as increasingly multi-vector, and common attack lists consistently include DoS and distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) as core examples.

- **DoS** usually comes from a single source or limited set of sources.
- **DDoS** uses many systems, often a **botnet**, to increase volume and resilience.
- Availability attacks may target bandwidth, connection tables, or application resources.

For exam prep, connect the attack to the effect:
- If the goal is to stop access, think **availability**.
- If the goal is to intercept or alter traffic, think **confidentiality** and **integrity**.

**How to recognize the attack type**

When you read a scenario, identify the attacker’s goal first. Then match the behavior to the most likely category.

| Scenario clue | Likely attack type | Why it fits |
|---|---|---|
| Traffic is secretly relayed through another system | On-path / MitM | The attacker sits between two endpoints |
| Users are sent to the wrong website or host | DNS spoofing | Name resolution is manipulated |
| Clients receive attacker-controlled network settings | DHCP spoofing | Network configuration is forged |
| Local traffic is redirected on a switched network | ARP spoofing | Address mapping is poisoned |
| Service becomes unreachable due to heavy traffic | DoS or DDoS | Availability is the target |

**Practical defense themes**

The evidence emphasizes protection against MitM-style attacks and the need to secure communication paths. For Security+ study, keep the defenses tied to the attack mechanism.

- **Encrypt communications** to reduce the value of intercepted traffic.
- **Harden network configuration** to reduce opportunities for interception.
- **Validate trust relationships** so clients do not accept forged network data.
- **Monitor for abnormal traffic patterns** that suggest redirection or flooding.
- **Use layered controls** because one defense rarely stops every network attack.

**Exam focus reminders**

Security+ questions often describe the symptom rather than naming the attack. Read carefully for clues about what the attacker changes.

- If the attacker changes where traffic goes, think **spoofing** or **on-path**.
- If the attacker changes whether a service is reachable, think **DoS/DDoS**.
- If the attacker sits between two parties and observes or alters data, think **MitM**.
- If the scenario mentions local network poisoning, look for **ARP** or **NDP** abuse.
