## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Indicators of compromise**

An <abbr title="Indicator of Compromise">IOC</abbr> is evidence that a system or network may already be breached. It is a forensic clue, not just a warning sign, so you use it after or during an incident to confirm malicious activity and scope the impact.

Security teams look for IOCs in places that collect security telemetry, including event logs, <abbr title="Extended Detection and Response">XDR</abbr> platforms, and <abbr title="Security Information and Event Management">SIEM</abbr> systems. Common IOC examples include:

- Unusual network traffic behavior
- Unexpected software installations
- Sign-ins from abnormal locations
- Large numbers of requests for the same file
- Suspicious IP addresses, domains, URLs, or email addresses
- File names, paths, or hashes linked to known malicious activity

IOCs help you respond quickly during an attack and help you understand what happened afterward. That post-incident analysis supports containment, recovery, and future hardening.

> <mark>IOC</mark> data is most useful when you treat it as evidence of compromise, not as proof of a future attack by itself.

**Baselines and normal behavior**

A baseline is your reference point for what normal activity looks like in a system, user account, application, or network segment. You compare current activity against that reference to spot meaningful change.

Baselines matter because many security tools generate large volumes of alerts. Without a normal pattern to compare against, it is harder to tell harmless variation from suspicious behavior. A good baseline reflects expected activity over time, such as:

- Typical login times
- Usual source locations
- Normal file access patterns
- Expected network traffic levels
- Standard software and permission changes

Baselines are not static. You update them as business processes, user roles, and system behavior change. If you do not refresh them, you risk treating normal growth or operational change as an incident.

**Anomaly detection**

Anomaly detection identifies activity that deviates from the baseline or from expected patterns. In practice, this helps you find subtle issues such as failed login bursts, suspicious file downloads, unusual login locations, or unexpected permission changes.

Modern detection approaches may use behavioral analysis and machine learning to establish patterns and flag deviations. The goal is not to replace investigation; it is to prioritize what deserves attention.

Use anomaly detection to:

- Surface unusual activity that may indicate compromise
- Reduce noise by focusing on meaningful deviations
- Support threat hunting and incident investigation
- Detect multi-stage attacks that do not match simple rule-based alerts

An anomaly is not automatically malicious. It is a signal that something changed enough to deserve review.

**IOC, baseline, and anomaly comparison**

| Concept | What it tells you | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| IOC | A breach may have already occurred | Confirm, scope, and investigate an incident |
| Baseline | What normal looks like | Compare current behavior to expected behavior |
| Anomaly | Something deviates from normal | Flag activity for review and deeper analysis |

**How they work together**

You use baselines to define normal behavior, anomaly detection to find deviations, and IOCs to confirm whether suspicious activity aligns with known compromise evidence. Together, they help you move from broad monitoring to focused investigation.

A practical workflow looks like this:

1. Establish a baseline for the asset, user, or service.
2. Monitor for deviations from that baseline.
3. Check suspicious activity against known IOC data.
4. Investigate, contain, and document confirmed compromise.
5. Update the baseline and detection logic after the incident.

**Operational considerations**

Detection is only as good as the data and tuning behind it. If your monitoring is incomplete or your baseline is outdated, you may miss real threats or generate too many false positives.

Keep these points in mind:

- Use multiple telemetry sources when possible.
- Revisit baselines after major business or infrastructure changes.
- Treat repeated anomalies as higher priority than one-off noise.
- Validate detections so they continue to reflect real-world behavior.

**Exam focus**

For Security+, remember the distinction between the three ideas:

- **IOC**: evidence of compromise
- **Baseline**: expected normal state
- **Anomaly**: deviation from expected behavior

If a question asks what helps identify suspicious behavior by comparing current activity to normal activity, the best answer is usually baseline-driven anomaly detection. If it asks for evidence that an attack has already occurred, choose IOC.
