## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ (Exam SY0-701)

**Operational procedures in security investigations**

Operational procedures help you preserve evidence, coordinate response, and keep investigations defensible. In practice, this means you handle evidence in a controlled way, document every transfer, and escalate incidents through the right channels before evidence is lost, altered, or destroyed.

The core idea is simple: if you cannot show what happened to evidence from collection to storage or court, its reliability drops. Digital evidence is especially fragile because it can be altered easily, so documentation and control matter at every step.

- **Chain of custody** is the recorded history of evidence handling.
- **Evidence preservation** starts as early as possible to reduce loss or tampering.
- **Escalation** ensures the right people respond before the situation worsens.
- **Documentation** supports legal integrity and authenticity.

**Chain of custody fundamentals**

Chain of custody is the documented, chronological record of evidence collection, handling, transfer, storage, and access. It verifies where evidence has been and who handled it. This record helps prevent substitution, tampering, misidentification, damage, alteration, contamination, misplacement, or falsification.

A broken chain of custody can make evidence less credible or even inadmissible. For that reason, you treat every handoff as a controlled event and keep records from the outset of the investigation.

- Record who collected the evidence.
- Record when and where it was collected.
- Record every transfer and storage location.
- Record who accessed it and why.
- Keep the evidence sealed and tracked when possible.

> If the chain of custody is incomplete, the evidence may lose legal value even if the data itself is technically useful.

**Evidence collection and preservation**

You should secure physical evidence early if there is a risk it could be removed or destroyed. In some investigations, that means taking control before witness statements or interviews, especially when a suspect might be alerted by overt action.

Digital evidence needs the same discipline, but with extra care because it can change quickly. Preserve it in a way that maintains its integrity and supports later review.

- Collect evidence before it can be altered or destroyed.
- Use controlled handling to reduce contamination.
- Keep accurate notes from the beginning.
- Preserve both the evidence and the context around it.
- Consult law enforcement early when a criminal act is suspected.

**Incident escalation and response workflow**

A security incident escalation process is a structured way to identify, assess, and route incidents to the right resources at the right time. This keeps small issues from becoming larger breaches and helps the organization respond in an organized way.

Security operations centers (SOCs) use workflow and escalation to monitor systems, prioritize issues, and involve higher-level experts when needed. Incident response then focuses on identifying scope, containing damage, and mitigating or eradicating the root cause.

1. Detect the issue and confirm it is an incident.
2. Assess impact, urgency, and scope.
3. Escalate to the appropriate team or authority.
4. Contain the damage and preserve evidence.
5. Document actions and outcomes.

**Practical handling rules**

Good operational procedure combines technical control with legal awareness. You do not just store evidence; you maintain its trustworthiness from collection through archiving.

- Use tamper-evident packaging or equivalent controls when handling physical evidence.
- Keep evidence secure and access-limited.
- Maintain chronological records of custody and handling.
- Preserve evidence before making changes to affected systems when possible.
- Avoid actions that could alert a suspect before evidence is secured.

**What exam questions usually test**

Security+ questions often focus on the purpose of these procedures rather than on legal detail. Expect scenarios that ask you to choose the action that best preserves evidence integrity or supports a proper response path.

- Which step protects evidence from tampering.
- When to escalate an incident.
- Why documentation matters.
- How chain of custody supports admissibility.
- Why early preservation matters in investigations.

**Quick comparison**

| Concept | Purpose | Key risk if ignored |
|---|---|---|
| Chain of custody | Tracks evidence handling and transfer | Evidence may be challenged or excluded |
| Evidence preservation | Protects evidence from loss or alteration | Data may be destroyed or contaminated |
| Incident escalation | Routes issues to the right responders | Delayed response increases impact |

**Memory aid**

Think of the process as: collect, document, secure, escalate, preserve. If any step is skipped, the investigation becomes harder to trust and harder to defend.
