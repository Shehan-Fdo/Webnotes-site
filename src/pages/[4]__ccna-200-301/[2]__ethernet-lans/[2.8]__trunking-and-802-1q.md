## **Course:** 200-301 CCNA

**Trunking overview**

- A **trunk** carries traffic for multiple VLANs over one physical link.
- Trunks use VLAN tagging so switches can identify which VLAN a frame belongs to.
- In Cisco CCNA material, `802.1Q` is the common trunking standard.
- `ISL` is Cisco proprietary and older, but the evidence notes it is not covered anymore in the CCNA course.
- Trunks are used only with `Fast Ethernet` and `Gigabit Ethernet` interfaces in the cited material.

**802.1Q tagging**

- `IEEE 802.1Q` is an open standard from the IEEE.
- It works on non-Cisco switches as well as Cisco switches.
- `802.1Q` inserts a `4-byte` tag into the Ethernet frame header.
- The tag sits between the source `MAC` address and the `EtherType`.
- The tag includes `TPID` and `TCI`.
- `TPID` means `Tag Protocol Identifier`.
- `TCI` means `Tag Control Information`.
- `TCI` includes `PCP`, `DEI`, and `VID`.
- `PCP` means `Priority Code Point`.
- `DEI` means `Drop Eligible Indicator`.
- `VID` means `VLAN Identifier`.
- `VID` identifies the VLAN that the frame belongs to.

**Native VLAN**

- The **native VLAN** handles untagged frames on an `802.1Q` trunk port.
- A frame without a VLAN tag is assumed to belong to the native VLAN.
- Untagged frames received on a trunk are not forwarded to any other VLAN except the native VLAN.
- The native VLAN matters when untagged traffic reaches a trunk through a layer 1 device or another path that strips tags.
- Both ends of a trunk normally send and receive tagged frames.

**Practical takeaways**

- Use `802.1Q` when you need one link to carry multiple VLANs.
- Check the native VLAN when troubleshooting untagged traffic on a trunk.
- Remember that `802.1Q` is the standard trunking method in modern Cisco environments.
- Keep in mind that `ISL` is legacy and not part of the current CCNA focus.
