## **Course:** 200-301 CCNA

**QoS overview**

- **QoS** means Quality of Service.
- It uses technologies and techniques to manage and prioritize network traffic.
- The goal is to give critical applications the network resources they need.
- QoS matters most when the network is congested.
- Without QoS, all traffic is treated equally.
- Equal treatment can hurt time-sensitive traffic like voice and video.

**Why QoS is needed**

- Enterprise networks often carry many users, servers, and devices at the same time.
- Heavy use can consume router resources.
- Congestion can force a router to queue or drop packets.
- Poor handling of traffic can reduce user experience.
- Service Level Agreement (**SLA**) targets may be harder to meet when all traffic is treated the same.
- QoS helps classify sensitive and non-sensitive traffic.

**Key performance concerns**

- Congestion is a central QoS problem.
- Delay affects time-sensitive applications.
- Jitter can disrupt voice and video quality.
- Packet loss can occur when devices cannot forward all traffic.
- Different traffic types need different handling.

**QoS models and marking**

- QoS uses classification to group traffic.
- QoS uses marking to identify traffic for later handling.
- **DSCP** means Differentiated Services Code Point.
- DSCP is used to implement QoS policies with packet marking.
- Marking helps devices recognize traffic priority across the network.
- **CoS** means Class of Service.
- CoS, QoS, and DSCP are related terms used in traffic prioritization.

**QoS mechanisms**

- QoS can prioritize traffic before congestion becomes severe.
- QoS can queue traffic when devices must wait to forward it.
- QoS can drop traffic when resources are limited.
- QoS can shape or police traffic to control how much enters or leaves an interface.
- Policing and shaping are part of QoS traffic control.
- These tools help match traffic flow to available bandwidth.

**Implementation considerations**

- You should identify which traffic is sensitive before applying QoS.
- You should decide how to classify traffic.
- You should decide how to mark traffic.
- You should decide where to apply QoS in the network.
- You should verify that the policy matches the traffic need.
- QoS design depends on the network layer and device role.

**Monitoring and verification**

- You should check whether QoS policies are active.
- You should confirm that traffic is classified as expected.
- You should confirm that marking values are applied correctly.
- You should watch for congestion, drops, and queueing behavior.
- Verification helps you confirm that QoS improves service for critical traffic.

**Best practices**

- Prioritize traffic based on business need.
- Keep classification rules clear and consistent.
- Use marking to preserve priority across devices.
- Apply QoS where it has the most effect.
- Review performance after deployment.
- Adjust policies when traffic patterns change.
