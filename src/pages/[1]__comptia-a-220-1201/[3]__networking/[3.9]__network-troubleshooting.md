## **Course:** Comptia A+ 220-1201

**Network Troubleshooting Overview**

Network troubleshooting starts by checking basic connectivity and then narrowing the problem step by step.

- Start with the local device.
- Work outward through the network path.
- Use simple tests before deeper analysis.

**Initial Connectivity Checks**

A wired connection often gives you the first clue through the **link light** on the Ethernet interface.

- A lit link light suggests physical connectivity.
- No light can indicate a bad cable or a broken connection.
- Check whether traffic is blinking on the interface.

You can also test the local stack by pinging the loopback address.

1. Ping `127.0.0.1`.
2. Confirm the device responds.
3. Use the result to separate local issues from network issues.

**Wired Network Problems**

Wired issues often begin with the physical layer.

- Inspect the cable and port.
- Verify the link light.
- Test with known-good hardware when possible.

If the device cannot reach the network, the fault may be in the cable, port, or interface.

**Wireless Connectivity Issues**

Wireless problems often come from signal quality or interference.

- Physical obstructions can weaken Wi-Fi signals.
- Dense objects can reduce coverage.
- Electronic interference can disrupt wireless stability.

Common interference sources include:

- Microwaves
- Cordless phones
- Bluetooth devices

> Wireless performance can vary by band, and the `5 GHz` band often has shorter range and weaker wall penetration than `2.4 GHz`.

**Latency, Jitter, and Voice Quality**

Network performance problems can affect real-time traffic.

- **Latency** is delay in packet delivery.
- **Jitter** is variation in packet timing.
- Poor VoIP (Voice over IP) quality often points to latency or jitter issues.

These symptoms matter most when users report choppy audio or delayed responses.

**Port Flapping**

Port flapping means a network port repeatedly goes up and down.

- It can interrupt connectivity.
- It can point to unstable cabling or interface problems.
- It can also affect network reliability for connected devices.

**Practical Troubleshooting Flow**

1. Confirm the user symptom.
2. Check physical connectivity.
3. Test the local device with `127.0.0.1`.
4. Verify wired or wireless link status.
5. Look for latency, jitter, or voice-quality complaints.
6. Narrow the fault to the device, cable, access point, or network path.

**Key Terms**

- **VoIP**: Voice over IP.
- **Latency**: Packet delay.
- **Jitter**: Timing variation.
- **Link light**: Physical connection indicator on a network interface.
