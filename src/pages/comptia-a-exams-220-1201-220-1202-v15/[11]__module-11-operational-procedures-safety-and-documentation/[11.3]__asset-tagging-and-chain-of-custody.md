## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Asset tagging overview**

Asset tagging helps you identify and track company equipment across its lifecycle. In practice, a tag gives each device a consistent reference so you can build a rolling inventory, locate assets, and understand what data they may hold. This matters more as devices move between users, remote locations, transit, and retirement.

A strong tagging process supports operational control and data protection. It also reduces the chance that equipment is lost, removed without authorization, or misidentified during disposal.

- **Asset tags** provide a visible identifier for tracking equipment.
- Tags support inventory accuracy when devices are issued, moved, or retired.
- Consistent labeling helps you match the physical device to records such as serial numbers.
- Tagging is especially useful when assets are in circulation across remote or distributed teams.

**Chain of custody fundamentals**

The chain of custody is a documented, verifiable record of an asset’s handling from the moment it leaves your facility until its final destination. For IT asset disposition (ITAD), that destination may be secure data destruction, redeployment, remarketing, or recycling.

This record is more than a shipping log. It captures who had the asset, when custody changed, how it was transported, where it was stored, and how it was finally processed. That documentation creates accountability and supports audits, compliance, and security reviews.

- **Chain of custody** tracks each transfer of an asset.
- It records serial numbers, custody changes, transport details, storage protocols, and final disposition.
- It helps prove the asset was handled in a controlled and traceable way.
- It is especially important for retired devices that may still contain sensitive data.

**Why it matters for retired IT assets**

Retired IT equipment often contains data that must be protected until the device is securely wiped, destroyed, or otherwise processed. If tracking breaks down, you risk data exposure, theft, regulatory issues, and disputes about where the asset went.

A documented chain of custody gives you evidence that the asset stayed under control throughout the ITAD process. That evidence is useful when you need to show compliance or investigate a missing device.

> Retired devices should not be treated like ordinary inventory. They require tighter handling because they may still contain sensitive information.

**Core controls for secure tracking**

You improve chain of custody by making each handoff visible and verifiable. The evidence highlights several practical controls that reduce risk during collection and transit.

- Scan assets at your site before they leave.
- Use one consistent label or identifier when scanning assets with multiple unique identifiers.
- Make sure no assets are added or removed after the scan list is finalized.
- Shrink-wrap pallets after scanning to help preserve the verified count.
- Keep mobile device management (MDM) enrollment active during transit when possible.
- Require wipe reports before unenrolling devices from MDM.
- Verify the logistics partner before handing over assets.

**Typical chain of custody workflow**

1. Identify the asset with a tag and record its serial number.
2. Scan the asset at your location and confirm the list is complete.
3. Secure the shipment so the verified contents do not change in transit.
4. Track custody transfers, transport details, and storage conditions.
5. Confirm the final disposition, such as destruction, redeployment, remarketing, or recycling.

**Documentation and audit readiness**

Good documentation makes the process traceable and auditable. If you can show the asset’s path from collection to final disposition, you have a stronger position for internal audits and compliance checks.

The goal is not just to know where an asset is. The goal is to prove what happened to it at each stage. That proof is what turns basic tracking into a defensible chain of custody.

**Practical exam takeaway**

For A+ operational procedures, remember the relationship between tagging and custody:

- **Tagging** identifies the asset.
- **Chain of custody** proves how the asset was handled.
- Together, they support security, accountability, and accurate disposition.
