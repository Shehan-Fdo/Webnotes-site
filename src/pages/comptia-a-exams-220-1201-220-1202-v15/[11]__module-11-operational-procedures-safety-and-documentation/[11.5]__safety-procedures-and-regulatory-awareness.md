## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Safety mindset and scope**

CompTIA A+ expects you to follow best practices for safety, environmental impacts, and effective communication. In practical terms, that means you protect yourself, the equipment, and the work environment before you begin hardware or lab tasks.

Safety procedures reduce the risk of injury and prevent avoidable damage to components. They also support professional behavior in support roles, where you may work around energized equipment, delicate parts, and regulated materials.

- **Safety-related work practices** help prevent electric shock and other injuries when you work near or on energized circuits.
- **Deenergize** live parts before working on them unless doing so creates additional hazards or is infeasible due to design or operational limits.
- Use the specific precautions that match the hazard level and the task.

**Electrical safety and energized equipment**

Electrical work requires conservative handling. OSHA guidance states that live parts should be deenergized before work begins unless there is a valid exception. This is the default mindset you should apply when servicing computers, monitors, or other electronic equipment.

When you must work around electrical equipment, keep your actions deliberate and controlled. Avoid shortcuts that increase exposure to shock, burns, or arc-related hazards.

> If equipment is energized, treat it as a hazard until you verify otherwise.

- Turn off and disconnect power before opening a device whenever the task allows it.
- Verify that the equipment is safe to access before touching internal components.
- Respect the limits of the equipment design and the work environment.

**Electrostatic discharge protection**

Electrostatic discharge (<abbr title="electrostatic discharge">ESD</abbr>) can damage sensitive components even when you do not feel a shock. Dell’s safety guidance emphasizes protecting against ESD before opening a computer, laptop, or monitor for internal access.

Use ESD precautions consistently during hardware replacement, upgrades, and internal repairs. The goal is to prevent electrostatic damage to components while you work.

- Use ESD protection before handling internal parts.
- Handle components carefully to reduce the chance of electrostatic damage.
- Apply the same precautions for desktops, laptops, and monitors when internal access is required.

**Safe handling and lifting**

Hardware safety is not limited to electricity. Dell also highlights precautions for lifting electronic equipment, which matters when you move systems, monitors, or other heavy devices.

Use proper lifting habits to protect yourself and the equipment. Keep the load stable and avoid sudden movements that can cause drops or strain.

- Plan the lift before you move the device.
- Use assistance when the equipment is awkward or heavy.
- Keep a secure grip and maintain control throughout the move.

**Lab procedures and tool use**

Safe lab procedures protect both people and equipment. Cisco’s lab guidance ties safety to accident prevention, injury reduction, and equipment protection, which makes it a core part of professional troubleshooting and maintenance.

You should treat tools and procedures as part of the safety process, not as separate concerns. The right tool used the wrong way can still create risk.

- Follow lab rules before starting work.
- Use tools only for their intended purpose.
- Keep the workspace organized to reduce accidental damage.

**Regulatory awareness and compliance**

Regulatory awareness helps you understand why some products and materials have restrictions. Compliance topics commonly include product safety standards and environmental rules for manufacturing and disposal.

Examples from industry sources include <abbr title="Restriction of Hazardous Substances">RoHS</abbr>, which restricts hazardous substances in electrical and electronic equipment, and <abbr title="Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment">WEEE</abbr>, which addresses disposal of such equipment. These rules matter because hardware must be safe for users and environmentally responsible at end of life.

- Know that regulations can affect product design, materials, and disposal.
- Recognize that compliance is part of safe manufacturing and responsible support.
- Follow organizational and legal requirements when handling regulated equipment or waste.

**Practical workflow for safe hardware work**

1. Identify the task and the hazards involved.
2. Power down and disconnect the device when possible.
3. Apply ESD protection before touching internal components.
4. Use proper lifting and handling techniques for the equipment.
5. Follow lab or workplace procedures and any applicable regulations.
6. Confirm the device is ready for service before returning it to use.

**Key takeaways**

Safety procedures are a routine part of A+ work, not an optional extra. You protect people and hardware by deenergizing equipment when possible, using ESD precautions, handling devices safely, and following lab and regulatory requirements.

A strong technician combines caution with consistency: verify the environment, use the right protection, and work within the limits of the equipment and the rules that govern it.
