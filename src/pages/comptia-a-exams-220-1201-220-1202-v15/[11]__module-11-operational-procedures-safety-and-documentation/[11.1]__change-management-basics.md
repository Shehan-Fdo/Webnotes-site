## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Change management overview**

Change management, also called change control, is the process you use to manage updates in a controlled way. In a home environment, you can often apply a change immediately. In a business environment, even a small modification can affect many systems, so you need a formal process to reduce risk and avoid downtime.

Change management applies to many kinds of updates, including software upgrades, application patches, firewall changes, switch configuration updates, and new routing interfaces. The goal is to make sure changes are intentional, tested, communicated, and traceable.

- **Controlled process** keeps changes from being made informally.
- **Enterprise impact** can be large when one change affects shared systems.
- **Repeatability** helps teams apply the same safe process each time.

**Why change control matters**

A change can introduce outages, confusion, security issues, or compliance problems if you do not manage it carefully. Large environments often require changes to be tested and deployed in a planned way so you can verify the effect before production use.

Without proper documentation and approval, even small changes can create chaos. A structured workflow helps you know what changed, who approved it, when it happened, and how to reverse it if needed.

- **Risk reduction** is the main reason for formal change control.
- **Traceability** helps you track ownership and evidence.
- **Consistency** supports repeatable production changes.

**Core change management flow**

Change management starts when you identify the need for a change and ends after the change is implemented and tested. A practical workflow includes analysis, planning, approval, execution, verification, and documentation.

1. Identify the change you need to make.
2. Analyze the risk and possible impact.
3. Create an implementation plan.
4. Test the change before production when possible.
5. Obtain approval from the appropriate reviewers.
6. Deploy the change in a controlled way.
7. Verify the result and document the outcome.

This process helps you align the change with operational needs while keeping users informed about timing and expected impact.

**Approvals and review**

Change requests are often reviewed by people with operational, technical, and business knowledge. In larger organizations, this review may be handled by a formal change authority such as a Change Advisory Board (CAB) or Change Control Board (CCB).

Reviewers check whether the request is complete, accurate, and ready for implementation. They may send incomplete requests back for more information before approval.

- **CAB** means Change Advisory Board.
- **CCB** means Change Control Board.
- **Review completeness** before a change moves forward.
- **Stakeholder input** helps ensure the change fits business needs.

**Rollback and recovery planning**

Rollback is the ability to reverse a change if it causes problems. You should plan rollback steps before you deploy, not after something fails. This is especially important for production systems where downtime or service disruption can affect many users.

A good rollback plan gives you a practical way to return to the previous working state if testing or verification shows an issue.

> Always plan rollback before deployment so you can recover quickly if the change fails.

**Documentation and communication**

Documentation keeps change management organized and auditable. It also helps other team members understand what was changed and why. Common supporting documents include standard operating procedures (SOPs), installation checklists, incident reports, and version-controlled knowledge base articles.

Communication is also part of the process. Users and support teams should know when a change will happen and what effect it may have.

- **SOP** means Standard Operating Procedure.
- **Checklists** help ensure repeatable execution.
- **Knowledge base articles** support centralized documentation.
- **Incident reports** capture what happened after a problem.

**Automation and workflow support**

Some organizations automate change request workflows to streamline approvals and updates. Automation can reduce manual effort and help keep the process consistent, especially when changes move from testing to production rollout.

Automated workflows are useful when you need faster handling of requests, clearer approval paths, and better audit readiness. Even with automation, you still need review, testing, and rollback planning.

**Practical exam takeaways**

For A+ purposes, focus on the purpose of change management and the controls that make it safe. You should recognize that change control is about managing risk, not blocking progress.

- Test changes before production when possible.
- Use approvals to authorize production changes.
- Keep documentation current and version controlled.
- Plan rollback for failed or harmful changes.
- Communicate timing and impact to affected users.
