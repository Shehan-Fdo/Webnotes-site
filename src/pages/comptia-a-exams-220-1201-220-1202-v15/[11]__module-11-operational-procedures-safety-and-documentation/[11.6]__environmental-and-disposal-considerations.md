## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**E-waste overview**

Electronic waste, or **e-waste**, is discarded electrical and electronic equipment that has reached the end of its useful life. It is one of the fastest-growing solid-waste streams in the world, and global collection and recycling rates remain low.

Proper disposal matters because electronics contain both valuable materials and hazardous substances. Recycling helps recover metals, glass, and plastics, while reducing the need to mine and manufacture new raw materials.

- E-waste includes devices that are broken, obsolete, donated, or sent to a recycler.
- Electronics can contain harmful materials such as mercury and beryllium.
- Informal handling, dumping, and open burning increase environmental and health risks.
- Recycling can conserve resources and reduce greenhouse gas emissions.

**Why improper disposal is a problem**

When electronics go to landfills or are incinerated, toxic chemicals can escape into the environment. Water near landfills can become contaminated, and unsafe recycling practices can release substances such as lead.

The environmental impact is not limited to pollution. Poor disposal also wastes recoverable materials that took energy to extract and process in the first place. Responsible recycling supports resource conservation and reduces the burden on waste systems.

> Improper e-waste handling can create both environmental damage and compliance risk.

**Responsible disposal practices**

You should treat end-of-life electronics as a managed disposal item, not ordinary trash. The safest approach is to use approved recycling or disposal channels that follow applicable rules for collection, transport, and processing.

1. Identify the device as e-waste when it is broken, obsolete, or no longer needed.
2. Remove or protect sensitive data before the device leaves your control.
3. Use a certified or otherwise approved recycler or disposal partner.
4. Keep records that show where the device went and how it was handled.
5. Follow local, state, federal, and international requirements that apply to the device.

**Compliance and stewardship**

E-waste disposal is governed by a patchwork of requirements in many places, so you need to follow the rules that apply to your location and organization. For businesses, compliance helps avoid fines, reputational damage, and data exposure from improperly handled hardware.

Environmental stewardship also appears in broader government and industry efforts. The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) works with international partners on e-waste management and supports electronics stewardship across the product lifecycle.

- Compliance reduces legal and operational risk.
- Documentation supports chain-of-custody and accountability.
- Secure handling protects both the environment and sensitive information.
- Partnering with qualified recyclers simplifies responsible disposition.

**Practical exam takeaways**

For A+ purposes, focus on the relationship between disposal method and impact. Recycling is preferred over dumping or burning because it recovers materials and reduces pollution. Unsafe disposal can release hazardous substances, contaminate water, and create health risks for workers and communities.

A good rule is simple: if a device contains a battery or plug and is no longer usable, treat it as e-waste and route it through a responsible disposal process.
