## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**AI risk overview**

Generative AI can speed up research, drafting, and support tasks, but it also introduces risks that affect accuracy, fairness, and confidentiality. For A+ work, the key concern is not whether the tool is impressive; it is whether the output is trustworthy and safe to use.

The most common risks in this topic are:

- **Hallucinations**: the model produces incorrect or fabricated information.
- **Bias**: the model returns skewed or misleading content that reflects unfair patterns in training data.
- **Privacy exposure**: prompts or outputs may include personal or sensitive information.
- **Data leakage**: confidential data can be revealed through prompts, logs, shared outputs, or third-party processing.

**Hallucinations**

An AI hallucination happens when a large language model (LLM) gives an answer that is wrong. The error may be subtle, such as a mistaken fact, or severe, such as inventing a source, event, or technical detail that sounds believable.

This is risky because AI output often reads confidently even when it is false. In a support environment, that can lead to bad troubleshooting steps, incorrect customer guidance, or inaccurate documentation.

- Treat AI output as a draft, not a final authority.
- Verify facts against trusted sources before using the result.
- Be especially careful with dates, product details, and technical claims.
- Watch for answers that sound polished but lack evidence.

> AI can produce convincing text that is entirely false, so confidence in the wording does not prove correctness.

**Bias in AI output**

Bias means the system produces content that is skewed, misleading, or unfair. Generative AI tools can reflect bias related to gender, race, political affiliation, and other attributes. This can affect text, images, and recommendations.

Bias matters because it can distort decisions and create unequal treatment. Even when the output seems neutral, the model may favor certain assumptions or patterns more than others.

- Review outputs for unfair assumptions or one-sided framing.
- Compare AI-generated content with other sources when the topic affects people or policy.
- Avoid using AI output as the sole basis for decisions involving users, employees, or customers.
- Remember that biased output can appear in both text and images.

**Privacy concerns**

Privacy risk increases when you enter personal or sensitive information into an AI tool. AI systems may use prompts, store interactions, or process data on third-party services, which can create exposure concerns.

In practical terms, you should assume that anything you submit may be retained, reviewed, or used beyond the immediate session unless the tool clearly states otherwise. Enterprise versions may offer stronger contractual protections and support, but you still need to follow policy and validate the tool’s behavior.

- Do not enter sensitive personal data unless the tool and policy explicitly allow it.
- Prefer enterprise or business versions when your organization requires stronger controls.
- Check whether your organization has rules for AI use, storage, and sharing.
- Validate outputs before using them in a workflow.

**Data leakage**

Data leakage happens when confidential information escapes intended control. With generative AI, leakage can occur if you paste private data into a prompt, share an AI-generated response that includes sensitive details, or send content to a service that processes data externally.

This risk is especially important in business settings where prompts may contain client information, internal plans, or account details. Once data leaves your control, it may be difficult to recover.

- Avoid including passwords, secrets, client records, or internal-only data in prompts.
- Review AI output before sharing it with others.
- Use approved tools and follow organizational data-handling rules.
- Consider whether the task can be completed without exposing sensitive information.

**Safe use habits**

A practical AI workflow focuses on validation and restraint. You use the tool to assist with drafting or brainstorming, then confirm the result before relying on it.

1. Enter only the minimum information needed for the task.
2. Check the output for factual accuracy, bias, and sensitive content.
3. Compare important claims with trusted references.
4. Remove confidential details before sharing or storing the result.
5. Use approved enterprise tools when your organization requires them.

**Exam focus**

For A+ purposes, remember the core distinction:

- **Hallucination** is an accuracy problem.
- **Bias** is a fairness and representation problem.
- **Privacy** is about personal information handling.
- **Data leakage** is about confidential information escaping control.

These risks often overlap, so the safest response is to validate output, limit sensitive input, and follow organizational policy.
