## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Security principles overview**

Security starts with a small set of goals that guide how you protect systems and data. The **CIA triad** is the common memory aid for these goals: confidentiality, integrity, and availability. You may also see it called the AIC triad, but the meaning stays the same.

- **Confidentiality** keeps private information from unauthorized access.
- **Integrity** ensures data stays accurate and unchanged unless an authorized action modifies it.
- **Availability** keeps systems and information accessible when users need them.

The CIA triad is a practical way to evaluate security controls. When you choose a control, ask which part of the triad it supports and what tradeoffs it introduces.

**Confidentiality, integrity, and availability**

Confidentiality focuses on preventing unauthorized disclosure. Integrity focuses on trust in the data itself, especially during transfer or storage. Availability focuses on keeping services up and running, including during disruptions.

- Confidentiality is about limiting who can see information.
- Integrity is about detecting or preventing unwanted changes.
- Availability is about maintaining access to systems and data.

> The CIA triad has nothing to do with the Central Intelligence Agency; it is simply a security mnemonic.

A useful way to remember the triad is to think about what can go wrong:
- Someone reads data they should not see.
- Data changes unexpectedly or is delivered incorrectly.
- A system goes offline when users need it.

**Least privilege**

The principle of **least privilege** means you give users and systems only the access they need to do their job. This reduces the chance that a mistake or compromise causes broader damage.

- Assign only the permissions required for the task.
- Avoid giving administrative access when standard access is enough.
- Review access when roles change or when access is no longer needed.

In Windows environments, access control is built around **security principals** such as user accounts, computer accounts, and security groups. These principals are authenticated by the operating system and represented by a unique security identifier, or **SID**.

**Defense in depth**

**Defense in depth** uses multiple layers of protection instead of relying on a single control. If one layer fails, another layer still helps protect the system.

- Use layered controls so one weakness does not expose everything.
- Combine technical, administrative, and procedural protections where appropriate.
- Expect that no single control is perfect.

This approach fits the broader A+ security mindset: protect systems, reduce risk, and support reliable operation. CompTIA’s A+ Core 2 exam emphasizes applying security measures to protect systems and mitigate threats.

**How the principles work together**

These principles are strongest when you apply them together. Least privilege limits what an account can do, which helps protect confidentiality and integrity. Defense in depth adds backup layers so availability and protection do not depend on one control.

- Least privilege reduces the blast radius of a compromised account.
- Layered controls improve resilience if one safeguard fails.
- The CIA triad helps you judge whether a control supports security goals.

**Practical review points**

Use these quick checks when you study or troubleshoot:
1. Identify which part of the CIA triad is most at risk.
2. Confirm whether access is limited to what is necessary.
3. Look for additional layers that can reduce the impact of failure.

If a control improves one goal but weakens another, choose the most practical balance for the situation. For example, stronger restrictions may improve confidentiality but can reduce convenience or availability.
