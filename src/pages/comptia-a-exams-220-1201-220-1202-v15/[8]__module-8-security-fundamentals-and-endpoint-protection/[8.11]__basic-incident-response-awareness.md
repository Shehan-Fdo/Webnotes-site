## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Incident response overview**

Incident response is the organized set of actions you take when you believe systems or data may have been breached. The goal is to limit damage, restore normal operations, and recover as quickly as possible.

A response process matters because incidents can disrupt operations, affect customers, and increase cost and downtime. A clear plan helps you act quickly instead of improvising during a security event.

- **Incident response** is planned and coordinated, not ad hoc.
- It focuses on detecting, responding to, and recovering from security events.
- A good process reduces damage and speeds recovery.

**Events, alerts, and incidents**

It helps to distinguish between related terms. An event is a routine action, such as creating a file or opening an email. An alert is a notification triggered by an event. An incident is a group of correlated alerts or evidence that humans or automation judge to be a likely threat.

This distinction matters because not every event is suspicious, and not every alert confirms a breach. You respond when the combined evidence suggests a real security issue.

- **Event**: a normal system action.
- **Alert**: a notification that may or may not indicate danger.
- **Incident**: correlated activity that likely represents a threat.

**Incident response plan**

An incident response plan gives you a set of instructions for handling security incidents. It defines roles, responsibilities, reporting steps, and the sequence of actions to follow during detection, response, and recovery.

A practical plan also includes communication and escalation guidance. That helps the team coordinate decisions, reduce confusion, and keep the response consistent under pressure.

- Define who does what during an incident.
- Document how to report and escalate issues.
- Include recovery and review steps, not just initial response.

**Core phases of response**

Common incident response frameworks use a similar lifecycle. The evidence points to these recurring phases:

1. **Preparation**: get ready before an incident happens.
2. **Detection and analysis**: identify suspicious activity and determine whether it is a real threat.
3. **Containment**: stop the threat from spreading and limit further damage.
4. **Eradication**: remove the cause of the incident.
5. **Recovery**: restore systems and services safely.
6. **Lessons learned**: review what happened and improve future response.

Containment is especially important because every second counts during an active attack. If you fail to contain the issue, later recovery work can be undermined by continued attacker activity.

- **Preparation** builds readiness before an incident.
- **Containment** limits spread and business impact.
- **Lessons learned** turns the incident into process improvement.

**Roles and coordination**

Incident response works best when responsibilities are clear. Teams may include security analysts, forensic specialists, communications staff, legal support, and an incident response manager. The exact structure can vary, but the key idea is that response is a coordinated effort.

You should also plan for third-party involvement when needed. External partners, vendors, or service providers may need to be contacted during investigation, containment, or recovery.

- Assign ownership before an incident occurs.
- Coordinate technical, legal, and communication tasks.
- Plan for outside support when the situation requires it.

**Training and readiness**

Organizations use incident response training to build skills and improve readiness. CISA offers incident response training to government employees, contractors, educational partners, critical infrastructure partners, and the general public.

Training helps you recognize suspicious activity, understand response steps, and practice decision-making before a real incident occurs. That preparation supports faster and more effective action when an incident happens.

- Practice response before you need it.
- Use training to reinforce roles and procedures.
- Treat readiness as part of security, not an afterthought.

**Practical exam takeaway**

For A+, focus on the purpose of incident response, the difference between events, alerts, and incidents, and the basic lifecycle of a response plan. If you can explain how a team detects, contains, removes, and recovers from a threat, you understand the core awareness expected at this level.
