## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Overview**

Physical security controls protect hardware, data carriers, and connected equipment from unauthorized physical access. Even strong logical controls such as firewalls, anti-malware, and two-factor authentication do not remove the need for physical protection. If an attacker can touch the device, they can often take, change, or destroy it.

Physical access can affect the same core security goals as cyberattacks:

- **Confidentiality** can fail when someone steals or views stored data.
- **Integrity** can fail when equipment or data is altered.
- **Availability** can fail when devices are removed, damaged, or unplugged.
- Business continuity can suffer when critical systems are physically disrupted.

**Why physical security matters**

Physical security is a foundational part of overall security controls. Security controls are commonly grouped into technical, administrative, and physical categories, and physical controls help reduce risk to people, assets, and information. In practice, physical protection is not separate from cybersecurity; it supports the same security objectives.

A useful way to think about physical security is that it creates barriers between an attacker and your assets. Those barriers can deter access, delay intrusion, detect suspicious activity, or help respond to an incident.

**Common physical security controls**

Physical controls include measures that restrict entry, protect equipment, and reduce the chance of tampering or theft.

- **Locks and keys** limit access to rooms, cabinets, racks, and devices.
- **Access control systems** restrict who can enter a facility or secure area.
- **Video surveillance** helps monitor activity and support investigation.
- **Security guards** provide a visible deterrent and can respond to incidents.
- **Barriers and enclosures** help protect equipment from casual access.
- **Cable locks** can secure portable devices or equipment that is easy to remove.
- **Alarm systems** can alert staff to unauthorized entry or tampering.

These controls are often layered. For example, a locked door protects the room, while a locked rack protects the server inside the room.

**How physical controls support security goals**

Physical security controls can serve different purposes depending on how they are used.

- **Preventive** controls try to stop unauthorized access before it happens.
- **Detective** controls help you notice suspicious activity or a breach.
- **Corrective** controls help restore security after an incident.
- **Deterrent** controls discourage attempts by increasing the chance of being caught.
- **Compensatory** controls provide an alternate safeguard when a primary control is not available.

This matters because no single control is enough on its own. A layered approach gives you more than one chance to stop or detect a threat.

**Practical examples**

Physical security is especially important for devices and locations that are easy to reach or easy to remove.

- A server room with restricted entry reduces the chance of someone unplugging or stealing equipment.
- A locked cabinet protects sensitive data carriers from casual access.
- A cable lock can help secure a laptop in a public or shared space.
- Surveillance can help confirm who entered an area and when.
- Alarms can notify staff when a door or enclosure is opened unexpectedly.

> If a device is physically accessible, logical protections alone are not enough.

**Exam focus**

For CompTIA A+, remember the main idea: physical security controls protect the hardware and the data it stores by limiting access, discouraging tampering, and helping detect unauthorized activity. They support confidentiality, integrity, and availability just like technical controls do.

**Quick review**

- Physical security protects against unauthorized access to equipment and data carriers.
- Logical controls do not replace physical controls.
- Physical compromise can lead to theft, alteration, destruction, or downtime.
- Controls can be preventive, detective, corrective, deterrent, or compensatory.
- Layered protection is more effective than relying on one safeguard.
