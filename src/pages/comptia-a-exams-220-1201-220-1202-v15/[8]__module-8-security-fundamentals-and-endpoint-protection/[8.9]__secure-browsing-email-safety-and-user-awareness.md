## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Secure browsing basics**

Web browsers are a common target because you use them for email, shopping, news, training, and entertainment. Safe browsing means recognizing browser warnings, avoiding dangerous sites, and limiting exposure to harmful downloads and extensions.

Google Safe Browsing helps protect users by warning them before they visit dangerous sites or download harmful files. It also helps webmasters identify compromised sites so they can fix them and protect visitors.

- Browser protections can warn you before you reach a risky site.
- Dangerous downloads and harmful extensions are common browser-based threats.
- Safe browsing is not just a browser feature; it is also a user habit.

> Use caution when a site or download triggers a warning. Treat the warning as a signal to stop and verify before continuing.

**Enhanced Safe Browsing**

Enhanced Safe Browsing provides faster, proactive protection against dangerous websites, downloads, and extensions. It works automatically in the background and can improve security in Google Chrome and Gmail when you are signed in.

It checks for risky URLs, downloads, browser extensions, system information, and a small sample of pages. The goal is to improve protection across Google apps while you are signed in.

- **URLs** are checked for risk before you interact with them.
- **Downloads** are scanned for dangerous content.
- **Extensions** are reviewed for suspicious behavior or risk.

**Email safety and phishing awareness**

Email remains one of the biggest attack routes for malware, spam, viruses, scams, and phishing. Attackers often rely on social engineering, which means they try to manipulate you into taking an unsafe action rather than breaking technical controls.

Phishing messages may look authentic and urgent. AI-driven tactics can make these messages harder to detect, so you cannot rely on filters alone. You need to inspect the message carefully and verify requests through trusted channels.

- Be suspicious of urgent requests, especially those involving money, credentials, or attachments.
- Check the sender, links, and file attachments before you act.
- Treat unexpected messages as untrusted until you confirm them.

**Account and message hygiene**

Strong account practices reduce the impact of a compromised inbox. Use unique passwords, keep them long and complex, and enable multi-factor authentication (MFA), which adds a second verification step beyond the password.

Email privacy also matters. Think before you send sensitive information, and remember that messages can be forwarded, stored, or exposed beyond the intended recipient.

- Use a unique password for each account.
- Turn on **MFA** wherever it is available.
- Avoid sending sensitive data unless you have a clear business need.

**User awareness and training**

Security awareness training helps people recognize threats that technology may miss. It works best when it recreates real-world threats, tracks how users respond, and adapts as risks change.

Good awareness habits reduce risk at work and at home. You improve your security when you understand current threats, question unexpected requests, and verify before you click.

- Training helps you spot phishing and social engineering attempts.
- Awareness is a practical defense, not just a policy requirement.
- Safe behavior matters across browsers, email, and social media.

**Safe online habits**

Social media can reveal useful information to attackers, including interests, job titles, and routines. Cybercriminals can use that information to profile potential victims and tailor messages that build trust.

Protect your browser and your personal information by using safe habits consistently. Review privacy and safety settings, limit what you share publicly, and be careful with links and messages that try to move you quickly into action.

1. Check browser warnings before you continue to a site or download a file.
2. Verify unexpected email requests through a trusted method.
3. Use strong account protection, including unique passwords and MFA.
4. Limit personal details shared on social media and other public platforms.
5. Complete security awareness training and apply what you learn.

**Key takeaways**

Secure browsing, email safety, and user awareness work together. Browser protections reduce exposure, email hygiene lowers the chance of phishing success, and awareness training helps you recognize threats that automated tools may miss.

When you combine technical safeguards with careful user behavior, you reduce the risk of malware, account compromise, and social engineering.
