## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**3D printing overview**

3D printing is a form of **additive manufacturing**, which means it builds an object layer by layer instead of cutting material away from a solid block. This approach reduces waste and supports complex internal shapes that are difficult to make with subtractive methods.

In A+ terms, you should recognize the basic purpose of a 3D printer and the common workflow around it. A printer takes a digital model, converts it into printable instructions, and then creates the physical part one layer at a time.

- **Additive** manufacturing adds material only where needed.
- **Subtractive** manufacturing removes material from a larger block.
- 3D printing supports rapid prototyping and custom parts.
- Layer-by-layer construction can create complex internal geometry.

**Main printer components**

A 3D printer combines motion, heat, and material delivery to build a part. In common fused filament systems, a heating element melts the material while a moving print head deposits it onto a build surface.

The exact layout varies by printer type, but the core idea stays the same: the machine controls where material goes, how much is placed, and how each layer aligns with the next.

- The **print head** or nozzle deposits material.
- The **heating element** melts thermoplastic in filament-based printers.
- The **build platform** supports the part during printing.
- Motion systems move the head and/or platform to form each layer.
- Some printers use a **flatbed scanner** or multifunction features in broader office environments, but that is separate from the printing process itself.

**Filament and resin concepts**

Filament and resin are the two material concepts you should know at a basic level. Filament is commonly used in printers that melt and extrude thermoplastic. Resin is used in liquid-based systems that cure material into solid layers.

Filament is often described by its cost and practicality for general use. Resin printing is associated with detailed output and a workflow that usually includes more post-processing. The material choice affects the printer design, the print process, and the cleanup steps.

- **Filament** is a solid spool-fed material used in extrusion-based printing.
- **Resin** is a liquid material used in vat-based printing systems.
- Material choice affects print quality, cleanup, and workflow.
- Supports may be needed depending on the geometry and printing technology.
- Different materials suit different applications, from prototypes to end-use parts.

> Support structures help maintain part geometry during printing, especially when overhangs or complex shapes need temporary support.

**Supports and part geometry**

Some prints need support structures to hold up overhangs or preserve shape during the build. Whether you need them depends on the printing technology and the geometry of the part.

Supports can improve print success, but they also add cleanup work and may affect surface quality. In resin and other technologies, support removal is part of the finishing process.

- **Supports** stabilize overhangs and complex features.
- Geometry with steep angles or bridges may require extra support.
- Support removal can affect surface finish.
- More supports can increase material use and post-processing time.

**Basic 3D printing workflow**

The workflow is straightforward: you create or obtain a model, prepare it for printing, print it, and then finish the part. The model is usually made in CAD (Computer-Aided Design) software or obtained from another source.

A common file path is to export the model as an STL or 3MF file, then slice it into layers. Slicing converts the model into printer instructions. After printing, you remove the part and complete any cleanup or curing steps.

1. Create or obtain a 3D model in CAD software or from another source.
2. Export the model as an STL or 3MF file.
3. Slice the file to generate layer-by-layer print instructions.
4. Print the part on the 3D printer.
5. Perform post-processing such as support removal or curing.

**File formats and slicing**

The STL file stores surface geometry for the object. A 3MF file also represents the model but can include more data than STL. Before printing, slicing software converts the model into instructions the printer can follow.

This step matters because the printer does not work directly from the design file alone. It needs layer data, movement paths, and material instructions.

- **STL** stores surface geometry.
- **3MF** can include additional model data.
- **Slicing** prepares the model for the printer.
- The slicer translates the design into printable layers.

**Post-processing**

After printing, the part often needs finishing work. This may include removing supports, cleaning the surface, or curing the material depending on the printer type and material used.

Post-processing is part of the overall workflow, not an optional extra in many cases. It helps turn the printed object into a usable final part.

- Remove supports when the print requires them.
- Clean or cure the part as needed by the material process.
- Inspect the final object for defects or incomplete features.
- Use post-processing to improve appearance and usability.

**Practical exam focus**

For A+, focus on identifying the printer type, the material used, and the stage of the workflow. You should be able to explain why a print needs supports, what slicing does, and how filament-based and resin-based systems differ at a high level.

Keep the process in mind as a sequence: model, file, slice, print, finish. That sequence is the easiest way to remember the basic 3D printing workflow.
