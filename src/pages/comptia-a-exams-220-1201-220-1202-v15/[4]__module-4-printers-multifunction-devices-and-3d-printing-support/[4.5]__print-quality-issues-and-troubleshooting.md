## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Overview**

Print quality problems often show up as white lines, streaks, smudges, banding, or faded output. These issues waste paper and time, but many are minor and can be fixed with basic troubleshooting rather than replacing the printer.

The key is to match the symptom to the likely cause. In inkjet printers, missing ink delivery can create white lines or banding. In laser printers, toner that does not fuse properly can smear or rub off. Across printer types, dirty components, worn parts, and incorrect paper handling are common causes.

**Common symptoms and likely causes**

- **White lines** often point to missing print coverage, such as blocked ink delivery or a transfer problem.
- **Smudges** usually indicate loose toner, dirty internal parts, or a fuser problem in laser printers.
- **Banding** appears as repeated lines or uneven stripes across the page.
- **Faded output** can result from worn components, toner issues, or poor toner bonding.
- **Vertical lines** on laser output commonly suggest an imaging drum issue.

> If toner rubs off easily after printing, treat it as a fusing problem until you prove otherwise.

**Printer components to check**

Several internal parts directly affect print quality. When output degrades, inspect the parts that move, transfer, and bond the image to paper.

- **Rollers** can collect dust and toner, which leads to streaking or uneven coverage.
- **Fuser unit** applies heat and pressure to bond toner to paper; wear can cause smudging or faded areas.
- **Drum unit** transfers toner to the page; scratches, contamination, or age can create lines or faded patches.
- **Printhead nozzles** on inkjet printers can contribute to white lines when ink delivery is blocked.
- **Toner cartridge** problems can cause poor coverage, smearing, or residue.

**Troubleshooting approach**

1. Identify the defect pattern on the page.
2. Determine whether the printer is inkjet or laser.
3. Check consumables first, including ink or toner levels and cartridge condition.
4. Inspect paper type and handling to confirm it matches the printer’s requirements.
5. Clean accessible internal components such as rollers.
6. Test again after each change so you can isolate the cause.
7. Replace worn parts such as the fuser or drum if cleaning does not restore quality.

**Laser printer-specific issues**

Laser printers rely on toner being fused permanently to the paper. If the toner stays loose, you see smearing, dirty streaks, or residue on the page. This often happens when the fuser is worn, the toner cartridge is faulty, or the paper type does not match the printer’s expectations.

Common laser-related causes include:

- **Improper toner bonding** that leaves toner on the surface.
- **Dirty internal components** that transfer marks onto the page.
- **Paper mismatch** that affects how toner adheres.
- **Worn fuser** that fails to apply enough heat and pressure.
- **Drum damage** that creates lines or faded sections.

**Inkjet-specific issues**

Inkjet print defects often come from blocked or inconsistent ink delivery. White lines, missing sections, and banding usually mean the printer is not placing ink evenly across the page.

Focus on:

- **Nozzle condition** if lines or gaps appear in the print.
- **Ink supply** if output is faint or incomplete.
- **Printhead behavior** if the defect repeats in the same pattern.

**Prevention**

Good maintenance reduces repeat failures and helps keep output consistent.

- Use the correct paper for the printer type.
- Keep rollers and internal paths clean.
- Replace consumables before they fail completely.
- Watch for gradual quality decline instead of waiting for a major failure.
- Follow printer maintenance guidance for high-use environments.

**Exam focus**

For A+ troubleshooting, connect the symptom to the component:

- **Smudging or toner that rubs off** points to the fuser.
- **Vertical lines on laser output** point to the imaging drum.
- **Streaks or uneven coverage** often point to rollers or contamination.
- **White lines in inkjet output** often point to blocked ink delivery or printhead issues.

A practical troubleshooting mindset matters more than guessing. Start with the simplest checks, verify the symptom after each step, and replace only the part that matches the failure pattern.
