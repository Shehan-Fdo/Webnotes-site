## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Printer type overview**

Printers convert digital output into a physical page or label, and the A+ exam expects you to recognize the major families by how they print and where they fit best. The core types you need to know are laser, inkjet, thermal, impact, and multifunction devices (MFDs).

- **Laser** printers are common for general office printing.
- **Inkjet** printers are common for color documents and home or small-office use.
- **Thermal** printers are used for labels, receipts, and similar output.
- **Impact** printers use physical striking to transfer output.
- **Multifunction devices** combine several functions in one unit.

**Laser printers**

Laser printers use a laser-based process to place toner on paper. They are a standard choice for office environments because they support routine document printing and are often discussed as a baseline printer type in A+ training.

A practical way to remember laser printers is that they are built for regular text-heavy output and are commonly found where consistent document production matters.

- Use toner rather than liquid ink.
- Fit well in office workflows.
- Are often paired with network printing and shared use.

**Inkjet printers**

Inkjet printers spray liquid ink onto paper. They are widely used when you need color output and flexible document printing without the heavier office footprint of a laser device.

Inkjet printers are a common choice for smaller environments because they are straightforward to deploy and handle a broad range of everyday print tasks.

- Use liquid ink cartridges.
- Produce color documents well.
- Are common in home and small-office settings.

**Thermal printers**

Thermal printers create output using heat. They are especially associated with receipts and labels, where fast, simple printing is more important than high-detail page output.

Because thermal printing is tied to specialized media, you usually recognize it by the job it performs rather than by general-purpose document printing.

- Use heat to produce output.
- Are common for receipts.
- Are common for labels and similar media.

**Impact printers**

Impact printers work by physically striking an inked ribbon against paper. This makes them different from non-impact printers such as laser and inkjet models.

They remain important to recognize because the exam may ask you to identify the printing method, especially in environments that still rely on multipart forms or legacy output needs.

- Use a striking mechanism.
- Transfer ink through a ribbon.
- Are a legacy-style printer type.

**Multifunction devices**

Multifunction devices, or **MFDs**, combine several output and imaging functions in one unit. They can act as a printer, scanner, copier, and sometimes fax device, which makes them useful in both home and office environments.

MFDs may connect through wired or wireless networks, and some support mobile printing or other shared-device workflows. Because they combine multiple features, they also create more configuration and troubleshooting points.

- Combine printing, scanning, copying, and sometimes faxing.
- May connect to wired or wireless networks.
- May support mobile printing.
- Can include features such as secure printing and printer language configuration.

**How to choose the right type**

The best printer type depends on the output you need and the environment where the device operates. For A+ purposes, focus on matching the printer to the task rather than memorizing brand-specific details.

- Choose **laser** for routine office documents.
- Choose **inkjet** for flexible color printing.
- Choose **thermal** for receipts and labels.
- Choose **impact** for legacy or multipart output needs.
- Choose **MFDs** when you want multiple functions in one device.

**Exam recognition tips**

You should be able to identify each printer type from its output method and typical use case. The exam often frames questions around what the device does, how it connects, or what kind of media it prints on.

- Laser: toner and office documents.
- Inkjet: liquid ink and color printing.
- Thermal: heat-based labels or receipts.
- Impact: physical striking and ribbon transfer.
- MFD: all-in-one printing and imaging.

**Troubleshooting context**

When you troubleshoot printers, start by identifying the device type and its expected behavior. A multifunction device may have more possible failure points than a single-function printer because it combines several subsystems in one chassis.

If a printer is not producing the expected output, check whether the issue is related to the print method, the media, the connection type, or a feature such as scanning or secure printing.
