## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**CPU heat and why cooling matters**

CPUs produce significant heat during operation, so thermal management is a core part of system reliability. A computer that cannot move heat away from the processor can become unstable or overheat.

- **Heat** is a normal byproduct of CPU activity.
- Cooling is not limited to the processor itself; the whole case airflow path matters.
- You should keep vents and airflow paths clear so warm air can leave the system.

The basic cooling pattern in many systems is simple: cool air enters the front, passes over hot components, and exits through the top and back. That airflow depends on the case design and on the placement of internal parts.

**Airflow and case layout**

Case fans help move air through the chassis, and their placement affects how well the system cools. If cables or components block the path, airflow becomes less effective.

- Front intake pulls cooler air into the case.
- Rear and top exhaust remove warmed air.
- Cable management helps prevent airflow obstruction.
- Motherboard layout can influence how easily air moves across the system.

> Do not block intake or exhaust openings. Restricted airflow reduces cooling performance and can raise internal temperatures.

**Common CPU cooling methods**

Several cooling approaches are used in desktop and similar systems. The goal is always the same: transfer heat away from the CPU and move it out of the system.

- **Heat sinks** absorb and spread heat away from the processor.
- **Fans** move air across the heat sink and through the case.
- **Liquid cooling** uses a fluid loop to carry heat away from the CPU area.
- Some systems combine a heat sink and fan as a single cooling assembly.

A cooling solution works best when it has good contact with the CPU and enough airflow to carry heat away from the cooler.

**Thermal paste and contact quality**

Thermal paste improves heat transfer between the CPU and the heat sink by filling tiny gaps between the surfaces. Poor contact can cause temperatures to rise quickly.

- Apply thermal paste when the cooler is removed and reinstalled, if the original paste is no longer usable.
- Clean the CPU and heat sink surfaces before applying new paste.
- Re-seat the cooler carefully so it makes even contact.

```text
CPU surface
  ↓ thermal paste fills microscopic gaps
Heat sink base
  ↓ fan and airflow remove heat
Case exhaust
```

**Thermal management troubleshooting**

When a system runs hot, start with the simplest physical checks. Dust, blocked airflow, missing thermal paste, and improperly seated coolers are common causes of heat problems.

1. Check that fans spin and that air moves through the case.
2. Verify that vents are not blocked by cables, dust, or nearby objects.
3. Confirm that the heat sink is firmly mounted on the CPU.
4. Inspect thermal paste if the cooler has been removed or reinstalled.
5. Review the overall airflow path from intake to exhaust.

**Practical exam focus**

For A+ purposes, you should recognize the main cooling components and explain how airflow supports thermal control. You should also connect overheating symptoms to physical causes such as blocked vents, poor fan operation, and inadequate thermal interface material.

- Cooling is a hardware and airflow problem, not just a CPU problem.
- Good thermal management depends on clean airflow and proper component contact.
- Heat sink, fan, and liquid cooling are all valid cooling concepts to identify.
