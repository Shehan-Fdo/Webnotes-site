## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Power supply fundamentals**

A power supply unit, or **PSU** (power supply unit), converts alternating current (**AC**) from the wall into direct current (**DC**) for computer components. When you work with computer power, you also need to understand **amps**, **volts**, and **watts**. Amps describe current flow, volts describe electrical pressure, and watts describe the amount of power being used.

The practical takeaway is that power planning is about compatibility and capacity. You need enough power for the system’s components, and the PSU must match the system’s electrical and physical requirements.

- **Amps** measure how many electrons flow past a point in a second.
- **Volts** measure the pressure pushing electrons through a connection.
- **Watts** represent power usage and help you size the PSU.
- PSU compatibility includes wattage, voltage, connection types, and form factor.

**Sizing and compatibility**

You size a PSU based on the needs of the installed hardware. Components such as the CPU, GPU, and peripherals draw power, so the PSU must support their combined requirements. If the PSU is undersized, the system may become unstable or fail to operate correctly. If it is oversized, you may waste money or choose a unit that does not fit the system’s needs.

Compatibility is not only about electrical output. You also need to check the PSU’s dimensions and form factor so it fits the case and aligns with the hardware design.

| Factor | What to check | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Wattage | Total power capacity | Must cover component demand |
| Voltage | Output requirements | Must match device needs |
| Connectors | Type and count | Must match motherboard and devices |
| Form factor | Physical size | Must fit the case |

**Connectors and power delivery**

Power connectors are part of PSU compatibility. Different components require different connection types, and the PSU must provide the right connectors for the motherboard and attached devices. When you evaluate a system upgrade, confirm that the PSU can physically and electrically connect to the hardware you plan to install.

Because power delivery is tied to the whole system, connector planning should happen before installation. That helps you avoid discovering too late that the PSU cannot support a required device or rail.

- Check that the PSU includes the connectors your components need.
- Verify that the PSU can support the CPU, GPU, and peripherals you plan to use.
- Confirm that the PSU dimensions fit the case before installation.

**Protection features**

Protection features help prevent damage when power conditions become unsafe. ATX power supplies use protection circuits, and the evidence specifically highlights **over-voltage protection** (OVP) and **under-voltage protection** as important safeguards. These protections can shut a supply down if voltage moves outside safe limits.

> Do not disable PSU protection features in normal use. Protection circuits exist to reduce the risk of damage and unexpected shutdowns.

Protection behavior matters because unstable or incorrect voltage can cause problems or damage components. In practical troubleshooting, a PSU that shuts off unexpectedly may be reacting to a protection condition rather than simply “failing” at random.

**Safety when working with power**

Always disconnect the computer from the power source before working inside it. Some components can retain charge in capacitors even after unplugging, so you should follow the manufacturer’s instructions and allow stored power to dissipate before touching internal parts.

- Disconnect from every power source before opening the system.
- Treat stored capacitor charge as a hazard even after unplugging.
- Avoid connecting yourself to the electrical system or ground path.

**Quick review**

Use these checkpoints when selecting or evaluating a PSU:

1. Identify the system’s power needs.
2. Confirm the PSU’s wattage is sufficient.
3. Verify voltage and connector compatibility.
4. Check the PSU form factor and dimensions.
5. Make sure protection features remain enabled.

**Key terms**

- **PSU**: The component that converts AC to DC for the computer.
- **Wattage**: The amount of power available or consumed.
- **Form factor**: The physical size and shape standard of the PSU.
- **OVP**: Over-voltage protection, which helps prevent damage from excessive voltage.
