## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Storage fundamentals**

Storage keeps data available when the system is powered off. Unlike volatile memory, storage devices retain information for later access. Common long-term storage options in A+ include hard drives, solid-state drives, PCIe storage, NVMe, SATA, M.2, flash memory, memory cards, and optical drives.

A **hard disk drive** (HDD) uses magnetic platters and moving parts. A **solid-state drive** (SSD) uses flash-based storage and has no spinning platters. In practice, HDDs are the traditional mechanical option, while SSDs are the faster, quieter alternative.

- **HDD**: magnetic storage with spinning platters
- **SSD**: flash storage with no moving parts
- **Long-term storage**: preserves data without power

**HDD versus SSD**

HDDs store data on rotating platters and access it by location. This makes them a random access storage device, but the mechanical design introduces latency compared with solid-state storage. SSDs avoid mechanical movement, which is why they are commonly used when speed and responsiveness matter.

When you compare the two, focus on the storage medium and the physical design rather than the connector alone. A drive can be an SSD and still use different interfaces, including SATA or PCIe-based connections.

| Feature            | HDD                           | SSD                   |
| ------------------ | ----------------------------- | --------------------- |
| Storage medium     | Magnetic platters             | Flash memory          |
| Moving parts       | Yes                           | No                    |
| General behavior   | Mechanical                    | Solid-state           |
| Typical exam focus | Traditional long-term storage | Faster modern storage |

**SATA, NVMe, and PCIe storage**

**SATA** (Serial ATA) is a storage interface used by many drives, especially older SSDs and HDDs. **NVMe** (Non-Volatile Memory Express) is a protocol designed for flash storage and commonly runs over **PCIe** (Peripheral Component Interconnect Express), which provides high-speed lanes for data transfer.

The key exam distinction is that SATA describes the interface, while NVMe describes the protocol used by certain SSDs. A drive may be physically similar in form factor to another drive but still use a different underlying interface or protocol.

> Do not assume that every M.2 drive is NVMe. M.2 is a form factor, not a storage protocol.

- **SATA**: common storage interface for HDDs and SSDs
- **NVMe**: protocol optimized for flash storage
- **PCIe**: high-speed bus often used by NVMe drives

**M.2 and related form factors**

**M.2** is a compact form factor used for storage devices and other components. It can support different types of drives, including SATA-based and PCIe-based devices. Because of that, the physical shape alone does not tell you the drive’s performance or protocol.

The same caution applies to related terms such as **mSATA**, which appears in storage discussions as another compact storage option. For exam purposes, separate the physical connector or form factor from the storage technology behind it.

- **M.2**: compact form factor
- **mSATA**: smaller storage form factor mentioned alongside M.2
- **Form factor**: physical size and shape, not the protocol itself

**How to reason about storage choices**

When you identify a storage device, first determine whether it is mechanical or solid-state. Then check the interface or protocol, because that affects compatibility and performance. This approach helps you avoid confusing terms that sound similar but describe different layers of the storage stack.

1. Identify whether the drive is an HDD or SSD.
2. Check whether the interface is SATA or PCIe-based.
3. Determine whether the protocol is NVMe.
4. Confirm the form factor, such as M.2, before assuming compatibility.

**Troubleshooting storage behavior**

Storage problems can show up as slow reads and writes, drive recognition issues, data loss, or corruption. A drive may repeatedly retry a bad area, which can make performance noticeably slower. Troubleshooting also includes checking for grinding noises on mechanical drives and reviewing drive health indicators such as S.M.A.R.T. (Self-Monitoring, Analysis, and Reporting Technology).

- **Slow performance** can indicate read/write retries
- **Drive not recognized** may point to connection or device issues
- **Grinding noises** are a strong warning sign on HDDs
- **S.M.A.R.T.** helps assess drive health

> If a drive is making grinding noises, treat it as a serious hardware warning and avoid unnecessary use.

**Exam-ready distinctions**

The most useful distinctions are simple and practical:

- **HDD** uses spinning magnetic platters
- **SSD** uses flash memory and no moving parts
- **SATA** is a storage interface
- **NVMe** is a flash-storage protocol
- **M.2** is a form factor that can host different storage types

Keeping those layers separate helps you answer questions about speed, compatibility, and device identification without mixing up the terms.
