## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Peripheral devices overview**

Peripheral devices are external hardware components that extend what a computer or mobile device can do. In A+ Core 1, you need to recognize common device types, their features, and the typical ways they connect. These devices often provide input, output, storage access, or shared control across multiple systems.

- **Printers** provide physical output such as documents or photos.
- **Scanners** convert printed material into digital form.
- **Mobile accessories** add input or usability features to phones and tablets.
- **Shared-control devices** let you manage more than one computer from one set of peripherals.

**Printers and multifunction devices**

Printers are used when you need hard-copy output. Common printer types include laser, inkjet, and dot matrix, and some printers are designed for color or black-and-white output. A multifunction device, often called an all-in-one (AIO), combines several functions in one unit.

- AIO devices can act as a printer, scanner, copier, and fax machine.
- USB is a common connection method for printers.
- Some printers also support Ethernet or 802.11 wireless connectivity.
- Certain models may include Bluetooth or infrared support for mobile device integration.

> Use the connection method that matches the device and environment; wireless support is useful, but wired connections are still common for printers in office settings.

**Scanners and capture devices**

Scanners take printed documents and images and turn them into files on a computer. They commonly connect by USB or 802.11 wireless. Scanner form factors vary, and the device you choose depends on the type of material you need to digitize.

- Flatbed scanners are common for documents and photos.
- Barcode scanners are used to capture encoded product or inventory data.
- Scanners are often paired with software that stores, edits, or routes the captured image.

**Monitors and visual peripherals**

Monitors are output devices that display the computer’s visual interface. Common monitor types include cathode ray tube (CRT) and liquid crystal display (LCD). While CRT is older, it still appears in study material as a recognized peripheral type.

- Monitors are essential for direct visual interaction with a system.
- Display peripherals may be used alongside projectors for larger audiences.
- Visual output devices are often part of a broader workstation setup.

**Input devices and accessories**

Input peripherals help you interact with a computer or mobile device. Some devices are standard, while others add specialized control or convenience features. Touch-based accessories are especially common on tablets and phones.

- A **touch pen** is also called a touchscreen stylus or capacitive stylus.
- A touch pen lets you write, sign, or select items without using your finger.
- Touch pens can improve visibility by keeping your hand away from the screen.
- Mice and keyboards may include additional features beyond basic input.
- Touchpads and signature pads are common in environments that need compact or handwritten input.

**Mobile device accessories**

Mobile accessories extend the usefulness of tablets and phones by improving input, audio, or control. These accessories are often chosen for convenience, handwriting, or media use.

- Active styluses support more precise interaction than basic finger input.
- Headsets provide private audio input and output.
- Speakers support shared audio playback.
- Trackpads can provide an alternate pointing method for mobile workflows.

**Shared-control and specialty peripherals**

Some peripherals are designed for specific workflows or for controlling multiple systems. These devices are common in labs, support desks, and presentation environments.

- A **KVM** switch stands for keyboard, video, and mouse.
- A KVM switch lets one user control multiple computers with a single set of peripherals.
- Game controllers, digital cameras, webcams, microphones, and projectors are also common peripheral examples.
- External optical drives and external storage devices expand system capability without internal installation.

**Practical recognition points**

When you identify a peripheral, focus on what it does, how it connects, and whether it is an input, output, or combined-function device. That approach helps you distinguish similar devices during troubleshooting or exam questions.

- Match the device to its primary function first.
- Check whether it uses USB, Ethernet, wireless, or another supported connection.
- Look for combined devices such as AIO printers or multi-function mobile accessories.
- Remember that some peripherals are designed for one computer, while others support multiple systems.
