## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Expansion cards and motherboard expansion slots**

Expansion slots let you add hardware to a motherboard after the system is built. An expansion card plugs into a compatible slot and extends the computer’s capabilities without replacing the motherboard.

These slots are also called **bus slots** or expansion ports. They are internal connections on the motherboard or riser board that let add-on cards communicate with the rest of the system.

- Common uses include adding a video card, network card, sound card, or storage controller.
- The slot must match the card type, so compatibility matters.
- Many older slot types are obsolete, and you are most likely to encounter `AGP`, `PCI`, and `PCI Express` in modern systems.

**Internal buses and communication paths**

A motherboard contains multiple components that must communicate with each other, including the CPU, memory, storage, and expansion slots. That communication happens over **buses**, which are shared pathways for data transfer.

A bus can connect the CPU to memory, or memory to expansion slots. This is what allows added hardware to work as part of the system instead of as a separate device.

- The **data bus** carries information between components.
- The **memory bus** connects memory modules to the rest of the motherboard.
- Expansion cards rely on these communication paths to exchange data with the CPU and memory.

**PCI and PCI Express**

`PCI` stands for **Peripheral Component Interconnect**. It is a common internal expansion bus used for add-on cards such as network cards, SCSI cards, sound cards, and video cards.

`PCI Express` or `PCIe` stands for **Peripheral Component Interconnect Express**. It is a high-speed interface standard used for internal components and is the primary modern choice for expansion cards.

- `PCIe` is commonly used for graphics cards, network cards, and storage controllers.
- `PCIe` slots come in different lane counts such as `x1`, `x4`, `x8`, and `x16`.
- A higher lane count generally provides more data transfer capacity between the card and the motherboard.

**Slot types and practical selection**

You choose the slot based on the card you want to install. A video card typically uses a `PCIe` slot, while older systems may still have `PCI` or `AGP` slots.

- `AGP` is associated with video cards in older systems.
- `PCI` supports a range of legacy expansion cards.
- `PCIe` is the preferred modern option because it offers higher speed and flexible lane configurations.

> Use the slot that matches the card and the motherboard’s supported bus type. A physically incompatible card will not install correctly.

**How expansion affects system capability**

Adding an expansion card is a practical way to upgrade a computer without changing the whole platform. The card becomes part of the motherboard’s internal communication structure through the bus system.

This modular design is why expansion slots matter in troubleshooting and upgrades. If you need more functionality, you can often add it through a compatible card instead of replacing the motherboard.

**Quick review**

- Expansion slots accept add-on cards.
- Internal buses move data between motherboard components.
- `PCI` and `PCIe` are the most relevant expansion technologies for modern A+ work.
- `PCIe` uses lane-based configurations such as `x1` and `x16`.
- Compatibility between the card, slot, and motherboard is essential.
