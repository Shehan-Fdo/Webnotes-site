## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Overview**

Input/output ports and connectors let you attach displays and other peripherals to a computer. For A+ purposes, you should be able to recognize common video connectors, understand what they carry, and match them to the right device or use case.

Modern systems commonly use digital display connections such as HDMI, DisplayPort, and USB-C. Older equipment may still rely on VGA or DVI, so you need to identify both current and legacy options.

**Common display connectors**

HDMI stands for **High Definition Multimedia Interface**. It carries both audio and video over a single digital connection and is common on monitors, televisions, and consumer electronics. HDMI Type A is a 19-pin connector, and the plug shape helps you identify it quickly.

- **HDMI** is widely used for displays and TVs.
- It carries audio and video together.
- It has an approximate maximum range of 20 meters before signal quality can degrade.

DisplayPort also carries video and audio. It is a standard released by the Video Electronics Standards Association, or **VESA**. DisplayPort is described as a royalty-free standard, and it comes in more than one connector type.

- **DisplayPort** supports both video and audio.
- It is associated with VESA.
- It has multiple connector sizes, including a standard connector and a smaller variant.

USB-C can also be used for video output on supported devices. In practice, this makes it useful when a laptop or other device needs to send display output through a reversible, compact connector.

**Legacy video connectors**

Older displays and computers may use VGA or DVI. These connectors still matter because you may need them when working with older hardware or adapters.

- **VGA** is a legacy display connector.
- **DVI** is another older video interface you may encounter.
- Legacy ports are common on older PCs, monitors, and projectors.

When you troubleshoot display issues, identifying the connector type is often the first step. A physical match matters because the port shape determines what cable can fit, and the signal type determines whether the device can actually communicate with the display.

**How to identify and choose a connector**

Use the device’s available ports, the display’s input options, and the required signal type to choose the correct cable. If the device and monitor support a modern digital standard, prefer that over a legacy analog option.

1. Identify the output port on the computer or adapter.
2. Identify the input port on the monitor or display.
3. Match the connector type and signal support.
4. Use an adapter only when the devices do not share a native port.

> If a connector does not fit cleanly, do not force it. Port shape is a strong clue, and forcing the wrong cable can damage the interface.

**Practical comparison**

| Connector | Signal type | Common use | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| HDMI | Digital audio and video | Monitors, TVs, consumer electronics | Very common; Type A is 19-pin |
| DisplayPort | Digital audio and video | PCs and monitors | Standard connector plus smaller variant |
| USB-C | Digital video on supported devices | Laptops and modern devices | Often used for compact, multifunction connections |
| DVI | Video | Older displays and PCs | Legacy option |
| VGA | Analog video | Older monitors, projectors, and PCs | Legacy option |

**Troubleshooting reminders**

A display problem is not always caused by the monitor itself. The cable, adapter, port, or signal standard may be the real issue. Start by checking the connector type, then verify that both devices support the same output and input method.

- Confirm the cable is fully seated.
- Check for bent or damaged pins on older connectors.
- Verify that the monitor is set to the correct input source.
- Replace adapters that do not support the needed signal conversion.

**Key takeaways**

The most important skill is fast visual identification. HDMI, DisplayPort, USB-C, DVI, and VGA each have distinct roles, and the right choice depends on the device, the display, and whether you need modern digital video or legacy compatibility.
