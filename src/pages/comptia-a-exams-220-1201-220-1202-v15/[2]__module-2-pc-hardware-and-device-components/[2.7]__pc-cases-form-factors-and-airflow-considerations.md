## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**PC case and motherboard fit**

A PC case is more than a shell. It determines what motherboard sizes you can install, how much room you have for components, and how easy the system is to build and upgrade. Motherboard form factor refers to the board’s size and layout, including where major components and ports are placed.

Choosing the right case and board together helps you avoid fit problems and supports the kind of system you want to build. Larger boards generally support more expansion, while smaller boards support compact systems.

- **ATX** is a common full-size form factor for larger builds.
- **Micro-ATX** offers a smaller footprint while still supporting many standard desktop needs.
- **Mini-ITX** supports very compact systems and is often chosen when space is limited.
- Case size must match the motherboard form factor you plan to use.

**Why form factor matters**

Form factor affects more than physical size. It influences component layout, available expansion, and the overall design of the computer. Smaller boards can fit into compact devices, while larger boards can better support more powerful or expandable systems.

You should think about the full build, not just the motherboard. The case must provide enough clearance for the board, cooling hardware, storage, and any add-in cards you plan to install.

- **Compatibility** is the first concern when selecting a case and motherboard.
- **Expansion** tends to be greater in larger form factors.
- **Compact builds** usually require more careful planning for space and cooling.
- **Layout** affects where connectors, slots, and headers are positioned.

**Airflow and cooling basics**

Good airflow helps keep components within safe operating temperatures. Modern systems generate heat, and poor cooling can reduce performance, shorten component life, or cause instability. Fan placement matters because simply adding more fans does not guarantee better cooling.

A practical airflow pattern is to bring cool air in from the front and move warm air out through the rear and top. This creates a path that helps remove heat from the case.

- Front fans usually act as **intake**.
- Rear fans usually act as **exhaust**.
- Top fans often help remove rising heat.
- Fan placement should support a clear airflow path through the case.

> Poor airflow can lead to reduced performance, component damage, or system crashes if heat is not managed effectively.

**Fan selection and placement**

When you choose case fans, you should consider both performance and fit. Fan size must match the case mounting location, and the connector must be supported by the motherboard or power source. Fan performance is often discussed in terms of airflow volume, with higher airflow generally helping cooling.

Some fans use 4-pin PWM (Pulse Width Modulation), while others use 3-pin connections. Older or cheaper fans may connect directly to the power supply and run at full speed.

- **CFM** stands for Cubic Feet per Minute and describes how much air a fan moves.
- **PWM** fans can support more flexible speed control when the motherboard supports them.
- **3-pin** fans are still common in some systems.
- Fan size and connector type must match the case and motherboard headers.

**Practical build checklist**

1. Choose the motherboard form factor you need based on size, expansion, and build goals.
2. Select a case that supports that form factor.
3. Verify clearance for cooling hardware and other components.
4. Plan fan placement so front intake and rear/top exhaust create a clear airflow path.
5. Confirm fan size and connector compatibility before installation.

**Key takeaways**

The best case is the one that fits your motherboard, supports your components, and allows air to move efficiently through the system. Form factor affects size and expandability, while airflow affects temperature and stability. If you plan both together, you reduce build problems and improve long-term reliability.
