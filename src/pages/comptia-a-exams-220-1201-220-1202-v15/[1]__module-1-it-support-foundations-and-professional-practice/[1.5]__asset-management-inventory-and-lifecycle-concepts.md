## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Asset management overview**

IT asset management (ITAM) is the process of tracking and controlling the technology assets that support services and operations. Assets can be physical items, such as laptops, printers, servers, routers, and mobile devices, or intangible items, such as software licenses.

Good asset management gives you visibility into what you have, where it is, and how it is being used. That visibility helps you support users, reduce loss, improve compliance, and make better purchasing decisions.

- **Hardware assets** are physical devices and equipment.
- **Software assets** include installed applications and licenses.
- **Asset tracking** helps you identify and monitor items across the environment.
- **Inventory accuracy** reduces guesswork when you need to troubleshoot, replace, or retire equipment.

**Inventory and identification**

An asset inventory is the record of assets an organization owns or manages. In practice, inventory management is about keeping that record current as devices are added, moved, repaired, reassigned, or removed.

Asset tagging is a common way to identify hardware. A tag gives each device a unique identifier, often using a barcode, QR code, or RFID (radio-frequency identification) label. Tags can store or reference details such as serial number and model information.

> Keep asset tags consistent and unique. If a device cannot be identified quickly, inventory data becomes less reliable.

- **Unique identifiers** make it easier to match a physical device to its record.
- **Serial numbers** help distinguish one device from another.
- **Model details** support maintenance and replacement planning.
- **Inventory records** should reflect the current status and location of each asset.

**Hardware asset management**

Hardware asset management (HAM) focuses on physical IT equipment throughout its life. It is more than counting devices; it is a systematic way to manage equipment from purchase to replacement.

HAM often uses tools that automate discovery, tracking, and reporting. These tools can identify device details such as hostname, make, model, serial number, memory, hard drive, and installed software. Some systems also integrate with remote support tools so you can connect to a device directly from the asset record.

- **Discovery** finds devices in the environment.
- **Tracking** follows devices as they move through use and support.
- **Reporting** helps you review what is deployed and what needs attention.
- **Automation** reduces manual spreadsheet work and improves consistency.

**Lifecycle concepts**

Asset lifecycle management follows an asset from acquisition through use and eventual retirement. The exact stages can vary by organization, but the core idea stays the same: manage the asset across its entire useful life.

A practical lifecycle view helps you plan for procurement, deployment, maintenance, replacement, and disposal. It also helps you understand when an asset is no longer cost-effective or suitable for service.

- **Procurement** is the purchase or acquisition stage.
- **Deployment** is when the asset is issued and put into service.
- **Maintenance** keeps the asset usable and supported.
- **Replacement** occurs when the asset no longer meets needs.
- **Disposition** is the final removal or retirement stage.

**Why lifecycle tracking matters**

Lifecycle tracking helps you avoid gaps between what is physically present and what the records show. It also supports budgeting because you can see when assets are nearing replacement and when software or hardware needs renewal.

This approach is especially useful in environments with many devices or geographically dispersed teams, where manual tracking becomes difficult. Better lifecycle control also supports compliance and operational efficiency.

- **Planning** improves purchasing and refresh cycles.
- **Compliance** is easier when assets are tagged and documented.
- **Efficiency** improves when support teams can find accurate asset data quickly.
- **Control** increases when assets are tracked from start to finish.

**Practical workflow**

1. Identify the asset and assign a unique tag.
2. Record key details such as serial number, model, and location.
3. Add the asset to the inventory system.
4. Track changes when the asset is moved, repaired, reassigned, or upgraded.
5. Retire the asset when it reaches end of life and update the record.

**Key takeaways**

Asset management, inventory, and lifecycle tracking work together. Inventory tells you what exists, tagging tells you which item is which, and lifecycle management tells you where each asset is in its useful life.

For A+ purposes, focus on the relationship between physical asset tracking, accurate records, and the full lifecycle from purchase to retirement.
