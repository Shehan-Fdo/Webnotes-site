## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Safety fundamentals**

Before you open a computer, laptop, monitor, or other electrical equipment, you should treat the device as a safety risk until you verify it is safe to work on. Basic precautions protect both you and the hardware. Dell’s repair guidance emphasizes that these steps matter before internal access, hardware replacement, or upgrades.

- **Power state** matters before service.
- **Electrical safety** starts with assuming internal components may still present risk.
- **Handling discipline** reduces accidental damage during repair.

A practical safety mindset also includes planning for the work area, the equipment’s weight, and the possibility of sharp edges or hot components. Use careful movement and stable placement when lifting or repositioning hardware.

> Do not work inside equipment until you have taken the appropriate safety precautions for the device and environment.

**Electrostatic discharge control**

<abbr title="Electrostatic Discharge">ESD</abbr> is the sudden transfer of static electricity that can damage sensitive components. The goal of ESD control is to reduce charge buildup, prevent discharge into parts, and define where protected handling should occur. The ESD Association describes this as part of a broader control program that includes protection design, environment control, and defined electrostatic protected areas (<abbr title="Electrostatic Protected Area">EPA</abbr>s).

- **ESD protection** helps prevent invisible damage to components.
- **EPA** boundaries define where static-sensitive handling is controlled.
- **Control materials** support safe handling during assembly and repair.

Common control practices focus on reducing static generation and safely dissipating charge. In practice, that means using approved handling methods and materials, keeping sensitive parts in protected areas, and avoiding unnecessary contact with exposed circuitry.

**Basic handling practices**

When you work on internal components, use a consistent routine that limits both physical and static risk. Keep parts organized, move deliberately, and avoid placing components where they can be contaminated, bent, or dropped.

1. Prepare the workspace and make sure the area is suitable for electronics work.
2. Reduce static risk before touching internal parts.
3. Handle components carefully and keep them in controlled areas.
4. Lift and move equipment using safe body mechanics and stable support.

- **Organization** helps prevent accidental damage and lost parts.
- **Controlled movement** lowers the chance of dropping or stressing hardware.
- **Clean handling** reduces contamination and contact damage.

**Environmental handling considerations**

Environmental handling means paying attention to the conditions around the equipment as well as the way you store and move it. Temperature, moisture, and general workspace conditions can affect component safety and reliability. A controlled environment supports safer repair and reduces the chance of avoidable damage.

- **Moisture** can increase risk to electronics and handling safety.
- **Workspace conditions** should support stable, careful service.
- **Storage and transport** should protect parts from physical and environmental stress.

When you are preparing equipment for service or transport, keep it protected from unnecessary exposure and avoid rough handling. If a component is sensitive, treat it as such even if it appears to be functioning normally.

**Exam-ready takeaways**

For A+ purposes, focus on the relationship between safety, ESD prevention, and environmental care. These are not separate ideas; they work together to protect people and hardware during maintenance.

- **Safety** protects the technician.
- **ESD control** protects the component.
- **Environmental handling** protects the equipment during service and movement.
