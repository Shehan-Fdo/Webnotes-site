## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Network device roles**

Network devices are the hardware that help computers communicate, share resources, and reach other networks or the internet. In practice, each device has a specific job: some direct traffic, some expand wired or wireless connectivity, and some protect the network from unwanted traffic.

- **Routers** connect different networks and make forwarding decisions based on the destination IP address.
- **Switches** expand wired connectivity inside a local network and help move traffic efficiently between devices.
- **Access points** provide wireless coverage so Wi-Fi clients can join the network.
- **Firewalls** filter traffic to help protect the network.
- **Modems** provide the link to an internet service such as cable or DSL.

**Routers**

A router sits between networks and sends traffic toward the correct destination. In a small office or home office (SOHO), a single device often combines routing with other functions, but the routing role is still the same: connect networks and forward packets between them.

- Routers are used when you need communication between IP subnets.
- They help manage traffic flow between local devices and external networks.
- In SOHO setups, the router may also include switching, wireless, and firewall features in one appliance.

**Switches**

A switch connects multiple devices on a local area network (LAN). Compared with older hub-style devices, switches are designed to forward traffic more intelligently and reduce unnecessary network noise.

- Switches provide multiple Ethernet ports for wired devices.
- They help reduce congestion by sending traffic only where it needs to go.
- Managed switches offer more control than unmanaged switches, while unmanaged switches are simpler to deploy.

**Access points**

An access point extends the network over Wi-Fi. It gives wireless devices a way to connect to the wired network and is especially useful when coverage must reach larger spaces or multiple floors.

- Access points provide wireless connectivity for laptops, phones, and other Wi-Fi clients.
- They extend coverage where a wired connection is not practical.
- In many SOHO devices, the access point is built into the same unit as the router.

**Firewalls**

A firewall controls traffic based on rules. Its purpose is to allow legitimate communication while blocking or limiting unwanted access.

- Firewalls filter traffic entering or leaving a network.
- They add a security layer between trusted and untrusted networks.
- In small networks, firewall functionality is often integrated into the router.

> A firewall is a security control, not a replacement for good network design. You still need proper device placement and sensible access rules.

**Modems**

A modem connects your network to the service provider’s line. It is the device that translates the connection from the ISP side into something your local network can use.

- Cable modems and DSL modems are common examples.
- A modem is often paired with a router in home and small office environments.
- For fiber connections, an optical network terminal (ONT) may serve a similar handoff role.

**How these devices work together**

In a typical home or SOHO network, these functions are often combined into one box. That makes setup simpler, but it can hide the individual roles of each component. Understanding the separate functions helps you troubleshoot connectivity problems more effectively.

- The modem or ONT connects to the service provider.
- The router connects the local network to that upstream link.
- The switch adds more wired ports.
- The access point provides wireless access.
- The firewall filters traffic to improve security.

**Quick comparison**

| Device | Main job | Common use |
|---|---|---|
| Router | Connects networks and forwards traffic | LAN to internet or between subnets |
| Switch | Connects wired devices on a LAN | Expanding Ethernet ports |
| Access point | Provides Wi-Fi access | Wireless client connectivity |
| Firewall | Filters traffic | Network protection |
| Modem | Connects to ISP service | Cable or DSL internet access |

**Exam focus**

For A+ purposes, focus on what each device does and where it fits in the network path. The most common confusion is between a router and a switch, or between a modem and a router.

- A **router** connects networks.
- A **switch** connects devices within a network.
- An **access point** adds wireless access.
- A **firewall** filters traffic.
- A **modem** connects to the ISP.
