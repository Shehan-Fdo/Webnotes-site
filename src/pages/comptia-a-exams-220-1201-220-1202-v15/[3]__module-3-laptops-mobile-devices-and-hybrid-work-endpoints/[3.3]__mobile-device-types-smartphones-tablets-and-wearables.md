## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Mobile device categories**

Mobile devices are designed for portability, touch-based interaction, and communication from almost anywhere. In A+ contexts, the main device types you need to recognize are smartphones, tablets, and wearables.

- **Smartphones** are compact mobile communication devices.
- **Tablets** use larger touchscreens and often support productivity and media tasks.
- **Wearables** are body-worn devices that extend mobile functionality.

**Smartphones**

Smartphones are described as the ultimate communication device. They support voice calls, text messaging, email, and video conferencing in a small handheld form factor.

- Typical screen size ranges from about `3.5 inches` to `5.5 inches` diagonal.
- They also work well as media viewers for audio and video content.
- They combine communication features with general-purpose app support.

A smartphone is usually the most portable of the three device types, so you use it when mobility matters more than screen size.

**Tablets**

Tablets are one of the fastest-growing mobile device categories. They usually run `iOS` or `Android` and use a touchscreen interface instead of a physical keyboard and mouse.

- Typical screen size is `7 inches` or larger.
- Input often uses a finger or stylus.
- A virtual keyboard may appear on screen for text entry.
- Common uses include productivity apps, games, and audio/video media.

Tablets sit between smartphones and laptops in size and capability. They are useful when you want a larger display without carrying a full computer.

**Wearables**

Wearable technology is a mobile device category designed to be worn on the body. The provided evidence identifies wearables as part of the mobile device landscape, but does not go into detailed subtypes.

- They extend mobile access in a hands-free or body-worn form.
- They are part of the broader set of devices used for mobile connectivity and convenience.

When you study wearables, focus on their role as compact companion devices rather than full replacement computers.

**Quick comparison**

| Device type | Typical form factor | Primary input | Common use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Smartphone | Small handheld device | Touchscreen, virtual keyboard | Calls, messaging, email, video conferencing |
| Tablet | Larger slate device | Touchscreen, stylus, virtual keyboard | Productivity, games, media consumption |
| Wearable | Body-worn device | Device-specific controls | Mobile convenience and companion functionality |

**Practical identification tips**

You can usually identify the device type by size, input method, and intended use.

- If it fits easily in one hand and centers on communication, it is likely a **smartphone**.
- If it has a larger touchscreen and is used for apps and media, it is likely a **tablet**.
- If it is worn on the body, it is a **wearable**.

> Tablets and smartphones may both use touch input, but the screen size and primary use case usually make the difference clear.

**Study focus for A+**

For exam purposes, know the basic characteristics of each device type and how they differ in form factor and usage.

- Recognize the typical size range of smartphones and tablets.
- Associate tablets with touch input and larger screens.
- Associate smartphones with communication-first functionality.
- Remember that wearables are part of the mobile device family.
