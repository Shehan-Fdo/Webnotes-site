## **Course:** CompTIA A+ (Exams 220-1201 & 220-1202) — V15

**Laptop hardware overview**

Laptops use many of the same core components as desktops, but the smaller form factor changes how those parts are arranged and serviced. That compact design makes laptops portable, but it also makes troubleshooting and repair more precise.

A technician often needs to work within manufacturer-specific layouts. Some laptops are designed for easier access to memory or storage, while others make internal replacement much harder.

- **Form factor** affects how components fit and how easily you can replace them.
- **Serviceability** varies by manufacturer and model.
- **Precision** matters because laptop parts are built to tight physical constraints.

**Keyboard and input components**

Laptop keyboards are more compact than desktop keyboards. Keys are placed closer together, and some dedicated key groups found on desktop systems may be reduced or omitted to save space.

Key caps and keyboard assemblies may be replaceable, but the exact repair approach depends on the laptop design. When you service input hardware, you need to match the replacement part to the specific model.

- **Compact layout** reduces available key spacing.
- **Function keys** are often integrated into a tighter top row.
- **Key cap replacement** may be possible on some models.

**Storage options**

Laptop storage commonly includes traditional hard drives and solid-state drives (SSDs). The evidence emphasizes the shift from hard drives to SSD technology, which is a common upgrade path in modern laptops.

Some laptops allow easier access to storage devices than others. If the system is designed for internal replacement, storage upgrades can be a practical maintenance task.

- **Hard drives** are older mechanical storage devices.
- **SSDs** provide a high-speed storage option.
- **Access design** determines how easy replacement will be.

**Memory and internal access**

Laptop memory can be replaceable, but access depends on the chassis design. Some systems let you open a service panel and swap memory modules, while others make internal access more difficult.

Because laptops are built to precise specifications, you should verify compatibility before replacing memory or other internal parts.

- **Memory access** may be straightforward or restricted.
- **Compatibility** matters when selecting replacement modules.
- **Chassis design** influences repair time and difficulty.

**Wireless and mobile features**

Laptops commonly include wireless adapters so you can connect without a wired network. Mobile systems may also include biometric integrations, cameras, microphones, and near-field communication (NFC), which support portability and convenience.

These components are part of what makes laptops useful as all-in-one mobile systems, but they also add more items to troubleshoot when a device has input, audio, or connectivity issues.

- **Wireless adapters** support network connectivity.
- **Biometrics** add user authentication options.
- **Cameras and microphones** are common built-in peripherals.
- **NFC** supports short-range communication features.

**Battery and power considerations**

A laptop is a battery-powered mobile system, so battery technology is a major part of its hardware design. Battery condition affects portability and overall usability, and replacement may be necessary over the life of the device.

Because the battery is central to mobile operation, power-related troubleshooting often starts there.

- **Battery technology** is essential to laptop mobility.
- **Replacement** may be needed as batteries age.
- **Power issues** can affect the entire system experience.

**Repair and replacement strategy**

Laptop repair is often more challenging than desktop repair because the components are smaller and more tightly integrated. You should expect variation between manufacturers and even between models from the same manufacturer.

When you plan a repair, focus on the parts that the design actually allows you to access. In many cases, the practical replacement list includes storage, memory, keyboard parts, battery components, and selected built-in devices.

1. Identify the exact laptop model.
2. Check the manufacturer’s service information.
3. Confirm which parts are replaceable.
4. Match the replacement part to the model and revision.
5. Perform the repair using the least invasive access path.

> Laptop hardware repair is often limited by the device’s physical design, so model-specific documentation is critical before you open the system.

**Key takeaways**

Laptop hardware combines familiar PC components with a compact, mobile design. The main exam focus is understanding which parts are commonly present, which parts may be replaceable, and why access varies so much across models.

- **Mobility** drives the design.
- **Replaceability** depends on the model.
- **Documentation** helps you avoid mismatched parts and unnecessary disassembly.
