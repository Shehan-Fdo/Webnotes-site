## **Course:** AWS Certified SysOps Administrator – Associate (Exam SOA-C02)

**Incident response lifecycle in AWS**

Incident response is the process you use to detect, analyze, contain, eradicate, recover from, and learn from a security event. In AWS, the workflow is shaped by shared responsibility, automation, and the need to preserve evidence while limiting blast radius.

For exam purposes, think in terms of a repeatable lifecycle:

- **Preparation**: define roles, guardrails, logging, and playbooks before an incident.
- **Detection and analysis**: identify suspicious activity using logs, alerts, and service telemetry.
- **Containment**: stop further damage without destroying evidence.
- **Eradication and recovery**: remove the threat and restore trusted workloads.
- **Post-incident review**: document lessons learned and improve controls.

A strong answer usually connects the incident phase to the right AWS service or control, not just the generic security concept.

**Preparation and governance**

Preparation is where you reduce decision-making during a crisis. You should define who does what, how escalation works, and how communication happens before an incident starts.

Key governance elements include:

- **Roles and responsibilities**: assign incident commander, security lead, operations lead, communications lead, and application owner.
- **Escalation paths**: define when to notify management, legal, compliance, and external responders.
- **Severity classification**: create criteria such as data exposure, privilege compromise, service outage, and scope of affected accounts.
- **Communication plans**: specify internal channels, status update cadence, and approved external messaging.
- **Runbooks and playbooks**: document actions for common scenarios such as compromised credentials, malware on EC2, or public S3 exposure.

A practical severity model often uses impact and urgency:

| Severity | Example | Typical response |
|---|---|---|
| Sev 1 | Active data exfiltration or root compromise | Immediate containment, executive escalation |
| Sev 2 | Suspicious privilege escalation or malware on one host | Rapid triage and isolation |
| Sev 3 | Misconfiguration with limited exposure | Controlled remediation |
| Sev 4 | Low-risk policy violation | Scheduled fix |

AWS-specific preparation should include:

- **AWS Organizations** to centralize governance across accounts.
- **Service Control Policies (SCPs)** to enforce pre-incident guardrails, such as blocking disabling of CloudTrail or restricting risky regions.
- **AWS Artifact** to retrieve compliance reports and agreements that may support governance and audit readiness.
- **AWS Incident Response playbooks** or internal runbooks aligned to AWS services and account structure.
- **Break-glass access** for emergency use, with tightly controlled credentials and monitoring.

> Use SCPs as guardrails, not as a substitute for incident response. They help prevent or limit risky actions before and during an incident, but they do not replace detection or containment.

**Logging, monitoring, and forensic readiness**

Forensics depends on having trustworthy data before the incident. If logs are missing, mutable, or scattered across accounts, you lose visibility and may lose evidence integrity.

Build a centralized logging architecture with:

- **AWS CloudTrail** for API activity, ideally organization trails covering all accounts and regions.
- **Amazon CloudWatch Logs** for application and system logs.
- **VPC Flow Logs** for network traffic metadata.
- **AWS Config** for resource configuration history and drift detection.
- **Elastic Load Balancing access logs**, **S3 server access logs**, and service-specific logs where relevant.

Forensic readiness practices:

- Store logs in a dedicated security account or log archive account.
- Use separate, restricted buckets and log groups with limited write access.
- Enable **S3 Object Lock** or write-once retention where appropriate.
- Use **KMS** encryption and tightly control key access.
- Set retention policies that match compliance and investigation needs.
- Time-synchronize systems so event correlation is reliable.

Evidence integrity and chain of custody matter when you may need to prove what happened:

- Preserve original logs and snapshots; do not overwrite them.
- Record who collected evidence, when, and from where.
- Use immutable storage or versioned storage for artifacts.
- Hash exported files when transferring them between teams.
- Restrict access to evidence repositories and audit all access.

A common exam pattern is centralized logging with a dedicated account:

```text
Management account
  └── AWS Organizations
      ├── Workload account A
      ├── Workload account B
      └── Log archive / security account
            ├── CloudTrail logs
            ├── Config snapshots
            ├── VPC Flow Logs
            └── Investigation artifacts
```

**Detection and triage**

Detection starts with a signal, but triage determines whether the signal is a real incident and how urgent it is. You should correlate multiple sources rather than relying on a single alert.

Useful AWS signals include:

- **GuardDuty** findings for suspicious API calls, credential compromise, or anomalous behavior.
- **CloudTrail** events for identity and API activity.
- **CloudWatch alarms** for metrics such as CPU spikes, failed logins, or unusual network traffic.
- **AWS Config** noncompliance for drift or unauthorized changes.
- **Security Hub** for aggregated findings across services.

During triage, ask:

- What resource or account is affected?
- Is the issue isolated or widespread?
- Is there evidence of credential compromise?
- Is data exposure or exfiltration possible?
- What is the minimum action needed to stop harm?

A practical triage workflow is:

1. Confirm the alert with logs and service telemetry.
2. Identify the affected principal, resource, region, and account.
3. Determine whether the activity is ongoing.
4. Classify severity and assign ownership.
5. Choose containment actions that preserve evidence.

**Containment strategies**

Containment limits damage while keeping the environment analyzable. In AWS, containment often means reducing permissions, isolating resources, or restricting network paths.

Common containment actions include:

- **Disable or rotate credentials** for compromised IAM users, access keys, or roles.
- **Detach policies** or remove session access where possible.
- **Isolate an EC2 instance** by moving it to a quarantine security group or removing outbound access.
- **Stop nonessential workloads** if the risk outweighs availability concerns.
- **Restrict network exposure** with security groups, NACLs, route tables, or load balancer changes.
- **Use AWS Organizations SCPs** to block destructive or risky actions across an account.
- **Suspend automation** that could spread compromise, such as deployment pipelines or scheduled jobs.

Account-level containment is especially important in AWS because compromise can spread through IAM and automation. If an account is suspected to be compromised, you may need to:

- Remove or restrict access keys and federated sessions.
- Apply an SCP that denies broad actions except a small set of emergency operations.
- Limit the account to incident response personnel only.
- Preserve logs and snapshots before making disruptive changes.
- Use a separate break-glass role from a trusted security account.

A common emergency SCP pattern is to deny everything except a small allowlist for responders:

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "DenyAllExceptIncidentResponse",
      "Effect": "Deny",
      "NotAction": [
        "iam:Get*",
        "iam:List*",
        "cloudtrail:LookupEvents",
        "ec2:Describe*",
        "s3:Get*",
        "s3:List*",
        "sts:AssumeRole"
      ],
      "Resource": "*"
    }
  ]
}
```

This kind of guardrail is useful when you need to freeze most activity in a compromised account while still allowing investigation.

**Break-glass access and emergency controls**

Break-glass access is an emergency access path used when normal administrative access is unavailable or untrusted. It should be rare, monitored, and protected by strong controls.

Good break-glass design includes:

- A dedicated emergency role or user with MFA.
- Separate credentials stored securely and accessed only during incidents.
- CloudTrail alerts on use of the role.
- Tight permissions scoped to incident response tasks.
- A documented approval process and post-use review.

Use break-glass access when:

- The primary admin path is compromised.
- You need to restore access to critical systems.
- You must perform urgent containment in a locked-down account.

Avoid using break-glass access for routine administration, because that weakens its value and increases risk.

**Eradication and remediation**

Eradication removes the cause of the incident. Remediation fixes the weakness that allowed it. These are related but not identical: you can remove malware from a host and still leave the original vulnerability unpatched.

Typical eradication tasks include:

- Rebuilding compromised instances from trusted images.
- Rotating secrets, keys, and passwords.
- Removing unauthorized IAM users, roles, policies, and access keys.
- Patching vulnerable software and operating systems.
- Correcting misconfigurations in security groups, bucket policies, or public access settings.

Practical AWS remediation examples:

- **EC2 compromise**: terminate the instance after preserving evidence, then launch a replacement from a known-good AMI.
- **EBS-backed workload**: create a snapshot for evidence, then restore data to a clean volume if needed.
- **RDS compromise or corruption**: restore from a snapshot or use **point-in-time recovery (PITR)** to a time before the incident.
- **S3 data loss**: recover deleted or overwritten objects using **versioning**; if enabled, use **S3 Object Lock** to prevent tampering with retained objects.
- **Lambda or container compromise**: redeploy from a trusted pipeline and rotate any exposed secrets.

A useful remediation mindset is:

- Fix the immediate issue.
- Remove persistence mechanisms.
- Close the root cause.
- Validate that the fix does not reintroduce exposure.

**Recovery patterns by AWS service**

Recovery restores business function after containment and eradication. The right recovery method depends on the service and the backup strategy you already have in place.

Common recovery options include:

- **Amazon EC2**
  - Restore from a golden **AMI**.
  - Reattach data volumes from clean **EBS snapshots**.
  - Recreate instances with infrastructure as code if possible.
- **Amazon RDS**
  - Restore from automated backups or a manual snapshot.
  - Use **PITR** to recover to a specific time.
  - Validate parameter groups, security groups, and subnet groups after restore.
- **Amazon S3**
  - Recover previous object versions if versioning is enabled.
  - Use delete markers carefully; remove them to expose prior versions.
  - Use Object Lock for immutable retention where required.
- **Amazon EFS**
  - Restore from backups if enabled.
- **Amazon DynamoDB**
  - Restore from point-in-time recovery if enabled.
- **Amazon EBS**
  - Restore a volume from snapshot and attach it to a clean instance.

A simple recovery decision table:

| Service | Preferred recovery method | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| EC2 | New instance from AMI | Best for clean rebuilds |
| EBS | Snapshot restore | Useful for data volumes |
| RDS | Snapshot or PITR | Choose the closest safe restore point |
| S3 | Versioning/Object Lock | Depends on versioning being enabled before incident |
| DynamoDB | PITR restore | Requires feature enabled in advance |

Recovery should also include validation:

- Confirm the workload starts cleanly.
- Verify logs, metrics, and alarms.
- Check that credentials and secrets have been rotated.
- Confirm that public exposure is removed.
- Test application functionality before declaring the incident closed.

**End-to-end incident scenario: compromised EC2 instance**

A realistic exam scenario often combines detection, containment, evidence preservation, and recovery.

Example flow:

1. GuardDuty reports suspicious outbound traffic from an EC2 instance.
2. You confirm unusual process activity in CloudWatch logs and a recent IAM role assumption in CloudTrail.
3. You isolate the instance by changing its security group to a quarantine group with no outbound internet access.
4. You preserve evidence by creating an EBS snapshot and exporting relevant logs to the log archive account.
5. You rotate any credentials that may have been exposed.
6. You terminate the instance and launch a replacement from a hardened AMI.
7. You patch the vulnerability or remove the exposed secret from the deployment pipeline.
8. You review why the alert was not caught earlier and update detections.

This scenario tests whether you know when to isolate, when to preserve, and when to rebuild.

**End-to-end incident scenario: public S3 exposure**

Another common pattern is accidental data exposure through bucket policy or ACL misconfiguration.

Example flow:

1. AWS Config flags the bucket as publicly accessible.
2. You verify the exposure and determine whether objects contain sensitive data.
3. You immediately block public access at the bucket or account level.
4. You review CloudTrail data events and S3 access logs for evidence of access.
5. You rotate or revoke any credentials that allowed the misconfiguration.
6. You restore the correct bucket policy and enable preventive controls such as S3 Block Public Access.
7. If objects were deleted or overwritten, you recover them from versioning if available.
8. You document the incident and update guardrails to prevent recurrence.

This is a good example of using both detective and preventive controls together.

**Automation and repeatability**

Automation reduces response time and helps ensure consistent actions. In AWS, you can automate parts of the workflow with event-driven services and infrastructure as code.

Useful automation patterns include:

- Triggering a Lambda function from EventBridge when a high-severity finding appears.
- Using Systems Manager Automation documents to quarantine instances or collect diagnostics.
- Storing incident runbooks as version-controlled documents.
- Using CloudFormation or Terraform to rebuild clean infrastructure quickly.
- Sending notifications through SNS, email, or chat integrations.

Example Systems Manager command to inspect a Linux instance:

```bash
aws ssm send-command \
  --instance-ids i-0123456789abcdef0 \
  --document-name "AWS-RunShellScript" \
  --parameters commands='["whoami","ps aux","netstat -plant","sudo lsof -i"]'
```

Example to create an EBS snapshot for evidence:

```bash
aws ec2 create-snapshot \
  --volume-id vol-0123456789abcdef0 \
  --description "Incident evidence snapshot before remediation"
```

Automation is most valuable when it reduces time to containment without destroying evidence or making the situation worse.

**Post-incident review and continuous improvement**

After recovery, you should perform a structured review. The goal is not blame; it is to improve resilience.

Review topics include:

- What was detected, and how quickly?
- Which controls worked as expected?
- Which logs or alerts were missing?
- Did the escalation path work?
- Were roles and responsibilities clear?
- Did containment preserve evidence?
- What preventive changes should be added?

Typical improvements include:

- Tightening SCPs and IAM permissions.
- Enabling additional logging or longer retention.
- Adding GuardDuty, Config, or Security Hub coverage.
- Improving backup frequency and restore testing.
- Updating playbooks and training responders.
- Adding automated quarantine or notification workflows.

**Exam-focused distinctions**

The exam often checks whether you can distinguish similar actions:

- **Containment vs eradication**
  - Containment stops spread.
  - Eradication removes the cause.
- **Recovery vs remediation**
  - Recovery restores service.
  - Remediation fixes the weakness.
- **Detection vs analysis**
  - Detection raises the alert.
  - Analysis determines scope and severity.
- **Guardrails vs response**
  - SCPs and preventive controls reduce risk.
  - Incident response actions handle the active event.

If a question asks for the best first step, prioritize preserving evidence and stopping further damage, not immediately deleting resources unless the scenario clearly requires it.

**Quick reference checklist**

- Define incident roles, escalation paths, and communication plans.
- Classify severity using impact, scope, and urgency.
- Centralize logs in a protected account with retention and immutability.
- Use AWS Organizations and SCPs as pre-incident guardrails.
- Preserve evidence with snapshots, logs, and access records.
- Isolate compromised resources before rebuilding them.
- Use break-glass access only when necessary and monitor it closely.
- Restore from trusted backups, AMIs, snapshots, or PITR as appropriate.
- Validate recovery and rotate secrets before closing the incident.
- Run a post-incident review and update controls.
