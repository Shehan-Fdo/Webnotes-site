## **Course:** AWS Certified SysOps Administrator – Associate (Exam SOA-C02)

**Least-Privilege Operational Access**

Least privilege means granting only the permissions required to perform a task, for only as long as needed, and only on the resources that are actually needed. In AWS, this is not just an IAM Identity and Access Management (IAM) design goal; it is an operational control that affects how you build roles, delegate administration, protect the root user, and constrain cross-account access.

For exam purposes, you should be able to choose the right control for the right layer:
- **Identity-based policies** control what an IAM user, group, or role can do.
- **Resource-based policies** control who can access a specific resource.
- **Permission boundaries** cap the maximum permissions an identity can ever receive.
- **Service control policies (SCPs)** set account-level guardrails in AWS Organizations.
- **Session policies** further reduce permissions during role assumption.
- **Trust policies** decide who can assume a role, which is separate from what the role can do after assumption.

**Core IAM policy types**

AWS uses multiple policy types, and exam questions often test whether you know which one applies at which layer. The most common mistake is confusing a role’s trust policy with its permissions policy.

- **Identity-based policies** attach to IAM users, groups, or roles and grant permissions to perform actions on resources.
- **Resource-based policies** attach to resources such as Amazon S3 buckets, Amazon SQS queues, Amazon SNS topics, AWS KMS keys, and IAM roles.
- **Managed policies** are reusable policies managed by AWS or by you.
  - **AWS managed policies** are created and maintained by AWS.
  - **Customer managed policies** are created and maintained in your account.
- **Inline policies** are embedded directly into a single user, group, or role.
  - They are tightly coupled to one identity.
  - They are harder to reuse and audit at scale.
- **Permission boundaries** define the maximum permissions an IAM principal can have, even if other policies allow more.
- **SCPs** apply to accounts, organizational units (OUs), or the entire organization and limit what identities in those accounts can do.
- **Session policies** are temporary policy restrictions passed when assuming a role.
- **Trust policies** are resource-based policies on roles that specify who can call `sts:AssumeRole` or related STS actions.

A practical operational distinction:
- Use **managed policies** when you want reusable, centrally maintained permission sets.
- Use **inline policies** when a permission set is unique and should not be reused.
- Use **permission boundaries** when you want delegated administrators to create roles but not exceed a safety limit.
- Use **SCPs** when you want to prevent entire accounts from using risky services or regions.

**Identity-based vs resource-based policies**

Identity-based policies answer: “What can this principal do?” Resource-based policies answer: “Who can access this resource?”

This difference matters because resource policies can sometimes grant access across accounts without an identity policy allow in the caller’s account, depending on the service and scenario. For example, an S3 bucket policy can grant a principal in another account access to the bucket even if that principal’s identity policy does not explicitly allow the action, as long as the resource policy and any other applicable controls permit it.

- **Identity-based policy**
  - Attached to the caller.
  - Common for broad operational permissions.
  - Evaluated with explicit allow and deny.
- **Resource-based policy**
  - Attached to the target resource.
  - Common for cross-account access and service integrations.
  - Often includes a `Principal` element.
- **Explicit deny**
  - Always wins over allow.
  - Can come from identity policies, resource policies, permission boundaries, or SCPs.

Example identity policy for read-only access to one S3 bucket:
```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "ReadOnlySpecificBucket",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Action": [
        "s3:GetObject",
        "s3:ListBucket"
      ],
      "Resource": [
        "arn:aws:s3:::example-bucket",
        "arn:aws:s3:::example-bucket/*"
      ]
    }
  ]
}
```

Example S3 bucket policy granting cross-account access:
```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "AllowCrossAccountRead",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Principal": {
        "AWS": "arn:aws:iam::111122223333:role/ReadOnlyRole"
      },
      "Action": [
        "s3:GetObject",
        "s3:ListBucket"
      ],
      "Resource": [
        "arn:aws:s3:::example-bucket",
        "arn:aws:s3:::example-bucket/*"
      ]
    }
  ]
}
```

**Managed policies vs inline policies**

Managed and inline policies can both grant permissions, but they behave differently operationally.

- **AWS managed policies**
  - Fast to attach.
  - Updated by AWS.
  - Useful for common job functions and service integrations.
- **Customer managed policies**
  - Reusable across many identities.
  - Easier to version and audit than inline policies.
  - Best choice for most custom permission sets.
- **Inline policies**
  - Exist only inside one identity.
  - Cannot be reused.
  - Useful for one-off exceptions, but they increase drift and maintenance burden.

Operationally, managed policies are easier to standardize across teams. Inline policies are harder to discover during audits because they are not separate reusable objects. For least privilege, prefer customer managed policies when the same permission pattern will be used more than once.

**Trust policies vs permission policies for roles**

A role has two distinct permission concepts:

- **Trust policy**
  - Controls who can assume the role.
  - Uses AWS Security Token Service (STS) actions such as `sts:AssumeRole`, `sts:AssumeRoleWithSAML`, or `sts:AssumeRoleWithWebIdentity`.
  - Is attached to the role as a resource-based policy.
- **Permissions policy**
  - Controls what the role can do after it is assumed.
  - Attached to the role as identity-based permissions.

This separation is a common exam trap. A principal may be allowed to assume a role by the trust policy, but still be unable to perform any useful action if the role’s permissions policy is empty or too restrictive.

Example trust policy allowing a specific account to assume a role:
```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "AllowCrossAccountAssumeRole",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Principal": {
        "AWS": "arn:aws:iam::111122223333:root"
      },
      "Action": "sts:AssumeRole"
    }
  ]
}
```

Example permissions policy attached to the role:
```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "AllowEC2Describe",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Action": [
        "ec2:DescribeInstances",
        "ec2:DescribeVolumes"
      ],
      "Resource": "*"
    }
  ]
}
```

**Session policies and temporary restriction**

When a principal assumes a role, AWS Security Token Service (STS) issues temporary credentials. You can pass a **session policy** to further restrict the permissions of that session. Session policies do not grant permissions; they only reduce what the assumed role can do.

This is useful when:
- You want a broad operational role but need a narrower scope for a specific task.
- You want to delegate access temporarily without creating a new role.
- You need to constrain automation for a single run or pipeline stage.

Common exam use cases:
- A support engineer assumes a general troubleshooting role but should only access one application account during a specific incident.
- A deployment pipeline assumes a role but should only deploy to one region or one stack.
- A break-glass workflow assumes a role with a session policy that limits destructive actions.

Example AWS CLI assume-role call with a session policy:
```bash
aws sts assume-role \
  --role-arn arn:aws:iam::123456789012:role/OpsRole \
  --role-session-name incident-2026-04-26 \
  --policy '{
    "Version":"2012-10-17",
    "Statement":[
      {
        "Effect":"Allow",
        "Action":[
          "ec2:DescribeInstances",
          "ec2:DescribeVolumes"
        ],
        "Resource":"*"
      }
    ]
  }'
```

Important nuance:
- The resulting session can only use the intersection of:
  - the role’s permissions policy,
  - the session policy,
  - any permission boundary on the role,
  - any SCPs affecting the account.
- Session policies are especially useful for temporary least privilege, but they do not replace role design.

**Permission boundaries**

A permission boundary sets the maximum permissions that an IAM user or role can have. It does not grant permissions by itself. Instead, it acts as a ceiling over the permissions granted by identity-based policies.

Use permission boundaries when:
- You delegate IAM administration to a team but want to prevent privilege escalation.
- You allow developers to create roles for applications, but only within approved limits.
- You want to enforce a safe maximum for self-service IAM workflows.

Practical scenario:
- A platform team allows application teams to create IAM roles for Lambda functions.
- The teams can attach their own customer managed policies.
- A permission boundary ensures those roles cannot access admin-level actions such as `iam:*`, `organizations:*`, or broad `s3:*`.

Example permission boundary:
```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "BoundaryAllowCommonAppOps",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Action": [
        "logs:*",
        "ec2:Describe*",
        "s3:GetObject",
        "s3:PutObject"
      ],
      "Resource": "*"
    },
    {
      "Sid": "BoundaryDenyPrivilegeEscalation",
      "Effect": "Deny",
      "Action": [
        "iam:*",
        "organizations:*",
        "sts:AssumeRole"
      ],
      "Resource": "*"
    }
  ]
}
```

> A permission boundary is not a substitute for a least-privilege identity policy. If the identity policy does not allow an action, the boundary cannot add it.

**Service control policies and organizational guardrails**

SCPs are used in AWS Organizations to define the maximum available permissions for accounts, OUs, or the entire organization. They are especially useful for enforcing guardrails that individual account admins should not be able to bypass.

Common operational uses:
- Prevent use of expensive or risky services in sandbox accounts.
- Restrict actions to approved regions.
- Block disabling security services such as AWS CloudTrail or AWS Config.
- Prevent root-level or admin-level actions in member accounts where possible.

Example SCP denying all actions outside approved regions:
```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "DenyOutsideApprovedRegions",
      "Effect": "Deny",
      "Action": "*",
      "Resource": "*",
      "Condition": {
        "StringNotEquals": {
          "aws:RequestedRegion": [
            "us-east-1",
            "us-west-2"
          ]
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

Example SCP preventing disabling logging:
```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "DenyCloudTrailTampering",
      "Effect": "Deny",
      "Action": [
        "cloudtrail:StopLogging",
        "cloudtrail:DeleteTrail",
        "cloudtrail:UpdateTrail"
      ],
      "Resource": "*"
    }
  ]
}
```

SCPs are evaluated before identity permissions can take effect. If an SCP denies an action, no identity policy in the account can override it.

**Root user limitations and protection**

The AWS account root user has unrestricted access to the account, which makes it the most sensitive credential in the environment. Least privilege means you should avoid using root for daily operations.

Best practices:
- Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) on the root user immediately.
- Do not create access keys for the root user unless absolutely required.
- Use the root user only for tasks that require it, such as some account-level settings or billing-related actions.
- Store root credentials securely and restrict knowledge of them.
- Prefer IAM roles, IAM Identity Center, or federated access for all operational work.

Operationally, the root user should be treated as a recovery and account-management identity, not as a normal admin account.

**Condition keys for least privilege**

Conditions are critical for fine-grained access control. They are often the difference between a broad allow and a safe, production-ready policy.

Commonly tested condition keys:
- **`aws:PrincipalTag`**
  - Matches tags on the calling principal.
  - Useful for attribute-based access control (ABAC).
- **`aws:RequestTag`**
  - Matches tags being applied in the request.
  - Useful when creating or tagging resources.
- **`aws:TagKeys`**
  - Restricts which tag keys can be used.
  - Useful to enforce tagging standards.
- **`aws:SourceArn`**
  - Restricts service-to-service access to a specific source resource.
  - Common in Lambda, SNS, SQS, and EventBridge integrations.
- **`aws:SourceAccount`**
  - Helps prevent confused deputy problems by ensuring the calling service request comes from the expected account.
- **`aws:RequestedRegion`**
  - Restricts actions to approved AWS Regions.

Example ABAC-style policy using principal and request tags:
```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "AllowStartStopOnlyForMatchingTeam",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Action": [
        "ec2:StartInstances",
        "ec2:StopInstances"
      ],
      "Resource": "arn:aws:ec2:us-east-1:123456789012:instance/*",
      "Condition": {
        "StringEquals": {
          "aws:PrincipalTag/Team": "${aws:ResourceTag/Team}"
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

Example enforcing tag-on-create:
```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "RequireProjectTagOnCreate",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Action": "ec2:RunInstances",
      "Resource": "*",
      "Condition": {
        "StringEquals": {
          "aws:RequestTag/Project": "Apollo"
        },
        "ForAllValues:StringEquals": {
          "aws:TagKeys": [
            "Project",
            "Owner"
          ]
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

Example service-to-service restriction:
```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "AllowSNSPublishOnlyFromSpecificTopic",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Principal": {
        "Service": "events.amazonaws.com"
      },
      "Action": "sns:Publish",
      "Resource": "arn:aws:sns:us-east-1:123456789012:alerts",
      "Condition": {
        "ArnEquals": {
          "aws:SourceArn": "arn:aws:events:us-east-1:123456789012:rule/critical-events"
        },
        "StringEquals": {
          "aws:SourceAccount": "123456789012"
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

**Cross-account access and external IDs**

When one account assumes a role in another account, the trust policy should be carefully scoped. An **external ID** is a unique value provided by the third party and checked in the trust policy. It helps prevent the **confused deputy problem**, where a third-party service could be tricked into using its access on behalf of the wrong customer.

Use external IDs when:
- A vendor or SaaS provider assumes a role in your account.
- You delegate access to a third party that manages resources across many customer accounts.
- You want to reduce the risk of a malicious actor exploiting a shared integration path.

Example trust policy requiring an external ID:
```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "AllowVendorWithExternalId",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Principal": {
        "AWS": "arn:aws:iam::444455556666:root"
      },
      "Action": "sts:AssumeRole",
      "Condition": {
        "StringEquals": {
          "sts:ExternalId": "vendor-unique-external-id-12345"
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

Operationally, external IDs are not secrets in the same way as passwords, but they should still be treated as controlled values. They are a key exam concept for third-party access.

**IAM Identity Center, roles, and service-linked roles**

IAM Identity Center is commonly used for workforce access. It provides centralized sign-in and permission sets that map to IAM roles in target accounts. For least privilege, it is often preferred over long-lived IAM users for human access.

- **IAM Identity Center**
  - Best for workforce access and centralized administration.
  - Uses permission sets that become roles in accounts.
  - Reduces the need for IAM users.
- **IAM roles**
  - Best for temporary access, workloads, automation, and cross-account delegation.
  - Use STS temporary credentials.
- **Service-linked roles**
  - Predefined roles linked to a specific AWS service.
  - Used when a service needs to perform actions on your behalf.
  - Usually managed by AWS and should not be edited casually.

**Practical decision guide**

| Control | Best for | Scope | Can grant permissions? | Can restrict permissions? | Typical exam clue |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| IAM Identity Center | Human workforce access | User/session | Yes | Yes | Centralized sign-in, permission sets |
| IAM role | Temporary access, workloads, cross-account access | Principal/session | Yes | Yes | `sts:AssumeRole` |
| Resource-based policy | Access to a specific resource | Resource | Yes | Yes | Bucket/topic/queue policy |
| Permission boundary | Delegated IAM administration | Principal ceiling | No | Yes | Maximum permissions cap |
| SCP | Organization guardrails | Account/OUs | No | Yes | Prevents actions across accounts |
| Service-linked role | AWS service operations | Service-managed | Yes | Limited | Managed by AWS service |

**IAM Access Analyzer and validation**

IAM Access Analyzer helps identify unintended access to resources and can validate policies for security findings. It is useful when you want to confirm whether a resource policy exposes data cross-account or publicly.

Use it to:
- Detect public or cross-account access on supported resources.
- Review whether a policy grants more access than intended.
- Validate policy syntax and some policy logic issues.

Example CLI usage:
```bash
aws accessanalyzer list-analyzers
aws accessanalyzer validate-policy \
  --policy-document file://policy.json \
  --policy-type IDENTITY_POLICY
```

In practice, Access Analyzer is a strong companion to least-privilege design because it helps catch accidental exposure before it becomes an incident.

**Operational patterns and examples**

A common least-privilege workflow is to combine multiple controls rather than rely on one policy alone.

Example pattern for a delegated application team:
- Use IAM Identity Center for human sign-in.
- Assign a permission set that maps to a limited role.
- Attach a permission boundary to any roles the team creates.
- Use SCPs to block risky services or regions.
- Use resource tags and ABAC to scope access by team or environment.

Example pattern for a third-party vendor:
- Create a dedicated role with a narrow permissions policy.
- Require an external ID in the trust policy.
- Add `aws:SourceAccount` and `aws:SourceArn` where service integrations are involved.
- Review access with IAM Access Analyzer.

Example pattern for incident response:
- Use a pre-approved break-glass role.
- Require MFA for assumption if supported by your process.
- Apply a session policy to narrow the session to read-only or specific resources.
- Log all activity with CloudTrail and review after the incident.

**Exam traps and high-value distinctions**

- A **trust policy** controls who can assume a role; a **permissions policy** controls what the role can do after assumption.
- A **session policy** only reduces permissions; it never adds them.
- A **permission boundary** limits the maximum permissions of a principal; it does not grant access by itself.
- An **SCP** can block actions even if IAM allows them.
- A **resource-based policy** can sometimes grant cross-account access without a matching identity policy allow in the caller account.
- An **external ID** is used to prevent the confused deputy problem in third-party role assumption.
- The **root user** should not be used for routine administration.
- **Explicit deny** always overrides allow.

**Quick operational checklist**

1. Prefer roles over long-lived IAM users for human and machine access.
2. Use managed policies for reusable permission sets.
3. Use inline policies only for narrow, one-off cases.
4. Separate trust policies from permissions policies on roles.
5. Add session policies for temporary reductions in access.
6. Apply permission boundaries to delegated IAM creators.
7. Use SCPs for organization-wide guardrails.
8. Protect the root user with MFA and minimal use.
9. Use conditions such as `aws:PrincipalTag`, `aws:RequestTag`, `aws:TagKeys`, `aws:SourceArn`, `aws:SourceAccount`, and `aws:RequestedRegion`.
10. Validate resource exposure with IAM Access Analyzer.

**Summary**

Least-privilege operational access in AWS is about layering controls so that each identity, role, and account can do only what is necessary. For SOA-C02, focus on how IAM policy types interact, how temporary credentials can be narrowed further with session policies, how trust policies differ from permissions policies, and how organizational guardrails such as permission boundaries and SCPs prevent privilege creep. The strongest designs combine identity policies, resource policies, conditions, and validation tools rather than relying on a single allow statement.
