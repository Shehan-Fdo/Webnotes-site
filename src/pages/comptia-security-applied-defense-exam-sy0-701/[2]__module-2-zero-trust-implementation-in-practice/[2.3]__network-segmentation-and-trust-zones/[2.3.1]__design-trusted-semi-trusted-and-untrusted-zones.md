## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**Trust zones and why they matter**

A trust zone is a network area grouped by how much confidence you place in the devices and traffic inside it. In practice, you use zones to separate systems with different risk levels so a compromise in one area does not automatically expose everything else.

VLANs (Virtual Local Area Networks) can help segment a network, but they are not a complete security control by themselves. Misconfiguration can leave traffic exposed, and past switch implementation issues show that you should not treat VLANs as inherently safe.

- **Trusted** zones usually hold internal systems with the highest confidence.
- **Semi-trusted** zones often contain shared services or a DMZ (demilitarized zone) that needs tighter control than internal systems.
- **Untrusted** zones include outside networks or other sources you do not control.

**Designing the separation**

When trust levels differ significantly, place them on separate physical switches instead of relying on one switch with multiple VLANs. This reduces the chance that a simple switch misconfiguration lets unfiltered traffic cross into protected networks.

At a minimum, use two switches in a firewall design:
- One switch for the outside network.
- One switch for the inside network.

In many environments, you also place the DMZ on a third switch so it stays separate from both the internal and external sides.

> VLANs improve segmentation, but they do not remove the need for careful design. If you depend on one switch for all trust levels, a configuration mistake can create a serious exposure.

**VLANs as a segmentation tool**

VLANs isolate subnetworks and help control traffic flow, but they work best as part of a broader segmentation strategy. They support security and performance goals, yet they do not replace physical separation when the trust gap is large.

A practical design approach is to combine:
- **Physical separation** for major trust boundaries.
- **VLAN segmentation** for internal grouping and traffic control.
- **Firewall policy** to enforce what can cross between zones.

This layered approach also supports zero trust thinking, where you avoid implicit trust and require checks before access is granted to a resource.

**Zero trust connection**

Zero trust assumes that nothing is trusted by default. Instead of allowing broad access once a user or device is inside the network, you verify access each time it is requested.

That mindset fits zone design because it pushes you to:
- Limit implicit trust between network segments.
- Treat internal traffic as something to inspect and control.
- Use secured zones to enforce stricter access boundaries.

**Exam focus points**

- VLANs segment networks, but they are not inherently secure.
- Misconfiguration is a major risk when different trust levels share infrastructure.
- Separate high-trust, semi-trusted, and untrusted areas with physical switches when possible.
- Use at least two switches for inside and outside networks in firewall-based designs.
- Place a DMZ on a separate switch when the environment requires stronger isolation.
- Zero trust reinforces the idea that internal access still needs verification.

**Quick comparison**

| Zone type | Typical role | Design concern |
|---|---|---|
| Trusted | Internal systems and resources | Protect against lateral movement |
| Semi-trusted | Shared services or DMZ | Limit exposure to both sides |
| Untrusted | External or unknown networks | Assume hostile traffic |

**Practical takeaway**

When you design zones, think beyond labels and focus on enforcement. The goal is not just to name a network segment as trusted or untrusted, but to make the architecture reflect that trust level through physical layout, VLAN use, and access controls.
