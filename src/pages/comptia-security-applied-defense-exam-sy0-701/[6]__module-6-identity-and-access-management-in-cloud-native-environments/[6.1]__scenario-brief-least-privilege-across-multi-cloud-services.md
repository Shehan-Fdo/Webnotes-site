## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**Least privilege in multi-cloud environments**

The principle of least privilege means identities only get the minimum access required to do their work, and only for as long as that access is needed. In cloud and multi-cloud environments, this matters because identity risk often comes from permissions that are too broad or paths that create unnecessary exposure.

Multi-cloud security adds complexity because each provider uses different terminology and implementation details, even when the underlying goals are similar. Across AWS, Azure, and GCP, the shared foundation is still identity and access management (IAM), policy enforcement, and encryption.

- **PoLP** stands for Principle of Least Privilege.
- Apply the same access-minimization mindset to both human and non-human identities.
- Treat temporary access as a security control, not just an administrative convenience.
- Review who and what has access before you expand permissions.

**Identity and access management across providers**

IAM is the control layer that helps you match the right permissions to the right people at the right time. It starts when a user or workload needs access and ends when that access is removed. In practice, that means you need to think about account provisioning, permission assignments, and access removal as part of one lifecycle.

Because cloud platforms differ in implementation, you should focus on the security objective first and the provider-specific mechanism second. The objective is consistent: control access, enforce rules, and protect data under defined conditions.

- Use IAM to separate access by role, task, and environment.
- Keep permissions aligned to current job needs.
- Remove access when the task or relationship ends.
- Avoid assuming that one cloud provider’s model maps directly to another’s.

**MFA and stronger authentication**

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) requires at least two different credential types. Common factors include something you know, something you have, and something you are. A common example is a password plus a code.

MFA does not replace least privilege, but it strengthens the identity layer that least privilege depends on. If access is tightly scoped but authentication is weak, the overall control is still exposed.

- **MFA** adds a second verification step beyond a password.
- Use factors from different categories when possible.
- Treat MFA as a baseline control for cloud access.
- Combine MFA with permission review for better risk reduction.

**Practical enforcement approach**

Least privilege is easy to describe and harder to implement. Older organizations often inherit all-or-nothing access patterns, while cloud-native teams may over-provision early to preserve developer speed. In both cases, the challenge is reducing permissions without breaking work.

A practical approach is to identify where permissions are broader than necessary, then narrow them in a controlled way. That makes it easier to prioritize the highest-risk access paths first.

1. Identify identities with broad access.
2. Check which permissions are actually required for the task.
3. Reduce access to the minimum needed.
4. Verify that the reduced permissions still support the workflow.
5. Remove access when it is no longer needed.

> When you tighten permissions, watch for productivity impact and adjust in small, verifiable steps rather than making large untested changes.

**What to remember for exam prep**

Least privilege is not just a policy statement. It is an operational discipline that depends on identity, access review, and timely removal of permissions. In multi-cloud environments, the core security goals stay the same even though the provider implementations differ.

- Focus on the access question: who can do what, under which conditions.
- Expect cloud platforms to differ in how they express the same control.
- Use MFA and IAM together to reduce identity risk.
- Prefer minimal, time-bound access for both users and services.
