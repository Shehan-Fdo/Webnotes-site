## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**IAM and organization controls**

Google Cloud Identity and Access Management (IAM) controls who can do what on cloud resources. The security goal is **least privilege**: give each user, service account, or application only the permissions needed to complete its job.

In production, avoid broad access when a narrower role works. Google Cloud guidance warns that basic roles include thousands of permissions across services, so you should prefer the most limited predefined role or a custom role that fits the task.

- Use **predefined roles** when they meet the need.
- Use **custom roles** when predefined roles are still too broad.
- Avoid basic roles unless there is no practical alternative.
- Use role recommendations to identify a narrower replacement for a basic role.
- Use the Policy Simulator to test whether a role change breaks access before you apply it.

> Least privilege is not just a design preference; it reduces the impact of compromised accounts, over-permissioned service accounts, and risky automation.

**Why least privilege matters**

Over-permissioned identities create unnecessary risk. A service account with broad project-level permissions can delete resources it should not touch, and a developer or pipeline with excessive access can expose data or change critical settings.

The practical rule is simple: match permissions to the exact job. If a workload only needs read access to one database, do not grant write access or access to unrelated resources. If a function only encrypts data, do not give it decrypt permissions.

**Organization Policy controls**

The Organization Policy Service gives you centralized, programmatic control over how Google Cloud resources are used. It acts as a governance layer above individual projects and helps you define guardrails that teams must follow.

Organization policies are useful when you want to:

- Limit resource sharing by domain.
- Restrict the use of IAM service accounts.
- Control the physical location where new resources can be created.
- Enforce other configuration constraints across the resource hierarchy.

These controls support defense in depth by preventing insecure configurations before they are deployed. They also help project teams move quickly because the guardrails are already defined.

**Resource hierarchy and inheritance**

Organization policies attach to resources in the hierarchy and flow downward. The hierarchy is:

1. Organization
2. Folder
3. Project

A policy set at a higher level can apply to lower levels, which makes it easier to enforce consistent rules across many projects. This is especially useful for security and compliance requirements that should not vary from team to team.

**IAM and Organization Policy together**

IAM and Organization Policy solve different problems:

- **IAM** decides who can perform actions on resources.
- **Organization Policy** decides what configurations or resource uses are allowed.

Used together, they create stronger control than either one alone. IAM limits access, while organization controls limit unsafe resource patterns and reduce configuration drift.

**Practical security workflow**

1. Identify the task or workload.
2. Grant the narrowest IAM role that still allows the task.
3. Check whether a custom role is needed.
4. Use role recommendations if you are replacing a basic role.
5. Validate the change with Policy Simulator.
6. Apply organization policies to enforce broader guardrails.
7. Verify that the policy fits the resource hierarchy and inherited scope.

**Exam focus points**

- **Least privilege** is the core IAM principle.
- Basic roles are broad and usually inappropriate for production.
- Predefined roles and custom roles are preferred for tighter access.
- Policy Simulator helps you test access changes before rollout.
- Organization Policy provides centralized guardrails across organization, folder, and project levels.
- Common policy use cases include domain restrictions, service account limits, and location controls.

**Quick comparison**

| Area | IAM | Organization Policy |
|---|---|---|
| Primary purpose | Control access to resources | Control allowed configurations and usage |
| Scope | Principals and permissions | Resource hierarchy and constraints |
| Typical outcome | Who can do what | What is allowed to exist or be configured |

**Key takeaway**

Use IAM to reduce who can act, and use Organization Policy to reduce what can be created or configured. Together, they help you enforce least privilege and maintain a secure, compliant Google Cloud environment.
