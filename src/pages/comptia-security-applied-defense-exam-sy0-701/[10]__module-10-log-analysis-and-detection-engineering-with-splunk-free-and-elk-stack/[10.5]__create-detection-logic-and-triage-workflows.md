## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**Detection logic fundamentals**

Detection engineering turns raw security telemetry into alerts that matter. You use it to identify adversary behavior, not just known malware hashes or signatures. That means your rules should focus on patterns such as suspicious process relationships, unusual authentication behavior, registry changes, or lateral movement indicators.

A practical detection program starts with the idea that different tools expose different levels of visibility. Some platforms, such as endpoint or intrusion tools, use proprietary detection engines that you cannot fully inspect. In those cases, track the tool as a broad detection mechanism and validate whether it catches specific threats during testing.

- **Behavioral** detections look for technique-level activity instead of a single file or domain.
- **Telemetry** from endpoints, networks, identity systems, and cloud services feeds the detection process.
- **False positives** matter because noisy rules can bury real incidents.

**Building and tracking detections**

You need a structured way to inventory detections, especially when rules are custom-built in SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) platforms. Tracking should capture how the rule is written, what it detects, and how it is maintained. This helps you compare rules across tools and avoid losing visibility into what is actually deployed.

When you work across multiple SIEMs, you also need to account for query-language differences. A detection that works in one platform may need to be rewritten for another. That makes portability and consistent documentation important parts of the workflow.

- Record the **detection format** so you know which query language or rule type is in use.
- Track the **scope** of the rule, including the behavior or threat it is meant to catch.
- Keep the **maintenance state** visible so you can tell whether a rule is active, tuned, or needs review.
- Treat vendor-managed tools differently from custom rules because you may not see the underlying logic.

> If a tool fails to detect a known attack, validate the result and engage the vendor for insight into the detection logic.

**Where detection ideas come from**

Good detection requirements come from people who see suspicious activity first. SOC analysts often notice patterns in the queue, such as suspicious PowerShell from service accounts or lateral movement discovered during an unrelated investigation. Those observations are valuable inputs for new detections.

You should not invent detections in a vacuum or rely only on vendor content hubs. Instead, capture operational observations and turn them into requirements that can be tested and tracked.

- Analyst observations often reveal gaps that generic content misses.
- Real incidents can expose behaviors worth turning into rules.
- Detection requirements should come from day-to-day investigation work.

**Precision and noise reduction**

A detection is only useful if it is precise enough to surface meaningful activity. Noisy rules generate too many false positives and slow down the security team. You improve precision by narrowing conditions and validating that the rule matches the intended behavior.

Behavior-based tuning can also make detections more adaptable. For example, a rule that detects failed logins from suspicious sources can be refined to reduce false positives and adapted to related attack patterns such as password spraying or brute force attempts.

- Tighten conditions to reduce irrelevant matches.
- Validate the rule against known benign and malicious examples.
- Reuse the behavioral pattern where the same technique appears in different protocols or services.

**Triage workflows**

Triage is the process of identifying which alerts need immediate attention and which can be deprioritized. In a busy SOC, alerts arrive from SIEM, endpoint detection and response (EDR), intrusion detection systems, email security, identity platforms, and cloud tools. Your workflow should help analysts focus on true positives and critical threats.

Effective triage uses automation, AI, personalized views, and playbooks to reduce noise and speed up response. The goal is not just to close alerts faster; it is to create a clearer incident story so analysts can make better decisions.

1. Collect the alert and related telemetry from the relevant tools.
2. Check whether the alert matches known benign activity or a true threat pattern.
3. Prioritize based on risk, context, and potential impact.
4. Escalate cases that need deeper investigation or human judgment.
5. Document the outcome so future triage is faster and more consistent.

**Automation and analyst handoff**

Automation works best when the underlying data is complete and timely. If logs from cloud, identity, or workload sources are missing or delayed, automated triage will still be slow and incomplete. Use automation to remove repetitive steps, but keep human judgment in the loop for ambiguous or high-risk cases.

A strong workflow improves handoffs between Tier 1, Tier 2, and Tier 3 analysts. It should provide enough context to explain what happened, why the alert matters, and what evidence supports the decision.

- Use automation for repetitive classification and enrichment.
- Preserve context so the next analyst does not restart the investigation.
- Escalate when the alert story is unclear or the risk is high.

**Key takeaways**

Detection logic and triage workflows work together. Better detections reduce noise at the source, and better triage ensures that the remaining alerts are handled efficiently. For exam purposes, focus on the relationship between behavioral detection, rule tracking, alert precision, and workflow-driven response.

- Detection engineering turns telemetry into actionable alerts.
- Structured tracking helps you manage custom and vendor-managed detections.
- Triage workflows reduce noise and prioritize true positives.
- Automation supports response, but it does not replace good data or analyst judgment.
