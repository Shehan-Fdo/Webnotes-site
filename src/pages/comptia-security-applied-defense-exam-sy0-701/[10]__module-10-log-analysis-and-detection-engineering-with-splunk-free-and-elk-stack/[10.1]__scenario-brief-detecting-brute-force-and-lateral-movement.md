## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**Scenario focus**

This scenario centers on two related attacker behaviors: repeated login attempts and movement deeper into the network after an initial compromise. Brute force activity often shows up as a burst of failed authentication events, while lateral movement appears when an attacker uses a foothold to reach additional systems.

A practical defense mindset is to assume compromise and then hunt for patterns across logs and network telemetry. That approach helps you separate isolated noise from a coordinated intrusion path.

- **Brute force** usually creates repeated authentication failures in a short time window.
- **Lateral movement** is the attacker’s effort to move from one internal system to another.
- Attackers may use compromised credentials, misconfigurations, or built-in tools such as PowerShell or Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP).
- Event correlation helps you connect related alerts instead of treating each one as a separate incident.

**What lateral movement looks like**

Lateral movement is the process of moving deeper inside an infected network to control more systems or reach sensitive data. It often happens after the initial breach, when the attacker explores internal hosts, looks for high-value targets, and tries to maintain access without drawing attention.

This behavior is especially dangerous because it can continue even after the first compromised host is discovered. If you only focus on the entry point, you may miss the attacker’s internal expansion.

- **Compromised credentials** can let an attacker reuse valid access.
- **Persistence** lets the attacker stay active while avoiding detection.
- **High-value targets** often include domain controllers or critical databases.
- **Built-in tools** can blend into normal administrative activity.

**Detection signals to watch**

You can detect these behaviors by combining authentication logs, host activity, and network flow data. A single failed login may be harmless, but repeated failures followed by a successful login and unusual internal connections can indicate a broader attack chain.

Look for patterns rather than isolated events. Correlation is useful because it aggregates and analyzes alerts from multiple systems, which reduces noise and helps you focus on incidents that matter.

- Repeated failed logins from the same account or source.
- A successful login after many failures.
- Unusual access to internal hosts that the user does not normally reach.
- Spikes in network traffic or new east-west connections.
- Administrative tools used in unexpected contexts.

**Log analysis and telemetry**

Log analysis gives you visibility into authentication behavior, while network telemetry shows how traffic moves across the environment. Together, they help you identify whether an attacker is only guessing passwords or is already moving through the network.

Use your data sources to answer three questions:
- Who is authenticating?
- Where are they connecting?
- Does the sequence match normal behavior?

**Correlation workflow**

1. Review authentication logs for repeated failures and any eventual success.
2. Check whether the successful session is followed by access to unusual internal systems.
3. Compare the activity against normal user and host behavior.
4. Correlate alerts from multiple sources to confirm whether the events form one incident.
5. Escalate if the sequence suggests internal spread or persistence.

**Defensive priorities**

Your goal is to reduce attacker freedom after the first foothold. That means improving visibility, correlating events, and watching for internal movement that does not match expected administrative patterns.

- Monitor authentication failures and unusual login success patterns.
- Track internal connections between hosts, not just inbound traffic.
- Pay attention to built-in tools that can be abused for remote access.
- Use correlation to reduce alert fatigue and improve response speed.

> If the attacker has already entered the network, perimeter-only monitoring is not enough. You need internal visibility to spot movement between systems.

**Key takeaway**

Brute force is often the entry attempt; lateral movement is what happens after access is gained. The strongest detection strategy combines authentication analysis, network telemetry, and event correlation so you can see the attack as a sequence instead of a set of unrelated alerts.
