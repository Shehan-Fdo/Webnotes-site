## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**Why headers matter**

Raw email headers are often the fastest source of truth when a message is delayed, rejected, spoofed, or delivered to spam. They act as the message audit trail and help you connect a user complaint to actual delivery evidence.

When you inspect headers, you are looking for:

- **Sender identity** and whether it matches the visible message
- **Routing hops** that show how the message traveled
- **Authentication results** for SPF, DKIM, and DMARC
- **Timing clues** that reveal delays or unusual relays

A header analyzer can make this evidence easier to read during delivery triage, spoofing investigation, and sender-authentication debugging.

**Core authentication signals**

Email authentication helps you judge whether a message is likely legitimate or impersonated. The three main signals are SPF, DKIM, and DMARC.

- **SPF** (Sender Policy Framework) checks whether the sending server is allowed to send for a domain.
- **DKIM** (DomainKeys Identified Mail) verifies that the message was signed and that the content was not altered in transit.
- **DMARC** (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting, and Conformance) ties SPF and DKIM results to the visible `From` domain and policy handling.

These controls work together. SPF, DKIM, and DMARC each serve a different purpose, and using all three improves protection against spoofing, phishing, and impersonation.

> If a message looks suspicious, do not rely on the display name alone. Compare the visible sender with the envelope sender and the authentication results in the headers.

**What to look for in the header chain**

The `Received` chain shows the path a message took through mail servers. It can help you identify:

- The first trusted hop and later relays
- Unexpected routing delays
- Suspicious or unfamiliar mail infrastructure
- Gaps between the visible sender and the actual sending path

The header record also helps you compare the visible sender with the envelope sender. That comparison is useful when a message claims to come from a trusted person or vendor but the underlying delivery path does not match.

**Practical triage workflow**

1. Open the raw message headers.
2. Check the visible sender and compare it with the envelope sender.
3. Review SPF, DKIM, and DMARC outcomes.
4. Trace the `Received` chain for routing and delay clues.
5. Look for signs of spoofing, misrouting, or suspicious relays.
6. Use the evidence to decide whether the message is legitimate, delayed, or malicious.

This workflow supports phishing triage because it turns a user report into verifiable message evidence. It also reduces guesswork when you need to explain why a message was blocked, delayed, or delivered to spam.

**Common investigation outcomes**

- **Pass results** do not guarantee trust, but they support legitimacy when the sender and routing also make sense.
- **Fail or misaligned results** can indicate spoofing, impersonation, or a broken mail configuration.
- **Unexpected delays** may point to routing issues rather than malicious activity.
- **Suspicious relays** deserve closer review, especially when the sender claims to be a trusted executive or vendor.

**Best-practice interpretation**

Use conservative judgment when the evidence is incomplete. A message can still be harmful even if one authentication check passes. Likewise, a failed check may reflect configuration problems rather than an attack.

Focus on the full picture:

- Header evidence
- Authentication outcomes
- Sender alignment
- Routing path
- User context and reported behavior

**Key terms**

<dl>
<dt>Envelope sender</dt>
<dd>The address used during mail transport, which may differ from the visible `From` address.</dd>
<dt>Visible sender</dt>
<dd>The sender shown to the recipient in the message header or mail client.</dd>
<dt>Authentication alignment</dt>
<dd>The relationship between the authenticated domain and the domain shown to the user.</dd>
</dl>

**Quick review**

- Headers are the fastest place to confirm what happened to a message.
- SPF, DKIM, and DMARC provide complementary authentication evidence.
- The `Received` chain helps you trace routing and timing.
- Compare the visible sender with the envelope sender to spot spoofing.
- Use header evidence to support phishing triage and delivery debugging.
