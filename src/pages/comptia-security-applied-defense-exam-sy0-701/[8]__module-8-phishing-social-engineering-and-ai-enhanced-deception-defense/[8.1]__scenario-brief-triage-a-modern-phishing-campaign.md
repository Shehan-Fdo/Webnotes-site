## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**Phishing triage overview**

A modern phishing campaign is not always obvious from the message alone. Attackers may use legitimate infrastructure, so authentication checks can still pass while the email remains malicious. Your job is to combine header analysis, sender infrastructure review, and indicator extraction to decide what happened and what to do next.

Phishing investigation guidance emphasizes a workflow-driven approach with prerequisites, a logical investigation flow, and a checklist for verification. In practice, that means you should confirm what logging and access you have before you start, then move through the evidence in a consistent order.

- **Email headers** help you trace the message path and identify sender infrastructure.
- **Authentication results** such as SPF, DKIM, and DMARC need context; passing results do not prove legitimacy.
- **Indicators of compromise** can include IP addresses, domains, URLs, and sender email addresses.
- **Threat intelligence** enrichment helps you validate suspicious artifacts and prioritize response.

**Authentication checks and their limits**

SPF, DKIM, and DMARC are email authentication methods that help protect domains from spoofing, phishing, and impersonation. SPF checks whether a sending server is allowed to send on behalf of a domain. DKIM adds a cryptographic signature to help verify message integrity. DMARC ties the results together and defines how a domain wants failures handled.

These controls matter, but they are not a complete phishing detector. Attackers can send phishing emails through legitimate services such as Gmail or Outlook, which means the message may still pass authentication. Treat authentication as one signal in the investigation, not the final verdict.

> Passing SPF, DKIM, and DMARC does not guarantee that an email is safe.

- **SPF** validates sending authorization for the domain.
- **DKIM** supports message integrity and domain association.
- **DMARC** coordinates policy and reporting across the other checks.
- **Legitimate infrastructure** can be abused, so sender reputation and content still matter.

**Investigation workflow**

Use a repeatable workflow so you do not miss evidence or jump to conclusions. Start with the message itself, then move outward to infrastructure and enrichment.

1. Review the email header and identify the apparent sender, routing path, and authentication results.
2. Extract suspicious artifacts such as domains, URLs, IP addresses, and sender addresses.
3. Check whether the sender infrastructure matches the claimed organization.
4. Enrich the indicators with threat intelligence sources.
5. Decide whether the message is malicious, suspicious, or benign based on the full evidence set.

This approach matches a SOC-style investigation and supports consistent remediation decisions. It also helps you document why a message is suspicious even when some technical checks appear normal.

**What to look for in a modern phishing message**

Modern phishing often tries to create urgency and trust at the same time. A message may impersonate a security alert, a login warning, or another high-value business notice. The content may be polished enough to bypass casual review, so you need to inspect the technical details.

- **Impersonation** of trusted brands or internal teams is a common lure.
- **Urgent language** can pressure the recipient into acting quickly.
- **Suspicious links** may lead to credential theft or malware delivery.
- **Sender mismatch** between the visible name and the underlying infrastructure is a key clue.

AI-driven social engineering increases the realism of these messages. Attackers can use personalized language and adapt their approach across email, voice, video, and social platforms. That makes human review and verification controls more important, not less.

**Practical triage checklist**

Use a checklist to keep the investigation consistent, especially in regulated environments where you need proof of completion.

- Confirm the message source and preserve the original email.
- Inspect headers for routing and authentication details.
- Compare the claimed sender with the actual sending infrastructure.
- Extract and record all relevant indicators.
- Validate suspicious artifacts with threat intelligence.
- Document the final assessment and any required response actions.

A checklist also helps you separate evidence collection from judgment. First gather what you can verify, then decide whether the message is part of a broader campaign.

**Response mindset**

The goal of triage is to reduce risk quickly while preserving evidence. If the message is malicious, you want to limit further exposure, protect information, and support follow-up investigation. If the message is inconclusive, keep the evidence and continue enrichment rather than assuming it is safe.

Use conservative phrasing in your notes when evidence is incomplete. For example, say a message is *suspicious* when authentication passes but the sender infrastructure, content, or indicators still raise concern.

**Key takeaways**

- Phishing triage depends on workflow, not a single control.
- Authentication checks are useful but not definitive.
- Header analysis and indicator extraction are core investigation tasks.
- Threat intelligence adds context for suspicious domains, URLs, and IPs.
- AI makes phishing more convincing, so verification discipline matters.
