## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**Phishing playbook overview**

A phishing playbook gives you a repeatable way to handle suspicious email reports and related user impact. The goal is to move quickly without losing control of the investigation. In practice, you investigate, contain, eradicate, and communicate in parallel when possible rather than waiting for one phase to finish before starting the next.

Phishing is a common entry point for attacks and a steady burden for security teams because user-reported messages arrive continuously. A structured playbook helps you reduce delay, keep ownership clear, and limit damage when a message slips past defenses.

- Treat the playbook as a coordinated workflow, not a strictly sequential checklist.
- Assign tasks to individuals or teams so work can happen concurrently.
- Use judgment to decide which actions must happen first and which can run in parallel.

**Initial response and scoping**

The first practical step is to scope the attack. In many cases, you learn about the event from a user, customer, or partner who reports a suspicious message. That report becomes the starting point for determining whether the issue is isolated or part of a broader campaign.

Your early questions should focus on what was received, who received it, and whether anyone interacted with it. Keep the scope narrow at first, then expand as evidence shows additional affected users, systems, or accounts.

- Identify the original reporter and preserve the message details.
- Determine whether the email was merely received or also opened, clicked, or acted on.
- Look for signs that the event extends beyond a single mailbox.

**Investigation focus**

Investigation centers on understanding the message, the target, and the effect. The evidence emphasizes expanding investigation steps with key questions and strategies, so your notes should support a methodical review rather than a one-off inspection.

You want to answer practical questions that help you decide whether the message is malicious and what damage may already exist. That includes checking for user interaction, possible credential exposure, and any signs that the attacker gained a foothold through the phishing attempt.

- Review the suspicious email and related context.
- Check whether the user interacted with the message.
- Look for indicators that the attack affected more than one account or endpoint.

**Containment, eradication, and communication**

Once you confirm risk, move to containment and eradication while keeping communication active. The playbook guidance stresses that these activities should happen in parallel when possible. That approach helps you reduce exposure faster and keeps stakeholders informed while technical work continues.

Containment limits further harm. Eradication removes the malicious presence or access path. Communication keeps the response coordinated across the people who need to act or make decisions.

- Contain the threat as soon as you confirm it is active.
- Eradicate malicious access or artifacts after containment is in place.
- Communicate status and next steps to the right parties throughout the response.

> If you wait to communicate until every technical task is complete, you can slow decisions that depend on timely status updates.

**Operational guidance**

A phishing playbook works best when it is easy to run under pressure. That means clear ownership, practical decision points, and enough flexibility to handle different phishing scenarios without rewriting the process each time.

The evidence also points to the value of standardized incident response playbooks for detecting and containing attacks. For phishing specifically, the playbook should support fast triage of user-reported emails and reduce repetitive investigation work.

- Standardize the response so analysts can act consistently.
- Keep the workflow flexible enough to adapt to the incident.
- Focus on fast triage to reduce backlog and missed threats.

**Key takeaways**

Phishing response is not just email review. It is a coordinated incident workflow that starts with a report, expands into scoping and investigation, and then moves into containment, eradication, and communication. The more clearly you define ownership and parallel work, the faster you limit impact.

- Start with the report and scope the incident quickly.
- Investigate for interaction and spread.
- Contain, eradicate, and communicate at the same time when possible.
