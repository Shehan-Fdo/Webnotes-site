## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**Post-incident review purpose**

A post-incident review is a structured look back at an incident after containment and recovery. It helps you understand what happened, how the response unfolded, and what should change next. The goal is improvement, not blame.

A strong review supports resilience by turning incident details into practical lessons. It also creates a written record that can be shared with stakeholders and used to improve future response, detection, and recovery efforts.

- Focus on **root cause** and contributing factors.
- Treat the review as a **no-fault** learning process.
- Use the output to strengthen future defenses and response actions.

**Incident report basics**

An incident report documents the event in a clear, consistent format. It should capture the facts needed for internal reporting and later analysis. Templates are commonly used to keep reports complete and easy to compare across incidents.

Useful report content includes:
- Date and time of the incident
- People, roles, and contact details involved
- What happened and where it happened
- Severity or impact
- Timeline of key events
- Actions taken during response
- Follow-up items and owners

A report should be concise enough to read quickly, but detailed enough to support investigation and lessons learned. In security contexts, note-taking may also include indicators of compromise (IOCs), which are signs that help identify malicious activity.

**What to capture in a post-mortem**

A post-mortem or after-action review usually combines a summary with a timeline and action items. Clear documentation makes it easier for teams to understand the incident and apply the lessons later.

Common sections include:
- **Incident summary**: brief description of what happened, why it mattered, severity, and duration of impact
- **Incident timeline**: sequence of events from detection through recovery
- **Contributors**: people or teams involved in the response
- **Mitigators**: actions that reduced impact or helped contain the incident
- **Learnings and risks**: what worked, what failed, and what remains exposed
- **Follow-up actions**: tasks to close gaps and improve readiness

A good summary gives enough context to orient the reader without forcing them to read the full timeline first. The timeline then provides the evidence trail behind the summary.

**Building a useful timeline**

A timeline is one of the most valuable parts of the review because it shows how the incident evolved. During forensic investigation and incident analysis, detailed note-taking helps you reconstruct events and identify decision points.

Keep the timeline factual and ordered:
1. Record the first observable event or alert.
2. Add each major response step in sequence.
3. Note when containment, eradication, and recovery actions occur.
4. Include important delays, escalations, or changes in scope.

> Keep the timeline objective. Avoid speculation unless you clearly label it as an assumption or open question.

**Lessons learned and follow-up actions**

The review should end with concrete improvements. Lessons learned are most useful when they lead to specific actions that reduce risk or improve response quality. This can include closing vulnerabilities, improving documentation, or refining operational procedures.

Practical follow-up items often include:
- Remediating known vulnerabilities promptly
- Updating response playbooks or templates
- Improving monitoring or alert handling
- Clarifying ownership for future actions
- Scheduling a review of unresolved risks

The value of the process comes from both the discussion and the written record. A clean, consistent format makes the report easier to digest and more likely to drive action.

**Documentation tips for exam prep**

For Security+ applied defense scenarios, think about the difference between the event record and the learning review:
- The **incident report** captures facts and response details.
- The **post-incident review** explains impact, root cause, and improvements.
- The **timeline** supports both investigation and communication.
- The **follow-up actions** turn lessons into measurable change.

When you see a scenario about incident handling, look for evidence that the team is documenting what happened, identifying gaps, and using the outcome to improve future security posture.
