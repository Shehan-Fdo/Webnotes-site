## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**Evidence preservation fundamentals**

Evidence preservation matters because incident response moves quickly, while digital evidence can change without obvious signs. If you do not preserve evidence correctly, you can weaken post-incident analysis and reduce the value of the evidence in legal or disciplinary review.

In practice, you focus on keeping evidence forensically sound from the moment you acquire it. That means you treat the original data carefully, document handling, and avoid actions that could alter what you are trying to prove.

- **Digital evidence** can be altered without obvious traces.
- Preservation supports a fuller incident overview and later investigation.
- Poor handling can undermine the legal position of the organization.
- The preservation process starts at data acquisition in a DFIR (digital forensics and incident response) workflow.

**Chain of custody**

Chain of custody is the documented history of evidence handling. It shows who collected the evidence, who accessed it, when it changed hands, and how it was protected. This record helps prove that the evidence presented later is the same evidence collected during the incident.

For Security+ purposes, think of chain of custody as both a process and a record. The process protects the evidence; the record proves the process happened.

- **Chain of custody** tracks evidence from collection through storage, transfer, and analysis.
- Forms and logs help document handling.
- The record should stay complete and consistent.
- Any unexplained gap can raise questions about integrity.

**Preservation practices**

You preserve evidence by minimizing unnecessary interaction and by documenting every meaningful step. In digital forensics, the original data is especially sensitive because copying, opening, or working on live systems can change the evidence state.

Use careful handling when you collect and store evidence, and prefer methods that reduce the risk of modification. The goal is to keep the evidence reliable enough for later analysis and possible court review.

- Collect evidence in a controlled way.
- Document acquisition details as soon as possible.
- Protect original evidence from unnecessary changes.
- Keep preservation steps aligned with incident response needs.

> Do not assume electronic evidence is stable just because it looks unchanged. Digital data can shift without visible signs, so documentation and controlled handling matter.

**Incident response context**

Evidence preservation is part of effective incident response, not a separate afterthought. You preserve evidence so you can understand what happened, contain the threat, and support eradication and recovery decisions.

This is especially important when the incident is active or rapidly changing. If you focus only on containment and ignore preservation, you may lose information needed to explain the attack later.

- Preservation supports threat containment and eradication.
- It helps build a complete incident timeline.
- It improves the quality of post-incident analysis.
- It supports both operational and legal needs.

**What to document**

Good documentation makes evidence defensible. Record enough detail that another person can understand what was collected, when it was collected, and how it was handled.

Typical documentation includes:
- What the evidence is
- Where it came from
- When you collected it
- Who handled it
- How it was stored or transferred
- Any actions taken during acquisition or analysis

A clear record reduces ambiguity and helps answer challenges about whether the evidence was altered.

**Practical workflow**

1. Identify the evidence that matters to the incident.
2. Acquire it using a controlled process.
3. Record the collection details immediately.
4. Maintain custody during storage and transfer.
5. Preserve the original evidence for later review.
6. Analyze copies or controlled working data when possible.
7. Keep the chain of custody record updated through the full lifecycle.

**Key takeaways**

Evidence preservation protects the value of digital evidence during a security incident. Chain of custody proves that the evidence stayed controlled and accounted for. Together, they support incident response, post-incident analysis, and legal defensibility.

- Preserve evidence early.
- Document every transfer or handling event.
- Avoid unnecessary changes to original data.
- Treat chain of custody as essential, not optional.
