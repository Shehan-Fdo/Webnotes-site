## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**IOC triage fundamentals**

An <abbr title="Indicator of Compromise">IOC</abbr> is a discrete forensic artifact or observable data point that suggests a system, account, workload, or network has been breached or is under active attack. In practice, you use IOCs to turn threat intelligence into action: detect, block, investigate, or contain malicious activity.

IOC triage focuses on deciding whether an indicator is credible, relevant, and urgent enough to drive response. Good triage reduces false positives and helps you prioritize what to contain first.

- **Atomic IOCs** are simple, direct indicators such as file hashes, IP addresses, domains, URLs, registry keys, process names, email addresses, and mutex values.
- **Higher-order IOCs** are derived artifacts such as YARA rules, TLS fingerprints, URI patterns, DGA seeds, macro signatures, and content heuristics.
- **Context matters** because the same indicator can be useful for one campaign and noisy in another.
- **Machine-actionable signals** let defensive tools enforce detections across EDR, NDR, SIEM, email security, DNS, proxies, and firewalls.

A practical triage mindset is to ask whether the IOC is still valid, whether it matches current activity, and whether it is specific enough to justify containment. Some indicators age out quickly, so you should avoid treating every old alert as an active compromise.

**How IOC triage supports containment**

Containment is the set of coordinated actions and technologies used to isolate, limit, or neutralize malicious activity before it spreads, escalates privileges, exfiltrates data, or impacts business operations. The goal is to stop attacker movement while preserving enough evidence for investigation.

IOC-driven containment often targets the fastest path to reducing risk:

- **Endpoint isolation** limits a compromised device’s ability to communicate laterally or externally.
- **Account lockdowns** reduce the attacker’s ability to reuse stolen credentials.
- **Network blocking** can stop known malicious IPs, domains, or URLs from being reached.
- **App restriction** can prevent suspicious or malicious execution from continuing.

> Containment should be precise. Overly broad blocking can disrupt business operations and create unnecessary alert fatigue.

**Common response actions**

Security platforms often expose response actions directly on a device or incident page. For example, Microsoft Defender for Endpoint includes actions such as isolating a device, collecting an investigation package, running an antivirus scan, restricting app execution, and initiating live response.

These actions support both immediate containment and follow-up analysis:

- **Isolate device** to cut off most network communication from the endpoint.
- **Collect investigation package** to preserve artifacts for later review.
- **Run antivirus scan** to check for known malicious content.
- **Restrict app execution** to limit suspicious software from running.
- **Initiate live response** when you need interactive investigation on the endpoint.
- **Action center** records activity details after response actions run.

**Containment workflow**

1. Confirm the IOC is credible and relevant to the current incident.
2. Identify the affected asset, account, or network path.
3. Choose the least disruptive action that still stops spread or abuse.
4. Apply containment quickly and verify that the malicious activity is blocked.
5. Preserve evidence so you can continue investigation and recovery.

**Practical triage and containment considerations**

Use conservative judgment when an IOC comes from older reporting or incomplete context. An indicator may still be useful for hunting, but not every match requires immediate isolation. If the indicator is tied to active compromise, containment should happen fast enough to prevent lateral movement and data loss.

- **Denying IOCs to adversaries** can reduce their ability to reuse known infrastructure or artifacts.
- **Alert fatigue** increases when stale indicators keep firing without added value.
- **Verification** helps you avoid disabling the wrong account or isolating the wrong endpoint.
- **Preservation** matters because containment should not destroy evidence needed for incident response.

**Account containment example**

When an account is suspected of compromise, disabling it is a common containment step. Playbooks may use a containment plan to disable a user account after verification.

- **User verification** helps confirm the account should be disabled.
- **Containment flags** can control whether the account is actually blocked.
- **Playbook outputs** may record the blocked account for incident tracking.

**Key exam takeaways**

- IOCs are observable artifacts that suggest compromise or active attack.
- Triage determines whether an IOC is actionable, current, and specific enough to respond to.
- Containment aims to isolate, limit, or neutralize malicious activity before it spreads.
- Endpoint isolation, account disablement, app restriction, and network blocking are common containment actions.
- Preserve evidence and verify scope before taking disruptive response actions.
