## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**Public exposure and attack surface**

Public exposure is the set of assets, services, and information that outsiders can discover without internal access. Attack surface mapping helps you identify what is visible, what is reachable, and what needs protection. In practice, you combine passive reconnaissance, active scanning, and <abbr title="Open Source Intelligence">OSINT</abbr> to build a realistic view of risk.

Attack surfaces are expanding quickly as organizations add devices, tools, cloud services, and remote work patterns. That growth often outpaces visibility, which makes it harder to know what is exposed and whether it is still needed.

- **OSINT** uses publicly accessible information from sources such as social media, search engines, and corporate content.
- **Passive reconnaissance** reduces direct interaction with targets while you gather exposure data.
- **Shadow IT** can create unknown assets that appear outside approved inventory.
- **Critical assets** or “crown jewels” deserve priority once you identify them.

**How OSINT supports mapping**

OSINT is a structured way to gather and analyze public information for security, risk assessment, and investigations. The OSINT framework organizes tools and techniques so you can move through public data sources in a repeatable way. It is useful because public data is broad, easy to overlook, and often enough to reveal relationships between domains, people, services, and infrastructure.

Use OSINT carefully. Ethical collection matters, and you should respect legal requirements, terms of service, and transparency expectations when gathering data.

**Attack surface mapping workflow**

Attack surface mapping is not just a scan; it is a process for defining scope, discovering assets, and keeping the map current. A practical workflow starts with boundaries and ends with validation.

1. Define what you are mapping and set the limits of scope.
2. Build a baseline map of known infrastructure.
3. Identify critical assets and likely attack vectors.
4. Use OSINT, automated discovery, and manual verification to fill gaps.
5. Update the map regularly so it stays accurate as the environment changes.

> Dynamic environments, cloud services, containers, and transitory assets can make maps stale quickly. Treat the map as a living artifact, not a one-time report.

**Techniques and validation**

A strong mapping effort combines multiple techniques because no single method gives a complete picture. Passive methods help you observe without creating unnecessary noise, while active methods confirm what is actually reachable.

- **Automated discovery** speeds up collection across large environments.
- **Manual verification** helps confirm ambiguous findings and reduce false assumptions.
- **Threat intelligence** adds context about likely abuse patterns and exposure trends.
- **Regular validation** keeps the map aligned with current infrastructure.

```text
Public exposure view
- Domains and subdomains

- Third-party and cloud dependencies
- Unknown or unmanaged assets
```

**Benefits for defense**

Mapping public exposure gives you a clearer starting point for hardening and monitoring. It helps you focus limited resources on the assets that matter most and reduces the chance that an exposed service or forgotten system becomes an easy entry point.

- Improves visibility into the external footprint
- Helps identify missing or unmanaged assets
- Supports prioritization of remediation work
- Reveals where exposure changes over time
- Strengthens collaboration across teams that own different parts of the environment

**Challenges to watch**

Attack surface management is difficult when the environment changes faster than the inventory. Cloud and containerized infrastructure can appear and disappear quickly, and that makes it easy for maps to drift from reality.

- **Limited visibility** can hide assets from security teams.
- **Transitory systems** may exist only briefly but still expose risk.
- **Resource constraints** can delay validation and updates.
- **Cross-team ownership** can slow remediation if responsibilities are unclear.

**Practical exam takeaway**

For Security+ style scenarios, look for clues that point to public exposure, unknown assets, or a need to reduce risk before an attacker finds the same information. The best answer usually emphasizes discovery, validation, scope control, and continuous updating rather than a one-time scan.
