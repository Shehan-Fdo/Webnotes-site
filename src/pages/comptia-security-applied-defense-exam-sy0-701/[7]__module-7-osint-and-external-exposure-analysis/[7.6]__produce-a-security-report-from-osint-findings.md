## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**Purpose of the report**

An OSINT (Open Source Intelligence) security report turns collected public information into a decision-ready document. The report should explain what you found, why it matters, and what the reader should do next.

Start by defining the report’s purpose before you write. The evidence emphasizes that you need to know the audience and the decisions the report will influence. That context determines how much detail you include and which findings deserve emphasis.

- Identify the **audience** first.
- State the decision the report supports.
- Match the detail level to the reader’s needs.
- Keep the focus on actionable insight, not raw collection.

**What to include in the report**

A practical OSINT report usually moves from summary to evidence to recommendations. The sources consistently point to clear communication, prioritization, and risk awareness.

- **Executive summary**: Give a short overview of the most important findings and their impact.
- **Scope**: State what you examined and what you did not examine.
- **Findings**: Present the relevant observations from public sources.
- **Risk assessment**: Explain why the findings matter and how they affect exposure.
- **Recommendations**: Tell the reader what to do next.

A strong report is concise enough for leadership but detailed enough for analysts. If the audience is technical, you can include more supporting evidence. If the audience is nontechnical, translate the findings into business impact and next steps.

**Attack surface context**

OSINT often supports attack surface analysis, which is the process of identifying and evaluating potential entry points that could be exposed to threat actors. The evidence highlights digital assets, network connections, human factors, and physical access as common areas to review.

- **Digital assets** can include websites, cloud instances, APIs, servers, databases, applications, code, and IoT devices.
- **Network connections** can include VPNs, open ports, email gateways, and remote access points.
- **Human factor** risks can include employees vulnerable to social engineering.
- **Physical access** can include unsecured devices or entry points.

Use this context to explain exposure in practical terms. A finding is more useful when you connect it to a possible attack path or a likely security consequence.

> Focus on externally visible risk when the report is based on OSINT. The OWASP guidance notes that attack surface analysis is mainly about what is open to attack from the outside.

**Writing the executive summary**

The executive summary should be short, direct, and readable without the rest of the report. It should answer three questions: what you found, why it matters, and what should happen next.

- Summarize the highest-priority findings.
- Describe the overall risk in plain language.
- Highlight the most important recommendation.
- Avoid deep technical detail in this section.

A good summary helps decision-makers act quickly. It should not repeat every observation from the body of the report.

**Organizing evidence and findings**

Present findings in a way that makes review easy. Use a consistent structure so the reader can compare items quickly and understand which issues are most urgent.

1. Group related findings together.
2. Describe the source or observation clearly.
3. Explain the security relevance.
4. Rank the issue by priority or impact.
5. Add a recommendation or next step.

Keep each finding tied to evidence. Do not overstate what the public data proves. If the evidence is incomplete, use conservative wording and note the limitation.

**Prioritizing risk**

Attack surface analysis and OSINT reporting both depend on prioritization. Not every exposed detail is equally important, so rank findings by how likely they are to be abused and how much damage they could cause.

- Prioritize items that expose reachable services or sensitive information.
- Treat repeated exposure across multiple sources as more significant.
- Consider how easily an attacker could use the information.
- Separate confirmed exposure from possible exposure.

This approach helps the report stay practical. It also prevents low-value observations from distracting from the most important risks.

**Recommendations and next steps**

Recommendations should be specific enough to act on. The report is most useful when it tells the reader how to reduce exposure, validate the findings, or monitor for change.

- Remove or reduce unnecessary public exposure.
- Review the affected assets and access paths.
- Validate whether the finding is still current.
- Monitor for new public information that changes the risk picture.

When possible, connect each recommendation to a finding. That makes the report easier to use and easier to defend during review.

**Report quality checks**

Before you finalize the report, check that it is clear, accurate, and aligned to the audience. The goal is to communicate findings effectively, not to overwhelm the reader with collected data.

- Confirm the purpose is stated clearly.
- Verify that the findings support the conclusion.
- Check that the summary matches the body.
- Remove unsupported claims.
- Make sure the recommendations are actionable.

A well-written OSINT security report is structured, evidence-based, and decision-oriented. It translates public information into a concise view of risk that supports security action.
