## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**Exposure risk fundamentals**

Exposure risk is what you can observe about an organization before touching a live system. Publicly available information can reveal assets, metadata, credentials, and other details that increase attack surface. The goal is to identify what is visible, determine what matters, and recommend actions that reduce risk without disrupting operations.

A structured risk assessment helps you avoid blind spots. It gives you a repeatable way to surface vulnerabilities, validate controls, and align mitigation efforts with business and regulatory requirements.

- **Exposure** includes public-facing systems, leaked details, and other externally visible information.
- **Risk assessment** prioritizes findings by impact and likelihood.
- **Attack surface** is the set of assets and entry points an attacker can reach or infer.

**Using OSINT for discovery**

Open-source intelligence (OSINT) is the practice of gathering and analyzing data from publicly available sources. It helps you map the external digital footprint before you interact with a target system. Common sources include forums, public records, social media, search engines, and forgotten web pages.

OSINT is useful because it turns scattered public data into actionable context. It can reveal exposed infrastructure, metadata, and credentials, and it supports both passive and active reconnaissance when properly authorized.

- **Passive reconnaissance** avoids direct interaction with the target system.
- **Active reconnaissance** validates specific exposures through direct probing and requires strict authorization.
- **Continuous monitoring** matters because exposure changes over time.

> Use OSINT only within legal and authorized boundaries. Direct probing, scanning, or banner grabbing without permission can cause disruption and create compliance issues.

**Risk assessment workflow**

A security risk assessment is most effective when it is structured and repeatable. The evidence emphasizes identifying threats, evaluating risks, and using a checklist or framework to prioritize defenses. That approach helps you focus on the highest-impact issues first.

1. Identify exposed assets and publicly visible information.
2. Evaluate the potential impact of each exposure.
3. Prioritize remediation based on business and regulatory needs.
4. Validate whether existing controls reduce the risk.
5. Reassess periodically because exposure is not static.

**Defensive actions to recommend**

Defensive actions should match the exposure you find. If public sources reveal too much, the response is not just to remove data; it is also to reduce future visibility and improve control validation.

- **Minimize exposure** by removing unnecessary public information.
- **Harden controls** around externally reachable systems and sensitive data.
- **Validate permissions** so exposed information does not lead to broader access.
- **Monitor continuously** for new leaks, metadata, or infrastructure changes.
- **Document remediation** so teams can track what changed and why.

**Privacy-aware handling of collected data**

When OSINT is used in assessments, privacy matters. The evidence highlights careful handling across identification, collection, processing, analysis, and reporting. Data minimization, anonymization, encryption, and secure handling reduce the chance that the assessment itself creates new risk.

- Collect only what you need for the assessment.
- Limit access to sensitive findings.
- Protect stored assessment data with secure handling practices.
- Report findings in a way that supports remediation without exposing unnecessary personal data.

**Practical interpretation for exam scenarios**

In exam questions, look for clues that an organization has exposed information before any exploit occurs. The correct response is usually to assess the exposure, prioritize the risk, and recommend a defensive action that reduces visibility or limits impact.

- If the issue is public data, recommend removal or reduction of exposure.
- If the issue is an exposed service, recommend validation and hardening.
- If the issue is recurring, recommend monitoring and periodic reassessment.
- If the issue involves sensitive personal data, recommend privacy-preserving handling and tighter controls.

**Key takeaway**

You are not just finding what is visible. You are translating visibility into risk context and then into practical defense. The strongest answer is the one that connects discovery, prioritization, and remediation.
