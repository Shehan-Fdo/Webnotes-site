## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**Scenario focus**

A compromised vendor update is a supply chain attack pattern. The attacker targets a trusted third party and uses that trust relationship to reach your systems or data. Because the update channel is already trusted, the malicious payload can bypass controls that focus only on direct access.

Third-party risk management (TPRM) helps you reduce this exposure by identifying, assessing, and mitigating risks from vendors, contractors, and service providers. In practice, you need visibility into the vendor relationship before an incident occurs and a clear response path when one does.

- **Supply chain attack**: an attack that uses a less secure partner or update mechanism to reach the primary target.
- **Trusted relationship**: a business or technical connection that systems normally accept without extra scrutiny.
- **Third-party risk management**: the process of managing security risk introduced by external vendors and service providers.

**Why this matters**

Organizations rely on external partners for critical operations, so a vendor weakness can become your weakness. Evidence from recent industry reporting shows that supply chain risk is a growing concern, and security leaders increasingly treat it as a core defensive issue rather than an optional add-on.

A compromised update can create both security and operational impact:

- Malicious code may enter through a legitimate software update path.
- A trusted vendor account or system may become an entry point into your environment.
- The incident can affect availability, integrity, and confidentiality at the same time.
- Response delays can increase financial and operational damage.

**What to do first**

When you suspect a vendor update is compromised, act quickly and conservatively. Your goal is to stop further exposure, preserve evidence, and confirm the scope before restoring trust.

1. **Contain the risk**
   - Pause deployment of the suspect update.
   - Block or isolate affected systems if the update has already been installed.
   - Prevent additional systems from pulling the same package or payload.

2. **Validate the vendor and update path**
   - Confirm whether the update came from the expected source.
   - Check whether the vendor has issued an advisory, revocation, or replacement.
   - Review whether the update mechanism, signing process, or distribution channel was altered.

3. **Preserve evidence**
   - Keep logs, hashes, timestamps, and affected host details.
   - Record which systems received the update and when.
   - Avoid actions that destroy forensic value unless containment requires it.

4. **Assess impact**
   - Identify which assets, accounts, and services are affected.
   - Determine whether the compromise is limited to the update package or extends to vendor access.
   - Prioritize systems that handle sensitive data or critical operations.

5. **Coordinate response**
   - Engage the vendor through the established incident path.
   - Notify internal security, operations, and leadership teams.
   - Follow your incident response plan for third-party events.

**Third-party risk management controls**

TPRM is not just a procurement activity. It is an ongoing security process that reduces the chance that a vendor issue becomes an enterprise incident.

- **Due diligence**: evaluate vendor security before onboarding.
- **Contractual requirements**: define security expectations, audit rights, and reporting obligations.
- **Ongoing monitoring**: watch for changes in vendor posture and compliance.
- **Incident response planning**: prepare for vendor-related breaches before they happen.

A practical TPRM program gives you better visibility into the extended enterprise. It also helps you verify that vendors meet your security standards throughout the relationship.

**Incident response considerations**

Third-party incidents often require faster coordination than internal incidents because the root cause may sit outside your direct control. A well-defined response plan helps you minimize damage and streamline investigation.

- **Containment** should stop propagation without erasing evidence.
- **Communication** should be timely and role-based.
- **Recovery** should wait until the update source is verified.
- **Lessons learned** should feed back into vendor review and control updates.

> If the vendor cannot quickly confirm the integrity of the update, treat the package as untrusted until you can independently verify it.

**Exam-ready distinctions**

Use these distinctions to answer scenario questions accurately:

| Concept | Practical meaning |
|---|---|
| Supply chain attack | The attacker uses a trusted supplier, update, or partner to reach the target. |
| Third-party risk management | The organization identifies and manages risk from vendors and service providers. |
| Incident response plan | The organization’s process for handling and recovering from a security event. |
| Vendor monitoring | Ongoing review of the third party’s security posture and compliance. |

**Key takeaways**

A compromised vendor update is dangerous because it exploits trust. Your response should focus on containment, verification, evidence preservation, and coordinated third-party incident handling.

- Do not assume a signed or routine update is safe without verification.
- Treat vendor trust as a security control that must be monitored.
- Use TPRM to reduce exposure before the incident occurs.
- Use incident response planning to limit damage after the incident begins.
