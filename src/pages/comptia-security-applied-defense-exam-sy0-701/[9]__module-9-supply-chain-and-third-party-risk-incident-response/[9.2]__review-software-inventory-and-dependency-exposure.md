## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**Software inventory and dependency exposure**

Software inventory tells you what software exists in your environment. Dependency exposure tells you which components, packages, and relationships create security, compliance, or supply chain risk. In practice, you use both views together: inventory gives visibility, while exposure analysis helps you prioritize what matters most.

- **Software inventory** is a list of known software in your organization.
- **Dependency exposure** focuses on third-party components and the risks they introduce.
- **Supply chain risk** includes vulnerable, outdated, malicious, or untrusted packages.
- **License compliance** matters because open source components can create legal and policy issues.

> True exposure management is more than asset discovery. If you only know what exists, but cannot connect vulnerabilities and misconfigurations to risk, you have inventory, not exposure management.

**Software Composition Analysis (SCA)**

Software Composition Analysis, or **SCA**, is an automated process that identifies and manages open source components in applications. It helps you detect vulnerabilities, license compliance issues, and outdated dependencies across your application portfolio.

SCA tools typically scan project manifests and build configurations, then compare components against vulnerability databases such as the National Vulnerability Database (NVD). This gives you a practical way to find risky dependencies before they reach production.

- Scan open source components for known vulnerabilities.
- Identify license risks tied to reused code.
- Detect outdated dependencies that may need updates.
- Support supply chain security by tracking third-party packages.

**Why this matters in modern development**

Modern applications often contain a large amount of open source code, so dependency risk is common rather than exceptional. That makes SCA useful for both security and compliance work. It also supports shift-left practices by moving checks earlier in the development lifecycle.

When you review software inventory and dependency exposure, you are looking for more than installed software names. You are looking for the hidden risk inside those components, including transitive dependencies that may not be obvious from the top-level application.

**How exposure data is used**

Exposure-oriented tools combine software data with vulnerability information so you can prioritize remediation. Microsoft notes that software inventory is a list of known software in your organization, and that vulnerability management data can be consumed in a unified exposure management location. That reflects the practical goal: connect inventory to risk so you can act on the most important issues first.

- Use inventory to confirm what is present.
- Use dependency analysis to find what is embedded in applications.
- Use vulnerability data to identify known weaknesses.
- Use prioritization to focus on the most critical exposure paths.

**SBOM and dependency visibility**

A Software Bill of Materials, or **SBOM**, is a structured view of software composition. It helps you understand what is inside a product or release and supports security, compliance, and risk management in the software supply chain.

SBOM-related workflows often include generation, import, validation, and transformation of component data. That makes SBOMs useful when you need to meet customer or industry requirements and when you need to validate vendor-provided files.

- SBOMs improve visibility into third-party components.
- SBOMs support compliance and supply chain governance.
- SBOMs help you track dependencies across releases.
- SBOMs can be used to validate vendor software claims.

**What to look for during review**

When you review software inventory and dependency exposure, focus on the practical indicators that affect risk.

- Known vulnerabilities in open source components.
- License conflicts or license restrictions.
- Malicious or suspicious packages.
- Outdated dependencies that remain in use.
- Hidden dependency trees that increase attack surface.

**Operational takeaway**

The goal is not just to count software. The goal is to understand how software composition affects security posture. Inventory tells you what is installed or used, while dependency exposure tells you where the risk lives. Together, they help you reduce supply chain threats and make better remediation decisions.
