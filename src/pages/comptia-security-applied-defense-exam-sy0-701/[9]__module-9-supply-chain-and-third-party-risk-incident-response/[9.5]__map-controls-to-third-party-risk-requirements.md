## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**Third-party risk management overview**

Third-party risk management (TPRM) is the process of managing risks introduced by vendors and other external parties that are integrated into your business IT infrastructure. It matters because modern organizations depend on many third parties, and those relationships can affect cybersecurity, compliance, financial stability, and reputation.

A practical TPRM program looks at the full vendor relationship lifecycle, not just the initial contract. The common phases are:

- Vendor due diligence and onboarding
- Risk assessment
- Remediation and mitigation
- Continuous monitoring
- Vendor offboarding

These phases help you identify, assess, monitor, and reduce risk before it becomes a business problem. In exam scenarios, map controls to the phase where they provide the most value.

**Mapping controls to vendor lifecycle requirements**

Controls should match the risk you are trying to manage. For example, onboarding controls help you decide whether a vendor is acceptable, while monitoring controls help you detect changes after the relationship begins.

- **Due diligence** focuses on evaluating the vendor before trust is extended.
  - Use questionnaires, reviews, and risk analysis to understand how the vendor handles your data.
  - Check whether the vendor’s services touch sensitive information such as payroll, customer data, or employee data.
- **Risk assessment** identifies the level and type of exposure.
  - Evaluate cybersecurity, compliance, operational, financial, and reputational risk.
  - Consider how much access the vendor has and what business impact a failure would create.
- **Remediation** addresses gaps found during assessment.
  - Require corrective actions before approval or continued use.
  - Track unresolved issues so they do not become accepted weaknesses by default.
- **Continuous monitoring** watches for changes over time.
  - Reassess vendors periodically instead of relying on a one-time review.
  - Watch for new risk signals, service changes, or security issues that affect trust.
- **Offboarding** reduces residual risk when the relationship ends.
  - Remove access, recover assets, and close out data-sharing paths.
  - Treat termination as a security activity, not just an administrative one.

**Common control types and what they support**

Some controls are especially useful in third-party relationships because they create accountability and visibility.

| Control / requirement | What it supports |
|---|---|
| **Right-to-audit clause** | Lets you verify vendor security and compliance when needed |
| **Vendor monitoring** | Supports continuous oversight of third-party risk |
| **Supply chain analysis** | Helps you understand dependencies and indirect exposure |
| **Risk assessment** | Identifies what data is shared and how sensitive the relationship is |
| **Incident response planning** | Prepares you to detect, respond to, and recover from incidents involving vendors |

A right-to-audit clause is especially useful when you need evidence that a vendor is meeting agreed security expectations. Vendor monitoring is important because third-party risk changes over time, and a vendor that was acceptable at onboarding may become a concern later.

**How to think about exam questions**

When a question asks you to map controls to third-party risk requirements, look for the control that best matches the lifecycle stage and the risk being addressed.

1. Identify the vendor relationship stage described in the scenario.
2. Determine whether the issue is pre-contract, active relationship, or termination.
3. Match the control to the goal:
   - Acceptance decision before onboarding
   - Ongoing visibility during service delivery
   - Corrective action after a gap is found
   - Safe exit at offboarding
4. Choose the control that reduces the specific exposure with the least assumption.

> If the scenario mentions shared data, external services, or a vendor handling sensitive information, assume you need a formal risk review rather than informal trust.

**Incident response and third-party risk**

Incident response planning supports third-party risk because vendor-related incidents can affect your ability to detect, contain, eradicate, recover, and learn from an event. A documented incident response plan gives your organization a clear process when something goes wrong.

This matters because third-party failures can create business disruption even when the incident starts outside your environment. If a vendor handles critical services or sensitive data, you need a response path that includes the vendor relationship in your planning.

**Practical exam takeaways**

- Third-party risk is not limited to cybersecurity; it also includes compliance, financial, operational, and reputational concerns.
- The vendor lifecycle is the best framework for mapping controls.
- Right-to-audit clauses, monitoring, and supply chain analysis are common controls tied to vendor oversight.
- Risk assessment should reflect what data is shared and how important the vendor is to business operations.
- Offboarding is part of risk reduction because access and data exposure should end cleanly.
