## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**Containment and remediation overview**

A containment and remediation plan helps you respond to a security incident in a controlled way. The goal is to reduce the **blast radius**, limit damage, and restore normal operations while keeping a clear record of what happened and what you changed.

Containment focuses on stopping or limiting the spread of the incident. Remediation focuses on fixing the underlying issue, documenting the response, and reducing the chance of recurrence. A strong plan is structured, prioritized, and tied to business impact.

- **Containment** limits immediate harm.
- **Remediation** removes or reduces the root problem.
- **Documentation** supports accountability and future review.
- **Prioritization** helps you address the riskiest issues first.

**What a remediation plan should include**

A remediation plan is a structured roadmap that translates findings from incidents, audits, inspections, or risk assessments into action. It should identify the problem clearly, show who owns each task, and define how success is measured.

Use the plan to capture both corrective and preventive work. Corrective actions address the immediate issue, while preventive actions help stop the same problem from returning.

- A clear problem statement with the affected systems, processes, or locations.
- Root cause analysis based on verified causes, not symptoms.
- Corrective actions and preventive actions ranked by risk and impact.
- Ownership, timelines, and success criteria.
- Evidence collection and retention requirements.
- Monitoring and evaluation to confirm the issue stays resolved.

**Containment actions**

Containment actions are the immediate steps you take to reduce exposure and stop further damage. The exact response depends on the incident, but the evidence consistently points to limiting harm, securing affected assets, and preserving the ability to investigate.

Typical containment work may include isolating impacted systems, securing compromised accounts, and applying temporary controls while you assess the scope of the event. You should also consider whether similar systems share the same weakness so you can prevent spread.

- Isolate affected infrastructure when needed.
- Secure compromised user accounts.
- Apply temporary or compensating controls.
- Identify similar systems that may share the same vulnerability.
- Preserve evidence so investigation and documentation remain reliable.

> Use caution when taking disruptive containment steps. If you remove evidence or change systems too aggressively, you can make investigation and recovery harder.

**Remediation actions**

Remediation addresses the underlying issue after containment is in place. The evidence supports a practical mix of technical fixes, process changes, and documentation.

Common remediation examples include patching vulnerable components, rolling back application code to known-good backups, adding security controls on impacted devices or networks, and improving business processes or policies to reduce recurrence.

- Patch impacted infrastructure components.
- Apply the same fix to similar components that may share the weakness.
- Roll back application code to pre-compromise backups when appropriate.
- Add security controls to affected devices, systems, or networks.
- Improve business processes to reduce repeat exposure.
- Revise policies and procedures to reduce recurrence or impact.

**Planning and prioritization**

A good plan does not treat every issue equally. You should prioritize based on risk, impact, and urgency so the most dangerous findings are handled first. Centralized and continuous scanning can help you identify risks faster and keep the plan current.

The plan should also define who is involved and how they are notified. That keeps response work coordinated and reduces delays when multiple teams need to act.

- Use scanning and review processes to identify findings quickly.
- Set acceptable risk thresholds to guide prioritization.
- Define the people or teams responsible for each action.
- Establish notification paths for new or changing risks.
- Re-evaluate controls regularly as threats evolve.

**Documentation and verification**

Documentation is part of remediation, not an afterthought. You need a clear record of the discovery, investigation, impact, mitigation steps, and evidence that the issue was addressed.

Verification should confirm that the fix worked and that the issue is not likely to return. This is where monitoring, evaluation, and retained evidence matter most.

1. Record how the incident was discovered and investigated.
2. Describe the impact on the organization or environment.
3. Document each mitigation step taken to limit harm.
4. Collect evidence that shows the issue was resolved.
5. Confirm whether additional preventive actions are needed.

**Key exam takeaways**

A containment and remediation plan is both technical and procedural. It helps you stop active harm, fix the cause, and show that the response was effective.

Focus on these ideas:

- Limit damage first, then correct the root issue.
- Document discovery, impact, mitigation, and follow-up.
- Use risk-based prioritization to decide what to fix first.
- Include preventive controls so the same issue does not recur.
- Keep the plan practical, measurable, and reviewable.
