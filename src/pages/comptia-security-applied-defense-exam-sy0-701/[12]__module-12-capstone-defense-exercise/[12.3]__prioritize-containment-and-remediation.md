## **Course:** CompTIA Security+ Applied Defense (Exam SY0-701)

**Incident response focus**

Incident response (<abbr title="Incident Response">IR</abbr>) is a structured process for detecting, containing, and recovering from security incidents. In this topic, the priority is to stop active damage first, then remove the threat and restore normal operations.

Containment matters because every second counts during an incident. If you do not limit attacker movement quickly, detection, eradication, and recovery efforts can be undermined by continued spread or cascading damage.

- **Containment** isolates affected systems and blocks malicious traffic.
- **Remediation** removes the cause of the incident and prepares systems for safe restoration.
- **Recovery** returns services to normal after you verify functionality.

**Where containment fits in the incident response lifecycle**

Common incident response models place containment after detection and analysis, then move into eradication or response actions before recovery. The exact phase names vary across frameworks, but the practical goal stays the same: reduce impact fast and prevent further compromise.

A concise incident response plan helps teams act quickly. It should be short, clear, and focused on the organization’s specific infrastructure, critical assets, and threat context.

- Preparation defines the plan, stakeholders, and communication channels.
- Assessment or analysis determines scope, severity, and impact.
- Containment limits damage by isolating systems and stopping malicious activity.
- Eradication or response removes malware, unauthorized access, or other causes.
- Recovery restores normal operations and verifies system health.
- Lessons learned or post-incident review improves future response.

**Containment priorities**

Containment is the most decisive phase when an incident is active. You focus on stopping the attacker’s progress and preventing the incident from spreading to additional systems or business processes.

Use practical, targeted actions rather than broad disruption when possible. The goal is to reduce risk while preserving enough evidence and service continuity to support the rest of the response.

- Isolate affected hosts or segments when compromise is confirmed.
- Block known malicious traffic or communication paths.
- Limit access to impacted accounts or services if they are being abused.
- Preserve evidence when actions could affect later investigation.

> Containment should be fast, but not careless. If you overreact without understanding scope, you can disrupt operations or destroy useful evidence.

**Remediation and eradication**

Remediation follows containment and addresses the root cause of the incident. In many frameworks, this work is described as eradication or corrective action. You remove malicious artifacts, close the weakness that enabled the incident, and prepare systems for restoration.

This phase is where you clean up the environment so the incident does not recur immediately after recovery. If the underlying issue remains, restored systems may be compromised again.

- Remove malware or unauthorized tools.
- Revoke or reset compromised credentials when needed.
- Patch or reconfigure vulnerable systems.
- Validate that the original attack path is no longer available.

**Recovery and verification**

Recovery restores normal operations after containment and remediation are complete. You do not simply bring systems back online; you verify that they function correctly and that the threat is no longer active.

A structured lifecycle helps teams learn from each incident and improve future response. Documentation, plans, and playbooks support consistent action from detection through recovery.

1. Confirm the incident is contained.
2. Complete remediation steps and verify the fix.
3. Restore services in a controlled way.
4. Check system functionality and monitor for recurrence.
5. Record lessons learned for future improvement.

**Practical exam takeaways**

For exam scenarios, choose the action that stops further damage first when the incident is still active. If the question asks what comes after containment, look for eradication, remediation, or recovery depending on the wording.

- **Containment** is about limiting spread.
- **Remediation** is about removing the cause.
- **Recovery** is about restoring operations safely.
- **Documentation** supports repeatable response and future improvement.
