## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Domain 5 at a glance**

Domain 5 covers Identity and Access Management (IAM): how you establish identity, prove it, authorize access, enforce policy, and review that access over time. CISSP questions often blend policy, architecture, and operations, so you need to know both the concepts and the practical tradeoffs.

A useful way to study this domain is to think in lifecycle order:

- **Identity proofing**: verify a person or entity before creating an account.
- **Authentication**: prove the claimed identity at login or during a transaction.
- **Authorization**: decide what the identity can do.
- **Accounting and review**: log, monitor, and recertify access.
- **Revocation**: remove or reduce access when risk changes.

**Identity proofing, registration, and assurance**

Identity proofing is the process of verifying that a subject is who they claim to be before you issue credentials. Registration is the administrative step where the identity is entered into the identity system and linked to an account. Assurance is the confidence level you have in that identity proofing process.

<dl>
<dt>Identity proofing</dt>
<dd>Verification of identity evidence before account creation.</dd>
<dt>Registration</dt>
<dd>Binding the verified identity to a digital account or credential.</dd>
<dt>Assurance level</dt>
<dd>The strength of confidence in the identity proofing and registration process.</dd>
</dl>

In exam terms, stronger assurance is appropriate when the impact of compromise is high. For example, a contractor account for a low-risk portal may require less rigorous proofing than a privileged administrator account for a production cloud tenant.

Common proofing methods include:

- Government-issued ID checks
- In-person verification
- Remote document validation with liveness checks
- Knowledge-based verification, though this is weaker and increasingly discouraged
- HR-backed onboarding for employees, often combined with manager approval

> <mark>Important</mark>: Identity proofing happens before access is granted. Authentication happens later when the user proves they are the same identity again.

**Identity as a Service (IDaaS) and enterprise identity governance**

Identity as a Service (IDaaS) is a cloud-delivered identity platform that centralizes authentication, single sign-on (SSO), lifecycle management, and often multifactor authentication (MFA). It is commonly used to reduce on-premises identity infrastructure and to support hybrid work.

Typical IDaaS capabilities include:

- Centralized user directory integration
- SSO across SaaS applications
- MFA and adaptive access policies
- User provisioning and deprovisioning
- Federation with external identity providers
- Access analytics and risk scoring

Identity governance focuses on controlling who should have access, why they have it, and whether that access is still appropriate. It is broader than authentication and includes:

- **Joiner-mover-leaver** processes
- Access request workflows
- Role mining and role engineering
- Periodic access reviews
- Segregation of duties (SoD) enforcement
- Privileged access governance
- Exception handling and compensating controls

A common exam scenario is a company that has SSO but still suffers from excessive access. That is an identity governance problem, not an SSO problem. SSO improves usability; governance improves entitlement quality.

**Authentication factors versus authentication mechanisms**

An authentication factor is the category of evidence used to prove identity. An authentication mechanism is the technical method used to collect or validate that evidence.

Factors are usually grouped as:

- **Something you know**: password, PIN, passphrase
- **Something you have**: smart card, hardware token, phone-based authenticator
- **Something you are**: fingerprint, face, iris
- **Something you do**: typing rhythm, gesture pattern
- **Somewhere you are**: geolocation, network location
- **Something you exhibit**: behavioral biometrics, often treated as a subtype of “something you are” or “something you do”

Mechanisms include:

- Password verification
- OTP (one-time password) generation and validation
- Push approval
- FIDO2/WebAuthn public-key authentication
- Certificate-based authentication
- Biometric matching
- Kerberos ticket validation

A factor is not the same as a mechanism. For example, a smart card is a factor in the “something you have” category, while certificate-based login is a mechanism that may use that smart card.

**Biometrics: tuning, false accepts, and false rejects**

Biometrics are convenient, but they are probabilistic rather than absolute. CISSP questions often test the tradeoff between usability and security.

Key terms:

- **FAR** (False Acceptance Rate): how often an impostor is accepted
- **FRR** (False Rejection Rate): how often a legitimate user is rejected
- **CER** (Crossover Error Rate): the point where FAR and FRR intersect
- **Threshold tuning**: adjusting sensitivity to favor security or convenience

If you tighten the threshold, you usually reduce FAR but increase FRR. If you loosen it, you improve convenience but increase the chance of impostor acceptance.

Practical implications:

- High-security environments may tolerate more FRR to reduce FAR.
- Consumer systems often optimize for convenience and lower FRR.
- Biometrics should be paired with another factor for stronger assurance.
- Biometrics are not secrets; if compromised, you cannot easily “reset” a fingerprint.

> <mark>Important</mark>: Biometrics are best treated as a convenience and assurance layer, not as a standalone secret.

**Authentication protocols and common use cases**

CISSP expects you to distinguish protocols by where they fit and what they are good at.

| Protocol | Typical use | Strengths | Common exam clue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Kerberos | Internal enterprise SSO | Mutual authentication, ticket-based, efficient after initial login | “Ticket granting ticket” or “domain controller” |
| RADIUS | Network access, VPN, Wi-Fi | Centralized AAA, widely supported | “Network device sends auth request to server” |
| TACACS+ | Administrative device access | Separates authentication, authorization, accounting; granular command control | “Router/switch admin login” |
| LDAP | Directory queries and authentication integration | Directory lookup, group membership | “User directory” or “attribute lookup” |
| SAML | Browser-based federation | Strong enterprise SSO across domains | “Assertion” or “service provider” |
| OIDC | Modern web and mobile federation | Lightweight, JSON-based, API-friendly | “ID token” or “OAuth 2.0 login” |
| OAuth 2.0 | Delegated authorization | Access delegation without sharing passwords | “App gets access to API on behalf of user” |

**Kerberos ticket flow**

Kerberos is a ticket-based authentication protocol that reduces password exposure after initial login.

Typical flow:

1. The user authenticates to the Key Distribution Center (KDC), usually via the Authentication Service (AS).
2. The KDC issues a Ticket Granting Ticket (TGT).
3. The user presents the TGT to the Ticket Granting Service (TGS) to request a service ticket.
4. The user presents the service ticket to the target service.
5. The service validates the ticket and grants access.

Why it matters:

- Passwords are not repeatedly sent to services.
- Tickets have lifetimes, which limits replay windows.
- Time synchronization is important because Kerberos is time-sensitive.

Example command on a Unix-like system:

```bash
kinit alice@EXAMPLE.COM
klist
kvno host/app01.example.com
```

**RADIUS versus TACACS+**

RADIUS is common for VPNs, wireless access, and network access control. TACACS+ is more common for device administration because it separates AAA functions and supports more granular command authorization.

Use RADIUS when:

- You need broad support across network access devices
- You are authenticating users for VPN or Wi-Fi
- You want centralized AAA for access to the network

Use TACACS+ when:

- You need detailed authorization for admin commands
- You want separate control of authentication, authorization, and accounting
- You are managing routers, switches, or firewalls

A practical clue: if the question mentions “allow `show` commands but deny `configure terminal`,” TACACS+ is the better fit.

**Federation, trust boundaries, and SSO**

Federation allows one organization to trust another organization’s identity assertions. This is common in B2B, partner access, and cloud SSO.

The trust boundary matters because the relying party does not directly authenticate the user. Instead, it trusts an identity provider (IdP) to do so.

Core roles:

- **Identity Provider (IdP)**: authenticates the user and issues assertions or tokens
- **Service Provider (SP)**: relies on the assertion to grant access
- **Relying Party**: the application or service that consumes the identity statement

Common federation technologies:

- **SAML**: XML-based, browser-centric, enterprise SSO
- **OIDC**: modern, token-based, built on OAuth 2.0
- **WS-Federation**: older Microsoft-centric federation in some environments

SAML is often used for enterprise SaaS login. OIDC is often used for modern web apps and mobile apps. OAuth 2.0 is not itself authentication; it is authorization delegation. In practice, OIDC adds an identity layer on top of OAuth 2.0.

A simple SAML flow:

1. User tries to access the service provider.
2. SP redirects the user to the IdP.
3. IdP authenticates the user.
4. IdP sends a signed SAML assertion back to the SP.
5. SP validates the assertion and grants access.

A simple OIDC flow:

1. User starts login at the client application.
2. Client redirects to the authorization server.
3. User authenticates and consents if needed.
4. Authorization server returns an authorization code.
5. Client exchanges the code for an ID token and access token.

Practical difference:

- SAML is strong for browser SSO and enterprise federation.
- OIDC is better for APIs, mobile apps, and modern cloud-native applications.

> <mark>Important</mark>: Federation trust is only as strong as the IdP’s identity proofing, MFA, token protection, and signing key management.

**Identity lifecycle and access administration**

Access administration is the operational side of IAM. It includes creating, changing, reviewing, and removing access throughout the user lifecycle.

The joiner-mover-leaver model is a common exam framework:

1. **Joiner**: new employee, contractor, or partner gets baseline access.
2. **Mover**: role changes require access adjustment.
3. **Leaver**: access is removed promptly when the relationship ends.

Important administration concepts:

- **Least privilege**: grant only the access needed for the task
- **Need to know**: limit access to information required for the job
- **Privilege creep**: access accumulates over time without removal
- **Role engineering**: designing roles based on job functions and business needs
- **Role explosion**: too many narrowly defined roles become hard to manage
- **Entitlement**: a specific permission or access right
- **Access recertification**: periodic review of who still needs access

Access review frequency should match sensitivity:

- High-risk or privileged access: more frequent reviews, often monthly or quarterly
- Moderate business access: quarterly or semiannual reviews
- Low-risk access: periodic review, often semiannual or annual
- Regulated data access: review frequency may be driven by policy or law

Service accounts and shared accounts need special handling:

- **Service accounts** support applications, jobs, and services.
- **Machine accounts** represent devices or systems in a directory.
- **Shared accounts** are used by multiple people and reduce accountability.

Best practice is to avoid shared accounts when possible. If you must use them, add compensating controls such as:

- Strong password vaulting
- Session recording
- IP restrictions
- MFA where feasible
- Named sub-accounts for accountability

Service accounts should have:

- Non-interactive login where possible
- Minimal privileges
- Credential rotation
- Ownership by a system or application team
- Monitoring for unusual use
- Clear exception handling for break-glass scenarios

API identities and service principals are increasingly important in cloud environments. A service principal is an application identity that can authenticate to cloud services and receive permissions. Treat it like a privileged non-human identity:

- Scope permissions narrowly
- Rotate secrets or use certificates
- Prefer managed identities where supported
- Log token issuance and use
- Review permissions regularly

**Authorization models**

Authorization determines what an authenticated subject can do. CISSP commonly tests the differences between access control models and when each is used.

| Model | Core idea | Best fit | Exam clue |
|---|---|---|---|
| DAC | Discretionary Access Control | Owner decides access | “File owner grants permissions” |
| MAC | Mandatory Access Control | Central authority enforces labels | “Security labels” or “clearance” |
| RBAC | Role-Based Access Control | Access based on job role | “Job function” or “group membership” |
| ABAC | Attribute-Based Access Control | Policy uses attributes and context | “Time, location, device, sensitivity” |
| Rule-based | If-then rules | Simple policy conditions | “If after hours, deny” |
| Constrained interface | Limits how users interact with a system | Prevents direct access to sensitive functions | “Only approved menu options” |
| Content-dependent | Access depends on object content | Decision based on what the data contains | “Document classified by content” |

DAC is flexible but can lead to inconsistent permissions. MAC is stricter and common in high-security or government contexts. RBAC is practical for enterprises because it maps well to job functions. ABAC is more dynamic and useful in cloud and zero trust environments because it can evaluate user, device, resource, and environmental attributes.

A real-world example:

- A hospital uses RBAC for nurses, doctors, and billing staff.
- It adds ABAC rules so a doctor can access a chart only when on the hospital network, during a shift, and for patients assigned to that doctor.
- This combination reduces overexposure while keeping workflows usable.

**Authorization enforcement points**

Authorization is not just a policy decision; it must be enforced somewhere.

Key components:

- **Policy Decision Point (PDP)**: evaluates policy and decides allow or deny
- **Policy Enforcement Point (PEP)**: intercepts the request and enforces the decision
- **Policy Administration Point (PAP)**: where policies are created and managed
- **Policy Information Point (PIP)**: supplies attributes used in the decision

In a zero trust architecture, the PEP may sit at the application gateway, API gateway, or endpoint agent. The PDP may be a cloud policy engine or identity platform. The PIP may query device posture, user risk, or resource sensitivity.

This matters because a policy that is never enforced is only documentation. CISSP questions often ask where the decision is made versus where it is applied.

**Access control types and control categories**

Access controls are often classified by their purpose.

- **Preventive controls** stop an event before it happens.
- **Detective controls** identify events after or during occurrence.
- **Corrective controls** restore systems or reduce impact after an event.
- **Deterrent controls** discourage misuse by increasing perceived risk.
- **Compensating controls** provide an alternative when the primary control is not feasible.

Examples in IAM:

- Preventive: MFA, least privilege, account lockout, conditional access
- Detective: log review, SIEM alerts, access anomaly detection
- Corrective: password reset, token revocation, account disablement
- Deterrent: login banners, warning messages, visible monitoring
- Compensating: session recording for shared admin access when individual accounts are not possible

A common exam trap is confusing preventive and detective controls. MFA is preventive because it blocks unauthorized access. Audit logs are detective because they reveal what happened.

**Remote access technologies and modern secure access**

Remote access is a major IAM topic because it combines identity, device trust, and network path control.

Common technologies:

- **VPN**: creates an encrypted tunnel to the network
- **Remote Desktop Gateway (RD Gateway)**: brokers remote desktop protocol (RDP) access through a controlled gateway
- **Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP)**: remote GUI access to Windows systems
- **SSH**: secure shell for command-line remote administration
- **SASE** (Secure Access Service Edge): cloud-delivered networking and security services
- **ZTNA** (Zero Trust Network Access): application-level access based on identity and context
- **NAC** (Network Access Control): controls device admission to the network

Split tunneling is a VPN configuration where some traffic goes through the VPN and some goes directly to the internet. It improves performance but can increase risk because the endpoint may bridge trusted and untrusted networks.

Use split tunneling carefully:

- It may be acceptable for low-risk productivity traffic.
- It is risky on unmanaged or compromised endpoints.
- It can bypass inspection and policy controls.

ZTNA differs from traditional VPN in that it grants access to specific applications rather than broad network segments. SASE combines networking and security functions, often including ZTNA, secure web gateway, firewall as a service, and cloud access security capabilities.

Practical exam distinction:

- VPN: “connect to the corporate network”
- ZTNA: “connect only to the payroll app”
- RD Gateway: “securely broker RDP sessions”
- NAC: “check device posture before network admission”

Example OpenVPN client snippet:

```conf
client
dev tun
proto udp
remote vpn.example.com 1194
auth SHA256
cipher AES-256-GCM
remote-cert-tls server
auth-user-pass
```

Example SSH hardening on a Linux server:

```conf
# /etc/ssh/sshd_config
PermitRootLogin no
PasswordAuthentication no
PubkeyAuthentication yes
AllowUsers alice bob
```

**Conditional access and device trust**

Conditional access evaluates context before granting access. It is often implemented in IDaaS platforms and cloud identity systems.

Typical conditions:

- User risk score
- Device compliance status
- Geographic location
- Network location
- Application sensitivity
- Time of day
- Authentication strength

Examples:

- Require MFA when the user signs in from a new country.
- Block access from jailbroken or rooted devices.
- Allow read-only access from unmanaged devices.
- Require a compliant device for finance applications.

Conditional access is a policy layer, not a replacement for authentication. It uses identity signals to make access decisions dynamically.

**Account types and special identities**

Not all identities are human users. CISSP expects you to understand the operational differences.

- **User accounts**: assigned to a person
- **Privileged accounts**: administrative or elevated access
- **Service accounts**: used by applications or services
- **Machine accounts**: represent systems or devices
- **Guest accounts**: limited access for temporary users
- **Emergency or break-glass accounts**: used during outages or lockouts
- **Shared accounts**: used by multiple people, usually discouraged

Special handling considerations:

- Break-glass accounts should be tightly controlled, monitored, and tested.
- Service accounts should not be used for interactive logon unless necessary.
- Machine accounts should be managed automatically where possible.
- Guest accounts should expire and be reviewed frequently.

A practical cloud example:

```bash
# Azure CLI example: assign a role to a service principal
az ad sp create-for-rbac --name app-ci-cd --role Reader --scopes /subscriptions/<sub-id>
```

This creates an application identity that can authenticate without a human user. In production, prefer managed identities or certificate-based auth over long-lived client secrets when possible.

**Provisioning, deprovisioning, and exceptions**

Provisioning is the process of creating accounts and assigning access. Deprovisioning removes access when it is no longer needed. Both should be automated as much as possible.

Good lifecycle controls include:

- HR-triggered onboarding and termination events
- Manager approval for access requests
- Automatic removal of access on termination
- Time-bound access for contractors
- Temporary elevation with expiration
- Exception workflows for unusual business needs

Exceptions should be documented with:

- Business justification
- Risk acceptance
- Expiration date
- Compensating controls
- Review owner

Service accounts and shared accounts often require exceptions because they do not fit standard joiner-mover-leaver workflows. Those exceptions should be explicit, not informal.

**Access review and governance questions**

Access reviews are periodic checks to confirm that access remains appropriate. They are especially important for privileged access, regulated data, and third-party accounts.

Questions to ask during review:

- Does the user still need this role?
- Has the user changed teams or responsibilities?
- Is the account still active?
- Is the access time-bound and still valid?
- Is the privilege level excessive?
- Are compensating controls still in place?

A good governance program tracks:

- Role definitions
- Entitlement catalogs
- Approval history
- Review outcomes
- Exception expiration
- SoD conflicts

**Practical scenario: choosing the right control**

Imagine a finance team wants remote access to a payroll application.

A strong design might include:

- IDaaS for centralized authentication
- MFA for all users
- RBAC for standard job roles
- ABAC for device compliance and location
- ZTNA for app-specific access
- Logging to a SIEM for detective control
- Quarterly access reviews for payroll entitlements
- Break-glass access for emergency support only

Why this works:

- It limits access to the application instead of the whole network.
- It reduces the chance of credential theft leading to broad compromise.
- It gives auditors evidence of review and enforcement.

**Common exam traps and how to avoid them**

- **Authentication vs authorization**: proving identity is not the same as granting permissions.
- **SAML vs OAuth**: SAML is federation for browser SSO; OAuth is delegated authorization.
- **OAuth vs OIDC**: OIDC adds authentication on top of OAuth 2.0.
- **RADIUS vs TACACS+**: RADIUS is common for network access; TACACS+ is stronger for device administration.
- **Preventive vs detective**: MFA prevents; logs detect.
- **VPN vs ZTNA**: VPN exposes a network path; ZTNA exposes an application path.
- **Role-based vs attribute-based**: RBAC maps to job function; ABAC evaluates context and attributes.
- **Shared accounts**: convenient but weak for accountability; use only with compensating controls.

**How to answer CISSP-style questions in Domain 5**

When you see a question, identify the layer first:

- Is it about proving identity?
- Is it about granting permissions?
- Is it about enforcing policy?
- Is it about reviewing or revoking access?
- Is it about human users or non-human identities?
- Is it about local access, remote access, or federation?

Then match the control to the problem:

- Use **MFA** for stronger authentication.
- Use **RBAC** for stable job-based access.
- Use **ABAC** for dynamic context-aware decisions.
- Use **SAML/OIDC** for federation and SSO.
- Use **ZTNA** for app-specific remote access.
- Use **TACACS+** for admin command control.
- Use **access reviews** to reduce privilege creep.
- Use **compensating controls** when ideal controls are not feasible.

**Final study takeaway**

Domain 5 is about controlling identity from cradle to grave: proofing, provisioning, authenticating, authorizing, monitoring, and revoking. The most common mistakes are mixing up authentication with authorization, treating SSO as governance, and ignoring non-human identities. If you can explain why a control exists, where it is enforced, and what risk it reduces, you are thinking like the exam expects.
