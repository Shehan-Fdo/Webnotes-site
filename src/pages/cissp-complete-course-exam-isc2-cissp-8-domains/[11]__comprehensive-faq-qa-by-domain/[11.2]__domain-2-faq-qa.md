## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Domain 2 overview**

Domain 2 focuses on **asset security**: how you identify, classify, protect, retain, share, and dispose of information and physical assets across their full lifecycle. In CISSP questions, the key is not just “where data is stored,” but how you govern it from **creation** through **retention**, **archival**, and **destruction**.

A strong exam answer usually considers:
- the **data lifecycle**
- the **data owner’s** responsibility for classification and handling rules
- the difference between **classification**, **sensitivity**, and **criticality**
- the right control for the right medium, system, and business context

**Data lifecycle model**

The lifecycle is broader than storage, transit, and use. CISSP expects you to think end to end:

- **Creation**: data is generated, collected, or received.
- **Classification and labeling**: you assign handling requirements.
- **Use**: users and systems process the data.
- **Storage**: data resides on endpoints, servers, cloud services, backups, and removable media.
- **Sharing/transmission**: data moves internally or externally.
- **Retention**: data is kept for legal, regulatory, or business reasons.
- **Archival**: data is preserved for long-term reference, often with reduced access.
- **Destruction**: data is securely disposed of when no longer needed.

A practical exam distinction:
- **Retention** means you still need the data and must keep it.
- **Archival** means you keep it for long-term preservation, often with lower access frequency.
- **Destruction** means you no longer need it and must eliminate it securely.

**Classification, sensitivity, and criticality**

These terms are related but not identical.

- **Classification** is the formal label or category assigned by policy, such as Public, Internal, Confidential, or Restricted.
- **Sensitivity** describes how harmful disclosure would be.
- **Criticality** describes how important the asset is to operations if it becomes unavailable or altered.

Example:
- A payroll database may be **highly sensitive** because it contains personal data.
- A safety monitoring system may be **highly critical** because downtime affects operations.
- A public marketing brochure may be low sensitivity and low criticality.

In exam scenarios, do not confuse:
- **Confidentiality** with sensitivity
- **Availability impact** with criticality
- **Classification labels** with the actual technical controls that enforce them

**Data classification and labeling**

Classification is only useful if it is operationally enforced. Labels must drive behavior in systems and by users.

Common label sets include:
- **Public**
- **Internal**
- **Confidential**
- **Restricted**
- **Secret/Top Secret** in government contexts

Operational enforcement can include:
- file and email labels
- access control lists (ACLs)
- encryption requirements
- DLP rules
- printing restrictions
- watermarking
- sharing limitations in cloud platforms
- retention and deletion policies

A label should answer:
- Who may access the data?
- Can it be copied, printed, or forwarded?
- Must it be encrypted at rest or in transit?
- How long must it be retained?
- What disposal method is required?

Example policy mapping:

```text
Public:
- May be shared externally
- No special encryption requirement beyond standard transport security

Internal:
- Access limited to employees and approved contractors
- No external sharing without approval

Confidential:
- Must be encrypted at rest and in transit
- DLP inspection enabled for email and web uploads
- Printing restricted

Restricted:
- Access only by named roles
- IRM applied
- Strong logging and approval workflow required
```

**Data handling controls**

The right control depends on the data state and the medium.

- **At rest**: use encryption, access control, backups, and physical protection.
- **In transit**: use TLS, VPNs, secure file transfer, and message protection.
- **In use**: use endpoint protection, memory protections, least privilege, and application controls.

Common controls include:
- **Access control**: least privilege, need to know, role-based access control (RBAC)
- **Encryption**: full-disk, file-level, database, object storage, and transport encryption
- **Tokenization**: replace sensitive values with non-sensitive tokens
- **Masking**: hide part of a value for display or testing
- **Anonymization**: remove identifying links so data cannot reasonably identify a person
- **Pseudonymization**: replace identifiers with pseudonyms, but re-identification remains possible with additional data
- **Logging and monitoring**: detect misuse and support investigations

**Privacy principles**

Domain 2 often overlaps with privacy. Privacy is about lawful and appropriate handling of personal data, not just protecting it from attackers.

Key privacy principles:
- **Consent**: collect and use personal data only with valid permission where required.
- **Purpose limitation**: use data only for the stated, legitimate purpose.
- **Data minimization**: collect only what you need.
- **Retention limitation**: keep personal data only as long as necessary.
- **Accuracy**: maintain correct and current records.
- **Transparency**: tell subjects what you collect and why.
- **Subject rights**: support access, correction, deletion, portability, and objection where applicable.

Exam-oriented interpretation:
- If a business wants to reuse customer data for a new purpose, you should look for **purpose limitation** and consent/legal basis.
- If a form asks for unnecessary personal details, the issue is **data minimization**.
- If a user requests deletion, the answer must consider **retention obligations** and legal holds.

> If a legal hold or regulatory retention requirement exists, you do not destroy the data just because a subject requests deletion.

**Data ownership and stewardship**

CISSP often tests governance roles.

- **Data owner**: accountable for classification, access approval, and protection requirements.
- **Data custodian**: implements and operates controls.
- **Data steward**: helps manage data quality, definitions, and lifecycle processes.
- **User**: follows policy and handles data appropriately.

The owner decides:
- classification level
- who may access
- retention period
- disposal requirements
- sharing restrictions

The custodian executes:
- backups
- encryption
- access provisioning
- storage administration
- secure deletion workflows

**Media-specific protection**

Different media require different handling because the risk profile changes by format and portability.

**Paper records**
- Store in locked cabinets or secure rooms.
- Use clean desk and clean screen practices.
- Apply shredding, pulping, or incineration for disposal.
- Use cover sheets or redaction for sensitive printouts.
- Track physical movement with sign-out logs when needed.

Paper is often overlooked because it bypasses technical controls. In exam questions, physical safeguards and disposal discipline matter as much as encryption.

**Optical media**
- CDs, DVDs, and Blu-ray discs are difficult to sanitize reliably once written.
- Treat them as write-once or hard-to-reuse media in many cases.
- Use physical destruction for highly sensitive content.
- Do not assume simple deletion is sufficient.

**Magnetic media**
- Hard drives and tapes may support overwriting, degaussing, or destruction depending on the medium and sensitivity.
- Degaussing can render some magnetic media unusable.
- Solid-state drives (SSDs) are different and may not be safely sanitized by the same overwrite assumptions.

**Solid-state and mobile devices**
- SSDs use wear leveling, so simple overwrite tools may not reach every block.
- Use vendor-supported secure erase, cryptographic erase, or physical destruction when required.
- Mobile devices should use full-device encryption, strong authentication, remote wipe, and mobile device management (MDM).
- Lost mobile devices are a common exam scenario because they combine portability, cloud sync, and cached data.

**Cloud and SaaS data**
- You still own the data even when a provider hosts it.
- Understand the shared responsibility model.
- Use tenant controls, identity and access management (IAM), encryption, logging, and retention policies.
- Review provider deletion behavior carefully; deletion may be asynchronous or subject to backup retention.
- Ensure contract terms cover data location, deletion, breach notification, and audit rights.

Cloud nuance:
- Deleting an object in a SaaS app may not immediately remove all replicas, backups, or logs.
- You need policy and contract language that define what “deleted” means operationally.

**Data loss prevention, information rights management, and content inspection**

These controls are often tested as practical protections for sensitive data.

**DLP (Data Loss Prevention)**
- Detects and blocks sensitive data from leaving approved boundaries.
- Can inspect email, web uploads, endpoints, and cloud traffic.
- Uses patterns, dictionaries, fingerprints, and context.

Example DLP rule logic:
```text
IF content contains 16-digit PAN pattern
AND destination is external
AND label is Confidential
THEN block send and alert security
```

**IRM (Information Rights Management)**
- Applies persistent usage controls to data itself.
- Can restrict printing, forwarding, copying, or offline access.
- Useful when data may leave the original system but must remain controlled.

**Content inspection**
- Examines files, messages, and traffic for sensitive content, malware, or policy violations.
- Can be integrated with secure email gateways, CASB (Cloud Access Security Broker), and web proxies.

Practical distinction:
- **DLP** prevents exfiltration.
- **IRM** controls what recipients can do after access.
- **Content inspection** identifies what the content is and whether it violates policy.

**Retention, archival, and legal hold**

Retention is not the same as keeping everything forever. Good governance balances business need, legal obligation, and privacy.

Retention drivers:
- legal and regulatory requirements
- business records management
- audit and investigation needs
- contract obligations

Archival characteristics:
- long-term preservation
- infrequent access
- integrity protection
- indexing and retrieval controls
- often lower-cost storage

A **legal hold** overrides normal deletion schedules when litigation or investigation is pending.

Retention policy example:
```yaml
records:
  invoices:
    retain: 7 years
    archive_after: 2 years
    disposal: secure_shred_or_cryptographic_erase
  employee_files:
    retain: 6 years_after_termination
    archive_after: 1 year
    disposal: approved_destruction
  security_logs:
    retain: 1 year
    archive_after: 90 days
    disposal: controlled_purge
```

**Secure disposal and sanitization**

When data reaches end of life, you must destroy it in a way that prevents recovery.

Common sanitization methods:
- **Clear**: remove data using standard methods that protect against casual recovery.
- **Purge**: make data recovery infeasible with advanced laboratory techniques.
- **Destroy**: physically damage the media so it cannot be reused.

Media-specific limitations:
- Overwriting may work for some magnetic media but is unreliable as a universal method.
- SSDs and flash media complicate overwrite assumptions because of wear leveling and hidden blocks.
- Cloud deletion depends on provider processes and may require cryptographic erasure or contractual assurance.
- Paper requires physical destruction.
- Optical media often requires shredding or incineration.

Verification methods:
- certificate of destruction from a vendor
- chain-of-custody records
- spot checks or audits
- sanitization logs
- serial number tracking
- media inventory reconciliation

A certificate of destruction is useful, but it is not a substitute for due diligence. You still need approved vendors, documented procedures, and verification.

**Example disposal workflow**

1. Identify the media and data classification.
2. Select the approved sanitization method for that media type.
3. Record chain of custody.
4. Perform sanitization or destruction.
5. Verify completion with logs, certificates, or inspection.
6. Update inventory and retention records.

**Access control and file protection examples**

File permissions and storage policies are common exam-friendly controls.

Linux example:
```bash
chmod 640 payroll.csv
chown finance:finance payroll.csv
setfacl -m u:auditor:r-- payroll.csv
```

Interpretation:
- owner can read and write
- group can read
- others have no access
- an auditor gets read-only access through ACL

Cloud storage example:
```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "DenyPublicRead",
      "Effect": "Deny",
      "Principal": "*",
      "Action": "s3:GetObject",
      "Resource": "arn:aws:s3:::confidential-bucket/*",
      "Condition": {
        "Bool": {
          "aws:SecureTransport": "false"
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

This policy pattern reinforces:
- no insecure transport
- no public read
- explicit control over sensitive objects

**Common exam traps**

- **Encryption is not classification**: encryption protects data; classification defines handling.
- **Deletion is not disposal**: deleting a file may only remove the pointer.
- **Archival is not backup**: archives preserve records; backups support recovery.
- **Privacy is not only confidentiality**: it also includes lawful collection, use, and retention.
- **Cloud hosting does not transfer ownership**: the organization still owns the data and accountability.
- **One control does not solve every medium**: paper, SSDs, tapes, and SaaS each need different handling.

**Quick Q&A patterns**

- If a question asks who approves data access, think **data owner**.
- If a question asks how to prevent employees from emailing sensitive data externally, think **DLP**.
- If a question asks how to keep a document controlled after it leaves the organization, think **IRM**.
- If a question asks how long to keep records, think **retention policy** and legal requirements.
- If a question asks how to dispose of an SSD, do not assume simple overwrite; consider **secure erase**, **cryptographic erase**, or **destruction**.
- If a question asks about personal data collection, think **consent**, **purpose limitation**, and **minimization**.

**Practical scenario examples**

A finance team stores payroll data in a cloud drive:
- classify it as confidential or restricted
- restrict access to named finance roles
- enable encryption at rest and in transit
- apply DLP to block external sharing
- set retention based on payroll and tax requirements
- archive older records
- destroy expired records with verified sanitization

A hospital prints patient summaries:
- use secure print release
- limit printer access
- watermark sensitive pages
- shred discarded printouts
- maintain retention according to medical and legal rules

A sales team shares customer lists:
- minimize collected fields
- ensure a lawful basis for processing
- label the file
- prevent external forwarding with IRM
- log access and sharing events
- delete obsolete copies after retention expires

**Exam mindset**

When answering Domain 2 questions, ask:
- What is the asset?
- What is its classification?
- What lifecycle stage is it in?
- What medium is involved?
- What control best fits the risk?
- Are privacy, legal, or retention obligations involved?
- Is the question about governance, technical enforcement, or disposal?

The best answer usually aligns policy, process, and technology rather than relying on a single control.
