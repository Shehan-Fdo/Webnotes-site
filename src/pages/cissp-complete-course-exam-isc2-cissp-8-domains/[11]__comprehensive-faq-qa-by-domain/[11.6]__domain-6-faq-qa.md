## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Domain 6 overview**

Domain 6 focuses on security assessment and testing. In practice, you use it to verify whether controls work, identify weaknesses, and measure risk reduction over time. The exam often tests whether you can distinguish between assessment methods, choose the least disruptive validation approach, and interpret results in a business context.

Security assessment is not just “run a scan.” It includes planning, scoping, evidence collection, validation, reporting, remediation tracking, and follow-up. You also need to understand the difference between testing for exposure and proving exploitability, because those activities serve different purposes in the lifecycle.

**Assessment lifecycle**

A mature assessment program usually follows a repeatable flow:

1. Define scope, objectives, and authorization.
2. Identify assets, controls, and test boundaries.
3. Select methods such as scanning, review, or validation.
4. Execute tests with safety controls.
5. Triage findings and confirm true positives.
6. Rank risk and assign remediation.
7. Re-test and close findings.
8. Report trends to stakeholders.

This lifecycle matters because a scan without validation can overstate risk, while exploit testing without controls can disrupt production. The right method depends on whether you need discovery, confirmation, or proof of impact.

**Vulnerability assessment vs penetration testing**

These terms are related but not interchangeable.

- **Vulnerability assessment** identifies known weaknesses.
  - It is usually broader and less invasive.
  - It answers: “What might be wrong?”
  - It often uses authenticated or unauthenticated scanning, configuration checks, and manual review.
- **Penetration testing** attempts to exploit weaknesses.
  - It is narrower but deeper.
  - It answers: “Can an attacker actually gain access or impact?”
  - It validates exploitability and potential business impact.

A vulnerability scan may report an outdated package, weak cipher, or missing patch. A penetration test may attempt to chain those issues into a working compromise. In the assessment lifecycle, scanning usually comes first, then validation, then exploitation attempts if the scope allows it.

> A scan is evidence of exposure; a successful exploit is evidence of impact.

**Common assessment methods**

Domain 6 expects you to recognize more than just application testing.

- **Code review**
  - Examines source code or binaries for insecure logic, injection paths, hardcoded secrets, and unsafe error handling.
  - Use it when you need to find flaws before deployment or when dynamic testing is limited.
- **Configuration review**
  - Checks system, network, cloud, and application settings against baselines or hardening guides.
  - Use it to find weak defaults, excessive permissions, insecure protocols, or missing logging.
- **Security control validation**
  - Confirms that controls work as intended.
  - Examples include verifying MFA enforcement, testing backup restore, checking alert generation, or confirming segmentation rules.
- **Vulnerability scanning**
  - Identifies known issues using signatures, checks, and authenticated inspection.
  - Best for breadth and recurring hygiene.
- **Penetration testing**
  - Simulates attacker behavior to validate exploit paths.
  - Best for proving risk and testing detection/response.
- **Application testing**
  - Includes dynamic testing, static testing, API testing, and input validation checks.
- **Physical testing**
  - Evaluates facility controls such as badges, locks, cameras, tailgating resistance, and visitor handling.
- **Social engineering testing**
  - Measures human susceptibility to phishing, pretexting, baiting, and impersonation.
  - Must be tightly authorized and carefully scoped.

These methods complement each other. For example, a configuration review may reveal that logging is disabled, while a penetration test may show that the missing logs prevent detection of lateral movement.

**Audit concepts**

Audits are different from assessments and tests. An auditor evaluates whether controls, processes, and evidence meet a standard or policy. An assessor or tester evaluates whether controls are effective in practice.

- **Internal audit**
  - Performed by the organization or an internal audit function.
  - Often focuses on governance, control design, and operational compliance.
- **External audit**
  - Performed by an outside party.
  - Often required for certification, regulation, or customer assurance.
- **Compliance audit**
  - Checks adherence to laws, regulations, standards, or contractual requirements.
  - Example: verifying that retention, access control, and logging meet a mandated framework.
- **Operational audit**
  - Evaluates how well processes support business objectives.
  - Focuses on efficiency, effectiveness, and control operation.

Auditors typically gather evidence, compare it to criteria, and report exceptions. They do not usually attempt exploitation. Testers and assessors may go deeper into technical validation, but they must stay within authorization and scope.

**Vulnerability management workflow**

A scan result is only useful if you can triage and remediate it.

1. Run the scan with the right scope and credentials.
2. Normalize results and remove duplicates.
3. Validate findings manually where needed.
4. Rank risk using severity, asset criticality, exposure, and exploitability.
5. Assign remediation owners and due dates.
6. Apply fixes or compensating controls.
7. Re-scan or re-test to confirm closure.
8. Track aging, exceptions, and recurring issues.

A practical triage example:

```text
Finding: OpenSSH 7.4 on internet-facing Linux host
Severity: High
Asset criticality: High
Exploitability: Known public exploit path
Action: Patch to supported version, restrict exposure, verify with authenticated rescan
```

A good workflow distinguishes between:
- **True positives** that need action.
- **False positives** that need suppression or tuning.
- **Accepted risks** that need documented approval.
- **Compensating controls** that reduce exposure when immediate remediation is not possible.

**Validation and exploit confirmation**

Vulnerability scans often detect potential issues, but they do not always prove exploitability. Validation means confirming whether the finding is real and relevant.

- Use validation when a scanner reports a weakness that may be a false positive.
- Use exploit confirmation when the scope explicitly allows proof of impact.
- Use non-destructive checks first whenever possible.
- Avoid destructive payloads in production unless the rules of engagement permit them.

Examples of safe validation:
- Checking banner versions against package inventory.
- Confirming a weak TLS configuration with a handshake test.
- Verifying an exposed admin page without submitting harmful input.
- Testing read-only access to confirm excessive permissions.

Examples of risky validation:
- Running denial-of-service payloads.
- Triggering data corruption.
- Using destructive malware-like behavior.
- Modifying production records without explicit approval.

> Production-safe testing should preserve availability, integrity, and confidentiality unless the engagement explicitly authorizes otherwise.

**Safety, scope, and test data protection**

Testing can create operational and legal risk if you are careless.

- Use a written authorization and clear rules of engagement.
- Prefer staging or isolated replicas for destructive tests.
- Limit test windows to reduce business impact.
- Protect sensitive data used in test cases.
- Mask, tokenize, or synthesize production data when possible.
- Log all actions for accountability and rollback.

If you must test in production, choose methods that are:
- Read-only where possible.
- Rate-limited.
- Reversible.
- Monitored by operations or incident response.

A safe approach for web testing might look like this:

```bash
# Non-destructive header and TLS checks
curl -I https://app.example.com
openssl s_client -connect app.example.com:443 -servername app.example.com
```

These checks confirm exposure and configuration without altering data.

**Log management and monitoring**

Logs are essential for detection, investigation, and compliance. Domain 6 expects you to know both log sources and how they are reviewed.

Common log types and sources include:
- **System logs** from operating systems, services, and kernel events.
- **Application logs** from business apps, web servers, and APIs.
- **Security logs** for authentication, authorization, and policy events.
- **Audit logs** for traceable user and administrative actions.
- **IDS/IPS logs** from intrusion detection and prevention systems.
- **Firewall logs** for allowed and denied network traffic.
- **Endpoint logs** from EDR, antivirus, and host agents.
- **Cloud logs** from control planes, storage access, and identity services.

A log review process usually includes:
- Centralizing logs in a SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) or log platform.
- Normalizing timestamps and formats.
- Correlating events across sources.
- Searching for anomalies, policy violations, and indicators of compromise.
- Escalating confirmed incidents.

Alert tuning is critical. Too many false positives cause alert fatigue, while too much suppression hides real attacks.

- Tune thresholds to reduce noise.
- Whitelist known benign activity carefully.
- Revisit rules after environment changes.
- Measure precision and recall where possible.
- Document why an alert was suppressed or changed.

Example log review workflow:

```text
1. Ingest firewall, auth, and endpoint logs into SIEM.
2. Correlate failed logins with unusual geolocation.
3. Check whether the source IP matches a VPN or known scanner.
4. Validate against change windows and admin activity.
5. Escalate only if the pattern remains suspicious.
```

**Metrics and reporting**

Assessment results must be communicated in a way that supports decision-making.

Useful reporting concepts include:
- **Risk ranking**
  - Prioritize by severity, exploitability, exposure, and business impact.
- **Remediation tracking**
  - Track owner, due date, status, and verification.
- **Trend analysis**
  - Show whether vulnerability counts, mean time to remediate, or repeat findings are improving.
- **Stakeholder communication**
  - Tailor detail for executives, operations, developers, and auditors.

Executives usually want business impact and risk trend. Technical teams need exact evidence, reproduction steps, and remediation guidance. Auditors need traceable proof that findings were addressed.

A concise remediation record might include:

```text
ID: VULN-2026-0142
Asset: payroll-api-03
Risk: High
Owner: Platform Team
Finding: Outdated library with known RCE exposure
Remediation: Upgrade dependency and redeploy
Due date: 2026-05-03
Status: In progress
Verification: Pending rescan
```

**Reporting best practices**

Good reports are actionable, not just descriptive.

- State the issue clearly.
- Explain the impact in business terms.
- Include evidence and affected assets.
- Provide a realistic remediation path.
- Note compensating controls if immediate fix is not possible.
- Distinguish confirmed findings from suspected ones.

Avoid vague language like “system is insecure.” Prefer specific statements such as “TLS 1.0 is enabled on the public endpoint, which weakens confidentiality and may violate policy.”

**Practical vulnerability management example**

A common workflow in a Windows environment might look like this:

```powershell
# Inventory installed hotfixes
Get-HotFix

# Check local firewall profile status
Get-NetFirewallProfile

# Review recent security events
Get-WinEvent -LogName Security -MaxEvents 20
```

You might then:
- Compare patch levels to the approved baseline.
- Confirm whether the host is internet-facing or internal only.
- Validate whether compensating controls exist.
- Patch the system.
- Re-scan to confirm closure.

**Common exam distinctions**

- **Assessment** finds and measures risk.
- **Testing** validates control effectiveness.
- **Audit** checks conformity to criteria.
- **Scanning** discovers potential issues.
- **Exploitation** proves impact.
- **Review** examines evidence and configuration.
- **Monitoring** watches for ongoing events and anomalies.

If a question asks for the least disruptive way to identify weaknesses, choose scanning or review. If it asks whether a weakness can be exploited, choose penetration testing or controlled validation. If it asks whether controls meet policy, think audit.

**Key takeaways**

- Use vulnerability assessments to find weaknesses and penetration tests to validate exploitability.
- Combine scanning with code review, configuration review, and control validation for broader coverage.
- Treat logs as evidence sources and tune alerts to reduce false positives without hiding real threats.
- Track remediation, risk ranking, and re-testing so findings lead to measurable improvement.
- Protect production systems and test data by using safe, authorized, and non-destructive methods whenever possible.
