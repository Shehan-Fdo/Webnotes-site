## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**What is Domain 1 about?**

Domain 1, **Security and Risk Management**, is the foundation of the CISSP exam. It focuses on how security supports business goals, how leaders govern risk, and how you make decisions that balance protection, cost, and operational impact.

This domain is less about tools and more about **policy, governance, legal obligations, ethics, and risk treatment**. In exam scenarios, the best answer usually aligns with business objectives, legal/regulatory requirements, and the organization’s risk appetite.

**Core governance concepts**

Governance is the framework of direction and oversight that ensures security supports the enterprise. It is not the same as day-to-day management.

- **Governance** sets direction, approves strategy, and monitors outcomes.
- **Management** implements controls and runs operations.
- **Oversight bodies** such as the board of directors, audit committee, risk committee, and executive leadership provide accountability and review.
- **Security governance** should align with business strategy, legal obligations, and stakeholder expectations.

A common exam distinction is that governance decides *what* the organization should do, while management decides *how* to do it.

**Due care and due diligence**

These two terms are frequently tested and often confused.

- **Due care** means taking reasonable steps to protect people, assets, and information.
- **Due diligence** means performing the investigation and ongoing review needed to make informed decisions.

In practice:

- Due diligence is the research and assessment phase.
- Due care is the action phase after a decision is made.

Example:
- You evaluate a cloud provider’s controls, certifications, and contract terms before signing. That is **due diligence**.
- You then implement monitoring, access controls, and contractual safeguards. That is **due care**.

> If a question asks whether leadership “should have known” about a risk, the concept often points to due diligence. If it asks whether reasonable safeguards were implemented, it often points to due care.

**Security program management**

A security program is the set of policies, processes, controls, and metrics used to protect the organization over time. It should be measurable, repeatable, and aligned to business priorities.

Key elements include:

- **Program charter** that defines scope, authority, and objectives.
- **Policies, standards, procedures, and guidelines** that translate governance into action.
- **Roadmap** that prioritizes initiatives over time.
- **Metrics** that show whether the program is effective.
- **Continuous improvement** based on audits, incidents, risk reviews, and business changes.

A practical roadmap often includes:
1. Baseline current controls and identify gaps.
2. Prioritize risks by business impact and likelihood.
3. Define target-state controls and milestones.
4. Assign owners, budgets, and deadlines.
5. Review progress regularly and adjust for new threats.

**Metrics, KPIs, and KRIs**

Security programs need measurement, but not all metrics mean the same thing.

- **Metric**: any measurable value, such as patch completion rate.
- **KPI** (Key Performance Indicator): a measure of success against a goal, such as 95% of critical systems patched within 14 days.
- **KRI** (Key Risk Indicator): a measure that signals increasing risk, such as a rising number of unpatched internet-facing assets.

Good metrics are:
- Relevant to business goals
- Actionable
- Consistent over time
- Hard to game
- Tied to ownership

Examples:
- Mean time to detect (MTTD)
- Mean time to respond (MTTR)
- Percentage of privileged accounts reviewed on schedule
- Number of critical findings older than SLA

**Maturity models**

A maturity model helps you assess how well a security capability is developed and how far it must evolve.

Common uses:
- Benchmarking current capability
- Setting realistic improvement targets
- Communicating progress to executives

Typical maturity stages:
- Initial/ad hoc
- Repeatable
- Defined
- Managed
- Optimized

Use maturity models carefully. A mature process is not always the most secure if it is misaligned with business needs. The exam often rewards the answer that improves governance and repeatability, not just the one that adds more technology.

**Risk management fundamentals**

Risk is the potential for loss when a threat exploits a vulnerability and causes impact to an asset.

A simple way to think about it:

- **Asset**: something of value
- **Threat**: something that can cause harm
- **Vulnerability**: weakness that can be exploited
- **Impact**: the consequence if the event occurs
- **Likelihood**: the chance it will occur

Risk is often expressed as:
- **Risk = likelihood × impact**

Risk management is the process of identifying, analyzing, treating, and monitoring risk.

**Risk analysis terminology**

CISSP questions often use risk language precisely.

- **Inherent risk**: risk before controls are applied
- **Residual risk**: risk remaining after controls
- **Accepted risk**: risk the organization chooses to retain
- **Transferred risk**: risk shifted to another party, often through insurance or contract
- **Avoided risk**: risk eliminated by not performing the activity
- **Mitigated risk**: risk reduced through controls

Other useful terms:
- **Exposure**: the degree to which an asset is subject to risk
- **Single loss expectancy (SLE)**: expected loss from one event
- **Annualized loss expectancy (ALE)**: expected yearly loss
- **Annualized rate of occurrence (ARO)**: expected frequency per year

Example:
- If a server has an SLE of $50,000 and an ARO of 0.2, the ALE is $10,000.

**Risk treatment and ownership**

Risk decisions should be owned by the business, not only by security.

- **Risk owner**: accountable for accepting or treating the risk
- **Control owner**: responsible for operating a specific control
- **Asset owner**: accountable for the asset’s value and protection needs
- **Data owner**: decides how data is classified, used, and shared

This distinction matters in exam scenarios:
- The **risk owner** approves whether the risk is accepted.
- The **control owner** ensures the control works.
- The **asset owner** determines business criticality and acceptable use.

Risk treatment options:
- **Mitigate** with controls
- **Transfer** via contract or insurance
- **Avoid** by stopping the activity
- **Accept** when the residual risk is within tolerance

**Threat modeling**

Threat modeling is a structured way to identify what can go wrong before a system is built or changed. It helps you design security into the solution rather than bolt it on later.

Common approaches include:
- **STRIDE**: Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information disclosure, Denial of service, Elevation of privilege
- **Attack trees**: map attacker goals and paths
- **Kill chain**: describe phases of an attack
- **Data flow diagrams**: show trust boundaries and data movement

A practical threat modeling workflow:
1. Define the system and its boundaries.
2. Identify assets, entry points, and trust boundaries.
3. Enumerate threats and abuse cases.
4. Rank risks by likelihood and impact.
5. Select controls and document residual risk.

Example scenario:
- A web app exposes an API to partners. Threat modeling may reveal spoofed API clients, weak authorization, and data leakage across tenants. Controls may include mutual TLS, scoped tokens, input validation, and tenant isolation.

**Security and governance frameworks**

Frameworks help standardize governance and control selection.

Common examples:
- **ISO/IEC 27001** for information security management systems
- **NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)** for identify, protect, detect, respond, recover
- **NIST Risk Management Framework (RMF)** for system authorization and continuous monitoring
- **COBIT** for enterprise governance of IT
- **COSO** for internal control and enterprise risk management

How they differ:
- **COBIT** emphasizes governance and business alignment.
- **NIST CSF** is practical for organizing security outcomes.
- **NIST RMF** is more control- and authorization-focused.
- **ISO 27001** is a certifiable management system standard.

Exam tip:
- If the question is about board-level oversight and business alignment, think **COBIT** or governance structures.
- If it is about control selection and authorization, think **NIST RMF**.
- If it is about a security management system, think **ISO 27001**.

**Legal and regulatory concepts**

Security decisions are constrained by law, regulation, and contract.

Important legal concepts:
- **Liability**: legal responsibility for harm or loss
- **Negligence**: failure to exercise reasonable care
- **Due care** and **due diligence**: often used to judge whether negligence occurred
- **Tort**: civil wrong that causes harm
- **Contract law**: obligations created by agreements
- **Criminal law**: offenses against the state
- **Administrative law**: rules from agencies and regulators

Intellectual property (IP) categories:
- **Copyright**: protects original creative works
- **Trademark**: protects brand identifiers
- **Patent**: protects inventions and processes
- **Trade secret**: protects confidential business information

Security implications:
- You must respect software licensing and copyright.
- You should control disclosure of trade secrets.
- You may need to preserve evidence for legal action.
- You should coordinate with legal counsel before monitoring, retention, or cross-border transfers.

Export controls can restrict the transfer of certain technologies, encryption, or technical data across borders. Privacy laws can also limit where data is stored and who can access it.

**Privacy concepts**

Privacy is about the proper handling of personal data and the rights of individuals.

Key terms:
- **Data subject**: the person the data describes
- **Controller**: determines why and how personal data is processed
- **Processor**: processes data on behalf of the controller
- **Purpose limitation**: use data only for stated purposes
- **Data minimization**: collect only what is needed
- **Retention limitation**: keep data only as long as necessary

Common data subject rights may include:
- Access
- Correction
- Deletion
- Portability
- Restriction of processing
- Objection to processing

Practical privacy controls:
- **Anonymization**: irreversibly removes identifying value
- **Pseudonymization**: replaces identifiers but allows re-identification with additional information
- Tokenization
- Encryption
- Access logging
- Retention schedules
- Consent management where required

Data lifecycle handling should cover:
1. Collection
2. Use
3. Storage
4. Sharing
5. Archival
6. Destruction

Example:
- A healthcare app should minimize collected fields, encrypt records at rest, restrict access by role, log disclosures, and securely destroy records when retention expires.

**Personnel security controls**

People controls reduce insider risk and support accountability.

Important controls:
- **Separation of duties**: split critical tasks so one person cannot complete a harmful action alone
- **Least privilege**: grant only the access needed
- **Job rotation**: move employees through roles to expose fraud or collusion
- **Mandatory vacations**: force time away so irregularities can surface
- **Background checks**: verify suitability before hiring, where lawful
- **Nondisclosure agreements**: reinforce confidentiality obligations

Termination and offboarding timing matters:
- Disable access promptly when employment ends or when a role changes.
- Recover badges, tokens, laptops, and keys.
- Revoke remote access, VPN, and privileged credentials.
- Preserve logs and evidence if misconduct is suspected.

> In exam scenarios, immediate access revocation is usually the safest answer when termination is involuntary or hostile.

**Security awareness and training**

Awareness programs teach users what to do, while training builds role-specific skills.

- **Awareness** is broad and recurring, such as phishing reminders.
- **Training** is targeted, such as secure coding or incident handling.
- **Education** is deeper and longer-term, such as formal degree or certification programs.

A good program uses:
- Role-based training
- Phishing simulations
- Policy acknowledgments
- Metrics on completion and behavior change
- Refreshers after incidents or policy updates

**Business continuity and disaster recovery**

Business continuity planning (BCP) keeps critical business functions running during disruption. Disaster recovery (DR) focuses on restoring IT and infrastructure after an outage.

Key recovery objectives:
- **RTO** (Recovery Time Objective): how long you can tolerate downtime
- **RPO** (Recovery Point Objective): how much data loss you can tolerate
- **MTD** (Maximum Tolerable Downtime): the longest acceptable outage

Site types:
- **Hot site**: fully equipped, near-immediate recovery
- **Warm site**: partially equipped, moderate recovery time
- **Cold site**: space and basic utilities only, longest recovery time

Backup types:
- **Full backup**: copies all selected data
- **Incremental backup**: copies changes since the last backup of any type
- **Differential backup**: copies changes since the last full backup
- **Snapshot**: point-in-time copy, often storage- or VM-based
- **Image backup**: captures a system image for faster restoration

Backup design considerations:
- Follow the 3-2-1 rule: three copies, two media types, one offsite copy
- Protect backups from ransomware with immutability or offline storage
- Test restore procedures, not just backup completion

Testing and exercise types:
- **Tabletop exercise**: discussion-based walkthrough
- **Simulation**: realistic scenario without full production impact
- **Full interruption**: actual failover or shutdown of primary operations
- **Walkthrough**: review of plans and roles
- **Parallel test**: alternate site runs alongside production without taking over

Example:
- A tabletop may validate decision-making and communications.
- A full interruption test validates whether the hot site can truly support operations.

**Scenario-based continuity examples**

- If a payment platform must recover within one hour, a **hot site** and frequent replication are more appropriate than a cold site.
- If the business can tolerate a day of downtime but only a small amount of data loss, a **warm site** with near-real-time backups may fit.
- If the system is low priority and budget is limited, a **cold site** may be acceptable.

**Risk response scenarios**

- If a vendor stores regulated data and cannot meet requirements, you may **transfer** some risk contractually, but you still retain accountability for compliance.
- If a legacy system cannot be patched and is no longer needed, **avoidance** by decommissioning may be better than expensive compensating controls.
- If a control costs more than the expected loss and the risk is within tolerance, **acceptance** may be justified by the risk owner.

**Compliance and ethics**

Compliance means following laws, regulations, standards, and internal policy. Ethics goes beyond minimum compliance and reflects professional responsibility.

Security professionals should:
- Protect confidentiality, integrity, and availability
- Report conflicts of interest
- Avoid misuse of privileged access
- Escalate material risks honestly
- Support lawful investigations and audits

Ethical decision-making often requires balancing:
- Privacy vs monitoring
- Security vs usability
- Cost vs risk reduction
- Local law vs global policy

**Common exam traps**

- Governance is not the same as management.
- Due diligence happens before a decision; due care happens after.
- Risk acceptance belongs to the risk owner, not the security analyst.
- A control that reduces risk is not always the best answer if it violates law or business constraints.
- The best continuity answer depends on RTO, RPO, and budget.
- Privacy controls should reflect data minimization and purpose limitation, not just encryption.

**Practical CLI and configuration examples**

Security governance often becomes operational through configuration and enforcement.

Linux file permission baseline:
```bash
chmod 640 /etc/app/config.yml
chown root:appsec /etc/app/config.yml
```

Audit a system for privileged users:
```bash
getent group sudo
getent group wheel
sudo -l
```

Check backup status on a Linux host using `rsync`:
```bash
rsync -aHAX --delete /srv/data/ /backup/data/
```

Example of a simple retention policy in a backup tool:
```yaml
retention:
  daily: 7
  weekly: 4
  monthly: 12
  immutable: true
```

Example of access control for a sensitive directory:
```bash
setfacl -m u:auditor:rx /var/log/app
setfacl -m g:secops:r-- /var/log/app/secure.log
```

Example of a privacy-minded log redaction pattern:
```python
import re

def redact_email(text):
    return re.sub(r'[\w\.-]+@[\w\.-]+', '[REDACTED_EMAIL]', text)
```

**How to answer Domain 1 questions**

Use a business-first sequence:
1. Identify the business objective.
2. Determine the legal, regulatory, and contractual constraints.
3. Identify the risk owner and the acceptable risk level.
4. Choose the least disruptive control that adequately reduces risk.
5. Confirm the decision supports governance and continuity.

When two answers seem possible, prefer the one that:
- Aligns with policy and law
- Preserves business operations
- Assigns accountability correctly
- Reduces risk sustainably
- Supports measurable oversight

**Quick reference**

- **Governance**: direction and oversight
- **Management**: execution and operations
- **Due diligence**: investigate before deciding
- **Due care**: act reasonably after deciding
- **Risk owner**: accepts or treats risk
- **Control owner**: operates the control
- **Asset owner**: owns business value
- **RTO/RPO**: time and data recovery targets
- **Hot/Warm/Cold**: recovery site options
- **Tabletop/Simulation/Full interruption**: exercise types
- **Anonymization/Pseudonymization**: privacy-preserving techniques

**Exam-style review questions**

- A board asks for evidence that security supports business goals. What should you present?  
  - Security metrics, risk trends, roadmap progress, and governance reporting.

- A vendor assessment is being performed before contract signing. What concept is this?  
  - Due diligence.

- A system owner wants to keep a risk but security disagrees. Who decides?  
  - The risk owner, within the organization’s risk appetite and governance process.

- A company needs recovery within 30 minutes and minimal data loss. What site type is most suitable?  
  - Usually a hot site, assuming budget and architecture support it.

- A privacy review finds that more customer data is collected than needed. What principle is violated?  
  - Data minimization.

- A fraud investigation requires one person to be unable to both approve and pay invoices. What control is this?  
  - Separation of duties.

**Final exam mindset**

Domain 1 rewards judgment. The best answer is rarely the most technical one; it is usually the one that reflects governance, accountability, legal compliance, and business-aligned risk management. If you can explain who owns the risk, what the business impact is, and how the control supports continuity and compliance, you are thinking like a CISSP.
