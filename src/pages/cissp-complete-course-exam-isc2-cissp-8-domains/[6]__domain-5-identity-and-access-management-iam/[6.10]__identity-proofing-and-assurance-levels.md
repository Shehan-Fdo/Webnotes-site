**Identity assurance overview**

Identity assurance is the set of controls that reduce the risk of impersonation, account takeover, and unauthorized access by increasing confidence in three things:

- **Identity proofing**: how you verify a person is who they claim to be before issuing an identity.
- **Authentication assurance**: how strongly you verify the person each time they log in.
- **Federation assurance**: how strongly you trust assertions passed between identity providers and relying parties.

In CISSP terms, the most common framework is the NIST SP 800-63 digital identity model:

- **IAL** = Identity Assurance Level
- **AAL** = Authenticator Assurance Level
- **FAL** = Federation Assurance Level

These levels are related but not interchangeable. A user can have a strong authenticator but weak identity proofing, or a strong federation transaction with a weaker local authenticator policy. Good security design matches the assurance level to the business risk.

**Core NIST model**

<dl>
<dt><strong>Identity proofing</strong></dt>
<dd>The process of collecting and validating evidence to establish that a claimed identity belongs to a real person.</dd>
<dt><strong>Authentication</strong></dt>
<dd>The process of verifying that the person presenting credentials is the same person previously enrolled.</dd>
<dt><strong>Federation</strong></dt>
<dd>The process of using assertions from an identity provider to let a relying party trust an authenticated identity.</dd>
</dl>

**IAL: Identity Assurance Level**

IAL describes how much confidence you have in the identity proofing process before issuing an identity. The key exam point is that higher IAL means stronger vetting of identity evidence and stronger confidence that the identity is real and belongs to the claimant.

- **IAL1**
  - Identity proofing is not required in the formal NIST sense.
  - The identity may be self-asserted.
  - Use it when the risk is low and you do not need to bind the account to a verified real-world identity.
  - Typical examples:
    - Newsletter signup
    - Anonymous or pseudonymous forum access
    - Low-risk trial accounts
- **IAL2**
  - Requires evidence that supports a real-world identity with reasonable confidence.
  - Usually involves remote or in-person proofing using authoritative evidence.
  - Common evidence examples:
    - Government-issued photo ID such as a passport or driver’s license
    - Authoritative records such as a government database or trusted employer record
    - Supporting evidence that links the applicant to the claimed identity
  - Use it when account compromise could cause meaningful harm, but the highest level of identity certainty is not required.
- **IAL3**
  - Requires the strongest identity proofing.
  - Typically requires in-person proofing or equivalent high-confidence remote proofing with strong anti-fraud controls.
  - Common evidence examples:
    - High-assurance government identity documents
    - Biometrics collected and compared under controlled conditions
    - Strong authoritative source validation with fraud-resistant procedures
  - Use it for high-impact systems where impersonation would be severe, such as privileged government access or highly sensitive regulated workflows.

> Identity proofing is about the **person behind the account**, not just the password or token used later.

**Identity evidence and vetting**

Identity proofing usually combines multiple evidence types:

- **Evidence**: documents or records used to support the claimed identity.
- **Validation**: checking that the evidence is genuine and belongs to the claimant.
- **Verification**: confirming the claimed identity matches the evidence and authoritative sources.
- **Identity vetting**: the overall process of evaluating evidence, validation, and verification to decide whether to issue an identity.

Common evidence categories include:

- **Something you have**
  - Passport
  - Driver’s license
  - National ID card
  - Employee badge tied to an authoritative HR record
- **Something you are**
  - Facial biometrics
  - Fingerprints
  - Iris patterns
- **Something you know**
  - Knowledge-based questions are generally weak and increasingly discouraged as primary proofing evidence because they are often guessable or publicly discoverable.
- **Authoritative records**
  - Civil registry
  - Government database
  - HR or student records
  - Utility or financial records, when appropriate and allowed

Practical exam takeaway:

- A scanned ID alone is not enough if you cannot validate authenticity.
- Biometrics help, but they do not replace identity proofing by themselves.
- Strong proofing usually combines document validation, authoritative source checks, and fraud-resistant workflow controls.

**AAL: Authenticator Assurance Level**

AAL measures how strongly the authentication process resists impersonation after the identity has been proofed and enrolled. It is about the strength of the authenticator and the authentication protocol, not about how the identity was originally verified.

- **AAL1**
  - Requires single-factor authentication or equivalent low-assurance authentication.
  - Examples:
    - Password only
    - Password plus a low-risk session mechanism that does not materially increase assurance
  - Appropriate for low-risk access where compromise impact is limited.
- **AAL2**
  - Requires multi-factor authentication (MFA), meaning two or more different factors are used.
  - Factors can include:
    - Something you know
    - Something you have
    - Something you are
  - Examples:
    - Password + authenticator app OTP
    - Password + hardware one-time password token
    - Password + push approval with anti-replay protections
  - AAL2 improves resistance to credential theft, but it is not automatically phishing-resistant.
- **AAL3**
  - Requires phishing-resistant MFA with stronger verifier impersonation resistance.
  - Typically uses hardware-based or equivalent cryptographic authenticators.
  - Examples:
    - FIDO2/WebAuthn security key
    - Smart card with certificate-based authentication
    - Platform authenticator with strong cryptographic binding and phishing-resistant protocol support
  - Use it for privileged access, high-value transactions, and environments where phishing is a major threat.

> Do not confuse **MFA** with **phishing resistance**. MFA can still be phishable if the protocol allows credential relay or token replay.

**AAL comparison**

| Level | Main requirement | Typical examples | Key risk reduction |
|---|---|---|---|
| AAL1 | Single-factor or low-assurance auth | Password only | Basic access control |
| AAL2 | MFA | Password + OTP, password + push | Better resistance to stolen passwords |
| AAL3 | Phishing-resistant MFA | FIDO2, smart card, certificate-based auth | Strong resistance to phishing and verifier impersonation |

**AAL implementation details**

AAL is not just about adding more factors. The protocol matters.

- **Replay resistance**
  - The authentication exchange should prevent captured credentials from being reused.
- **Verifier impersonation resistance**
  - The authenticator should help the user verify the legitimate verifier, reducing phishing and man-in-the-middle attacks.
- **Cryptographic binding**
  - The authenticator should be bound to the relying party or session where possible.
- **Rate limiting and lockout**
  - Controls should slow online guessing and brute force attacks.
- **Secure recovery**
  - Account recovery should not become the weakest link.

Practical examples:

```bash
# Pseudocode: enforce MFA for privileged admin access
auth_policy:
  role: privileged_admin
  required_aal: AAL3
  allowed_authenticators:
    - fido2_security_key
    - smart_card
  deny_if:
    - otp_only
    - sms_otp
```

```bash
# Pseudocode: lower-risk workforce app
auth_policy:
  role: standard_employee
  required_aal: AAL2
  allowed_authenticators:
    - password_plus_totp
    - push_mfa
```

**FAL: Federation Assurance Level**

FAL measures the strength of the federation transaction between an identity provider and a relying party. It focuses on how assertions are presented, protected, and bound to the transaction.

- **Assertion**: a statement from the identity provider about the authenticated user.
- **Relying party**: the application or service that trusts the assertion.
- **Identity provider (IdP)**: the system that authenticates the user and issues the assertion.

FAL is not the same as AAL:

- **AAL** answers: “How strong was the user authentication?”
- **FAL** answers: “How strongly is the federation assertion protected and bound to the transaction?”

Typical federation protocols and controls:

- **SAML 2.0**
  - Signed assertions
  - Assertion audience restriction
  - Assertion expiration
  - Optional encrypted assertions
- **OpenID Connect (OIDC)**
  - ID token signature validation
  - `aud` claim validation
  - `iss` claim validation
  - Nonce validation
  - Token lifetime checks
- **OAuth 2.0**
  - Authorization framework, not an identity protocol by itself
  - Often paired with OIDC for authentication
  - Requires careful token handling and audience scoping

**FAL practical meaning**

- **FAL1**
  - Basic signed assertions with limited binding requirements.
  - Suitable when the federation trust is modest and the transaction risk is lower.
- **FAL2**
  - Stronger assertion protection and better binding to the transaction.
  - Often involves proof-of-possession style protections or stronger assertion presentation controls.
- **FAL3**
  - Strongest federation assurance.
  - Requires stronger binding of the assertion to the presenter and the transaction, reducing assertion replay and interception risk.

Because federation implementations vary, focus on the exam concept:

- Signed assertions alone are not enough to guarantee high federation assurance.
- The assertion must be validated for issuer, audience, lifetime, integrity, and binding.
- Higher FAL reduces the risk that a stolen assertion can be replayed at a different relying party or by a different attacker.

**Federation controls you should know**

- **Signed assertions**
  - Protect integrity and origin.
- **Audience restriction**
  - Ensures the assertion is intended for the correct relying party.
- **Expiration**
  - Limits replay window.
- **Nonce or transaction binding**
  - Helps prevent replay in browser-based flows.
- **Holder-of-key / proof-of-possession**
  - Binds the token or assertion to a key held by the client.
- **Token binding concepts**
  - Reduce token theft usefulness by tying the token to a specific client or TLS channel, where supported.

Example OIDC validation logic:

```python
def validate_id_token(token, expected_issuer, expected_client_id, expected_nonce):
    assert token["iss"] == expected_issuer
    assert expected_client_id in token["aud"]
    assert token["exp"] > now()
    assert token["nonce"] == expected_nonce
    assert verify_signature(token)
```

**How IAL, AAL, and FAL work together**

A secure identity system usually sets all three levels based on risk:

- **IAL** determines how sure you are about the person’s real-world identity.
- **AAL** determines how hard it is to impersonate the user during login.
- **FAL** determines how safely identity information moves across trust boundaries.

Example combinations:

- **Low-risk consumer portal**
  - IAL1
  - AAL1 or AAL2
  - FAL1 if federation is used
- **Enterprise SaaS with standard workforce access**
  - IAL2
  - AAL2
  - FAL2
- **Privileged admin access to sensitive systems**
  - IAL2 or IAL3 depending on onboarding risk
  - AAL3
  - FAL2 or FAL3
- **High-impact regulated or government workflow**
  - IAL3
  - AAL3
  - FAL3

**Policy selection examples**

Use the assurance level that matches the business impact, legal requirements, and threat model.

1. Identify the data sensitivity and transaction impact.
2. Decide whether the user must be tied to a verified real-world identity.
3. Choose the minimum AAL that resists the expected attack methods.
4. If federation is involved, choose the FAL that protects assertions against replay and misuse.
5. Reassess when the risk changes.

Example policy matrix:

| Scenario | Suggested IAL | Suggested AAL | Suggested FAL |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Public content access | IAL1 | AAL1 | FAL1 |
| Employee self-service portal | IAL2 | AAL2 | FAL2 |
| Admin console | IAL2 or IAL3 | AAL3 | FAL2 or FAL3 |
| Sensitive government workflow | IAL3 | AAL3 | FAL3 |

**Identity lifecycle and re-proofing**

Identity assurance is not a one-time event. You must manage the identity over its lifecycle.

- **Enrollment**
  - Create the identity and bind it to proofing evidence.
- **Activation**
  - Issue authenticators and enable access.
- **Maintenance**
  - Update attributes, rotate authenticators, and monitor risk.
- **Recovery**
  - Restore access after loss or compromise.
- **Re-proofing**
  - Re-establish identity confidence when risk changes or evidence becomes stale.
- **Deprovisioning**
  - Remove access when the identity is no longer valid.

Common re-proofing triggers:

- Lost or stolen high-assurance authenticator
- Suspected identity fraud
- Major change in legal identity attributes
- Long inactivity followed by sensitive access request
- Assurance downgrade after recovery through weaker channels
- Expired identity documents or authoritative records

> Recovery should not silently lower assurance for high-risk accounts. If you weaken recovery, you often need compensating controls or re-proofing.

**Biometrics in proofing and authentication**

Biometrics can support both proofing and authentication, but they have important limitations.

- **Enrollment quality matters**
  - Poor enrollment increases false rejects and false accepts later.
- **False Accept Rate (FAR)**
  - The chance an impostor is incorrectly accepted.
- **False Reject Rate (FRR)**
  - The chance a legitimate user is incorrectly rejected.
- **Liveness detection**
  - Helps detect spoofing attempts such as photos, masks, or replayed samples.
- **Fallback risk**
  - If biometric fallback is too weak, attackers may bypass the stronger factor.

Practical considerations:

- Use biometrics as part of a broader control set, not as the only trust anchor.
- Protect biometric templates as sensitive data.
- Prefer local matching and secure enclaves where possible.
- Plan for accessibility and failure cases.
- Provide secure fallback methods that do not reduce the overall assurance below policy.

**Common pitfalls and exam traps**

- **IAL is not AAL**
  - Proofing strength is not the same as login strength.
- **MFA is not automatically phishing-resistant**
  - OTP and push can still be phished or relayed.
- **Signed assertions are not enough by themselves**
  - You still need audience, issuer, lifetime, and binding checks.
- **Biometrics are not secrets**
  - They are identifiers with error rates and spoofing concerns.
- **Recovery can weaken assurance**
  - A strong login policy is undermined by weak account recovery.
- **Higher assurance is not always better**
  - Overly strict controls can reduce usability and increase unsafe workarounds.

**Practical configuration patterns**

A simple enterprise policy might look like this:

```yaml
identity_policy:
  workforce:
    proofing: IAL2
    authentication: AAL2
    federation: FAL2
  privileged_admin:
    proofing: IAL2
    authentication: AAL3
    federation: FAL3
  public_portal:
    proofing: IAL1
    authentication: AAL1
    federation: FAL1
```

A more realistic access decision can include step-up authentication:

```yaml
access_rules:
  - resource: payroll
    base_aal: AAL2
    step_up:
      condition: "change_bank_account OR export_bulk_data"
      required_aal: AAL3
  - resource: admin_console
    base_aal: AAL3
    required_ial: IAL2
```

This pattern is useful because you do not always need the highest assurance for every action. You can require stronger proof only when the transaction risk increases.

**Quick exam memory aids**

- **IAL** = “How sure are you about the person?”
- **AAL** = “How hard is it to fake the login?”
- **FAL** = “How safe is the assertion across federation?”
- **IAL1** = self-asserted or no formal proofing
- **IAL2** = validated identity evidence
- **IAL3** = strongest proofing, often in-person or equivalent
- **AAL2** = MFA, but not necessarily phishing-resistant
- **AAL3** = phishing-resistant MFA
- **FAL** = assertion protection and binding in federated trust

**What to remember for CISSP**

- Match assurance to risk, not to convenience.
- Separate identity proofing from authentication.
- Treat federation as its own trust problem.
- Prefer phishing-resistant authenticators for privileged access.
- Design recovery and re-proofing with the same care as initial enrollment.
- Use biometrics carefully, with liveness, fallback, and privacy controls.
