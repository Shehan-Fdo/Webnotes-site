## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Case study context: privileged access in finance**

Finance environments concentrate high-value assets, sensitive personal data, and regulated workflows. Privileged accounts can approve payments, change ledger records, administer ERP systems, manage databases, and access security tooling. That makes them a primary target for attackers and a major audit focus for governance teams.

The core security goal is to reduce standing privilege while preserving operational continuity. You want to know who can do what, when they can do it, why they need it, and how every privileged action is recorded.

- **Privileged Access Management (PAM)** centralizes control over elevated accounts and sessions.
- **Least privilege** limits users and services to the minimum access needed for their role.
- **Separation of duties** reduces fraud risk by preventing one person from controlling an entire sensitive process.
- **Accountability** requires strong identity binding, logging, and review.

**Threats and business risks**

Finance systems are attractive because privileged misuse can directly affect money movement, reporting integrity, and compliance posture. Risks include insider fraud, credential theft, ransomware lateral movement, and unauthorized changes to financial records.

Common privileged-access failure modes include:

- Shared admin accounts with no individual accountability.
- Permanent membership in high-privilege groups.
- Direct root or domain admin logons from user workstations.
- Unmonitored vendor access into production systems.
- Weak approval workflows for emergency access.
- Incomplete logs that cannot support forensic review.

A good design assumes compromise and limits blast radius. If an attacker steals one privileged credential, the environment should still resist rapid escalation and unauthorized transaction approval.

**Privileged account types and control objectives**

Finance organizations usually manage several privileged identity classes, each with different control needs.

| Account type | Typical use | Key control objective |
|---|---|---|
| Human admin account | System, database, or application administration | Individual accountability and MFA |
| Break-glass account | Emergency access during outages | Strong protection, offline storage, tight monitoring |
| Service account | Application-to-application or scheduled tasks | Non-interactive use, secret rotation, minimal rights |
| Vendor account | Third-party support | Time-bound access, session recording, approval |
| Domain or cloud admin | Directory and platform control | Tiered administration and restricted endpoints |

Use separate accounts for normal work and privileged work. This reduces phishing exposure and makes audit trails clearer.

**Core design principles**

A finance PAM program should combine technical controls, process controls, and governance.

- **Least privilege**
  - Grant only the permissions required for the task.
  - Review rights regularly and remove stale access.
- **Just-in-time (JIT) access**
  - Elevate privileges only for a limited window.
  - Prefer approval-based workflows for sensitive systems.
- **Just-enough administration (JEA)**
  - Expose only the commands or functions needed for a role.
  - Reduce the need for full admin shells.
- **Separation of duties**
  - Split request, approval, execution, and review.
  - Prevent one person from creating and approving the same payment or vendor change.
- **Defense in depth**
  - Combine MFA, PAM vaulting, session monitoring, endpoint hardening, and logging.
- **Zero trust mindset**
  - Verify identity, device posture, location, and context before granting elevation.

**Authentication and access control**

Privileged access should require stronger authentication than standard user access. In finance, MFA is usually mandatory for admin actions, remote access, and sensitive approvals.

Recommended controls:

- **Multi-factor authentication (MFA)**
  - Use phishing-resistant methods where possible, such as FIDO2 security keys or certificate-based authentication.
  - Avoid SMS as a primary factor for privileged access.
- **Conditional access**
  - Restrict admin logons to managed devices, trusted networks, or privileged access workstations.
  - Block elevation from high-risk geolocations or impossible-travel events.
- **Role-based access control (RBAC)**
  - Map permissions to job functions rather than individuals.
  - Keep roles narrow and review them against business need.
- **Attribute-based access control (ABAC)**
  - Use context such as device compliance, time of day, or ticket number to decide access.
  - This is useful for temporary elevation and vendor support.

A practical pattern is to require a ticket number before granting elevation. The ticket becomes part of the approval record and helps auditors verify business justification.

**Privileged Access Management architecture**

A PAM platform typically provides vaulting, checkout, rotation, session brokering, and recording. It reduces direct exposure of secrets and creates a controlled path to privileged systems.

Typical components:

- **Vault**
  - Stores privileged credentials, API keys, and secrets.
  - Encrypts secrets at rest and controls retrieval.
- **Password rotation**
  - Changes passwords automatically after use or on a schedule.
  - Reduces the value of stolen credentials.
- **Session proxy or broker**
  - Mediates RDP, SSH, web admin, or database sessions.
  - Prevents direct exposure of target credentials.
- **Session recording**
  - Captures commands, keystrokes, and screen activity for review.
- **Workflow engine**
  - Enforces approvals, ticket validation, and time limits.
- **Discovery and onboarding**
  - Finds unmanaged privileged accounts and brings them under control.

Example PAM workflow:

1. User requests elevation for a finance application server.
2. Manager or system owner approves the request.
3. PAM issues a time-limited credential or session token.
4. User connects through the broker, not directly to the target.
5. Session is recorded and logs are forwarded to the SIEM.
6. Credential is rotated after use.

**Practical CLI and configuration examples**

Even when a PAM platform is in place, you still need secure host-level configuration.

Linux sudo example:

```bash
# Allow a finance admin group to restart a specific service only
Cmnd_Alias FINANCE_APP = /bin/systemctl restart finance-app.service, /bin/systemctl status finance-app.service
%finance-admins ALL=(root) NOPASSWD: FINANCE_APP
```

This is safer than granting full root access because it limits the command set. In many environments, you should still require authentication rather than `NOPASSWD`, depending on risk and policy.

SSH hardening example:

```sshconfig
PermitRootLogin no
PasswordAuthentication no
PubkeyAuthentication yes
AllowGroups finance-admins
```

This reduces password-based attack surface and prevents direct root logons. Pair it with MFA at the PAM layer or via a bastion host.

Windows local policy example:

```powershell
# Example: add a user to a local admin group only through controlled change management
Add-LocalGroupMember -Group "Administrators" -Member "FINANCE\j.smith"
```

Use this sparingly and prefer temporary elevation through a PAM or privileged access workstation model.

**Privileged access workstations and bastions**

A **Privileged Access Workstation (PAW)** is a hardened endpoint used only for administrative tasks. A bastion host is a controlled jump point into sensitive networks.

Use these patterns to reduce credential theft:

- **PAW**
  - Separate admin activity from email, web browsing, and general productivity.
  - Enforce device compliance, disk encryption, and strong endpoint protection.
- **Bastion host**
  - Centralize inbound admin access.
  - Restrict source IPs and require MFA.
- **Jump server**
  - Provide a monitored path into segmented finance networks.
  - Log all administrative connections.

A common finance topology is:

- User workstation
- PAM portal or bastion
- Segmented admin network
- Finance application servers
- Database tier
- Directory services and logging infrastructure

This design helps prevent direct lateral movement from a compromised user endpoint into production systems.

**Session monitoring and logging**

Privileged activity must be observable. Logging is not just for incident response; it is also a control for deterrence and compliance.

Capture:

- Authentication events
- Privilege elevation requests
- Approval decisions
- Session start and end times
- Commands executed
- Configuration changes
- Failed access attempts
- Secret checkout and rotation events

Forward logs to a **Security Information and Event Management (SIEM)** platform for correlation and alerting. Alert on:

- Use of break-glass accounts
- Privileged logons outside business hours
- Multiple failed elevation attempts
- New admin group membership
- Disabled logging or tamper attempts
- Unusual database export activity

> Critical warning: session recording is only useful if it is protected from tampering and retained according to policy. If admins can delete or alter their own audit trail, accountability is lost.

**Service accounts and secrets management**

Service accounts often become hidden privilege risks because they are forgotten, over-permissioned, or shared across applications. Treat them as first-class assets.

Best practices:

- Assign unique service accounts per application or function.
- Disable interactive logon where possible.
- Store secrets in a vault, not in scripts or spreadsheets.
- Rotate passwords and API keys automatically.
- Prefer managed identities or workload identities when the platform supports them.
- Scope permissions narrowly to the target resource.

Example of a safer secret retrieval pattern:

```bash
# Pseudocode example: retrieve a secret at runtime from a vault
export DB_PASSWORD="$(vault kv get -field=password finance/prod/db)"
```

Avoid hardcoding secrets in configuration files:

```ini
# Bad practice
db_password=SuperSecret123
```

Instead, use environment injection, secret managers, or platform-native identity mechanisms.

**Emergency access and break-glass controls**

Break-glass accounts are for emergencies such as PAM outage, directory failure, or critical production recovery. They are necessary, but they must be tightly controlled.

Controls to apply:

- Store credentials offline or in a highly protected vault.
- Require dual control for retrieval when feasible.
- Use long, random passwords.
- Monitor every use with high-priority alerts.
- Rotate immediately after use.
- Test the process regularly so it works during a real outage.

Break-glass access should be rare and documented. Frequent use usually indicates a design problem or operational gap.

**Governance, risk, and compliance**

Finance environments often face audit requirements that demand evidence of control effectiveness. Your privileged access program should support review and reporting.

Key governance activities:

- Periodic access recertification
- Role and entitlement review
- Approval workflow validation
- Segregation-of-duties conflict checks
- Log retention and integrity verification
- Exception management with expiration dates

Useful evidence for auditors includes:

- Access review reports
- PAM checkout logs
- Session recordings
- MFA enforcement settings
- Rotation schedules
- Break-glass test results
- Change tickets linked to privileged actions

If a control cannot be evidenced, auditors may treat it as ineffective even if it exists in theory.

**Incident response considerations**

Privileged access controls should support containment and investigation.

During an incident, you may need to:

1. Disable compromised privileged accounts.
2. Revoke active sessions and tokens.
3. Rotate secrets and API keys.
4. Review recent privileged actions.
5. Check for unauthorized group membership changes.
6. Preserve logs and recordings for forensics.

A PAM system can speed containment because it centralizes credential rotation and session termination. It also helps identify which systems were accessed through a given account.

**Common exam angles**

For CISSP-style questions, focus on the control that best reduces risk while preserving business need.

- Choose **least privilege** over broad admin access.
- Choose **MFA** for privileged authentication.
- Choose **PAM** for vaulting, checkout, and session recording.
- Choose **separation of duties** to reduce fraud.
- Choose **JIT access** over permanent elevation.
- Choose **PAWs** and bastions to protect admin credentials.
- Choose **logging and monitoring** for accountability and detection.

When two answers seem plausible, prefer the one that addresses root cause and supports governance. For example, if the issue is shared admin credentials, the best fix is usually individual accounts with PAM and strong auditing, not just a stronger password policy.

**Quick reference checklist**

- Separate standard and privileged accounts.
- Require MFA for all privileged access.
- Use PAM for vaulting, checkout, and session recording.
- Restrict admin access to PAWs or bastions.
- Rotate secrets automatically.
- Disable direct root or domain admin logons where possible.
- Enforce separation of duties for financial approvals.
- Review privileged access regularly.
- Alert on break-glass use and unusual admin activity.
- Preserve logs and recordings for audit and incident response.

**Study takeaway**

Privileged access in finance is about controlling power, not just protecting passwords. The strongest design combines identity assurance, time-bound elevation, monitored sessions, and auditable governance. If you can explain how the environment prevents misuse, detects abuse, and proves compliance, you are thinking like a CISSP.
