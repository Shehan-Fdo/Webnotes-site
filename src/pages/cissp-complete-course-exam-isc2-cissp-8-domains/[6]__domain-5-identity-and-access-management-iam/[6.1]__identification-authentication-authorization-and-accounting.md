## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**IAAA overview**

Identification, Authentication, Authorization, and Accounting (IAAA) describes the core sequence used to control access to systems and data. In practice, you first establish who the subject claims to be, then verify that claim, then decide what the subject can do, and finally record what happened.

- **Identification** is the claim of identity, such as a username, employee ID, or service principal.
- **Authentication** is the proof of that claim, such as a password, token, certificate, or biometric factor.
- **Authorization** is the decision about permissions after authentication succeeds.
- **Accounting** is the logging and review of actions for traceability, billing, compliance, and incident response.

A common CISSP framing is that identification and authentication answer “Who are you?”, authorization answers “What can you do?”, and accounting answers “What did you do?”

**Identification**

Identification is the initial assertion of identity. It does not prove anything by itself; it simply creates a subject record that can be checked later.

- Usernames, email addresses, employee numbers, and device IDs are common identifiers.
- Service accounts and machine identities are also subjects and need unique identifiers.
- Good identifiers are unique, stable, and not easily confused with other identities.
- Shared identifiers reduce accountability and weaken auditability.

In enterprise environments, identity proofing often happens before account creation. Identity proofing is the process of validating a person’s real-world identity before issuing credentials. This is especially important for privileged accounts, remote onboarding, and regulated environments.

Examples of identifiers:
- `j.smith`
- `employee-10482`
- `svc-backup-prod`
- `CN=Alice Nguyen,OU=Users,DC=example,DC=com`

**Authentication factors and methods**

Authentication verifies the claimed identity using one or more factors. The classic factor categories are:

- **Something you know**: password, PIN, passphrase, security question
- **Something you have**: smart card, hardware token, OTP (one-time password) device, phone-based authenticator
- **Something you are**: fingerprint, face, iris, voice
- **Something you do**: typing rhythm, gesture patterns, behavioral biometrics
- **Somewhere you are**: location, network zone, geofencing
- **Something you exhibit**: a certificate or signed challenge response, depending on context

Strong authentication usually combines multiple factors. Multi-factor authentication (MFA) uses two or more different factor types. Two-factor authentication (2FA) is a subset of MFA that uses exactly two factors.

> A password plus a security question is not true MFA if both are “something you know.”

Common authentication mechanisms:
- Passwords and passphrases
- One-time passwords via TOTP (time-based one-time password) or HOTP (HMAC-based one-time password)
- Push-based approval
- Smart cards and PIV (Personal Identity Verification) cards
- Public key certificates and mutual TLS (Transport Layer Security)
- Kerberos tickets in domain environments
- Federated login using SAML (Security Assertion Markup Language) or OpenID Connect
- Biometrics with liveness detection

**Passwords and passphrases**

Passwords remain common because they are easy to deploy, but they are vulnerable to phishing, reuse, brute force, credential stuffing, and social engineering.

Best practices:
- Use long passphrases instead of short complex passwords when policy allows.
- Store passwords with strong salted hashing algorithms such as bcrypt, scrypt, Argon2, or PBKDF2.
- Enforce rate limiting, lockout controls, and breached-password checks.
- Avoid periodic forced changes unless compromise is suspected or policy requires it; modern guidance often favors change on compromise rather than arbitrary rotation.

Example password policy snippet:
```ini
min_length = 14
require_uppercase = true
require_lowercase = true
require_numbers = true
require_symbols = true
lockout_threshold = 5
lockout_duration_minutes = 15
password_history = 10
```

**Tokens, OTP, and cryptographic authenticators**

Tokens prove possession of a secret or device. They can be software-based or hardware-based.

- **TOTP** generates codes based on time and a shared secret.
- **HOTP** generates codes based on a counter and a shared secret.
- Hardware security keys often support FIDO2/WebAuthn, which resists phishing better than OTP because the authenticator binds to the origin.

Example TOTP provisioning URI:
```text
otpauth://totp/Example:alice@example.com?secret=JBSWY3DPEHPK3PXP&issuer=Example
```

Practical note:
- OTPs are better than passwords alone, but they can still be phished in real time.
- FIDO2/WebAuthn is generally stronger for interactive user authentication because it is origin-bound and resists replay.

**Biometrics**

Biometrics compare a physical or behavioral trait against a stored template. They are convenient, but they are not secrets and cannot be changed if compromised.

Important biometric concepts:
- **False acceptance rate (FAR)**: probability of accepting an impostor
- **False rejection rate (FRR)**: probability of rejecting a legitimate user
- **Equal error rate (EER)**: point where FAR and FRR are equal
- **Liveness detection**: checks that the sample comes from a live person, not a photo or replay

Use biometrics carefully:
- Prefer them for convenience and local unlock, not as the only factor for high-risk remote access.
- Protect biometric templates as sensitive data.
- Combine biometrics with another factor for stronger assurance.

**Authentication protocols and federation**

Authentication can be local, centralized, or federated.

- **Local authentication** validates credentials on the same system.
- **Centralized authentication** uses a directory or identity provider for consistent control.
- **Federated authentication** allows one organization to trust another identity provider.

Common enterprise technologies:
- **Kerberos** uses tickets and symmetric cryptography in domain environments.
- **LDAP** (Lightweight Directory Access Protocol) stores and queries directory information; it is not itself an authentication protocol, though it is often used with authentication systems.
- **RADIUS** and **TACACS+** are common for network device authentication and authorization.
- **SAML** is widely used for browser-based single sign-on (SSO).
- **OpenID Connect** adds identity layers on top of OAuth 2.0 for modern web and mobile SSO.

A simple SSO flow:
1. You access an application.
2. The application redirects you to the identity provider.
3. You authenticate to the identity provider.
4. The identity provider issues a signed assertion or token.
5. The application trusts the assertion and grants access.

**Authorization models**

Authorization determines what an authenticated subject can access. It is based on policy, roles, attributes, or context.

Common models:
- **Discretionary Access Control (DAC)**: the owner decides access, often via file permissions
- **Mandatory Access Control (MAC)**: the system enforces labels and clearance levels
- **Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)**: permissions are assigned to roles
- **Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC)**: decisions use attributes such as user, resource, action, and environment
- **Rule-Based Access Control**: access is determined by rules, often time or network based

Comparison:
- DAC is flexible but can be inconsistent.
- MAC is strict and common in high-security or government contexts.
- RBAC scales well in enterprises with job functions.
- ABAC is powerful for dynamic, context-aware decisions.

Example RBAC mapping:
- `helpdesk` role: reset passwords, view limited user profile data
- `hr-manager` role: view employee records
- `db-admin` role: manage database instances, not application code

Example ABAC policy idea:
- Allow access if `department == resource.department` and `device_compliant == true` and `time_of_day` is within business hours.

**Least privilege and separation of duties**

Authorization should enforce least privilege, meaning each subject gets only the access required to perform assigned tasks.

Related principles:
- **Need to know** limits access to information based on task requirement.
- **Separation of duties (SoD)** divides critical tasks so one person cannot complete a risky action alone.
- **Dual control** requires two authorized people to approve or perform a sensitive action.
- **Job rotation** can help detect fraud and reduce dependency on a single individual.

Examples:
- A developer should not also approve production deployment without review.
- A finance user should not create and approve the same payment.
- A database administrator should not have unrestricted access to audit logs if that would allow tampering.

**Accounting and audit logging**

Accounting records actions so you can reconstruct events, detect misuse, support investigations, and meet compliance requirements.

Good audit logs typically include:
- Who performed the action
- What action occurred
- When it occurred
- Where it occurred from
- What resource was affected
- Whether the action succeeded or failed

Useful log fields:
```json
{
  "timestamp": "2026-04-26T12:34:56Z",
  "user": "alice",
  "source_ip": "203.0.113.10",
  "action": "file_delete",
  "resource": "/finance/q1.xlsx",
  "result": "denied",
  "reason": "insufficient_privilege"
}
```

Logging best practices:
- Synchronize time with NTP (Network Time Protocol) so events correlate correctly.
- Protect logs from tampering with append-only storage, centralized logging, or write-once media where appropriate.
- Separate log access from administrative access when possible.
- Retain logs according to legal, regulatory, and business requirements.
- Monitor for anomalies such as repeated failures, impossible travel, privilege escalation, and unusual data access.

Example Linux auditd rule:
```bash
auditctl -w /etc/sudoers -p wa -k sudoers_changes
auditctl -w /var/log/auth.log -p wa -k auth_log_changes
```

Example Windows auditing concept:
- Enable logon auditing, object access auditing, and privilege use auditing.
- Forward events to a SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) platform for correlation.

**Identity lifecycle management**

Identity management is not just login; it covers the full lifecycle from creation to deprovisioning.

Lifecycle stages:
1. **Provisioning**: create the account and assign initial access.
2. **Modification**: update roles, groups, or attributes as job duties change.
3. **Review**: periodically recertify access to confirm it is still needed.
4. **Deprovisioning**: disable or remove access when the user leaves or no longer needs it.

Common failures:
- Orphaned accounts after termination
- Privileged access that is never reviewed
- Shared admin accounts with no accountability
- Service accounts with passwords that never rotate

Practical controls:
- Automate joiner-mover-leaver workflows.
- Disable accounts immediately on termination.
- Review privileged access more frequently than standard access.
- Use vaults or managed identities for service credentials.

**Single sign-on and session control**

Single sign-on (SSO) reduces password fatigue and centralizes authentication, but it also concentrates risk. If the identity provider is compromised, many applications may be affected.

Session controls matter after authentication:
- Use secure session cookies with `HttpOnly`, `Secure`, and `SameSite` where appropriate.
- Set reasonable session timeouts and idle timeouts.
- Reauthenticate for sensitive actions such as changing MFA settings or approving payments.
- Invalidate sessions on logout, password reset, or suspected compromise.

Example secure cookie header:
```http
Set-Cookie: sessionid=abc123; HttpOnly; Secure; SameSite=Lax; Path=/; Max-Age=1800
```

**Remote access and privileged authentication**

Remote access should use stronger controls than standard user access because exposure is higher.

Recommended patterns:
- VPN plus MFA for administrative access
- Bastion hosts or jump servers for privileged sessions
- Privileged Access Management (PAM) for just-in-time elevation and session recording
- Separate admin and user accounts
- Certificate-based authentication for device trust where appropriate

Example SSH hardening snippet:
```conf
PermitRootLogin no
PasswordAuthentication no
PubkeyAuthentication yes
AuthenticationMethods publickey,keyboard-interactive
AllowUsers alice bob
```

**Common attacks against IAAA**

Attackers target each stage of the access process.

- **Identification attacks**: account enumeration, username harvesting
- **Authentication attacks**: phishing, brute force, password spraying, credential stuffing, replay attacks, man-in-the-middle attacks
- **Authorization attacks**: privilege escalation, broken access control, insecure direct object references
- **Accounting attacks**: log deletion, log injection, time manipulation, disabling audit settings

Defensive measures:
- Use MFA, especially phishing-resistant methods for high-risk users.
- Rate limit and monitor authentication attempts.
- Validate authorization on the server side for every request.
- Centralize logs and restrict who can alter them.
- Alert on privilege changes and suspicious access patterns.

**Practical command examples**

Linux user and group inspection:
```bash
id alice
getent passwd alice
getent group admins
```

Check SSH authentication logs:
```bash
grep -i "failed password" /var/log/auth.log
journalctl -u ssh --since "1 hour ago"
```

List Windows local group membership:
```powershell
Get-LocalGroupMember -Group "Administrators"
```

Inspect a certificate used for authentication:
```bash
openssl x509 -in client.crt -text -noout
```

Test a RADIUS or TACACS+ deployment conceptually with vendor tools and verify:
- shared secret correctness
- source IP allowlisting
- accounting packet delivery
- failover behavior

**Exam focus and distinctions**

CISSP questions often test subtle differences.

- Identification is a claim; authentication is proof.
- Authentication verifies identity; authorization grants permissions.
- Accounting records activity; it does not grant access.
- Authentication factors are not the same as authentication mechanisms.
- SSO improves usability, but it increases dependency on the identity provider.
- Biometrics are convenient, but they are not revocable like passwords or tokens.
- RBAC is role-driven; ABAC is attribute-driven and more dynamic.
- Logging without review is not effective accounting.

**Quick memory aid**

- **I**dentification: who you say you are
- **A**uthentication: how you prove it
- **A**uthorization: what you may do
- **A**ccounting: what you did

**Summary**

IAAA is the foundation of access control. Strong security designs use unique identities, robust authentication, explicit authorization, and trustworthy accounting. The best implementations combine technical controls with lifecycle governance, monitoring, and periodic review so access remains appropriate over time.
