## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Case study context**

A multinational enterprise typically operates across multiple legal jurisdictions, business units, cloud providers, and legacy platforms. An effective **Identity and Access Management (IAM)** policy must balance security, usability, regulatory compliance, and operational scale.

In CISSP terms, IAM is not just about authentication. It includes identity lifecycle management, authorization, federation, privileged access, logging, governance, and periodic review. The policy must support local legal requirements while preserving global consistency.

**Business and security objectives**

A strong IAM policy for a multinational enterprise usually aims to:

- Enforce consistent identity standards across regions.
- Support local privacy, labor, and data residency requirements.
- Reduce account sprawl and orphaned access.
- Apply least privilege and separation of duties.
- Enable centralized visibility with delegated administration.
- Support workforce, contractor, partner, and customer identities.
- Integrate with cloud, SaaS, on-premises, and OT/ICS environments where needed.

The policy should be written at the governance level, while standards and procedures define implementation details.

**Core IAM policy principles**

The policy should define the enterprise’s identity model and control expectations.

- **Unique identity**: Each user, service, and privileged account should be uniquely identifiable.
- **Least privilege**: Access should be limited to the minimum required for the role.
- **Need to know**: Sensitive data access should be restricted based on business necessity.
- **Separation of duties**: Critical tasks should not be controlled by a single identity.
- **Accountability**: Actions should be traceable to a specific identity.
- **Lifecycle control**: Joiner, mover, and leaver events must trigger access changes.
- **Risk-based access**: Stronger controls should apply to higher-risk systems and contexts.

A multinational policy should also state that local exceptions require documented approval and expiration dates.

**Identity governance model**

A practical enterprise IAM program uses centralized governance with federated execution.

- A global IAM policy defines mandatory controls.
- Regional or business-unit standards adapt to local law and operations.
- Application owners approve access based on business need.
- HR acts as the authoritative source for workforce identity events.
- Vendor management or procurement may be the source for contractor identities.
- Customer identity systems may be managed separately from workforce IAM.

This model reduces inconsistency while allowing regional flexibility.

**Identity lifecycle management**

Identity lifecycle management is one of the most important controls in a multinational environment because employee movement across countries and business units is common.

Typical lifecycle stages:

1. **Provisioning**
   - Create the account after identity proofing and approval.
   - Assign baseline access based on role and location.
   - Record sponsor, manager, and expiration date where applicable.

2. **Modification**
   - Update access when the user changes role, region, or employment type.
   - Remove access that is no longer needed.
   - Re-evaluate privileged or sensitive access after transfers.

3. **Deprovisioning**
   - Disable accounts immediately upon termination or contract end.
   - Revoke tokens, certificates, API keys, and sessions.
   - Transfer ownership of business data and shared resources.
   - Preserve logs and records according to retention policy.

A common failure in global enterprises is delayed deprovisioning across shadow IT systems and regional applications.

**Authentication controls**

Authentication should be proportional to risk and user type.

- **Single sign-on (SSO)** improves usability and centralizes control.
- **Multi-factor authentication (MFA)** should be required for privileged access and remote access.
- **Adaptive authentication** can increase assurance based on device posture, geolocation, impossible travel, or unusual behavior.
- **Password policy** should avoid outdated complexity-only rules and instead emphasize length, reuse prevention, and breach detection.
- **Passwordless methods** such as FIDO2 security keys or platform authenticators can reduce phishing risk.

For multinational enterprises, authentication policy should account for:
- Local device availability and user experience.
- Regulatory constraints on biometric use.
- Network latency and resilience.
- Emergency access when identity providers are unavailable.

**Authorization models**

Authorization determines what an authenticated identity can do.

Common models include:

- **Role-based access control (RBAC)**: Access is assigned by job role.
- **Attribute-based access control (ABAC)**: Access is based on attributes such as department, country, device trust, or data classification.
- **Rule-based access control**: Access is granted by predefined rules, often for specific conditions.
- **Discretionary access control (DAC)**: Resource owners control access, but this is usually less suitable for enterprise governance.
- **Mandatory access control (MAC)**: Strong policy enforcement based on labels and clearance, useful in high-security environments.

For a multinational enterprise, RBAC is often the baseline, with ABAC used to handle regional, legal, and contextual constraints.

**Example access decision logic**

```text
Allow access if:
- user.department = "Finance"
- user.country in allowed_countries
- device.compliant = true
- resource.classification <= user.clearance
- MFA = satisfied
```

This kind of policy is useful when access must vary by geography, device trust, and data sensitivity.

**Privileged access management**

Privileged accounts require stronger controls than standard user accounts.

Key controls include:

- Separate admin accounts from normal user accounts.
- Use just-in-time (JIT) elevation where possible.
- Require MFA for all privileged actions.
- Store privileged credentials in a vault.
- Rotate secrets regularly and after suspected compromise.
- Record and review privileged sessions.
- Restrict use of shared admin accounts; if unavoidable, compensate with session recording and strong logging.

A multinational enterprise should also define how regional administrators are approved, monitored, and revoked.

**Federation and trust relationships**

Federation allows one organization or domain to trust identity assertions from another.

Common federation technologies include:
- **SAML** for enterprise web SSO.
- **OpenID Connect (OIDC)** for modern web and mobile authentication.
- **OAuth 2.0** for delegated authorization, not authentication by itself.

Federation is useful when:
- Employees need access to SaaS applications.
- Partners require controlled access without local account creation.
- Multiple identity providers must coexist across regions.

The policy should require:
- Trust agreements and technical standards.
- Certificate and metadata lifecycle management.
- Periodic validation of relying parties and identity providers.
- Revocation procedures for compromised trust relationships.

**Data residency and privacy considerations**

Multinational IAM policy must reflect privacy and sovereignty requirements.

Important considerations:
- Personal data may need to remain in specific regions.
- Identity logs may be subject to retention limits.
- Biometric data may require explicit consent or special handling.
- Cross-border identity synchronization may be restricted.
- Access reviews may need to avoid exposing unnecessary personal information.

The policy should coordinate with legal, privacy, and records management teams. In some jurisdictions, employee monitoring and session recording may require notice or approval.

**Segregation of duties and conflict management**

Separation of duties is critical in finance, procurement, HR, and security administration.

Examples:
- The person who creates a vendor should not approve payment.
- The person who deploys code should not approve production release alone.
- The person who manages IAM policy should not approve their own privileged access.

Controls to support this include:
- Dual approval workflows.
- Compensating controls for small teams.
- Periodic SoD conflict analysis.
- Emergency access with post-event review.

**Access reviews and recertification**

Periodic review is essential in large enterprises because access drift accumulates quickly.

Review types:
- **User access recertification**: Managers confirm access remains appropriate.
- **Privileged access review**: Security or system owners validate elevated rights.
- **Application entitlement review**: Owners validate application-specific permissions.
- **Service account review**: Confirm ownership, purpose, and rotation status.

Best practices:
- Review high-risk access more frequently.
- Use risk-based sampling for low-risk entitlements.
- Remove access by default when review is not completed.
- Track exceptions and remediation deadlines.

**Service accounts and non-human identities**

Modern enterprises rely heavily on non-human identities such as:
- Service accounts
- API keys
- Certificates
- Workload identities
- Automation accounts
- Bots and scripts

These identities should be governed with the same rigor as user accounts:
- Assign an owner.
- Define purpose and scope.
- Use least privilege.
- Rotate secrets or eliminate static secrets where possible.
- Log usage and alert on anomalies.
- Disable unused identities promptly.

A common weakness is overprivileged service accounts with no clear owner.

**Cloud and hybrid IAM considerations**

Multinational enterprises often use hybrid identity architectures.

Typical patterns:
- On-premises directory synchronized to cloud identity provider.
- Cloud identity as the primary authentication plane.
- Federation to SaaS applications.
- Conditional access based on device compliance and risk.

Important controls:
- Protect directory synchronization accounts.
- Limit privileged cloud roles.
- Separate tenant administration from application administration.
- Monitor for misconfigured external sharing.
- Use infrastructure as code for repeatable IAM configuration where possible.

Example cloud CLI checks:

```bash
# Example: list IAM users in a cloud environment
aws iam list-users

# Example: inspect a role's trust policy
aws iam get-role --role-name FinanceReadOnlyRole

# Example: review active sessions or sign-in logs in a directory platform
# (Command varies by platform; use the provider's audit and sign-in logs)
```

**Directory and group design**

A multinational enterprise should design directory groups carefully to avoid entitlement chaos.

Recommended practices:
- Use role groups for job-based access.
- Use resource groups for application or system access.
- Use nested groups cautiously, because they can obscure effective permissions.
- Avoid direct user-to-resource assignments when group-based assignment is possible.
- Standardize naming conventions by region, function, and environment.

Example naming pattern:
- `GRP-FIN-APAC-READ`
- `GRP-HR-EMEA-APPROVER`
- `GRP-PRIV-DBA-PROD`

This improves auditability and reduces manual errors.

**Logging, monitoring, and auditability**

IAM logs are essential for detection and investigation.

Log events should include:
- Authentication successes and failures.
- MFA challenges and bypasses.
- Privilege elevation events.
- Account creation, modification, and deletion.
- Group membership changes.
- Federation assertions and token issuance.
- Policy changes and admin actions.

The enterprise should forward logs to a centralized security information and event management system (**SIEM**) and retain them according to legal and operational requirements.

Useful monitoring examples:
- Impossible travel or anomalous geolocation.
- Excessive failed logins.
- Privileged access outside business hours.
- New service account creation without approval.
- Dormant account reactivation.

**Incident response implications**

IAM policy should support rapid containment.

During an identity-related incident, you may need to:
- Disable compromised accounts.
- Revoke sessions and refresh tokens.
- Rotate secrets and certificates.
- Remove malicious federation trust.
- Freeze privileged role assignments.
- Preserve evidence and audit logs.

The policy should define who can execute emergency actions and how those actions are reviewed afterward.

**Sample policy statement**

```text
All workforce identities shall be uniquely assigned, centrally governed, and reviewed at least quarterly for privileged access.
Access shall be granted based on approved business need, least privilege, and separation of duties.
MFA is mandatory for remote access, privileged access, and access to sensitive systems.
Accounts shall be disabled immediately upon termination or contract expiration.
Exceptions require documented risk acceptance, compensating controls, and an expiration date.
```

**Practical implementation checklist**

1. Define the authoritative identity sources for employees, contractors, partners, and customers.
2. Standardize identity proofing and onboarding requirements.
3. Map roles to baseline access using RBAC.
4. Add ABAC conditions for region, device trust, and data sensitivity.
5. Enforce MFA and conditional access for high-risk scenarios.
6. Implement privileged access management with JIT elevation.
7. Centralize logging and integrate with SIEM.
8. Schedule access reviews and automate deprovisioning.
9. Document exception handling and risk acceptance.
10. Test incident response for account compromise and federation failure.

**Common pitfalls**

- Treating IAM as an IT-only issue instead of a governance function.
- Allowing regional exceptions without expiration or review.
- Using shared accounts for convenience.
- Failing to remove access after role changes.
- Overrelying on passwords instead of stronger authentication.
- Ignoring service accounts and API credentials.
- Not aligning IAM controls with privacy and residency laws.
- Leaving orphaned accounts in acquired companies or legacy systems.

**CISSP exam focus**

For CISSP, focus on the policy and governance perspective rather than product-specific features.

You should be able to distinguish:
- **Authentication** vs. authorization
- **RBAC** vs. **ABAC**
- **Federation** vs. directory synchronization
- **Privileged access** vs. standard access
- **Policy** vs. standard vs. procedure
- **Risk acceptance** vs. compensating control

When answering scenario questions, choose the option that best supports:
- Least privilege
- Business alignment
- Auditability
- Scalability
- Legal and regulatory compliance

**Summary**

A multinational enterprise IAM policy must be globally consistent, locally adaptable, and operationally enforceable. The strongest designs combine centralized governance, federated identity, least privilege, strong authentication, privileged access controls, and continuous review. In CISSP scenarios, prioritize controls that reduce risk while supporting business operations across jurisdictions.
