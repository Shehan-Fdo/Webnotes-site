## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**How to use this final checklist**

This is a last-pass review for the CISSP exam. Use it to confirm that you can recognize the best answer in scenario-based questions, not just define terms. CISSP questions often test governance, risk, and decision-making before technical implementation.

Focus on:
- What the business is trying to protect
- Who owns the risk
- Which control is most appropriate
- Whether the control is preventive, detective, corrective, deterrent, or compensating
- Whether the answer supports policy, process, or technology in the right order

> If two answers look technically correct, CISSP usually favors the one that best supports governance, risk reduction, least privilege, and business continuity.

**Exam mindset and question strategy**

CISSP questions are often written to test judgment. You should read for:
- The subject of the question: policy, architecture, operations, or incident response
- The time horizon: immediate containment vs long-term remediation
- The control layer: administrative, technical, or physical
- The priority: safety, legality, confidentiality, integrity, availability, or accountability

A practical approach:
1. Identify the domain and the business objective.
2. Eliminate answers that are too technical, too narrow, or too early in the lifecycle.
3. Prefer the answer that reduces risk at the highest level.
4. Choose governance and process before tools when both are plausible.
5. Verify whether the question asks for the *best* answer, not merely a *correct* one.

**Domain 1: Security and Risk Management**

This domain anchors the exam. You should be able to explain how security supports business goals, how governance works, and how risk is owned and treated.

Must-know checklist:
- **Security governance** aligns security strategy with business objectives, legal requirements, and organizational culture.
- **Security policy hierarchy** includes policies, standards, procedures, guidelines, and baselines.
- **Roles and responsibilities** are distinct:
  - **Data owner** classifies data and approves access.
  - **System owner** is accountable for a system’s operation and protection.
  - **Custodian** implements and maintains controls.
  - **User** follows policy and uses data appropriately.
  - **Risk owner** accepts or treats the risk and remains accountable for the decision.
- **Due care** means acting prudently; **due diligence** means verifying that controls are effective.
- **Risk management** includes identify, assess, treat, monitor, and communicate.
- **Risk treatment** options are mitigate, transfer, avoid, and accept.
- **Qualitative risk analysis** uses categories and judgment; **quantitative risk analysis** uses numeric values such as SLE, ARO, and ALE.
- **Security awareness** should be role-based, measurable, and reinforced over time.
- **Metrics** matter:
  - **KPI** (Key Performance Indicator) measures performance against goals.
  - **KRI** (Key Risk Indicator) signals increasing risk exposure.
- **Third-party risk** includes vendors, cloud providers, contractors, and supply chain dependencies.
- **Security program effectiveness** is measured through audits, metrics, incident trends, phishing simulations, and control testing.

Key exam distinctions:
- A policy states intent; a standard states mandatory specifics; a procedure explains steps.
- The business owner decides risk acceptance, not the security team alone.
- A control framework helps structure governance, but it does not replace risk analysis.

**Domain 2: Asset Security**

This domain focuses on protecting information throughout its lifecycle and ensuring proper handling based on classification and sensitivity.

Must-know checklist:
- **Data classification** defines handling requirements based on sensitivity and business impact.
- Common labels include public, internal, confidential, and restricted, though naming varies by organization.
- **Data states**:
  - **At rest**: stored on disk, backups, databases, removable media
  - **In transit**: moving across networks, APIs, email, VPNs
  - **In use**: actively processed in memory or by applications
- **Protection methods** should match the state:
  - At rest: full-disk encryption, database encryption, file-level encryption, access control
  - In transit: TLS, IPsec, VPNs, secure messaging
  - In use: memory protection, trusted execution environments, strict access control, tokenization where appropriate
- **Key management** is as important as encryption:
  - Protect keys separately from encrypted data
  - Rotate keys according to policy
  - Use secure storage such as HSMs for high-value keys
- **Labeling and handling rules** should define storage, transmission, printing, disposal, and sharing requirements.
- **Data retention** must balance legal, regulatory, and business needs.
- **Data destruction** should be appropriate to media type:
  - Clear, purge, or destroy depending on sensitivity and reuse requirements
- **Privacy** requires minimizing collection, limiting use, and protecting personally identifiable information (PII).

Practical example:
- If a laptop is stolen, full-disk encryption protects data at rest.
- If a database backup is copied to cloud storage, encryption and access control must still protect the backup.
- If a user sends confidential data by email, TLS protects transit, but content encryption may still be needed.

**Domain 3: Security Architecture and Engineering**

This domain tests how you design secure systems, choose controls, and understand trust boundaries.

Must-know checklist:
- **Security models** include Bell-LaPadula for confidentiality, Biba for integrity, Clark-Wilson for transaction integrity, and Brewer-Nash for conflict of interest.
- **Trusted computing** concepts include:
  - **TPM** (Trusted Platform Module) for hardware-backed key storage and platform integrity
  - **HSM** (Hardware Security Module) for strong key protection and cryptographic operations
  - **Secure enclave** or trusted execution environment for isolated processing of sensitive code and data
- **Cryptography use cases**:
  - Symmetric encryption for bulk data
  - Asymmetric encryption for key exchange, digital signatures, and identity assurance
  - Hashing for integrity
  - Digital signatures for nonrepudiation and integrity
- **Hashing vs encryption**:
  - Hashing is one-way
  - Encryption is reversible with the correct key
- **PKI** (Public Key Infrastructure) supports certificates, trust chains, revocation, and identity binding.
- **Secure design principles** include least privilege, defense in depth, fail secure, complete mediation, and separation of duties.
- **Hardware and firmware security** should address secure boot, measured boot, firmware integrity, and supply chain trust.
- **Physical and environmental controls** protect availability and safety through power, HVAC, fire suppression, and site design.

Decision point:
- Use an HSM when you need strong protection for private keys or signing operations.
- Use a TPM when you need platform trust and device-bound key protection.
- Use a secure enclave when sensitive processing must be isolated from the main OS.

**Domain 4: Communication and Network Security**

This domain covers secure network design, segmentation, remote access, and traffic control.

Must-know checklist:
- **Segmentation** limits lateral movement and reduces blast radius.
- **VLANs** separate broadcast domains logically.
- **Subnets** separate networks at Layer 3.
- **DMZ** (demilitarized zone) isolates public-facing services from internal networks.
- **NAT** (Network Address Translation) hides internal addressing and conserves public IP space, but it is not a security control by itself.
- **Proxy servers** mediate client requests and can filter, log, and cache traffic.
- **Firewalls** enforce policy at network boundaries.
- **IDS** (Intrusion Detection System) alerts on suspicious activity.
- **IPS** (Intrusion Prevention System) blocks or drops malicious traffic.
- **NAC** (Network Access Control) checks device posture before granting access.
- **Load balancers** distribute traffic and can improve availability and resilience.
- **VPNs** provide encrypted tunnels for remote access and site-to-site connectivity.
- **TLS** secures application traffic such as web, API, and email transport.
- **Zero trust** assumes no implicit trust based on network location.

Practical topology example:
```text
Internet
   |
[WAF/Proxy]
   |
[Firewall]
   |
   +---- DMZ ---- [Web Server]
   |
[Internal Firewall]
   |
[App Network] ---- [Database Network]
```

Remote access considerations:
- Use MFA (Multi-Factor Authentication) for remote users.
- Prefer device posture checks and least privilege access.
- Restrict administrative access through jump hosts or bastion hosts.
- Separate user VPN access from privileged management access.

**Domain 5: Identity and Access Management**

This domain is heavily tested. You should know how identities are created, authenticated, authorized, and audited.

Must-know checklist:
- **Identification** is claiming an identity.
- **Authentication** is proving the claim.
- **Authorization** is determining what the identity can do.
- **Accounting** is logging actions for traceability.
- **Federation** allows trust across organizations or domains.
- **SSO** (Single Sign-On) reduces repeated logins and improves usability.
- Common federation and authentication technologies:
  - **SAML** for enterprise federation and browser-based SSO
  - **OAuth** for delegated authorization
  - **OpenID Connect** for authentication built on OAuth 2.0
  - **Kerberos** for ticket-based authentication in trusted domains
- **PAM** (Privileged Access Management) controls and monitors elevated accounts.
- **Least privilege** and **need to know** limit access.
- **RBAC** (Role-Based Access Control) assigns permissions by role.
- **ABAC** (Attribute-Based Access Control) uses attributes such as user, device, location, and time.
- **DAC** (Discretionary Access Control) lets owners decide access.
- **MAC** (Mandatory Access Control) uses labels and centralized policy.
- **MFA** reduces the risk of credential compromise.
- **Account lifecycle management** should cover provisioning, modification, review, suspension, and deprovisioning.

Compare access models:
| Model | Decision basis | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| DAC | Owner discretion | Small, flexible environments |
| MAC | Security labels and policy | High-security or classified environments |
| RBAC | Job role | Enterprise applications |
| ABAC | Attributes and context | Dynamic cloud and zero-trust environments |

Practical example:
- A contractor should receive time-bound access, a limited role, and periodic review.
- A privileged admin should use a separate admin account, MFA, session logging, and just-in-time access where possible.

**Domain 6: Security Assessment and Testing**

This domain focuses on proving that controls work and that evidence is trustworthy.

Must-know checklist:
- **Assessment** verifies whether controls are implemented and effective.
- **Testing** should be planned, repeatable, and documented.
- **Test plans** define scope, objectives, methods, and success criteria.
- **Test reports** summarize findings, risk, evidence, and remediation status.
- **Baseline comparisons** show whether systems deviate from approved secure configurations.
- **Vulnerability scanning** identifies known weaknesses.
- **Penetration testing** attempts to exploit weaknesses to demonstrate impact.
- **Code review** examines source code for logic flaws, insecure patterns, and policy violations.
- **Static testing** analyzes code without execution.
- **Dynamic testing** evaluates behavior while running.
- **Audit evidence** must be relevant, reliable, sufficient, and collected with chain of custody when needed.
- **Control testing** should verify both design and operating effectiveness.

Important boundary:
- A penetration test is not the same as a code review.
- A vulnerability scan is not proof of exploitability.
- An audit is not the same as an attack simulation; it is evidence-based verification.

**Domain 7: Security Operations**

This domain covers day-to-day protection, monitoring, incident handling, backups, recovery, and operational resilience.

Must-know checklist:
- **Logging and monitoring** support detection, investigation, and accountability.
- **SIEM** (Security Information and Event Management) centralizes logs and correlation.
- **SOAR** (Security Orchestration, Automation, and Response) automates response workflows.
- **Incident response** phases typically include preparation, detection, containment, eradication, recovery, and lessons learned.
- **Escalation procedures** should define severity levels, notification paths, legal involvement, and executive communication.
- **Roles** may include incident commander, communications lead, forensic lead, legal, HR, and business owner.
- **Backups** should be tested, protected, and recoverable.
- Backup types:
  - **Full**: complete copy
  - **Incremental**: changes since last backup
  - **Differential**: changes since last full backup
- Rotation strategies:
  - Grandfather-father-son
  - Offsite copies
  - Immutable or write-once storage where appropriate
- **DR** (Disaster Recovery) site types:
  - Hot site: near-immediate recovery
  - Warm site: partial readiness
  - Cold site: lowest cost, slowest recovery
- **BCP** (Business Continuity Planning) focuses on keeping critical business functions running.
- **Media protection** includes secure storage, transport, sanitization, and destruction.
- **Change management** prevents unauthorized or risky changes from destabilizing production.
- **Configuration management** maintains approved settings and supports rollback.
- **Patch management** balances security urgency with testing and operational stability.

Operational example:
- If a critical server patch is available, you should test it in a staging environment, assess business impact, approve through change control, deploy, verify, and document rollback steps.

**Domain 8: Software Development Security**

This domain tests how security is built into software from requirements through deployment and maintenance.

Must-know checklist:
- **SDLC** (Software Development Life Cycle) integrates security into planning, design, build, test, deploy, and maintain phases.
- **SSDLC** (Secure SDLC) adds security requirements, threat modeling, secure coding, and security testing throughout the lifecycle.
- **Development methodologies** may include waterfall, agile, DevOps, and DevSecOps.
- **Threat modeling** identifies assets, trust boundaries, threats, and mitigations early.
- **Secure coding** addresses input validation, output encoding, authentication, authorization, error handling, and logging.
- **Application security testing** includes:
  - SAST (Static Application Security Testing)
  - DAST (Dynamic Application Security Testing)
  - IAST (Interactive Application Security Testing)
  - SCA (Software Composition Analysis) for third-party dependencies
- **Code review** catches logic flaws and insecure patterns before release.
- **Container and cloud-native security** should address image trust, least privilege, secrets management, runtime isolation, and infrastructure as code review.
- **CI/CD** (Continuous Integration/Continuous Delivery or Deployment) pipelines should include security gates, signed artifacts, and approval controls.
- **Change control** applies to code, infrastructure, and configuration changes.
- **Baseline management** ensures approved builds and configurations can be compared, restored, and audited.

Practical example:
```yaml
# Example CI pipeline security gate
stages:
  - build
  - test
  - scan
  - approve
  - deploy
```

A secure development program should answer:
- Are security requirements defined before coding starts?
- Are dependencies scanned for known vulnerabilities?
- Are secrets excluded from source control?
- Are production changes reviewed and traceable?
- Are containers built from trusted images and run with minimal privileges?

**High-yield control and lifecycle comparisons**

| Topic | Best exam distinction | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Policy vs standard vs procedure | Intent vs mandatory detail vs step-by-step method | Helps you choose the right governance artifact |
| Risk owner vs system owner | Risk acceptance vs system accountability | Clarifies who makes the final decision |
| Preventive vs detective vs corrective | Stops, finds, or fixes an issue | Helps match control to scenario |
| Full vs incremental vs differential backup | Scope of copied data | Impacts recovery time and storage |
| Hot vs warm vs cold site | Recovery speed vs cost | Guides disaster recovery planning |
| SAML vs OAuth vs OIDC | Federation vs authorization vs authentication | Prevents protocol confusion |
| SAST vs DAST | Source analysis vs runtime testing | Helps place testing in the SDLC |

**Final readiness self-check**

Before the exam, make sure you can answer these without hesitation:
- Who owns risk, and who implements controls?
- When do you choose policy, standard, procedure, or guideline?
- How do you protect data at rest, in transit, and in use?
- When do you use TPM, HSM, or a secure enclave?
- Which network control best fits segmentation, remote access, or traffic inspection?
- How do SAML, OAuth, OpenID Connect, and Kerberos differ?
- What evidence proves a control works?
- What is the difference between incident response, disaster recovery, and business continuity?
- Where do SDLC, SSDLC, DevSecOps, and change control fit?

**Practical last-week review routine**

1. Review one domain per day and recite the key roles, controls, and lifecycle concepts.
2. Rework missed practice questions and explain why the correct answer is best.
3. Revisit weak areas with compare/contrast tables and scenario questions.
4. Practice identifying the business-first answer, not the tool-first answer.
5. Sleep, pace yourself, and avoid last-minute cramming of new material.

**Mini scenario drills**

- A vendor needs access to a production system for two weeks. Use time-bound access, least privilege, MFA, logging, and approval by the appropriate owner.
- A laptop containing sensitive data is lost. Confirm encryption, remote wipe capability, asset tracking, and incident reporting.
- A new web app is going live. Verify threat modeling, secure coding review, DAST/SAST, change approval, and rollback planning.
- A ransomware event encrypts file servers. Prioritize containment, preserve evidence, activate incident response, and restore from tested backups.

**Final exam reminder**

CISSP rewards broad judgment. When in doubt, choose the answer that:
- Protects the business
- Respects governance and ownership
- Reduces risk in the most durable way
- Supports policy, process, and accountability before technology alone
