## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**How to use these notes**

These notes are organized by CISSP domain and emphasize concepts that commonly appear in scenario-based questions. Focus on the *why* behind each control, not just the definition. CISSP questions often test governance, risk, and the best answer in context rather than a single technical fact.

- Prioritize **risk-based thinking** over tool-first answers.
- Treat controls as part of a lifecycle: identify, protect, detect, respond, recover.
- Look for the most appropriate answer for the business, not the most aggressive technical option.

**Domain 1: Security and Risk Management**

This domain sets the foundation for the rest of the exam. It covers governance, policy, compliance, ethics, risk, and business continuity. In CISSP terms, security is a business enabler, so you should understand how leadership, policy, and risk decisions shape the program.

- **Security governance** defines direction, accountability, and oversight.
  - A security program aligns with business objectives, legal requirements, and risk appetite.
  - Security committees help coordinate priorities across IT, legal, HR, audit, and business units.
  - Metrics and reporting translate security performance into language leadership can act on.
- **Policies, standards, procedures, and guidelines** differ by rigidity.
  - Policies state mandatory intent.
  - Standards define required specifics.
  - Procedures describe step-by-step execution.
  - Guidelines are recommended, not mandatory.
- **Risk management** is the process of identifying, analyzing, treating, and monitoring risk.
  - Common treatments are **avoid**, **mitigate**, **transfer**, and **accept**.
  - Qualitative analysis uses categories such as high/medium/low.
  - Quantitative analysis uses numeric values such as ALE (Annualized Loss Expectancy).
- **Risk frameworks** provide structure.
  - ISO 31000 is a general risk management framework.
  - NIST RMF (Risk Management Framework) is common in U.S. federal environments.
  - COSO is often associated with enterprise risk and internal controls.
- **Due care** means acting prudently; **due diligence** means actively investigating and validating.
- **Legal and regulatory concepts** matter.
  - Common law relies on precedent and judicial decisions.
  - Civil law relies more on codified statutes and codes.
  - Administrative law comes from regulatory agencies.
  - Criminal law addresses offenses against society.
- **Privacy principles** include data minimization, purpose limitation, consent, and retention control.
  - Data classification supports handling requirements.
  - Personal data and sensitive data may trigger jurisdiction-specific obligations.
- **Ethics** are part of professional conduct.
  - CISSP candidates are expected to follow the (ISC)² Code of Ethics.
  - Confidentiality, integrity, and availability must be balanced with lawful and ethical behavior.
- **Business continuity and disaster recovery** are core exam topics.
  - BCP focuses on keeping critical business functions operating.
  - DR focuses on restoring IT services after disruption.
  - A BIA (Business Impact Analysis) identifies critical processes, dependencies, and impact over time.
  - MTD (Maximum Tolerable Downtime) or MTPD (Maximum Tolerable Period of Disruption) defines the longest acceptable outage.
  - RTO (Recovery Time Objective) is the target time to restore service.
  - RPO (Recovery Point Objective) is the maximum acceptable data loss measured in time.
- **Recovery strategy selection** depends on cost, criticality, and feasibility.
  - Hot sites provide fast recovery but cost more.
  - Warm sites balance cost and recovery speed.
  - Cold sites are cheaper but slower to activate.
  - Redundancy, replication, and cloud recovery options can reduce downtime.
- **Testing and exercises** validate plans.
  - Tabletop exercises discuss response actions.
  - Walkthroughs review procedures step by step.
  - Functional tests validate specific capabilities.
  - Full interruption tests are the most disruptive because they actually interrupt production or primary operations.
- **Personnel security** supports governance.
  - Background checks, onboarding, offboarding, and separation of duties reduce insider risk.
  - Least privilege limits access to what is necessary.
  - Job rotation and mandatory vacations can help detect fraud.

**Domain 2: Asset Security**

This domain focuses on protecting information and assets throughout their lifecycle. The exam often tests who owns data, who handles it, and how classification drives protection.

- **Data ownership and stewardship** are distinct responsibilities.
  - The **data owner** is accountable for classification, access decisions, and protection requirements.
  - The **data custodian** implements and maintains controls, such as backups, permissions, and storage security.
  - The **data steward** manages data quality, definitions, and operational handling rules.
  - The **data user** consumes data according to policy and authorization.
- **Data lifecycle governance** covers creation, use, storage, sharing, archiving, and destruction.
  - You should classify data early and re-evaluate it when context changes.
  - Retention schedules should reflect legal, regulatory, and business needs.
  - Secure disposal must prevent recovery of sensitive information.
- **Data states** are commonly described as:
  - **At rest**: stored on disk, backup media, or archives.
  - **In transit**: moving across a network or between systems.
  - **In use**: actively processed in memory or by a running application.
- **Classification** drives handling.
  - Public, internal, confidential, and restricted are common labels.
  - Marking and labeling help users apply the right controls.
  - Need-to-know complements least privilege.
- **Ownership and accountability** matter more than technical custody.
  - The business owner decides the value and sensitivity of information.
  - IT may operate the system, but the business still owns the risk.
- **Media protection** includes encryption, access control, sanitization, and chain of custody.
  - Sanitization methods include clearing, purging, and destruction.
  - Degaussing applies to magnetic media, not solid-state drives.
  - SSDs often require cryptographic erase or physical destruction depending on policy and assurance needs.
- **Data remanence** is residual data left after deletion.
  - Secure wipe tools and approved destruction methods reduce recovery risk.
- **Privacy and data handling** require purpose limitation and retention discipline.
  - Minimize collection to what is necessary.
  - Restrict secondary use unless authorized.
- **Asset inventory** supports control and accountability.
  - You cannot protect what you do not know exists.
  - Inventory should include hardware, software, virtual assets, cloud resources, and data repositories.

**Domain 3: Security Architecture and Engineering**

This domain is broad and heavily tested. It covers security models, cryptography, physical security, hardware, and trusted computing concepts. Scenario questions often ask you to choose the control that best preserves confidentiality, integrity, and availability.

- **Security design principles** include:
  - Least privilege
  - Separation of duties
  - Defense in depth
  - Fail secure
  - Economy of mechanism
  - Open design
  - Complete mediation
  - Least common mechanism
  - Psychological acceptability
- **Security models** describe how access and information flow are controlled.
  - Bell-LaPadula focuses on confidentiality with “no read up, no write down.”
  - Biba focuses on integrity with “no read down, no write up.”
  - Clark-Wilson emphasizes well-formed transactions and separation of duties.
  - Brewer-Nash, also called the Chinese Wall model, prevents conflicts of interest.
- **Trusted computing base (TCB)** is the set of hardware, firmware, and software components that enforce security policy.
  - The smaller the TCB, the easier it is to trust and validate.
- **Cryptography basics** are high yield.
  - **Symmetric encryption** uses one key for encryption and decryption; it is fast and suited for bulk data.
  - **Asymmetric encryption** uses a public/private key pair; it is slower but useful for key exchange, digital signatures, and identity assurance.
  - **Hashing** produces a fixed-length digest and is one-way.
  - **MAC/HMAC** provides integrity and authenticity using a shared secret plus a hash function.
- **Common cryptographic use cases**
  - AES is a common symmetric cipher for data at rest and in transit.
  - RSA and ECC are common asymmetric algorithms.
  - TLS uses asymmetric cryptography for authentication and key exchange, then symmetric cryptography for session traffic.
  - Digital signatures provide integrity, authenticity, and nonrepudiation when properly implemented.
- **Digital certificates and PKI (Public Key Infrastructure)** are central to trust.
  - A certificate binds an identity to a public key.
  - A CA (Certificate Authority) signs certificates.
  - A CRL (Certificate Revocation List) and OCSP (Online Certificate Status Protocol) help check revocation status.
  - Certificate lifecycle includes enrollment, issuance, validation, renewal, and revocation.
- **Cryptographic attacks** are commonly tested.
  - Brute force tries all possible keys or passwords.
  - Dictionary attacks use likely words or password lists.
  - Rainbow tables attack unsalted hashes by using precomputed lookup tables.
  - Birthday attacks exploit collision probability in hash functions.
  - Replay attacks reuse captured authentication data.
- **Password storage** should use salted, slow hashes.
  - Use algorithms designed for passwords, such as bcrypt, scrypt, or Argon2, where appropriate.
  - Salting defeats precomputed attacks and identical-password matching.
- **Key management** is often more important than algorithm choice.
  - Protect keys at rest and in use.
  - Rotate keys according to policy and risk.
  - Use HSMs (Hardware Security Modules) for high-value key protection.
- **Physical security** is part of architecture, not an afterthought.
  - Fences, bollards, lighting, guards, locks, and badges create layered protection.
  - Mantraps reduce tailgating and piggybacking.
  - CCTV, alarms, and environmental monitoring support detection and response.
  - Fire suppression, HVAC, humidity control, and water detection protect availability.
- **Hardware and firmware security**
  - Secure boot verifies trusted code during startup.
  - TPM (Trusted Platform Module) can store keys and support attestation.
  - BIOS/UEFI protections help prevent unauthorized firmware changes.
  - Supply chain assurance reduces the risk of tampered components.
- **Virtualization and cloud security**
  - Hypervisors isolate guest systems.
  - Shared responsibility means the provider and customer each own different controls.
  - Misconfiguration is a major cloud risk.
  - Container security should include image trust, runtime restrictions, and minimal privileges.

**Domain 4: Communication and Network Security**

This domain covers network design, protocols, segmentation, secure communications, and wireless security. CISSP questions often ask you to choose the right layer, protocol, or architecture control.

- **OSI and TCP/IP models** help you place controls correctly.
  - Physical and data link layers handle media and switching.
  - Network layer handles routing and IP addressing.
  - Transport layer handles end-to-end sessions with TCP or UDP.
  - Application layer includes services such as DNS, HTTP, SMTP, and SSH.
- **Common ports and protocols** are worth memorizing.
  - `22` SSH
  - `23` Telnet
  - `25` SMTP
  - `53` DNS
  - `67/68` DHCP
  - `80` HTTP
  - `110` POP3
  - `123` NTP
  - `143` IMAP
  - `161/162` SNMP
  - `389` LDAP
  - `443` HTTPS
  - `445` SMB
  - `514` Syslog
  - `636` LDAPS
  - `3389` RDP
- **Network segmentation** limits blast radius.
  - VLANs separate broadcast domains.
  - ACLs (Access Control Lists) filter traffic.
  - Subnets support routing boundaries and policy enforcement.
  - DMZs isolate public-facing services from internal networks.
- **NAT (Network Address Translation)** hides internal addressing and conserves IPv4 space.
  - It is not a security control by itself, but it can reduce direct exposure.
- **Tunneling and secure transport**
  - VPNs protect traffic across untrusted networks.
  - IPsec can secure network-layer traffic.
  - TLS secures application sessions such as HTTPS.
  - SFTP is generally preferred over SCP for secure file transfer in modern environments.
- **Wireless security**
  - WPA2-Enterprise remains common in many organizations.
  - WPA3-Enterprise is the enterprise-grade WPA3 mode and improves protection over WPA2.
  - 802.1X provides port-based network access control.
  - EAP (Extensible Authentication Protocol) methods include EAP-TLS, PEAP, and EAP-TTLS.
  - EAP-TLS is strong because it uses client and server certificates.
- **Authentication infrastructure**
  - RADIUS centralizes authentication, authorization, and accounting.
  - In classic RADIUS, only the user password is protected in the base protocol; the rest of the packet is not fully encrypted.
  - TACACS+ separates authentication, authorization, and accounting more cleanly and is often used for device administration.
  - Kerberos uses tickets and symmetric cryptography for mutual authentication in domain environments.
- **Network security devices**
  - Firewalls enforce policy.
  - IDS (Intrusion Detection Systems) alert on suspicious activity.
  - IPS (Intrusion Prevention Systems) can block malicious traffic.
  - Proxies mediate requests and can improve filtering and logging.
  - WAFs (Web Application Firewalls) protect web applications from common attacks.
- **DNS and email security**
  - DNSSEC adds integrity to DNS responses.
  - SPF, DKIM, and DMARC help reduce email spoofing and phishing.
- **Network management**
  - SNMPv3 is preferred because it adds authentication and encryption.
  - Secure logging and time synchronization support incident response and forensics.

**Domain 5: Identity and Access Management**

This domain is about proving identity, granting access, and managing the full access lifecycle. Exam questions often hinge on authentication versus authorization versus accounting.

- **Core IAM concepts**
  - **Identification** is claiming an identity.
  - **Authentication** is proving that identity.
  - **Authorization** is determining what the identity can do.
  - **Accounting** is recording actions for audit and traceability.
- **Access control models**
  - DAC (Discretionary Access Control) lets the owner decide access.
  - MAC (Mandatory Access Control) uses labels and centralized policy.
  - RBAC (Role-Based Access Control) assigns permissions by job role.
  - ABAC (Attribute-Based Access Control) evaluates attributes such as user, resource, time, and location.
- **Account types**
  - User accounts are tied to individuals.
  - Service accounts support applications and services.
  - Privileged accounts have elevated rights and require tighter controls.
  - Shared accounts reduce accountability and should be avoided where possible.
- **Authentication factors**
  - Something you know: password, PIN, passphrase.
  - Something you have: token, smart card, phone-based authenticator.
  - Something you are: biometrics.
  - Somewhere you are: location-based context.
  - Something you do: behavioral biometrics.
- **MFA (Multi-Factor Authentication)** combines two or more different factor types.
  - Strong MFA reduces the risk of credential theft.
  - Biometrics should be paired with another factor because they are not secrets.
- **Password policy**
  - Favor long passphrases over short complex passwords when appropriate.
  - Use rate limiting, lockout controls, and breached-password checks.
  - Avoid overreliance on forced periodic changes without evidence of compromise.
- **Session management**
  - Sessions should expire after inactivity and on logout.
  - Session identifiers must be unpredictable and protected in transit and at rest where applicable.
  - Reauthentication is appropriate for sensitive actions.
- **Federation and single sign-on**
  - SSO (Single Sign-On) reduces repeated logins and improves usability.
  - SAML is common for enterprise web federation.
  - OAuth 2.0 is an authorization framework, not an authentication protocol by itself.
  - OpenID Connect adds authentication on top of OAuth 2.0.
- **Provisioning and deprovisioning**
  - Joiner, mover, leaver processes should be timely and auditable.
  - Access reviews help remove stale permissions.
  - Privileged access should be approved, monitored, and time-bound.
- **Directory services**
  - LDAP is a common directory access protocol.
  - Centralized directories simplify authentication and authorization decisions.
- **Physical access control**
  - Badges, biometrics, guards, and mantraps can be combined.
  - Tailgating and piggybacking remain common weaknesses.

**Domain 6: Security Assessment and Testing**

This domain focuses on how you verify that controls work. The exam often distinguishes between vulnerability scanning, penetration testing, audits, and control testing.

- **Assessment types**
  - Vulnerability scans identify known weaknesses.
  - Penetration tests attempt to exploit weaknesses to prove impact.
  - Security audits evaluate compliance with policy, standards, or regulations.
  - Log reviews and monitoring validate operational effectiveness.
- **Test methodologies**
  - Black-box testing uses little or no prior knowledge.
  - White-box testing uses full knowledge.
  - Gray-box testing uses partial knowledge.
  - Manual testing can uncover logic flaws that automated tools miss.
- **Penetration testing governance**
  - Rules of engagement define scope, timing, targets, constraints, and escalation paths.
  - Authorization must be explicit before testing begins.
  - Safety controls prevent unnecessary disruption to production systems.
- **Scan limitations**
  - Scanners can miss authenticated issues, business logic flaws, and chained vulnerabilities.
  - False positives and false negatives are common.
  - Network reachability and credential quality affect scan results.
- **Evidence and audit artifacts**
  - Logs, screenshots, packet captures, configuration exports, and ticket records can support findings.
  - Chain of custody matters when evidence may be used in legal or disciplinary action.
- **Metrics and reporting**
  - Findings should be prioritized by risk, not just severity score.
  - Trend reporting helps leadership see whether controls are improving.
- **Control testing**
  - Preventive controls stop events before they occur.
  - Detective controls identify events after they occur.
  - Corrective controls reduce impact and restore normal operations.
- **Continuous monitoring**
  - SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) platforms correlate logs and alerts.
  - EDR (Endpoint Detection and Response) improves endpoint visibility and response.
  - Baselines help identify abnormal behavior.

**Domain 7: Security Operations**

This domain covers day-to-day security work: incident response, logging, patching, change management, backups, media handling, and operational resilience.

- **Change management** reduces unintended outages and security regressions.
  - Changes should be requested, reviewed, approved, tested, and documented.
  - Emergency changes still need post-implementation review.
  - Configuration management helps maintain approved system states.
- **Patch management** is a lifecycle.
  - Inventory assets.
  - Assess vulnerability and exposure.
  - Test patches in a controlled environment.
  - Deploy based on risk and criticality.
  - Verify installation and monitor for issues.
- **Asset and media handling**
  - Track ownership, location, and status.
  - Protect removable media from loss and unauthorized copying.
  - Encrypt portable devices and backups when appropriate.
  - Use approved destruction methods for retired media.
- **Backups**
  - Full, incremental, and differential backups support different recovery objectives.
  - Follow the 3-2-1 principle: three copies, two media types, one offsite or isolated copy.
  - Test restores regularly; a backup is only useful if it can be restored.
- **Logging and monitoring**
  - Logs should be time-synchronized and protected from tampering.
  - Centralized logging improves correlation and retention.
  - Retention periods should satisfy legal, operational, and investigative needs.
- **Incident response**
  - Prepare, detect, contain, eradicate, recover, and learn.
  - Preserve evidence before making changes when possible.
  - Document actions, timestamps, and chain of custody.
  - Containment may be short-term or long-term depending on business impact.
- **Forensics basics**
  - Order of volatility matters: volatile data should be collected first when appropriate.
  - RAM, running processes, and network connections are more volatile than disk data.
  - Write blockers help preserve evidence integrity.
- **Malware response**
  - Isolate affected systems.
  - Identify persistence mechanisms.
  - Remove malicious artifacts.
  - Reimage when trust cannot be restored.
- **Operational resilience**
  - Redundancy, failover, clustering, and load balancing improve availability.
  - Single points of failure should be identified and reduced.
- **Media sanitization and disposal**
  - Follow policy and classification requirements.
  - Verify destruction or sanitization with records when needed.

**Domain 8: Software Development Security**

This domain covers secure development, application security, DevSecOps, and supply chain risk. The exam often asks where security fits into the SDLC (Software Development Life Cycle) and how to reduce application risk early.

- **SDLC integration**
  - Security should start in requirements and continue through design, implementation, testing, deployment, and maintenance.
  - Finding flaws earlier is cheaper than fixing them after release.
- **Process models**
  - Waterfall is sequential and can make late security changes expensive.
  - Agile delivers in iterations and supports continuous feedback.
  - DevSecOps integrates security into automated pipelines and shared responsibility.
- **Secure coding**
  - Validate input and encode output.
  - Use parameterized queries to reduce SQL injection risk.
  - Handle errors safely without exposing sensitive details.
  - Apply least privilege to application accounts and services.
- **Common application risks**
  - Injection flaws
  - Cross-site scripting (XSS)
  - Cross-site request forgery (CSRF)
  - Broken authentication
  - Insecure deserialization
  - Improper access control
  - Security misconfiguration
- **Testing methods**
  - SAST (Static Application Security Testing) analyzes code without running it.
  - DAST (Dynamic Application Security Testing) tests a running application.
  - IAST (Interactive Application Security Testing) combines runtime and code awareness.
  - Fuzzing sends malformed inputs to uncover crashes and logic errors.
- **Supply chain security**
  - Verify code signing and package integrity.
  - Control third-party dependencies and update sources.
  - Review build pipelines and artifact repositories.
  - Protect secrets used in CI/CD (Continuous Integration/Continuous Delivery) systems.
- **API security**
  - Use strong authentication and authorization.
  - Apply rate limiting and input validation.
  - Prefer short-lived access tokens with appropriate refresh mechanisms.
  - Log sensitive actions without exposing secrets.
- **Container and orchestration security**
  - Use minimal base images.
  - Drop unnecessary Linux capabilities.
  - Run as non-root where possible.
  - Restrict privilege escalation and writable filesystem access.
  - Apply network policies and image scanning.
- **Example hardening snippet**
  - This is a partial baseline, not a complete container security standard.

```yaml
apiVersion: v1
kind: Pod
metadata:
  name: secure-app
spec:
  securityContext:
    runAsNonRoot: true
    seccompProfile:
      type: RuntimeDefault
  containers:
    - name: app
      image: example/app:1.0
      securityContext:
        allowPrivilegeEscalation: false
        readOnlyRootFilesystem: true
        capabilities:
          drop:
            - ALL
```

**High-yield comparisons**

| Topic | Key distinction | Exam focus |
|---|---|---|
| Authentication vs authorization | Identity proof vs permission decision | Know the order and purpose |
| Hash vs MAC/HMAC | One-way digest vs integrity with shared secret | Integrity and authenticity |
| SAML vs OAuth 2.0 vs OIDC | Federation vs authorization vs authentication layer | Pick the right protocol |
| IDS vs IPS | Alerting vs blocking | Detection versus prevention |
| RTO vs RPO | Time to restore vs acceptable data loss | Recovery planning |
| BCP vs DR | Business continuity vs IT restoration | Scope and objective |
| SAST vs DAST | Code analysis vs running app testing | When each is used |

**Practical command examples**

Use commands as operational references, but remember CISSP usually tests concepts and decision-making rather than syntax.

```bash
# Inspect listening ports and services
ss -tulpn

# Review firewall rules on Linux
sudo nft list ruleset

# Check certificate details
openssl x509 -in server.crt -text -noout

# Verify a file hash
sha256sum release.tar.gz

# Inspect DNS records
dig example.com any

# Test TLS connectivity
openssl s_client -connect example.com:443 -servername example.com
```

**Exam strategy reminders**

CISSP questions often contain multiple technically correct answers. Choose the answer that best reflects governance, risk reduction, least privilege, or business continuity. If a question asks for the “best” control, prefer preventive and policy-aligned solutions before detective or corrective ones, unless the scenario clearly requires response or recovery.

- If the question is about leadership, think **governance**.
- If the question is about protecting data, think **classification and lifecycle**.
- If the question is about access, think **least privilege and accountability**.
- If the question is about architecture, think **defense in depth**.
- If the question is about operations, think **change control, logging, and recovery**.
- If the question is about development, think **secure by design and shift left**.

**Quick memory anchors**

- **CIA triad**: confidentiality, integrity, availability.
- **AAA**: authentication, authorization, accounting.
- **BIA**: business impact analysis.
- **RTO/RPO**: restore time and data loss tolerance.
- **SALT**: protects password hashes from precomputation.
- **MFA**: multiple factor types, not multiple passwords.
- **Defense in depth**: layered controls, not a single product.
