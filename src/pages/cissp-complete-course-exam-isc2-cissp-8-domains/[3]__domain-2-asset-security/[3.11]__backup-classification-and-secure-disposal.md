## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Backup classification overview**

Backup classification helps you decide what to protect, how often to protect it, where to store it, and how long to keep it. In CISSP terms, this is part of the broader information lifecycle: you classify data, apply handling requirements, back it up according to business need, and dispose of it securely when it is no longer required.

The main exam idea is that backup controls are not just about availability. They also support integrity, confidentiality, legal hold, and recovery objectives. A backup that cannot be restored, is retained too long, or is destroyed incorrectly can create security, compliance, and operational risk.

- **Full backup** copies all selected data each time it runs.
- **Incremental backup** copies only data changed since the last backup of any type.
- **Differential backup** copies data changed since the last full backup.
- **Snapshot** captures a point-in-time view, often at the storage or VM layer, but is not always a true backup by itself.
- **Archive** preserves data for long-term retention, usually for compliance or historical reference, and is not the same as a recovery backup.

A practical way to think about classification is by business value and recovery need:
- Critical systems may need frequent backups, short recovery point objective (<abbr title="Recovery Point Objective">RPO</abbr>), and tested restores.
- Regulated records may need long retention, immutability, and legal hold support.
- Low-value or transient data may need minimal backup coverage or may be excluded by policy.

**Backup types and when to use them**

Different backup types trade off speed, storage, and restore complexity. CISSP questions often test whether you understand the operational consequences of each type.

| Type | What it captures | Strengths | Weaknesses | Typical use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Full | Entire dataset | Simplest restore, easiest verification | Slowest, highest storage use | Weekly baseline, small systems |
| Incremental | Changes since last backup | Fastest backup, least storage | Restore requires chain of backups | Daily protection with limited windows |
| Differential | Changes since last full backup | Faster restore than incremental | Grows until next full backup | Balanced approach for many systems |
| Snapshot | Point-in-time state | Very fast, low disruption | May depend on source storage; not always off-host | VM rollback, storage recovery |
| Archive | Long-term preserved copy | Supports retention and compliance | Not optimized for rapid restore | Records management, legal retention |

A common backup schedule is a weekly full backup with daily incrementals. That reduces backup time, but restore time increases because you must apply the full backup plus each incremental in sequence. Differential backups reduce restore complexity because you only need the last full backup and the latest differential.

**Backup classification by sensitivity and handling**

Backups inherit the sensitivity of the source data. If the source contains confidential, regulated, or restricted information, the backup must be protected to the same standard or higher. This includes encryption, access control, logging, and secure transport.

- **Public data** may still need backup for availability, but confidentiality controls are lighter.
- **Internal data** usually needs standard access restrictions and integrity protection.
- **Confidential data** should be encrypted in transit and at rest, with strict access control.
- **Restricted or regulated data** may require stronger segregation, immutable storage, legal hold, and documented retention.

Backups often become a hidden concentration of risk because they aggregate large volumes of sensitive data. That makes them attractive targets for ransomware and insider misuse. For that reason, backup repositories should be isolated from production networks where possible and protected with separate administrative credentials.

**Retention policy versus legal retention requirements**

Retention is a frequent exam distinction. A **backup retention policy** defines how long backups are kept for operational recovery. A **data retention requirement** defines how long records must be kept for business, regulatory, contractual, or legal reasons.

- **Backup retention policy**
  - Focuses on recovery windows and storage management.
  - Defines backup frequency, rotation, version count, and expiration.
  - Is usually enforced by backup software or storage lifecycle rules.
- **Legal or regulatory retention**
  - Focuses on records preservation and admissibility.
  - May require specific data to be retained for years.
  - Can override normal deletion schedules through legal hold.

This distinction matters because a backup can expire operationally while the underlying record must still be preserved. In that case, you need an archive or records management process, not just a backup job. If litigation or investigation is pending, a **legal hold** suspends deletion even if the normal retention schedule says the data should be removed.

A good retention program defines:
- The data class and owner.
- The retention period.
- The trigger for retention start, such as creation date, last modification, or contract end.
- The disposal method at end of life.
- Exceptions such as legal hold, audit hold, or regulatory freeze.

**Retention schedules and enforcement**

Retention schedules are the operational rules that turn policy into action. They are usually enforced by backup software, storage lifecycle policies, records management systems, or a combination of all three.

Common enforcement mechanisms include:
- Expiration dates on backup sets.
- Version limits, such as keeping the last 30 daily backups.
- Object storage lifecycle transitions from hot to cold tiers.
- Immutability windows that prevent deletion before a minimum age.
- Legal hold flags that block deletion regardless of age.

Example lifecycle policy for object storage:
```json
{
  "Rules": [
    {
      "ID": "backup-retention",
      "Status": "Enabled",
      "Filter": { "Prefix": "backups/" },
      "Transitions": [
        { "Days": 30, "StorageClass": "STANDARD_IA" },
        { "Days": 90, "StorageClass": "GLACIER" }
      ],
      "Expiration": { "Days": 365 }
    }
  ]
}
```

Example with AWS CLI to enable versioning and object lock on a bucket used for backups:
```bash
aws s3api put-bucket-versioning \
  --bucket corp-backups \
  --versioning-configuration Status=Enabled

aws s3api put-object-lock-configuration \
  --bucket corp-backups \
  --object-lock-configuration '{
    "ObjectLockEnabled": "Enabled",
    "Rule": {
      "DefaultRetention": {
        "Mode": "GOVERNANCE",
        "Days": 30
      }
    }
  }'
```

Versioning helps preserve older copies, while object lock adds immutability. For ransomware resilience, immutable backups are often more valuable than simple offline copies because they resist deletion and tampering.

**Backup validation and testing**

A backup is only useful if you can restore it. CISSP exam questions often expect you to verify backups rather than assume success from a completed job status.

Validation methods include:
- **Checksum or hash validation** to confirm the backup copy matches the source or expected digest.
- **Test restores** to a non-production environment to confirm recoverability.
- **Integrity checks** to detect corruption in backup sets, catalogs, or media.
- **Application-consistent recovery tests** for databases and transactional systems.
- **Chain verification** for incremental backups to ensure all dependent restore points are intact.

A backup job that reports success may still be unusable if:
- The encryption key is unavailable.
- The backup catalog is corrupted.
- An incremental chain is broken.
- The restore target lacks compatible software or drivers.
- The backup contains silent corruption.

Practical validation workflow:
1. Restore a representative sample to an isolated test system.
2. Verify file counts, timestamps, and application startup.
3. Compare hashes for critical files where feasible.
4. Confirm database consistency with native tools.
5. Document restore time and issues for recovery planning.

Example Linux hash verification:
```bash
sha256sum important.tar.gz > important.tar.gz.sha256
sha256sum -c important.tar.gz.sha256
```

Example database-oriented validation concept:
```bash
# Example only: verify a PostgreSQL backup by restoring to a test instance
pg_restore -d test_restore /backups/prod_dump.backup
psql -d test_restore -c "SELECT count(*) FROM critical_table;"
```

For cloud object storage, you can also use ETag or checksum features where supported, but be careful: ETag is not always a simple MD5 hash, especially for multipart uploads. Use the platform’s documented checksum mechanism when available.

**Backup architecture and practical controls**

Backups should be designed for both recovery and resistance to attack. A common secure pattern is the 3-2-1 approach:
- Keep at least three copies of data.
- Store copies on two different media or platforms.
- Keep one copy offsite or offline.

A stronger modern variant is 3-2-1-1-0:
- Three copies total.
- Two different media types.
- One offsite copy.
- One offline or immutable copy.
- Zero known verification errors after testing.

Practical controls include:
- Separate backup credentials from domain admin credentials.
- Restrict backup console access with least privilege.
- Encrypt backup data in transit and at rest.
- Store encryption keys separately from the backup media.
- Monitor backup jobs and restore events.
- Use immutable storage or write-once media for critical backups.

Example `restic` backup with encryption and retention:
```bash
export RESTIC_REPOSITORY=s3:s3.amazonaws.com/corp-backups/restic
export RESTIC_PASSWORD='use-a-strong-passphrase'

restic backup /srv/data
restic forget --keep-daily 7 --keep-weekly 4 --keep-monthly 12 --prune
restic check
```

Example `borg` retention policy:
```bash
borg create --stats /backup::'{hostname}-{now:%Y-%m-%d}' /home /etc
borg prune -v --list /backup \
  --keep-daily=7 \
  --keep-weekly=4 \
  --keep-monthly=12
borg check /backup
```

These tools illustrate the exam concept: retention is not just a policy document. It is enforced by software behavior, storage controls, and operational review.

**Encryption, key management, and backup disposal**

Backup encryption protects confidentiality, but it also creates a key management dependency. If you dispose of media without handling keys correctly, you may leave recoverable data behind. If you destroy keys too early, you may lose the ability to restore data that still must be retained.

Key lifecycle considerations:
- **Key generation** should use approved cryptographic standards and strong entropy.
- **Key storage** should use secure key management, such as a KMS (Key Management Service), HSM (Hardware Security Module), or controlled escrow process.
- **Key rotation** should follow policy and reduce exposure if a key is compromised.
- **Key escrow** may be required for business continuity or legal recovery, but it increases access risk and must be tightly controlled.
- **Key revocation or destruction** should align with the end of the data retention period and disposal plan.

Cryptographic erasure is appropriate when:
- Data is encrypted with strong algorithms.
- The encryption keys are destroyed or rendered unrecoverable.
- You no longer need to restore the data.

Cryptographic erasure is insufficient when:
- The data must remain recoverable for legal or regulatory retention.
- Keys are still accessible in escrow or backups of the key store.
- The media contains unencrypted copies, metadata, or residual data outside the encrypted container.

> If the backup media is encrypted but the keys remain available, the data is not effectively disposed of.

**Secure disposal and media sanitization**

Secure disposal means removing data so it cannot be recovered by unauthorized parties. In CISSP contexts, this is often tied to media sanitization guidance such as the concepts in NIST SP 800-88: **clear**, **purge**, and **destroy**.

- **Clear**
  - Uses logical techniques to protect against simple recovery.
  - Examples include overwrite or reset functions.
  - Suitable when the media stays within controlled environments and the threat is low.
- **Purge**
  - Makes recovery infeasible using advanced laboratory methods.
  - Examples include cryptographic erase, degaussing for magnetic media, or secure erase commands.
  - Appropriate for higher sensitivity or media reuse.
- **Destroy**
  - Physically renders media unusable.
  - Examples include shredding, pulverizing, incineration, or melting.
  - Used when reuse is not required or when the highest assurance is needed.

Media-specific considerations:
- **HDDs** may be degaussed or physically destroyed, but degaussing can make the drive unusable.
- **SSDs** and flash media do not respond well to traditional overwrite assumptions because of wear leveling and hidden blocks; use vendor secure erase, cryptographic erase, or destruction.
- **Tape** often requires degaussing or shredding, depending on sensitivity and reuse requirements.
- **Optical media** usually requires physical destruction for high assurance.
- **Cloud backups** require provider-side deletion, lifecycle expiration, and key destruction where applicable.

A secure disposal workflow often includes:
1. Identify the media and data classification.
2. Confirm retention and legal hold status.
3. Choose clear, purge, or destroy based on sensitivity and reuse needs.
4. Execute the approved sanitization method.
5. Verify completion and document chain of custody.
6. Update asset inventory and disposal records.

**When to use cryptographic erasure versus physical destruction**

Cryptographic erasure is efficient when the data is already strongly encrypted and the keys are under your control. It is especially useful for cloud storage, virtual disks, and encrypted backup repositories.

Use cryptographic erasure when:
- You need fast disposal at scale.
- The media is encrypted end-to-end.
- You can reliably destroy the relevant keys.
- The organization accepts the residual risk of encrypted remnants.

Use physical destruction when:
- The media is damaged or cannot be securely erased.
- The data is extremely sensitive.
- You cannot prove encryption coverage.
- The media is leaving a high-security environment.
- Regulatory or contractual requirements demand destruction.

A common exam trap is assuming that deleting a file or formatting a disk is secure disposal. It is not. Those actions usually remove pointers, not the underlying data.

**Backup disposal and cloud object storage**

Cloud backup systems often combine versioning, lifecycle rules, immutability, and key management. Disposal must account for all of them.

Important cloud concepts:
- Deleting the current object may leave older versions intact.
- Object lock or retention mode may prevent deletion until the retention period ends.
- Replication may create copies in another region or account.
- Snapshots and backups may be retained by the provider even after the source is deleted, depending on policy.
- Customer-managed keys can enable cryptographic erasure if the key is destroyed.

Example S3-style lifecycle with version expiration:
```json
{
  "Rules": [
    {
      "ID": "expire-old-versions",
      "Status": "Enabled",
      "Filter": { "Prefix": "backups/" },
      "NoncurrentVersionExpiration": { "NoncurrentDays": 30 },
      "Expiration": { "Days": 365 }
    }
  ]
}
```

Example conceptual Azure Blob immutability policy:
```bash
az storage container immutability-policy create \
  --account-name corpstorage \
  --container-name backups \
  --period 30
```

The exam-relevant point is that disposal in cloud environments is often a combination of:
- Lifecycle expiration.
- Version cleanup.
- Replication deletion.
- Key destruction.
- Audit confirmation.

**Operational examples and exam cues**

Backup classification and disposal questions often ask you to choose the best control for a scenario. Look for the business objective first.

- If the goal is fast recovery, choose a backup design that minimizes restore complexity.
- If the goal is ransomware resistance, choose immutable or offline copies.
- If the goal is compliance retention, use archive and records management, not just backup rotation.
- If the goal is secure disposal, use approved sanitization and key destruction, not simple deletion.

Common exam cues:
- **RPO** tells you how much data loss is acceptable.
- **RTO** (<abbr title="Recovery Time Objective">RTO</abbr>) tells you how quickly you must restore service.
- **Legal hold** overrides normal deletion.
- **Immutability** protects against tampering and ransomware.
- **Checksum validation** proves integrity.
- **Test restore** proves recoverability.
- **NIST SP 800-88 concepts** guide media sanitization decisions.

**Quick comparison for decision-making**

| Requirement | Best-fit control |
|---|---|
| Fast operational recovery | Full plus incremental or differential backups |
| Strong ransomware resistance | Immutable backup copy, offline copy, separate credentials |
| Long-term regulatory retention | Archive with retention schedule and legal hold support |
| Proof of backup integrity | Hash validation and test restores |
| Secure media retirement | Purge or destroy based on sensitivity and reuse needs |
| Fast disposal of encrypted data | Cryptographic erasure with verified key destruction |

**Key takeaways**

Backup classification is about matching data value, recovery needs, and retention obligations to the right backup method. Secure disposal is about ensuring data cannot be recovered after its retention period ends, using the right sanitization method for the media and the right key handling for encrypted backups.

The strongest CISSP answer usually combines:
- Clear classification.
- Defined retention schedules.
- Verified restore testing.
- Separate legal and operational retention logic.
- Approved sanitization or destruction.
- Documented key lifecycle management.
