## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Core concepts**

Data governance separates **accountability**, **decision authority**, and **operational handling**. CISSP questions often test whether you can identify who sets policy, who approves classification, who maintains the data, and who physically or logically protects it.

- **Data owner**: accountable for the data and its protection requirements.
- **Data steward**: manages data quality and meaning on behalf of the owner.
- **Data custodian**: implements and operates the technical controls that store, process, and protect the data.

A useful exam shortcut is to map the roles to decisions:

- The **owner** decides what the data is, how sensitive it is, and who may access it.
- The **steward** ensures the data remains accurate, consistent, and usable.
- The **custodian** enforces the controls and executes the owner’s requirements.

**Data ownership and accountability**

The data owner is usually a business leader, process owner, or executive with authority over the information asset. Ownership is about **accountability**, not necessarily day-to-day handling. The owner establishes the classification criteria, approves access rules, and accepts the risk of how the data is used.

Ownership is often confused with system ownership or business ownership:

- **Data owner**: accountable for the information itself.
- **System owner**: accountable for the application, platform, or infrastructure that hosts or processes the data.
- **Business owner**: accountable for the business process or service that uses the data.

These roles can overlap in small organizations, but on the CISSP exam they are distinct. A system owner may administer a database, but that does not make them the data owner. A business owner may sponsor a service, but the data owner still decides classification and access policy for the information.

The owner can delegate authority, but not accountability. For example:

- The owner may delegate access approval to a manager for low-risk records.
- The owner may delegate classification review to a steward.
- The owner still remains accountable if the delegated decision is wrong.

> **Exam nuance:** delegation transfers decision-making authority within limits, but the owner retains ultimate accountability.

**Classification ownership responsibilities**

Classification is not a one-time label. The owner establishes the classification scheme and criteria, then reclassifies data when business context changes. This matters when data becomes more sensitive, less sensitive, or is combined with other datasets.

Typical owner responsibilities include:

- Defining classification levels such as public, internal, confidential, and restricted.
- Setting handling requirements for each class.
- Approving exceptions and compensating controls.
- Reclassifying data after mergers, regulatory changes, product launches, or new analytics use cases.

A practical example is customer data:

- A marketing list may be **internal** when it contains only names and business emails.
- The same list may become **confidential** if it includes purchase history.
- It may become **restricted** if it includes payment data, health data, or government identifiers.

The owner should also define who can trigger reclassification. In mature governance programs, a steward may recommend a change, but the owner approves it.

**Data lifecycle responsibilities**

Data governance applies across the full lifecycle: creation, use, storage, archival, and destruction. CISSP questions often hide the correct answer in the lifecycle stage.

- **Creation**
  - The owner defines what data may be collected and why.
  - The steward ensures required fields, naming conventions, and metadata standards.
  - The custodian configures capture systems, validation rules, and secure intake channels.

- **Use**
  - The owner approves access and acceptable use.
  - The steward monitors quality, consistency, and business meaning.
  - The custodian enforces access controls, logging, and session protections.

- **Storage**
  - The owner determines retention and protection requirements.
  - The steward ensures metadata, lineage, and master records remain accurate.
  - The custodian applies encryption, backups, redundancy, and physical or cloud safeguards.

- **Archival**
  - The owner decides when data moves to long-term retention.
  - The steward preserves context so archived data remains interpretable.
  - The custodian manages archive media, retrieval controls, and retention enforcement.

- **Destruction**
  - The owner authorizes destruction based on policy, law, and business need.
  - The steward confirms records are no longer needed for operational or analytical use.
  - The custodian performs secure deletion, media sanitization, or destruction verification.

A simple lifecycle workflow might look like this:

```text
Create -> Classify -> Use -> Retain -> Archive -> Dispose
   |         |         |        |         |         |
 owner     owner     owner   owner/steward  owner   owner
 steward   steward   steward    steward     steward  steward
 custodian custodian custodian  custodian   custodian custodian
```

**Stewardship responsibilities**

Stewardship focuses on the meaning, quality, and consistency of data. A steward is not usually the final authority, but the steward is essential for operational governance.

Common stewardship activities include:

- Maintaining the **data dictionary** so terms have consistent definitions.
- Managing **metadata** such as source, owner, sensitivity, retention, and usage constraints.
- Tracking **data lineage** so you can trace where data came from and how it changed.
- Supporting **master data management (MDM)** to keep core entities like customer, product, and supplier records consistent.
- Resolving data quality issues such as duplicates, missing values, and conflicting formats.
- Coordinating business rules for naming, validation, and reference data.

Example stewardship tasks in practice:

- A steward updates the data dictionary when a new field is added to a customer profile.
- A steward traces a reporting error back to a broken ETL (extract, transform, load) mapping.
- A steward reconciles duplicate customer records across CRM and billing systems.
- A steward documents lineage for a regulatory report so auditors can verify the source.

Stewardship is especially important when multiple teams use the same data differently. Without stewardship, the organization may have inconsistent definitions for “active customer,” “incident,” or “account balance.”

**Custodianship responsibilities**

Custodianship is operational and technical. The custodian implements the controls that the owner requires and the steward helps define.

Typical custodian responsibilities include:

- Provisioning storage, databases, and backup systems.
- Enforcing access control lists, roles, and permissions.
- Applying encryption at rest and in transit.
- Monitoring logs, alerts, and integrity checks.
- Performing backups, restores, patching, and media sanitization.
- Supporting availability, resilience, and disaster recovery.

Custodians are often IT administrators, cloud operators, managed service providers, or platform teams. They do not decide the classification of the data, but they must understand it to apply the right controls.

Example command-line tasks a custodian might perform:

```bash
# Verify file permissions on a sensitive export
ls -l /srv/data/customer_export.csv

# Restrict access to the file
chmod 640 /srv/data/customer_export.csv
chown appsvc:datateam /srv/data/customer_export.csv

# Check encryption status on a Linux volume
lsblk -f
cryptsetup status securedata
```

In a database environment, custodial controls may include role-based access and auditing:

```sql
-- Example: grant read-only access to a reporting role
GRANT SELECT ON sales.customer_profile TO reporting_role;

-- Example: revoke broad access
REVOKE ALL PRIVILEGES ON sales.customer_profile FROM temp_user;
```

**Legal and regulatory accountability**

The organization may not always be the legal owner of the data, but it can still have custodial duties and governance obligations. This is common with outsourced processing, cloud services, and regulated data sharing.

Key distinctions:

- **Legal accountability** may rest with the controller, processor, or regulated entity depending on the jurisdiction and contract.
- **Internal governance accountability** still requires the organization to classify, protect, and manage the data properly.
- **Custodial duty** means the organization must safeguard data it holds even if it does not own it outright.

Examples:

- A payroll provider processes employee data for a client. The client may remain the data owner, while the provider acts as custodian or processor.
- A cloud provider stores encrypted backups. The customer may own the data and the provider may have operational custody.
- A hospital may share records with a third-party billing vendor. The hospital still has governance obligations for patient data, and the vendor must follow contractual and regulatory controls.

> **Critical warning:** do not assume “not the legal owner” means “not responsible.” Custodial and contractual obligations still apply.

**Third-party vendors and outsourcing**

When data is handled by vendors, the roles and boundaries must be explicit in contracts and governance documents. CISSP exam questions often test whether you know that outsourcing does not outsource accountability.

Important artifacts include:

- **SLA**: service level agreement, which defines availability, response times, and support expectations.
- **DPA**: data processing agreement, which defines how a processor may handle personal or sensitive data.
- **Security addendum**: contract language for encryption, logging, breach notification, and audit rights.
- **Shared responsibility matrix**: clarifies which party secures which layer.

A vendor relationship should specify:

- Who classifies the data.
- Who may approve access.
- Who performs backups and restores.
- Who notifies whom after an incident.
- Who destroys data at contract end.
- Who audits the vendor and how often.

Example shared responsibility snippet:

| Activity | Customer | Vendor |
|---|---:|---:|
| Data classification | R/A | C |
| Access approval | R/A | C |
| Encryption configuration | A | R |
| Backup execution | C | R/A |
| Retention policy | R/A | C |
| Secure destruction | A | R |

Legend: **R** = Responsible, **A** = Accountable, **C** = Consulted.

**Governance artifacts**

Governance becomes easier when you document decisions in repeatable artifacts. These are common in mature programs and useful for exam scenarios.

A simple RACI matrix can clarify role boundaries:

| Task | Data Owner | Data Steward | Data Custodian | System Owner |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Define classification criteria | A | C | I | I |
| Label new dataset | A | R | I | I |
| Maintain data dictionary | C | A/R | I | I |
| Implement encryption | I | C | R/A | R |
| Approve access exception | A | C | I | I |
| Restore from backup | I | I | R/A | R |

A policy workflow template might look like this:

```text
1. Business requests new data use.
2. Steward validates definitions and metadata.
3. Owner classifies data and approves access rules.
4. Custodian implements technical controls.
5. Security reviews logging, retention, and monitoring.
6. Changes are recorded in the governance register.
```

An approval matrix can also help:

```text
Low-risk internal data:
- Steward may recommend
- Manager may approve
- Owner receives notification

Confidential or regulated data:
- Owner must approve
- Security must review
- Legal or privacy must review if required
```

**Exam-oriented distinctions**

CISSP questions often use similar-sounding roles. Focus on the decision being made.

- If the question asks who decides the classification, choose the **data owner**.
- If the question asks who maintains definitions, lineage, or quality, choose the **data steward**.
- If the question asks who configures controls, backups, or permissions, choose the **data custodian**.
- If the question asks who owns the server or application, that is usually the **system owner**.
- If the question asks who owns the business process, that is usually the **business owner**.

Common traps:

- A database administrator may be a custodian, not the owner.
- A department head may sponsor the system, but still not own the data.
- A vendor may process the data, but the client may retain ownership and accountability.
- A steward may recommend reclassification, but the owner approves it.

**Practical scenario examples**

A healthcare organization stores patient records in a cloud platform:

- The hospital is the data owner for classification and access policy.
- The clinical data steward maintains terminology, metadata, and lineage.
- The cloud provider is the custodian for infrastructure controls.
- The DPA and SLA define breach notification, retention, and support obligations.

A retail company outsources analytics to a third party:

- The business owner wants sales insights.
- The data owner classifies customer purchase data as confidential.
- The steward ensures the analytics dataset uses approved definitions.
- The vendor may process the data, but only within the contract and approved scope.

A merger introduces duplicate customer records:

- The steward identifies conflicting master records.
- The owner decides which source system is authoritative.
- The custodian updates access, storage, and synchronization jobs.
- The MDM process prevents the issue from recurring.

**Key takeaways**

- **Ownership** is accountability for the data itself.
- **Stewardship** is governance of meaning, quality, and lineage.
- **Custodianship** is technical operation and protection.
- The owner sets classification criteria and retains accountability even when delegating authority.
- Data governance applies across the full lifecycle, not just during storage.
- Vendor contracts must define shared responsibility, especially for regulated or outsourced data.
