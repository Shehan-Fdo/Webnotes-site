## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Media management overview**

Media management covers the lifecycle of information-bearing assets: creation, use, transport, storage, retention, sanitization, reuse, and disposal. In CISSP scenarios, you are usually deciding how to protect confidentiality, integrity, and availability while preserving legal admissibility when evidence is involved.

The key exam distinction is that normal operational media handling focuses on asset control and business continuity, while evidence handling focuses on preserving authenticity and proving who had control at every step. A drive can be both an operational backup and potential evidence, but the handling rules change once legal or investigative requirements apply.

- **Media** includes hard drives, SSDs, tapes, optical media, USB devices, mobile devices, memory cards, printed records, and cloud-hosted storage artifacts.
- **Retention** means keeping information for a defined period based on legal, regulatory, contractual, or business requirements.
- **Disposition** means the approved end-of-life action, such as reuse, destruction, archival transfer, or secure deletion.

**Media classification and handling goals**

You should classify media by sensitivity and business purpose before deciding how to store or dispose of it. Classification drives the required protection level, retention schedule, and sanitization method.

Common handling goals include:
- Prevent unauthorized disclosure during storage or transit.
- Preserve integrity so data is not altered unintentionally.
- Maintain availability for backups, archives, and recovery.
- Support legal hold, discovery, and forensic review when needed.

A practical rule is that the more sensitive or legally significant the media, the more tightly you control access, movement, and destruction. Evidence media requires stronger controls than ordinary operational media because a single gap can undermine trust in the record.

**Retention schedules and approval**

Retention schedules define how long different records or media types must be kept and when they may be destroyed. These schedules are usually driven by legal, regulatory, tax, HR, privacy, or contractual requirements, and they should be approved by records management, legal counsel, compliance, and business owners as appropriate.

Typical retention concepts:
- **Operational records** are kept only as long as needed for business use.
- **Backup media** is retained for recovery windows and disaster recovery objectives.
- **Archives** are retained for long-term reference, historical value, or compliance.
- **Evidence** is retained until the case is closed, appeals expire, and legal hold is released.

Examples of retention logic:
- Transaction logs may be retained for months or years depending on regulation.
- Financial records often require multi-year retention.
- Security incident evidence may be retained until litigation, investigation, and appeal periods end.
- Backups are usually rotated and expired according to policy rather than kept indefinitely.

> Retention and disposal must be approved by the appropriate authority. Do not destroy records that are under legal hold, active investigation, or regulatory preservation requirements.

A simple records retention workflow often looks like this:

1. Identify the record type and applicable requirement.
2. Assign a retention period and owner.
3. Place a legal hold if litigation or investigation is pending.
4. Review for expiration and approval to dispose.
5. Sanitize, archive, or destroy using the approved method.
6. Document the action and retain the disposal record if required.

**Media types and practical handling**

Different media types have different physical and logical risks. CISSP questions often test whether you know that the same data may need different handling depending on the medium.

- **Magnetic disks and tapes** can be affected by strong magnetic fields and physical shock.
- **Solid-state drives (SSDs)** use wear leveling and may require different sanitization approaches than spinning disks.
- **Optical media** is durable but can be scratched, degraded by heat, or exposed to light and contamination.
- **USB and removable media** are easy to lose and often require encryption and strict inventory control.
- **Paper records** still matter for contracts, signatures, and legal evidence.
- **Cloud and virtual media** may exist as snapshots, object storage, virtual disks, logs, or provider-managed backups.

For transit and storage, use:
- Anti-static packaging for electronic media.
- Shock-resistant containers for fragile devices.
- Environmental controls for temperature and humidity.
- Protection from dust, water, fire, and electromagnetic exposure where relevant.
- Tamper-evident seals for evidence or high-value media.

A practical storage environment for sensitive media should include:
- Controlled access with logging.
- Stable temperature and humidity.
- Fire suppression appropriate to the environment.
- Separation from strong electromagnetic sources.
- Inventory checks and periodic inspection.

**Backup, archive, and evidence media**

Backup, archival, and evidence media are not the same thing, even if they contain similar data.

| Type | Primary purpose | Typical retention | Handling emphasis |
|---|---|---:|---|
| Backup | Recovery from failure or ransomware | Short to medium, rotation-based | Restoreability and integrity |
| Archive | Long-term preservation and reference | Long, policy-driven | Readability and retention compliance |
| Evidence | Legal or investigative use | Case-driven, often until release | Chain of custody and admissibility |

Operational backups are usually tracked as assets and rotated according to recovery objectives. Evidence media requires a stricter record of every transfer, access, and action. If a backup becomes evidence, you must preserve it in a way that supports legal review rather than normal restore operations.

**Chain of custody fundamentals**

Chain of custody is the documented, continuous history of evidence from collection to final disposition. Its purpose is to prove that the item presented in court is the same item collected, and that it was not altered, substituted, or contaminated.

A strong chain of custody record typically includes:
- Unique evidence identifier.
- Description of the item.
- Date and time of collection.
- Collector identity and signature.
- Location found and circumstances of collection.
- Every transfer of possession or control.
- Date, time, and purpose of each transfer.
- Recipient identity and signature where applicable.
- Condition of the item and any seals or packaging.
- Final disposition or return.

The chain must be unbroken. If there is a gap, unexplained access, missing signature, or inconsistent timestamp, the opposing party may challenge admissibility or weight in court. Even if the evidence is still useful operationally, its legal credibility can be reduced.

Common chain-of-custody controls:
- Use tamper-evident bags or seals.
- Label items immediately.
- Limit handlers to authorized personnel.
- Log every handoff.
- Store evidence in a secure evidence locker or vault.
- Maintain witness signatures when policy or procedure requires them.
- Photograph items before and after packaging when appropriate.

**Collection and preservation procedures**

When you collect media as evidence, your goal is to preserve state and context. You should avoid unnecessary interaction with the device or data.

Typical steps:
1. Secure the scene and prevent unauthorized access.
2. Document the item in place with photos, notes, and identifiers.
3. Record date, time, location, and collector identity.
4. Package the item using appropriate protective materials.
5. Seal and label the package.
6. Transfer custody using the evidence log.
7. Store in a secure, access-controlled location.

For digital evidence, you often create a forensic image rather than working on the original media. A write blocker may be used to prevent modification during acquisition. Hash values such as SHA-256 are used to verify integrity before and after imaging.

Example acquisition workflow:
```bash
# Identify the device
lsblk

# Create a bit-for-bit image with hashing
dd if=/dev/sdb of=/evidence/host123.img bs=4M status=progress conv=noerror,sync
sha256sum /evidence/host123.img > /evidence/host123.img.sha256
```

A more forensic-friendly tool such as `dc3dd` or `ddrescue` may be used when media is failing or when you need built-in hashing and logging.

**Sanitization methods and when to use them**

Sanitization is the process of removing data so it cannot be recovered by unauthorized means. CISSP commonly distinguishes among **clear**, **purge**, and **destroy**. These map well to NIST-style guidance.

- **Clear**: Overwrite or use logical techniques to remove data so it is not readily recoverable through normal system functions.
- **Purge**: Use stronger methods that resist advanced recovery, such as cryptographic erase or degaussing where appropriate.
- **Destroy**: Physically render the media unusable, such as shredding, pulverizing, incinerating, or melting.

Use the least destructive method that meets the required risk and compliance level:
- Clear when media will be reused internally and the threat model is moderate.
- Purge when media leaves controlled custody or when stronger assurance is required.
- Destroy when media is highly sensitive, damaged, or cannot be safely reused.

Examples:
- A reused office laptop drive may be cleared or purged depending on sensitivity.
- A decommissioned SSD with confidential data may require cryptographic erase or physical destruction.
- A classified or highly sensitive removable device may require destruction rather than reuse.

> Degaussing is effective for some magnetic media but is generally not appropriate for SSDs and many modern storage devices. Always match the method to the media type.

**Media reuse, reassignment, and repurposing**

Before you reissue, reassign, or repurpose a drive or device, you must sanitize it according to policy. Asset tracking alone is not enough because a device can remain in inventory while still containing recoverable data.

Common reuse controls:
- Verify ownership and authorization for reassignment.
- Sanitize the media before redeployment.
- Reimage the device with approved software.
- Update asset records and custody logs.
- Reclassify the media if its sensitivity or use case changes.

Reclassification matters when custody or ownership changes. For example, a drive moved from a secure engineering lab to general office use should be re-evaluated for classification, retention, and access controls. If a device is transferred to a third party, you should treat it as leaving trusted custody and apply stronger sanitization and documentation.

**Declassification and reclassification**

Declassification is the formal reduction of a media item’s sensitivity label after it has been sanitized or otherwise approved for lower protection. Reclassification is the opposite: the item is assigned a higher sensitivity level when new information, new custody, or new use makes it more sensitive.

Use these concepts when:
- A classified or restricted device is sanitized and approved for reuse.
- A media item changes business unit, jurisdiction, or ownership.
- New data is added that raises the sensitivity of the medium.
- Evidence is released from legal hold and returned to normal records management.

The exam point is that sanitization alone does not always equal declassification. You need policy approval and documentation to change the media’s status.

**Cloud and virtualized media considerations**

Cloud and virtualized environments complicate media management because the physical storage is abstracted away. You may not control the underlying disk, but you still control the data lifecycle, retention, and evidence handling requirements.

Important cloud considerations:
- Snapshots, object versions, and backups may persist after deletion.
- Provider retention settings may override or extend deletion timelines.
- Shared responsibility means you must understand what the provider handles versus what you must configure.
- Evidence may include logs, API activity, object metadata, VM snapshots, and exported images.
- Chain of custody may involve provider attestations, ticket records, export logs, and hash verification.

For virtual machines and cloud disks:
- Capture the image or snapshot in a controlled way.
- Record the account, region, timestamp, and object identifiers.
- Preserve hashes and export logs where possible.
- Restrict access to the evidence copy.
- Document any provider actions that affect the data.

Cloud evidence is often challenged on provenance, so you should preserve metadata such as:
- Object version IDs.
- Snapshot IDs.
- Tenant or subscription identifiers.
- Access logs.
- Time synchronization source.

**Environmental and transit controls**

Media in transit is vulnerable to loss, theft, and damage. Media in storage is vulnerable to environmental degradation and unauthorized access. You should protect both.

Recommended controls:
- Use sealed containers or tamper-evident packaging.
- Maintain a courier log for handoffs.
- Avoid leaving media unattended in vehicles or unsecured rooms.
- Keep storage areas dry, cool, and stable.
- Separate media from magnets, power equipment, and other electromagnetic sources where relevant.
- Use anti-static bags for electronic components and drives.
- Protect tapes and optical media from heat, humidity, and dust.

For long-term storage, periodic inspection matters. Media can degrade even when unused, so archives should be checked for readability and migrated before obsolescence or failure.

**Evidence admissibility and court readiness**

For evidence to be credible in court, you need more than a file copy. You need a defensible process that shows the item was preserved, tracked, and protected from tampering.

Court-ready evidence usually requires:
- A documented collection procedure.
- A complete chain of custody.
- Integrity verification through hashes or comparable checks.
- Secure storage with access logs.
- Clear explanation of tools and methods used.
- Consistent timestamps and identity records.

If the chain is broken, the evidence may still be admitted in some cases, but its weight can be reduced. In other cases, the evidence may be excluded or heavily challenged. CISSP questions often test this distinction: admissibility depends on process, not just on the existence of the data.

**Operational tracking versus forensic tracking**

Operational asset tracking answers, “Where is the device and who owns it?” Forensic tracking answers, “Who had it, when, why, and can we prove it was unchanged?”

| Aspect | Operational asset tracking | Chain of custody |
|---|---|---|
| Goal | Inventory and accountability | Legal admissibility and integrity |
| Scope | Device ownership and location | Every transfer and handling event |
| Evidence of control | Asset tag, CMDB, ticket | Signatures, timestamps, seals, hashes |
| Tolerance for gaps | Moderate | Very low |
| Typical use | IT operations | Investigations and litigation |

A device can be properly inventoried and still fail chain-of-custody requirements if the evidence record is incomplete. That is why normal asset management is not enough for legal evidence.

**Practical examples**

A ransomware incident may require you to preserve a server disk, a backup set, and cloud logs. The server disk becomes evidence, the backup set supports recovery, and the logs support timeline reconstruction. Each item may have different retention and handling rules.

A departing employee’s laptop may be sanitized and reissued after data is backed up and retention obligations are met. If the laptop is part of an investigation, you must preserve it as evidence instead of wiping it for reuse.

A cloud storage bucket may contain records subject to legal hold. Even if a user deletes objects, versioning or provider retention may keep them recoverable. You must coordinate with legal and the cloud provider before disposal.

**Exam cues and decision points**

Look for these clues in CISSP questions:
- If the item may be used in court, think chain of custody, not just backup.
- If the media will be reused, think sanitize before reissue.
- If the data is highly sensitive, think purge or destroy rather than clear.
- If the item is in cloud or virtual storage, think snapshots, logs, and provider retention.
- If records are involved, think retention schedule and approval before disposal.
- If custody changes, think reclassification or declassification as needed.

**Quick reference commands and checks**

Useful operational commands can support evidence handling and media verification:

```bash
# Show block devices and partitions
lsblk -o NAME,SIZE,TYPE,MOUNTPOINT,MODEL

# Check mounted filesystems
mount | column -t

# Verify a hash after imaging
sha256sum -c /evidence/host123.img.sha256

# View file metadata and timestamps
stat suspicious.log
```

For Linux evidence handling, you may also use:
```bash
# Read-only mount example
mount -o ro,noload /dev/sdb1 /mnt/evidence

# Capture a disk image with logging
dc3dd if=/dev/sdb of=/evidence/disk01.img hash=sha256 log=/evidence/disk01.log
```

These commands do not replace policy, but they help you preserve integrity and document what you did.

**Key takeaways**

Media management is about controlling the full lifecycle of information-bearing assets. Chain of custody is the evidentiary discipline that proves an item stayed authentic and untampered. For CISSP, always separate normal operational handling from legal evidence handling, and always match retention, sanitization, and disposal to the sensitivity, custody, and purpose of the media.
