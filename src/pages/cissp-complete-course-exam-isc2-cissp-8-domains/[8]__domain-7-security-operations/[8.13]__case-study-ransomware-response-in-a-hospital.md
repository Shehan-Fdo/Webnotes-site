## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Scenario overview**

A hospital ransomware event is a high-impact security incident because it affects both information systems and patient care. You must think beyond data loss and focus on safety, continuity of operations, legal obligations, and coordinated response across clinical, IT, legal, and executive teams.

Ransomware in healthcare often targets:
- **Electronic health records** (EHR) and scheduling systems
- **Imaging and lab platforms**
- **Identity services** such as Active Directory
- **File shares** used for clinical documents
- **Backups** to prevent recovery

The response goal is not only to remove malware. You must restore safe care delivery, preserve evidence, and reduce the chance of reinfection.

**Initial detection and triage**

Early indicators can come from endpoint alerts, user reports, unusual file extensions, inaccessible shares, or ransom notes. In a hospital, you should treat even a partial outage as urgent because clinical workflows may depend on the affected systems.

Common signs include:
- Rapid file renaming or encryption activity
- Multiple authentication failures or suspicious privileged logons
- Disabled security tools
- Unusual outbound traffic to command-and-control infrastructure
- Backup jobs failing or repositories becoming unavailable

A practical triage mindset is:
- Confirm whether the event is isolated or widespread
- Identify which systems support patient care
- Determine whether encryption is still active
- Preserve volatile evidence before making major changes

**Immediate priorities: patient safety first**

In healthcare, the first decision is often operational, not technical. You must ensure clinicians can continue safe care using downtime procedures if systems are unavailable.

Typical actions include:
- Switching to paper charting or downtime EHR procedures
- Redirecting emergency intake if needed
- Prioritizing life-critical systems such as medication administration, imaging, and lab interfaces
- Notifying clinical leadership and the incident commander immediately

> If patient safety is at risk, containment actions must be balanced against the possibility of disrupting critical clinical services.

A hospital should already have:
- Downtime forms and manual workflows
- Alternate communication channels
- Escalation paths for clinical leadership
- Tested business continuity and disaster recovery plans

**Containment strategy**

Containment aims to stop spread without destroying evidence or causing unnecessary operational harm. In ransomware cases, you often isolate affected endpoints and segments while keeping essential services running.

Practical containment steps:
1. Disconnect infected hosts from the network.
2. Disable compromised accounts and reset privileged credentials.
3. Block malicious indicators at firewalls, proxies, and DNS.
4. Segment or shut down affected VLANs if spread is active.
5. Suspend nonessential remote access and VPN sessions.
6. Preserve logs, memory, and disk images before reimaging.

Useful commands and checks may include:
```bash
# Linux host isolation checks
ip addr
ip route
ss -tulpn
journalctl -xe

# Windows triage examples
ipconfig /all
netstat -ano
tasklist /v
wevtutil qe Security /f:text /c:20
```

If you suspect lateral movement, inspect:
- Domain controller authentication logs
- Remote desktop protocol activity
- SMB file access patterns
- Service creation events
- Scheduled task creation
- PowerShell execution logs

**Evidence preservation and forensics**

You should preserve evidence before wiping systems whenever possible. Ransomware incidents may involve initial access, privilege escalation, exfiltration, and double extortion, so forensic artifacts matter.

Preserve:
- Memory captures from key systems
- Disk images of infected hosts
- Security, system, and application logs
- Firewall, proxy, DNS, and VPN logs
- EDR telemetry and alert timelines
- Backup logs and repository access records

A simple acquisition workflow might look like:
```bash
# Example Linux memory and disk preservation workflow
sudo dd if=/dev/sda of=/evidence/host01-disk.img bs=4M status=progress
sudo sha256sum /evidence/host01-disk.img > /evidence/host01-disk.img.sha256
sudo journalctl --since "2026-04-26 08:00" --until "2026-04-26 12:00" > /evidence/host01-journal.txt
```

For chain of custody, document:
- Who collected the evidence
- When and where it was collected
- What tool and method were used
- Hash values for integrity verification
- Storage location and access controls

**Communication and escalation**

Ransomware response requires coordinated communication. You should avoid ad hoc messaging that creates confusion or leaks sensitive details.

Key stakeholders often include:
- Incident response lead
- CIO, CISO, and executive leadership
- Clinical operations leadership
- Legal counsel and privacy officer
- Compliance and risk management
- Public relations and communications
- Law enforcement and cyber insurance contacts

Important communication principles:
- Use a single incident commander or coordinated command structure
- Share only verified facts
- Separate technical updates from patient-facing messaging
- Track decisions and approvals
- Avoid discussing ransom payment without legal and executive review

A hospital may also need to consider:
- Breach notification obligations
- Protected health information (PHI) exposure
- Regulatory reporting timelines
- Contractual obligations with vendors and partners

**Recovery and restoration**

Recovery should be deliberate. You do not simply restore from backup and reconnect everything. You first confirm the threat is removed, the environment is clean, and the restore point is trustworthy.

Recovery sequence:
1. Identify the attack scope and root cause.
2. Eradicate malware and persistence mechanisms.
3. Rebuild systems from known-good images where needed.
4. Validate backup integrity and restore priority services first.
5. Reset credentials and rotate secrets.
6. Monitor closely for reinfection or dormant persistence.

Priority restoration usually follows clinical impact:
- Identity services and authentication
- Network core services
- EHR and patient administration
- Lab, pharmacy, and imaging integrations
- File services and departmental applications

A restore plan should include:
- Offline or immutable backups
- Tested recovery time objective (RTO) and recovery point objective (RPO)
- Clean-room restoration procedures
- Validation of application dependencies
- Post-restore security hardening

Example backup verification:
```bash
# Verify backup integrity before restore
sha256sum -c host01-disk.img.sha256

# Example rsync restore from a trusted backup source
rsync -avh --progress /backup/ehr/ /restore/ehr/
```

> Never assume backups are safe just because they exist. Attackers often target backup servers, backup credentials, and snapshot systems.

**Root cause and attack path analysis**

After stabilization, you should determine how the attacker entered and moved through the environment. This supports remediation and future prevention.

Common initial access vectors in hospitals include:
- Phishing with malicious attachments or links
- Exposed remote access services
- Stolen credentials reused from other breaches
- Vulnerable VPN, gateway, or web application appliances
- Third-party vendor access
- Unpatched servers or legacy systems

Typical attack chain elements:
- Initial access through email or remote service
- Credential theft or token abuse
- Privilege escalation to domain admin or equivalent
- Lateral movement to file servers and backups
- Data exfiltration before encryption
- Ransom note deployment across endpoints

Useful investigation questions:
- Which account was first compromised?
- Was multifactor authentication (MFA) enabled and bypassed?
- Were backups reachable from the compromised domain?
- Did the attacker exfiltrate PHI before encryption?
- Which controls failed to detect or stop the spread?

**Preventive controls for future resilience**

A hospital should use layered controls to reduce ransomware impact. No single control is sufficient.

Strong preventive measures include:
- **MFA** for remote access, privileged accounts, and administrative portals
- Least privilege and role-based access control
- Network segmentation between clinical, administrative, and backup environments
- Application allowlisting for critical endpoints
- Email filtering and attachment sandboxing
- Patch management for internet-facing systems
- Endpoint detection and response (EDR) with tamper protection
- Immutable, offline, or air-gapped backups
- Regular restore testing and tabletop exercises

A practical segmentation model might separate:
- User workstations
- Clinical devices and medical IoT
- Server VLANs
- Backup infrastructure
- Management networks
- Vendor remote access zones

Example firewall policy concept:
```text
Allow:
- Clinician workstations -> EHR application servers on required ports
- Backup server -> backup repository on backup protocol ports
- Admin jump host -> management interfaces

Deny:
- Workstations -> backup repository directly
- Clinical VLAN -> server admin ports
- Internet -> internal management networks
```

**Business continuity and disaster recovery in healthcare**

Business continuity planning (BCP) keeps essential services running during disruption. Disaster recovery (DR) restores technology and data after the incident. In a hospital, both are necessary.

BCP focuses on:
- Manual patient registration
- Paper medication administration records
- Alternate communication methods
- Diversion procedures for emergency departments
- Staffing adjustments and escalation paths

DR focuses on:
- Restoring servers, applications, and data
- Rebuilding identity and network services
- Validating integrity and dependencies
- Meeting RTO and RPO targets

You should understand the difference:
- **RTO** is how long a service can be down before unacceptable impact
- **RPO** is how much data loss is acceptable measured in time

**Decision-making about ransom**

From a CISSP perspective, ransom payment is not a default solution. It introduces legal, ethical, operational, and strategic risks.

Considerations include:
- No guarantee of decryption or data deletion
- Potential sanctions or legal restrictions
- Encouragement of future attacks
- Possible exposure of stolen PHI even after payment
- Insurance and legal approval requirements

The preferred approach is to restore from clean backups and rebuild securely. If leadership considers payment, the decision must involve legal counsel, executive management, law enforcement guidance where appropriate, and insurer coordination.

**Lessons learned and post-incident improvement**

After recovery, conduct a formal post-incident review. The goal is to improve resilience, not assign blame.

Review:
- Detection gaps and alerting delays
- Identity and privilege weaknesses
- Backup design and restore performance
- Segmentation and lateral movement controls
- Vendor access governance
- Staff awareness and phishing resistance
- Incident communication effectiveness
- Downtime procedure usability

Deliverables may include:
- Updated incident response plan
- Revised backup and recovery architecture
- Improved logging and monitoring
- Stronger MFA and privileged access management
- More frequent tabletop and restoration exercises

**Exam-focused takeaways**

For CISSP, remember that the best answer usually prioritizes:
- Patient safety and continuity of care
- Containment with evidence preservation
- Coordinated communication and legal review
- Clean recovery from trusted backups
- Root cause analysis and control improvement

A hospital ransomware case tests your ability to balance security, operations, and governance. The correct response is disciplined, documented, and recovery-focused.
