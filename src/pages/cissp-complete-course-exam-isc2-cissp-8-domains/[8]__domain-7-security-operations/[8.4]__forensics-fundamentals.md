## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Forensics fundamentals overview**

Digital forensics is the disciplined process of identifying, preserving, collecting, examining, analyzing, and reporting digital evidence in a way that supports legal, regulatory, or internal investigative needs. The core goal is to produce findings that are **defensible**, **repeatable**, and **minimally intrusive**.

For CISSP exam prep, focus on the relationship between evidence handling and investigative integrity:

- **Preservation** protects evidence from alteration, loss, or contamination.
- **Collection** acquires data using methods appropriate to the situation and authority.
- **Examination** extracts relevant artifacts without over-interpreting them.
- **Analysis** correlates artifacts into timelines, user actions, and events.
- **Reporting** communicates findings clearly, with limitations and assumptions.

A forensic process is not just “copy data and inspect it.” It requires legal authority, documented procedures, chain of custody, and a method that can be repeated by another examiner with the same result.

**Forensic soundness and evidence integrity**

Forensic soundness means your process preserves the evidentiary value of the data. In practice, that means you minimize changes, document every action, and can explain why each step was necessary.

Key principles include:

- **Repeatability**: the same process should produce the same result when applied to the same evidence.
- **Reproducibility**: another qualified examiner should be able to reach the same conclusion using the same evidence and method.
- **Minimization of alteration**: you avoid unnecessary writes to source media and systems.
- **Documentation**: you record tools, versions, timestamps, hashes, and operator actions.
- **Validation**: you verify tools and methods before relying on them in an investigation.

> A common exam trap is assuming “read-only” automatically means “forensically sound.” Read-only access helps, but soundness also depends on proper authority, documentation, hashing, and controlled handling.

**Legal authority, scope, and admissibility**

You need lawful authority before collecting evidence. That authority may come from a warrant, consent, policy, contract, incident response authorization, or regulatory obligation, depending on the environment and jurisdiction.

Important considerations:

- **Warrants** are typically required for criminal searches in many jurisdictions.
- **Consent** must be valid, informed, and within scope.
- **Corporate policy** may authorize monitoring and internal investigations on company-owned systems.
- **Employee-owned devices** often require extra care because ownership, privacy, and consent boundaries differ.
- **Cloud and third-party systems** may require provider cooperation, contractual rights, or legal process.
- **Cross-border investigations** can trigger data residency and privacy law issues.

For exam purposes, remember that authority and scope define what you can collect, where you can collect it from, and how broadly you can search. Over-collection can create legal and privacy problems even if the data is technically accessible.

**Evidence handling and chain of custody**

Chain of custody is the documented history of evidence from collection to final disposition. It shows who had the evidence, when they had it, what they did with it, and whether it remained intact.

A strong evidence handling process includes:

- **Unique evidence labels** with case ID, item number, date, time, collector, and description.
- **Tamper-evident packaging** for physical media and removable devices.
- **Secure transport** using controlled handoff and restricted access.
- **Evidence logs** that record every transfer, access, and analysis step.
- **Hash values** to support integrity verification.
- **Storage controls** such as locked cabinets, access logs, and segregation of cases.

Example evidence log entry:

```text
Case: 2026-0412-17
Item: 03
Description: 1 TB external SSD, serial SN12345678
Collected by: A. Analyst
Date/Time Collected: 2026-04-26 09:14 UTC
Location: Finance office, desk drawer
Seal: Tamper seal #A88421 applied intact
Hash at acquisition: SHA-256 9f2c...e31a
Transferred to evidence locker: 2026-04-26 10:02 UTC
Received by: J. Custodian
Condition: No visible damage
```

Practical handling controls:

- Photograph the item before and after packaging when policy allows.
- Record device identifiers such as serial number, MAC address, or asset tag.
- Use anti-static and shock protection for sensitive hardware.
- Keep original evidence separate from working copies.
- Restrict access to authorized personnel only.

**Acquisition types and when to use them**

Acquisition method depends on the system state, legal authority, and investigative goal. No single method is always best.

Common acquisition types:

- **Dead-box acquisition**: collect data from a powered-off system or removed media.
  - Useful when you can safely remove storage and preserve state.
  - Often preferred for traditional disk imaging when the system is not needed for immediate response.
- **Live acquisition**: collect data from a running system.
  - Necessary when volatile data matters, such as memory, active connections, or encryption keys.
  - May alter the system, so document the impact carefully.
- **Physical acquisition**: capture all accessible sectors of a storage device, including unallocated space and slack space when possible.
  - Valuable for deep artifact recovery.
  - May be limited by encryption, damaged media, or modern storage behavior.
- **Logical acquisition**: collect selected files, folders, or application data.
  - Faster and less intrusive.
  - Useful when full imaging is impractical or unnecessary.
- **Targeted triage collection**: gather a focused set of artifacts from endpoints or servers.
  - Useful for rapid incident response and large-scale environments.
  - Prioritizes speed and relevance over completeness.

> Physical acquisition is not universally “best.” On encrypted devices, cloud services, mobile platforms, or time-sensitive incidents, logical, live, or targeted collection may be more practical and defensible.

**Volatile data and order of volatility**

Volatile data disappears quickly when a system is powered off or changes state. The order of volatility is a guideline for what to collect first, not a rigid universal list.

Typical priority is to collect the most transient data first, such as:

- **CPU state and cache** where relevant and accessible.
- **RAM** contents.
- **Network connections and session state**.
- **Running processes and services**.
- **Temporary files and clipboard data**.
- **System and application logs**.
- **Persistent storage** and disk artifacts.

The exact order depends on the incident. For example, if you suspect malware with in-memory payloads or encryption keys, memory collection becomes urgent. If the system is already powered off, you shift to disk and storage artifacts.

Useful live-response commands on Linux:

```bash
who
w
ps auxf
ss -tulpn
ip addr
lsof -nP
last
journalctl --since "1 hour ago"
```

Useful Windows live-response commands:

```powershell
Get-Process
Get-NetTCPConnection
Get-Service
quser
wevtutil qe Security /c:20 /f:text
```

**Memory forensics**

Memory analysis is critical because many artifacts never reach disk. RAM can contain active malware, decrypted content, credentials, injected code, and transient system state.

Common memory artifacts include:

- **Running processes** and parent-child relationships.
- **Loaded modules** and DLLs.
- **Injected code** or hollowed processes.
- **Open handles** to files, registry keys, and objects.
- **Network sockets** and active connections.
- **Kernel artifacts** such as drivers, hooks, and callbacks.
- **Command history fragments**, chat content, or decrypted documents.

Memory forensics often uses tools such as Volatility or Rekall-style workflows. A typical approach is:

1. Acquire memory with a trusted method.
2. Verify the image hash.
3. Enumerate processes, modules, sockets, and handles.
4. Look for anomalies such as hidden processes, suspicious injections, or mismatched parentage.
5. Correlate memory findings with disk and log artifacts.

Example workflow:

```bash
volatility3 -f memory.raw windows.pslist
volatility3 -f memory.raw windows.netscan
volatility3 -f memory.raw windows.dlllist --pid 1234
volatility3 -f memory.raw windows.handles --pid 1234
```

Memory evidence is highly volatile, so document acquisition time precisely. If the system is encrypted, memory may be the only place where keys or decrypted content exist.

**Disk and file system artifacts**

File system forensics focuses on how data is stored, modified, and deleted. Many important artifacts are not in file contents but in metadata and allocation structures.

Common artifacts include:

- **Metadata** such as timestamps, ownership, permissions, and file attributes.
- **MFT** (Master File Table) records on NTFS, which can reveal file names, timestamps, and references.
- **Journals** such as NTFS `$LogFile` or file system journals that may show recent activity.
- **Slack space** containing remnants of previously stored data.
- **Unallocated space** where deleted data may still exist until overwritten.
- **Prefetch, shortcut, and recent-file artifacts** on supported systems.
- **Registry hives** and application configuration files.
- **Browser artifacts** such as history, cache, and downloads.

Useful disk-oriented commands:

```bash
mmls disk.dd
fls -r -m / disk.dd
icat disk.dd 128-128-3 > recovered_file.bin
sha256sum recovered_file.bin
```

On Windows, common artifact locations include:

```text
C:\Windows\Prefetch\
C:\Users\<user>\AppData\Roaming\
C:\Users\<user>\AppData\Local\Microsoft\Windows\INetCache\
C:\Windows\System32\winevt\Logs\
```

File system analysis often answers questions such as:

- When was a file created, modified, accessed, or changed?
- Was a file deleted, renamed, or copied?
- Did the user interact with the file, or was it created by a process?
- Do timestamps align with log entries and network activity?

**Timeline analysis**

Timeline analysis correlates events across multiple sources to reconstruct what happened and when. It is one of the most important investigative techniques because a single artifact rarely tells the whole story.

Common sources for timeline correlation:

- **File system MAC times**: modified, accessed, and changed/created timestamps.
- **Event logs**: authentication, process creation, service changes, and policy events.
- **Network logs**: firewall, proxy, DNS, VPN, and IDS/IPS records.
- **Application logs**: database, web server, EDR, and SaaS audit logs.
- **Memory artifacts**: active sessions and process lifetimes.

A practical timeline workflow:

1. Normalize timestamps to a common time zone, usually UTC.
2. Validate clock drift and system time settings.
3. Correlate events from multiple sources.
4. Identify gaps, contradictions, and likely causal sequences.
5. Build a narrative supported by evidence.

Example correlation idea:

- A suspicious login appears in the authentication log.
- A new process starts shortly after.
- A network connection to an external host follows.
- A file is created or modified on disk.
- Memory shows the process still running with injected code.

This sequence is stronger than any single artifact alone.

**Encryption, SSDs, and cloud limitations**

Modern environments create forensic challenges that affect acquisition and recovery.

Important limitations:

- **Full-disk encryption** can make offline disk analysis useless without keys.
- **Live systems** may be the only place to capture decryption material.
- **SSDs** use wear leveling and TRIM, which can reduce the usefulness of deleted-file recovery.
- **Cloud systems** may not provide raw disk access, so you rely on logs, snapshots, exports, and provider APIs.
- **Mobile devices** often require specialized acquisition methods and may restrict access through secure enclaves or app sandboxing.
- **Remote services** may retain data in ways that differ from local file systems.

For exam prep, remember that “physical acquisition” is not always possible or meaningful. In encrypted or cloud-native environments, the best evidence may come from logs, snapshots, API exports, or targeted collection.

**Practical acquisition commands and examples**

Use tools and commands that match the situation and preserve evidence integrity. The examples below are illustrative, not universal prescriptions.

Linux disk imaging with hashing:

```bash
sudo dd if=/dev/sdb of=/evidence/case-2026-0412-17/disk1.img bs=4M conv=noerror,sync status=progress
sha256sum /evidence/case-2026-0412-17/disk1.img > /evidence/case-2026-0412-17/disk1.img.sha256
```

A safer forensic workflow is to hash the acquired image and, when possible, compare it to a hash captured from the source in a controlled read-only process or by a trusted acquisition tool that records hashes during acquisition.

Example with `dc3dd`-style hashing:

```bash
dc3dd if=/dev/sdb of=/evidence/case-2026-0412-17/disk1.img hash=sha256 log=/evidence/case-2026-0412-17/acquisition.log
```

Windows memory capture example:

```powershell
# Example only: use approved tooling in your environment
.\winpmem.exe --output C:\Evidence\memory.raw
```

Linux triage collection example:

```bash
mkdir -p /evidence/triage
cp /var/log/auth.log /evidence/triage/
cp /var/log/syslog /evidence/triage/
tar -czf /evidence/triage/home-artifacts.tgz /home/alice/.ssh /home/alice/.bash_history
```

> Avoid presenting recovery-oriented tools as standard forensic acquisition methods unless you clearly explain their limitations and the investigative context.

**Evidence examination workflow**

A disciplined workflow helps you avoid contamination and missed artifacts.

1. Confirm legal authority and scope.
2. Photograph or document the evidence condition.
3. Assign a case ID and evidence ID.
4. Acquire the evidence using the least intrusive suitable method.
5. Hash the acquired data and record the values.
6. Work from a verified copy, not the original.
7. Examine file system, memory, logs, and application artifacts.
8. Correlate findings into a timeline.
9. Validate conclusions against alternate sources.
10. Preserve notes, outputs, and hashes for reporting and review.

This workflow supports both operational investigations and courtroom scrutiny.

**Reporting and communication**

Forensic reporting translates technical findings into a clear narrative for stakeholders such as legal counsel, management, incident responders, or law enforcement. The report should be understandable, accurate, and limited to what the evidence supports.

A strong report usually includes:

- **Case summary** with scope and objectives.
- **Authority and constraints** such as warrant, consent, or policy basis.
- **Evidence inventory** with identifiers and hashes.
- **Methods used** including tools and versions.
- **Findings** with supporting artifacts and timestamps.
- **Timeline** of key events.
- **Limitations** such as encryption, missing logs, or incomplete access.
- **Conclusion** that distinguishes facts from interpretation.
- **Appendices** with hashes, screenshots, command output, and chain-of-custody records.

Example report language:

```text
At 14:22 UTC, the endpoint established an outbound TLS connection to 198.51.100.24.
Within 18 seconds, a new process launched from the user's Downloads directory.
Memory analysis showed injected code in the process and a matching network socket.
These findings are consistent with unauthorized execution, but the exact payload could not be fully recovered due to partial memory overwrite.
```

Keep conclusions conservative. State what you observed, what it likely means, and what remains uncertain.

**Common exam distinctions**

- **Preservation vs collection**: preservation protects evidence; collection acquires it.
- **Physical vs logical**: physical aims for broad storage capture; logical targets selected data.
- **Live vs dead-box**: live captures volatile state; dead-box minimizes system interaction.
- **Evidence vs intelligence**: evidence is handled for defensibility; intelligence may be useful but not always admissible.
- **Artifact vs conclusion**: artifacts are data points; conclusions come from correlation and analysis.

**Quick reference checklist**

- Verify authority before touching evidence.
- Minimize changes to the source.
- Document every action.
- Use hashes to verify integrity.
- Maintain chain of custody.
- Collect volatile data first when needed.
- Correlate memory, disk, and logs.
- Account for encryption, SSD behavior, and cloud limitations.
- Report findings clearly with limitations.
