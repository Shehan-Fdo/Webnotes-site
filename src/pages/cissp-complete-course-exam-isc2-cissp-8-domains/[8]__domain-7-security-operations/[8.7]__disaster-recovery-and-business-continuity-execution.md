## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Business continuity and disaster recovery overview**

Business continuity planning (BCP) keeps critical business functions operating during disruption, while disaster recovery (DR) focuses on restoring IT services and data after an incident. In CISSP scenarios, you usually execute both together: business leadership decides what must continue, and technical teams restore systems in the order that supports those priorities.

The key exam idea is that recovery is not just “bring servers back.” You must preserve safety, legal obligations, data integrity, and stakeholder trust while restoring services within acceptable time and data-loss limits.

- **BCP** addresses people, process, facilities, suppliers, and communications.
- **DR** addresses systems, applications, data, infrastructure, and technical restoration.
- **Execution** means moving from plan to action under incident conditions, often with incomplete information.

**Core recovery objectives and terminology**

Recovery targets drive every execution decision. You should know how the terms relate because CISSP questions often mix them.

- **RTO** (Recovery Time Objective) is the maximum acceptable downtime for a service.
- **RPO** (Recovery Point Objective) is the maximum acceptable data loss measured in time.
- **MTD** (Maximum Tolerable Downtime) or **MAO** (Maximum Acceptable Outage) is the longest the business can survive before severe harm occurs.
- RTO is usually set to be less than or equal to MTD/MAO.
- RPO determines how far back you can restore data without unacceptable loss.
- A low RTO often requires more expensive, more automated recovery capabilities.
- A low RPO often requires replication, frequent backups, or near-real-time data protection.

A practical way to think about it:

| Term | Focus | Typical question |
|---|---|---|
| RTO | Time to restore service | “How fast must the system be back?” |
| RPO | Data currency | “How much data can be lost?” |
| MTD/MAO | Business survival limit | “How long until the outage becomes unacceptable?” |

> If a scenario says the business can tolerate 8 hours of outage but only 15 minutes of data loss, you need a recovery design that meets both constraints, not just one.

**Governance, authority, and activation**

A strong recovery plan defines who can declare a disaster, who can activate BCP/DR, and what authority levels apply. This prevents delays and conflicting instructions during a crisis.

- **Incident response** may identify and contain the event.
- **BCP activation** is usually a business decision based on operational impact.
- **DR activation** is usually a technical execution decision once recovery is authorized.
- **Executive leadership** or a designated crisis manager often has authority to declare a disaster.
- **IT operations** executes technical recovery under that authority.
- **Business unit owners** validate whether restored services meet operational needs.

A typical governance structure includes:

- **Crisis management team**
  - Makes high-level decisions.
  - Declares disaster status.
  - Approves major tradeoffs, such as failover, vendor escalation, or public statements.
- **BCP coordinator**
  - Coordinates business function continuity.
  - Tracks critical processes, staffing, and alternate work arrangements.
- **DR lead**
  - Directs technical restoration.
  - Coordinates infrastructure, application, database, and network recovery.
- **Communications lead**
  - Manages internal and external messaging.
  - Coordinates with legal, compliance, and public relations.
- **Security lead**
  - Assesses whether the event is malicious.
  - Ensures evidence preservation and containment do not conflict with recovery.
- **Business owners**
  - Confirm priority services and accept restored functionality.

**Activation sequence and integration of BCP with DR**

BCP and DR should not operate as separate silos during execution. BCP determines what the business needs first; DR determines how to restore the supporting technology.

A common activation flow is:

1. Detect and assess the incident.
2. Contain immediate threats and preserve evidence where needed.
3. Determine business impact and whether recovery thresholds are exceeded.
4. Obtain formal disaster declaration from authorized leadership.
5. Activate BCP for critical business functions.
6. Activate DR runbooks for prioritized systems.
7. Coordinate communications, vendor escalation, and regulatory notifications.
8. Validate service restoration and business acceptance.
9. Transition from emergency operations to normal operations or phased return.

BCP and DR integration matters because a technically restored system may still be unusable if staff, facilities, identity services, telecom, or suppliers are unavailable.

**Recovery site strategies and alternate processing options**

Recovery sites differ by cost, speed, and readiness. CISSP questions often test which site type best matches the required RTO/RPO.

- **Hot site**
  - Fully equipped and ready to operate quickly.
  - Best for very low RTO.
  - Usually expensive to maintain.
- **Warm site**
  - Partially equipped and requires additional setup.
  - Balances cost and recovery speed.
- **Cold site**
  - Basic facility with power and space, but little or no equipment.
  - Lowest cost, slowest recovery.
- **Mobile site**
  - Portable recovery capability delivered in trailers or containers.
  - Useful when fixed facilities are unavailable.
- **Mirrored site**
  - Near-identical environment with synchronous or near-synchronous data replication.
  - Supports very low RPO and fast failover.
- **Reciprocal agreement**
  - Two organizations agree to share recovery capacity.
  - Lower cost, but availability and compatibility risks are higher.
- **Phased recovery / roll-forward approach**
  - Restore core services first, then progressively bring up dependent systems.
  - Useful when full simultaneous recovery is not feasible.

Other execution patterns may include:

- **Active-active**
  - Multiple sites serve production simultaneously.
  - Failover can be nearly transparent if designed well.
- **Active-passive**
  - One site serves production while another waits in standby.
  - Simpler to manage, but failover may take longer.
- **Cloud-based recovery**
  - Uses infrastructure as a service or platform services for rapid provisioning.
  - Requires identity, networking, and data replication planning.

> A reciprocal agreement is not the same as a guaranteed hot site. If the partner site is already using its own capacity, your recovery may be delayed or constrained.

**Backup restoration methods and verification**

Restoring data correctly is as important as restoring systems. You should prioritize the most recent valid backup that meets the recovery objective and verify integrity before declaring success.

Common backup types:

- **Full backup**
  - Complete copy of selected data.
  - Simplest restore path.
- **Incremental backup**
  - Captures changes since the last backup of any type.
  - Fast to create, slower to restore because multiple sets may be needed.
- **Differential backup**
  - Captures changes since the last full backup.
  - Faster to restore than incremental, but grows over time.

Restore sequencing often follows this logic:

1. Restore the base full backup.
2. Apply differential backups if used, or chain incremental backups in order.
3. Validate file integrity and application consistency.
4. Reconcile transaction logs or database redo/undo logs if applicable.
5. Confirm application startup and user access.

Practical verification steps include:

- **Checksum validation**
  - Compare hashes to detect corruption.
- **Restore testing**
  - Periodically test restores in a nonproduction environment.
- **Application consistency checks**
  - Verify databases, queues, and dependent services are coherent.
- **Spot checks**
  - Confirm critical records, timestamps, and transaction counts.
- **Backup catalog review**
  - Ensure the correct backup set and retention point are selected.

Example restore workflow:

```bash
# Example: verify backup archive integrity
sha256sum -c backup.sha256

# Example: restore a database backup
pg_restore -d appdb /backups/appdb_full.dump

# Example: inspect restored files
ls -lah /restore/appdata
```

**Recovery sequencing and operational runbooks**

Recovery should follow dependency order, not just asset order. If you restore an application before identity, DNS, or database services, users may still be unable to connect.

A typical sequencing model is:

- **Foundation services**
  - Power, network, storage, virtualization, and monitoring.
- **Identity and access**
  - Directory services, authentication, federation, MFA, and privileged access.
- **Core data services**
  - Databases, message queues, file stores, and replication services.
- **Application tier**
  - Web, API, middleware, and batch services.
- **Business workflows**
  - Reporting, integrations, and downstream jobs.

Practical runbook examples:

```bash
# Example: verify DNS cutover
dig app.example.com
nslookup app.example.com

# Example: check service health after failover
systemctl status nginx
curl -I https://app.example.com/health

# Example: confirm database replication role
psql -c "SELECT pg_is_in_recovery();"
```

A DNS cutover runbook may include:

1. Lower DNS TTL in advance of planned failover.
2. Update A, AAAA, CNAME, or load balancer records.
3. Verify propagation from multiple resolvers.
4. Confirm certificates and hostnames still match.
5. Test application access from internal and external networks.

Identity federation restoration may require:

1. Restoring directory services or cloud identity connectors.
2. Re-establishing trust relationships and certificates.
3. Validating SSO (Single Sign-On) and MFA flows.
4. Confirming service accounts and privileged roles.
5. Testing access to critical applications.

Database replication failover may require:

1. Promote the standby replica.
2. Freeze writes on the old primary if reachable.
3. Repoint applications to the new primary.
4. Validate schema version and transaction logs.
5. Rebuild replication in the reverse direction after stabilization.

**Failover, failback, and return to primary operations**

Failover moves operations to an alternate site or system. Failback returns operations to the primary environment after it is repaired and validated. CISSP questions often ask when you should fail back, and the answer is: only after the primary environment is stable, secure, and fully tested.

Failover considerations:

- Trigger failover when the primary site cannot meet the RTO.
- Prefer automated failover for tightly controlled environments.
- Use manual approval when data consistency or security risk is high.
- Confirm that dependencies such as DNS, identity, and telecom are available at the alternate site.

Failback criteria:

- Primary site infrastructure is repaired and hardened.
- Data is synchronized or reconciled.
- Security controls are restored and validated.
- Business owners approve the return.
- A rollback plan exists if failback fails.

Failback often requires a controlled cutover:

1. Freeze changes on the recovery environment.
2. Synchronize data back to the primary site.
3. Validate application and database consistency.
4. Update routing, DNS, and load balancers.
5. Monitor closely for errors and performance issues.
6. Keep the alternate site ready until stability is confirmed.

> Do not fail back just because the primary site is “up.” It must be operationally trustworthy, not merely powered on.

**Data integrity after manual workarounds**

During a disruption, staff may use manual forms, spreadsheets, paper logs, or offline queues to keep the business moving. Once systems return, you must reconcile those transactions carefully.

Key integrity tasks include:

- **Reconciliation**
  - Compare manual records with system records.
  - Identify duplicates, omissions, and conflicting entries.
- **Validation**
  - Confirm totals, timestamps, approvals, and account balances.
  - Check that business rules were applied consistently.
- **Exception handling**
  - Resolve mismatched records through approved review.
  - Escalate unresolved discrepancies.
- **Rollback or roll-forward decision**
  - Roll back if the restored system state is unusable or corrupted and no acceptable transaction continuity exists.
  - Roll forward if you can safely apply missing transactions and preserve auditability.

Practical example:

- A retail store processes sales on paper during an outage.
- After recovery, the team imports transactions into the POS (point of sale) system.
- Finance compares cash totals, card settlements, and inventory decrements.
- Any mismatch is documented and approved before the day is closed.

This is important because recovery is not complete until the business record is trustworthy.

**Crisis communications and notification trees**

Communication failures can turn a technical outage into a business crisis. A good execution plan defines who is notified, in what order, and through which channels.

Internal notification tree:

- Frontline operations and service desk
- Technical responders and incident commander
- Business owners and department leaders
- Executive leadership and crisis management
- Legal, compliance, HR, and security teams

External notification tree:

- Critical vendors and managed service providers
- Telecom and cloud providers
- Customers and partners
- Regulators and auditors, when required
- Public relations and media contacts, if public disclosure is needed

Messaging should be:

- Accurate
- Timely
- Approved by the right authority
- Consistent across channels
- Limited to verified facts

Crisis communication procedures should define:

- Who drafts the message.
- Who approves it.
- Which channels are used, such as email, SMS, status page, or hotline.
- How often updates are issued.
- How rumors and misinformation are corrected.

Regulatory notification timing depends on the jurisdiction and incident type. In exam scenarios, the safe answer is to notify according to legal, contractual, and policy requirements as soon as the facts are sufficient and approval is obtained. Do not delay required notifications while waiting for perfect certainty.

Public relations coordination matters when the outage affects customers, brand reputation, or safety. PR should align with legal and security teams so public statements do not expose sensitive details or contradict operational facts.

**Vendor, telecom, and supply chain dependencies**

Recovery often fails because of external dependencies, not internal systems. You should account for third-party services in the execution plan.

Common dependency issues:

- Cloud provider region outage
- ISP or telecom circuit failure
- DNS provider unavailability
- Managed security service interruption
- Hardware replacement delays
- SaaS (Software as a Service) vendor outage
- Contractual support gaps during off-hours

Execution controls include:

- Maintain vendor contact lists and escalation paths.
- Confirm support SLAs (Service Level Agreements) and recovery obligations.
- Test alternate communication methods if primary telecom fails.
- Keep spare hardware or preapproved procurement paths for critical components.
- Document dependencies for identity, payment processing, shipping, and customer notification services.

A practical example is a payment platform that depends on a third-party fraud service and a separate SMS provider. Even if the core application is restored, checkout may still fail until those vendors recover or are bypassed through an approved degraded mode.

**Decision-making during degraded operations**

Sometimes you cannot restore everything at once. In that case, you operate in a degraded mode while preserving the most critical functions.

Examples of degraded operation choices:

- Disable nonessential reporting to preserve transaction processing.
- Queue outbound emails until the mail service is restored.
- Allow read-only access while write services are being repaired.
- Use manual approval for high-risk transactions.
- Prioritize customer-facing services over internal convenience tools.

The decision should be based on:

- Business criticality
- Security risk
- Data integrity risk
- Regulatory impact
- Operational feasibility

**Testing, exercises, and readiness**

Execution quality depends on preparation. Plans that are never tested usually fail under pressure.

Useful exercise types:

- **Tabletop exercise**
  - Walk through decisions and communications.
  - Good for validating roles and escalation.
- **Functional test**
  - Test a specific recovery component, such as restore or failover.
- **Full interruption test**
  - Simulate real outage conditions.
  - Highest realism, highest risk.
- **Parallel test**
  - Run recovery systems alongside production without taking over.
  - Useful for validation before cutover.

Testing should verify:

- Recovery time and recovery point targets
- Backup restore success
- Alternate site readiness
- Communications and notification trees
- Vendor response times
- Failover and failback procedures
- Data reconciliation and audit trails

**Exam-focused distinctions**

- **BCP** keeps the business operating; **DR** restores IT.
- **RTO** is about time; **RPO** is about data loss.
- **MTD/MAO** is the business survival threshold.
- **Failover** moves to the alternate environment; **failback** returns to primary.
- **Hot/mirrored** solutions support faster recovery than **cold** sites.
- **Incremental** backups restore slower than **differential** backups because more restore points must be applied.
- **Manual workarounds** require reconciliation before closure.
- **Communication** is part of recovery, not an afterthought.
- **Vendor dependencies** can block recovery even when internal systems are ready.

**Practical recovery checklist**

1. Confirm incident severity and business impact.
2. Obtain formal disaster declaration if thresholds are met.
3. Activate BCP and DR governance roles.
4. Notify internal stakeholders and external partners as required.
5. Restore foundational services first.
6. Recover identity, network, and data services.
7. Bring up applications in dependency order.
8. Validate data integrity and business transactions.
9. Reconcile manual workarounds and exceptions.
10. Decide on failback only after the primary site is stable and approved.
