## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**BCP/DR exercise overview**

A business continuity plan (BCP) and disaster recovery (DR) exercise validates that your organization can continue critical operations and restore services after disruption. In CISSP terms, this is not just a documentation check; it is a control validation activity that tests people, process, technology, and third-party dependencies under realistic conditions.

A good exercise answers three questions:

- Can you meet the recovery objectives you defined?
- Do the right people know their roles and escalation paths?
- Do your technical and operational procedures actually work when time is limited?

BCP focuses on sustaining essential business functions during an incident. DR focuses on restoring IT services and data after an outage. In practice, the two overlap, so exercises should test both continuity and recovery capabilities together.

**Core objectives**

A BCP/DR exercise should validate that the organization can:

- Protect life and safety first.
- Maintain or restore critical business services within acceptable timeframes.
- Recover systems and data to a known-good state.
- Communicate clearly with internal teams, executives, customers, regulators, and vendors.
- Identify gaps in plans, dependencies, and assumptions before a real incident occurs.

The exercise is also a governance tool. It provides evidence for risk management, audit readiness, and due care by showing that plans are not static documents.

**Key planning inputs**

Before you design the exercise, you need the recovery requirements that drive it.

- **Business Impact Analysis (BIA)** identifies critical processes, dependencies, and the effect of downtime.
- **Recovery Time Objective (RTO)** is the maximum acceptable time to restore a service.
- **Recovery Point Objective (RPO)** is the maximum acceptable data loss measured in time.
- **Maximum Tolerable Downtime (MTD)** is the longest outage the business can tolerate.
- **Criticality tiers** help you prioritize which services, sites, and teams to test first.

These inputs determine the scenario, the scope, and the success criteria. If the exercise does not test the stated RTO and RPO, it does not prove much.

**Exercise types**

Different exercise types provide different levels of realism and risk.

| Type | Purpose | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| Tabletop | Discuss roles, decisions, and communications | Early validation, low cost, policy review |
| Walkthrough | Step through procedures in sequence | Training and plan familiarization |
| Simulation | Mimic an incident with injects and timed decisions | Test coordination and escalation |
| Functional test | Validate a specific capability, such as failover or backup restore | Technical verification |
| Parallel test | Run recovery systems alongside production | Higher confidence without full cutover |
| Full interruption test | Move operations to the recovery site or alternate process | Highest realism, highest risk |

A mature program usually starts with low-risk exercises and progresses toward more realistic tests. You do not begin with a full interruption test unless the business accepts the operational risk.

**Exercise design principles**

A useful exercise is realistic, measurable, and safe.

- **Realistic** means the scenario reflects actual threats and dependencies, such as ransomware, cloud region failure, power loss, or loss of a key supplier.
- **Measurable** means you define pass/fail criteria in advance, such as restore time, communication time, or percentage of critical systems recovered.
- **Safe** means the exercise does not create unacceptable production risk, safety hazards, or compliance violations.

You should also avoid “scripted success.” If participants already know every answer, the exercise only proves that they can follow a script, not that they can respond under pressure.

**Common scenario themes**

Typical BCP/DR scenarios include:

- Data center fire, flood, or extended utility outage
- Ransomware encrypting production and backup systems
- Cloud service or availability zone failure
- Loss of network connectivity to a site or region
- Corruption of a critical database
- Failure of a key supplier, telecom provider, or SaaS platform
- Pandemic or workforce unavailability
- Insider sabotage or accidental deletion of critical data

A strong scenario includes both the triggering event and the cascading effects. For example, a ransomware event may also disable identity services, backup access, and ticketing systems.

**Roles and responsibilities**

Exercises work best when responsibilities are explicit.

- **Exercise sponsor** approves scope, budget, and business priorities.
- **Exercise coordinator** plans the event, injects scenarios, and tracks outcomes.
- **Incident commander** leads response decisions during the exercise.
- **Technical leads** execute restore, failover, and validation tasks.
- **Business owners** confirm process continuity and acceptable workarounds.
- **Communications lead** manages internal and external messaging.
- **Observers/evaluators** record timing, decisions, and deviations.

Clear role assignment matters because recovery often fails at the handoff points between teams. The exercise should test those handoffs, not just technical restoration.

**Planning the exercise**

A structured planning process reduces ambiguity and improves results.

1. Define the objective.
   - Example: validate that the organization can restore the customer portal within the RTO and recover data within the RPO after a regional outage.
2. Select the scenario.
   - Choose a realistic event that matches the organization’s risk profile.
3. Set scope and boundaries.
   - Identify which systems, sites, teams, vendors, and business processes are included.
4. Define success criteria.
   - Include timing, communication, decision-making, and technical recovery targets.
5. Assign roles.
   - Name participants, observers, and decision-makers.
6. Prepare injects and artifacts.
   - Use alerts, logs, outage notices, and status updates to drive the exercise.
7. Obtain approvals.
   - Ensure leadership accepts the risk and understands any production impact.
8. Conduct the exercise.
   - Follow the plan, but allow realistic decision points.
9. Debrief and document lessons learned.
   - Capture gaps, corrective actions, and owners.
10. Track remediation to closure.
   - Update plans, training, architecture, and controls.

**Example tabletop inject flow**

A tabletop exercise often uses timed injects to simulate an evolving incident.

```text
T+00:00  Security monitoring reports unusual encryption activity on a file server.
T+00:10  Help desk receives multiple calls about inaccessible shared drives.
T+00:20  Backup admin reports the latest backup job failed.
T+00:30  Executive team requests an impact estimate and customer communication plan.
T+00:45  Cloud identity service becomes unavailable due to credential lockout.
T+01:00  Recovery site access is confirmed, but DNS failover has not been tested.
```

This kind of flow helps you evaluate whether the team can prioritize actions, escalate correctly, and communicate under pressure.

**Technical validation areas**

A BCP/DR exercise should test more than “can we restore a server.”

- **Backups**
  - Verify backup integrity, retention, encryption, and restore procedures.
  - Confirm that backups are isolated from the same failure domain when possible.
- **Failover**
  - Test active-passive or active-active switching where architecture supports it.
  - Validate DNS, load balancers, routing, and application dependencies.
- **Identity and access**
  - Confirm emergency access, break-glass accounts, and MFA (multifactor authentication) recovery procedures.
- **Data recovery**
  - Restore databases, file shares, and application state in the correct order.
- **Logging and monitoring**
  - Ensure security logs, system logs, and alerting continue during recovery.
- **Third-party services**
  - Validate vendor support contacts, SLAs (service level agreements), and escalation paths.
- **Manual workarounds**
  - Test paper-based or alternate-process operations if systems are unavailable.

**Practical restore examples**

A DR exercise often includes real commands or platform actions to prove recovery steps.

```bash
# Verify backup archives
sha256sum -c backups.sha256

# List available backup sets
restic snapshots

# Restore a specific snapshot
restic restore latest --target /restore/test

# Check database consistency after restore
pg_isready -h db-recovery.example.com -p 5432
psql -h db-recovery.example.com -U appuser -d appdb -c "SELECT count(*) FROM orders;"
```

For virtualized or cloud environments, you may also validate infrastructure recovery.

```bash
# Example: confirm a failover target is healthy
kubectl get pods -n critical-app
kubectl rollout status deployment/customer-portal -n critical-app

# Example: test DNS cutover
dig +short portal.example.com
```

These examples are not a substitute for a full runbook, but they show how to make the exercise concrete and measurable.

**Communication and escalation**

Communication failures are a common reason recovery efforts stall. Your exercise should test message flow as deliberately as system recovery.

- Internal updates should go to operations, security, leadership, legal, and service desk.
- External notifications may be required for customers, regulators, insurers, and partners.
- Status updates should be time-based, factual, and consistent.
- Messages should distinguish confirmed facts from assumptions.

A simple status template helps reduce confusion:

```text
Incident: Production outage affecting customer portal
Start time: 09:12 UTC
Current impact: Login and checkout unavailable
Actions taken: Failover initiated, backup integrity under review
Next update: 09:45 UTC
Owner: Incident Commander
```

**Metrics and evaluation**

You need evidence to judge whether the exercise succeeded.

- Time to detect the incident
- Time to declare the event and activate the plan
- Time to assemble the response team
- Time to restore critical services
- Time to communicate with stakeholders
- Accuracy of recovery order and dependencies
- Number of procedural errors or missing steps
- Whether RTO and RPO were met
- Whether manual workarounds were usable

Use observers to record timestamps and decisions. Without measurement, the exercise becomes anecdotal and hard to improve.

**After-action review**

The after-action review is where the exercise creates value.

- Capture what happened, what worked, and what failed.
- Separate technical issues from process issues.
- Assign owners and due dates for corrective actions.
- Update the BCP, DR plan, contact lists, diagrams, and runbooks.
- Retest any major remediation.

A good review does not blame individuals. It identifies control gaps, training gaps, and design weaknesses.

**Common weaknesses uncovered by exercises**

Exercises often reveal predictable problems:

- Contact lists are outdated.
- Key staff do not know their alternates.
- Backup restores are untested or too slow.
- Dependencies on DNS, identity, or certificates were missed.
- Vendor support paths are unclear.
- Recovery order is wrong, causing avoidable delays.
- Documentation is incomplete or inaccessible during outages.
- The alternate site lacks capacity for real workloads.
- Security controls block recovery because emergency exceptions were not planned.

These findings are valuable because they show where the plan is theoretical rather than operational.

**Security considerations during DR**

Recovery must not weaken security beyond what is necessary.

- Use break-glass access with strong logging and post-event review.
- Protect backup repositories with immutability or offline copies where feasible.
- Verify that restored systems are patched and scanned before returning to production.
- Re-enable monitoring and alerting as early as possible.
- Review any emergency changes after the event and remove temporary access.

A DR event is a high-risk period for attackers because teams are distracted and may bypass normal controls. The exercise should include security checkpoints, not just availability goals.

> A recovery that restores service but exposes sensitive data, weak authentication, or unmonitored systems is not a successful recovery.

**CISSP exam perspective**

For CISSP-style questions, focus on priorities and governance.

- Protect human life and safety first.
- Follow the approved plan and escalation chain.
- Validate business impact, not just technical uptime.
- Use exercises to identify gaps before a real incident.
- Document results and drive continuous improvement.
- Balance recovery speed with security and compliance.

If a question asks what to do first, choose the action that preserves safety, supports business continuity, and aligns with the organization’s approved recovery strategy.

**Quick comparison: BCP vs DR in an exercise**

| Area | BCP | DR |
|---|---|---|
| Primary focus | Continue business operations | Restore IT services and data |
| Typical participants | Business owners, operations, communications | Infrastructure, app, database, security teams |
| Success measure | Work can continue through disruption | Systems and data are recovered correctly |
| Common test | Manual workaround, alternate site, communications | Backup restore, failover, replication validation |

**Practical checklist**

Use this checklist when preparing a BCP/DR exercise:

- Confirm the BIA, RTO, and RPO for the systems in scope.
- Choose an exercise type that matches maturity and risk tolerance.
- Include business, technical, security, and communications roles.
- Prepare realistic injects and evidence artifacts.
- Define pass/fail criteria before the exercise starts.
- Record timestamps, decisions, and deviations.
- Validate restore order, dependencies, and access paths.
- Conduct an after-action review and track remediation.
- Retest major fixes after changes are implemented.

**Key takeaway**

A BCP/DR exercise proves whether your continuity and recovery plans work under pressure. The best exercises are realistic, measurable, and safe, and they produce actionable improvements in people, process, and technology.
