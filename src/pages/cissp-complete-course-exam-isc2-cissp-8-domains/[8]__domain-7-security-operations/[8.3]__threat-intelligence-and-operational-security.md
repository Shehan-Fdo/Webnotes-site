## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Threat intelligence overview**

Threat intelligence is the process of collecting, analyzing, and applying information about adversaries, their capabilities, infrastructure, and intent so you can make better security decisions. In CISSP terms, it is not just about indicators like IP addresses or hashes; it is about turning raw data into actionable knowledge for defense, risk reduction, and response.

Threat intelligence supports:
- **Risk management** by identifying likely threats and attack paths.
- **Incident response** by enriching alerts and speeding triage.
- **Vulnerability management** by prioritizing patching based on active exploitation.
- **Security architecture** by informing control selection and detection design.
- **Business decisions** by translating technical findings into impact and likelihood.

A useful mental model is the progression from data to intelligence:
- **Data** is raw, unprocessed observation.
- **Information** is data with context.
- **Intelligence** is analyzed information that supports a decision.
- **Action** is the control, alert, or response taken from that decision.

**Threat intelligence consumers and stakeholders**

Different audiences need different outputs. CISSP questions often test whether you can tailor intelligence to the consumer.

- **SOC analysts** need fast, operational outputs such as enriched alerts, indicators of compromise (IOCs), and detection logic.
- **Incident response (IR)** teams need timelines, scope, attacker tactics, and containment guidance.
- **Threat hunters** need hypotheses, behavioral patterns, and telemetry pivots.
- **Management** needs business impact, trends, risk posture, and decision options.
- **Legal** needs evidence handling, chain of custody, disclosure considerations, and regulatory implications.
- **Privacy** teams need data minimization, lawful processing, and cross-border handling concerns.
- **Business owners** need operational impact, likelihood, and prioritization in business language.
- **Security architecture** teams need adversary tactics, control gaps, and design recommendations.

The same intelligence product should not be delivered identically to every audience. For example:
- A SOC alert may include a malicious domain, related hashes, and a recommended block action.
- A management brief may summarize that a ransomware group is targeting the industry and that backup resilience is the key control gap.
- A legal review may focus on whether sharing the data externally violates contractual or privacy restrictions.

**Intelligence lifecycle and workflow**

Threat intelligence is usually managed as a cycle rather than a one-time report.

1. **Plan and direct**
   - Define intelligence requirements.
   - Identify consumers and decision needs.
   - Set priorities based on risk and business objectives.

2. **Collect**
   - Gather data from internal telemetry, external feeds, open-source intelligence (OSINT), commercial feeds, ISACs, and partner sharing.
   - Use collection methods that match the requirement, not just the easiest source.

3. **Process**
   - Normalize, deduplicate, enrich, and structure the data.
   - Convert raw logs and reports into usable records.

4. **Analyze**
   - Correlate sources, identify patterns, and assess confidence.
   - Map activity to tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs).

5. **Disseminate**
   - Deliver the right format to the right audience.
   - Apply classification, handling instructions, and need-to-know restrictions.

6. **Feedback**
   - Measure usefulness.
   - Refine requirements and collection priorities.

**Intelligence requirements and collection management**

CISSP exam questions often distinguish between broad curiosity and actionable requirements. Intelligence collection should be driven by requirements, not by random feed consumption.

- **Priority Intelligence Requirements (PIRs)** are the most important questions leadership needs answered to support decisions.
- **Specific Intelligence Requirements (SIRs)** break PIRs into narrower, collectible questions.
- **Essential Elements of Information (EEIs)** are the specific facts needed to answer the SIRs.

A practical hierarchy looks like this:
- PIR: “Are we likely to be targeted by ransomware in the next quarter?”
- SIR: “Which ransomware groups are active against our sector?”
- EEI: “What initial access methods, malware families, and victim profiles are associated with those groups?”

Requirements drive collection in several ways:
- They determine which sources matter.
- They define collection frequency and timeliness.
- They help avoid collecting irrelevant data.
- They support prioritization when resources are limited.

Collection management is the process of assigning, coordinating, and validating collection tasks so requirements are met efficiently. In practice, you decide:
- What to collect.
- From where to collect it.
- How often to collect it.
- How to validate that the collection is complete enough.

**Sources, reliability, and confidence**

Not all intelligence sources are equal. CISSP-style analysis expects you to evaluate both the source and the content.

- **Source reliability** is your assessment of the source’s historical trustworthiness.
- **Information credibility** is your assessment of whether the specific item is believable.
- **Confidence** is the analyst’s judgment about how strongly the evidence supports the conclusion.

A common analytic approach is to separate source and content evaluation:
- A source may be generally reliable but provide a weak or incomplete report.
- A low-trust source may still provide accurate information if corroborated.
- A single indicator rarely justifies a high-confidence conclusion.

Validation methods include:
- Correlating with multiple independent sources.
- Checking whether indicators appear in internal telemetry.
- Comparing claims against known attacker TTPs.
- Testing indicators in a sandbox or lab.
- Reviewing timestamps, provenance, and consistency.

Example confidence language:
- **High confidence**: multiple independent sources and internal telemetry support the conclusion.
- **Moderate confidence**: evidence is consistent but incomplete.
- **Low confidence**: limited corroboration or a single unverified source.

A simple internal scoring model might look like this:

```text
Source reliability: A = completely reliable, B = usually reliable, C = fairly reliable, D = not usually reliable, E = unreliable, F = cannot be judged
Information credibility: 1 = confirmed, 2 = probably true, 3 = possibly true, 4 = doubtful, 5 = improbable, 6 = cannot be judged
Confidence: High / Medium / Low based on corroboration and analytic strength
```

**Threat intelligence frameworks and models**

Several frameworks help structure analysis and detection engineering.

- **Cyber Kill Chain** describes stages of an intrusion from reconnaissance through actions on objectives.
- **MITRE ATT&CK** catalogs adversary tactics, techniques, and procedures across enterprise, mobile, and cloud environments.
- **TTPs** are the behaviors and methods attackers use; they are more durable than individual indicators.

How they relate:
- The Cyber Kill Chain is useful for understanding attack progression and where to disrupt it.
- MITRE ATT&CK is useful for mapping observed behavior to known techniques and building detections.
- TTP analysis helps you move beyond single IOCs and focus on repeatable attacker behavior.

Example:
- A phishing email with a malicious attachment is an initial access technique.
- PowerShell download-and-execute behavior is a technique that may recur across campaigns.
- A detection rule based only on one hash is brittle; a rule based on suspicious PowerShell behavior is more resilient.

A practical ATT&CK-oriented detection workflow:
- Identify the observed behavior.
- Map it to ATT&CK techniques.
- Determine what telemetry can detect it.
- Create or tune detections.
- Validate with test activity and red-team or purple-team exercises.

**Operational security as a process**

Operational security (OPSEC) is the process of identifying critical information, analyzing threats, assessing vulnerabilities, and applying countermeasures to reduce the chance that an adversary can infer sensitive facts from your actions.

OPSEC is not just a set of tools. It is a disciplined process:
- Identify what must be protected.
- Determine what an adversary can observe.
- Analyze how the adversary could infer sensitive information.
- Apply countermeasures to reduce exposure.

Adversaries often infer information from:
- Public job postings.
- Metadata in documents and images.
- DNS, email, and certificate records.
- Social media posts and conference talks.
- Traffic patterns, timing, and infrastructure reuse.
- Cloud asset naming conventions and exposed services.

Examples of indicators of exposure:
- A build server name reveals a product codename.
- A document author field reveals an internal team.
- Repeated login times reveal shift schedules.
- A staging hostname reveals a new service before launch.

Countermeasures reduce what can be observed or inferred:
- Strip metadata from documents and images.
- Use separate identities and infrastructure for sensitive work.
- Minimize public disclosure of internal details.
- Randomize or standardize naming conventions.
- Limit information in logs, tickets, and public repositories.
- Review external communications for accidental disclosure.

> OPSEC failures often come from small, repeated leaks rather than one dramatic mistake.

**Threat intelligence sharing and governance**

Sharing intelligence can improve collective defense, but it must be governed carefully. CISSP questions often focus on whether sharing is allowed, appropriate, and properly marked.

Key governance concerns:
- **Legal restrictions** such as contracts, NDAs, export controls, and privacy laws.
- **Ethical concerns** about exposing personal data or over-sharing sensitive details.
- **Privacy requirements** including data minimization and purpose limitation.
- **Classification and handling** rules that define who may access or redistribute the information.
- **Attribution risk** if sharing reveals sources, methods, or ongoing investigations.

When sharing intelligence, consider:
- Whether the data contains personal information.
- Whether the recipient is authorized and has a legitimate need.
- Whether the content should be sanitized or aggregated.
- Whether the report needs classification markings and handling caveats.
- Whether the original source permits redistribution.

Common handling practices:
- Mark reports with classification or sensitivity labels.
- Add caveats such as “For internal use only” or “Do not redistribute.”
- Remove unnecessary identifiers before sharing.
- Separate raw evidence from sanitized intelligence products.
- Track provenance so recipients know where the information came from.

Example of a sanitized sharing note:
```text
Classification: Internal Use Only
Handling: Do not redistribute outside approved partners
Content: Indicators derived from internal telemetry and partner reporting
Privacy: Personal data removed
```

**Intelligence products and outputs**

Different products serve different purposes. The format should match the audience and decision need.

- **Strategic intelligence** supports executives and long-term planning.
- **Operational intelligence** supports campaigns, incidents, and near-term defense.
- **Tactical intelligence** supports detections, blocking, and immediate response.
- **Technical intelligence** focuses on indicators, malware artifacts, and infrastructure.

Examples of outputs:
- Executive summary on threat trends and business impact.
- Analyst report on a threat actor’s TTPs and likely targets.
- IOC package for SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) or EDR (Endpoint Detection and Response) ingestion.
- Detection engineering note with ATT&CK mappings and telemetry requirements.
- Incident enrichment brief with scope, timeline, and containment actions.

Sample intelligence report structure:
```text
Title: Suspected credential theft campaign targeting finance users
Summary: Multiple phishing emails delivered a fake login page and captured credentials.
Confidence: Moderate
Observed indicators:
- Domain: login-example[.]com
- URL path: /auth/verify
- Hash: 9f2c...
Assessment:
- Initial access via phishing
- Likely credential harvesting
- Possible follow-on MFA fatigue attempts
Recommended actions:
- Block domain and related IPs
- Hunt for similar email subjects
- Reset exposed credentials
```

**Practical workflow example: from collection to action**

A full end-to-end use case helps connect the concepts.

1. **Requirement**
   - The business wants to know whether a ransomware group is targeting the organization’s sector.

2. **Collection**
   - You gather OSINT, commercial feed data, ISAC alerts, and internal detections.
   - You review recent phishing reports, malware telemetry, and dark web mentions if legally permitted.

3. **Processing**
   - You normalize domains, hashes, and IPs.
   - You remove duplicates and enrich with WHOIS, passive DNS, and sandbox results.

4. **Analysis**
   - You map observed behavior to ATT&CK techniques.
   - You compare the campaign to known ransomware TTPs.
   - You assess confidence based on corroboration.

5. **Dissemination**
   - SOC receives IOCs and detection logic.
   - IR receives a likely attack path and containment priorities.
   - Management receives a short risk summary and recommended actions.

6. **Action**
   - You block malicious infrastructure.
   - You tune detections for suspicious PowerShell and credential dumping.
   - You prioritize patching of exposed remote access systems.
   - You brief leadership on backup readiness and recovery assumptions.

**How intelligence supports other security functions**

Threat intelligence is most valuable when it changes decisions.

- **Risk management**
  - Helps estimate likelihood and impact using current threat activity.
  - Supports risk acceptance, mitigation, transfer, or avoidance decisions.

- **Incident response**
  - Speeds triage by identifying known adversary infrastructure and behaviors.
  - Helps determine scope, dwell time, and likely next steps.

- **Vulnerability management**
  - Prioritizes remediation for vulnerabilities under active exploitation.
  - Helps distinguish theoretical exposure from real-world risk.

- **Security architecture**
  - Informs segmentation, hardening, logging, and identity controls.
  - Supports design choices based on likely attack paths.

- **Detection engineering**
  - Converts TTPs into rules, queries, and behavioral analytics.
  - Reduces dependence on fragile single-indicator detections.

**Operational security countermeasures in practice**

OPSEC countermeasures should be proportional to the sensitivity of the activity.

- Use separate accounts and devices for sensitive research.
- Avoid reusing usernames, emails, or infrastructure across contexts.
- Limit public exposure of internal naming conventions.
- Review metadata before publishing documents or images.
- Use least privilege for access to sensitive intelligence.
- Train staff to avoid oversharing in tickets, chats, and social media.

Example metadata removal on Linux:
```bash
exiftool -all= report.pdf
exiftool -all= image.jpg
```

Example of checking DNS and certificate exposure:
```bash
dig example.internal
whois example.com
openssl s_client -connect example.com:443 -servername example.com
```

Example of a simple enrichment workflow:
```bash
# Check whether an IP appears in passive DNS or reputation tools
curl -s "https://api.example.com/reputation?ip=203.0.113.10"

# Search internal logs for a domain
grep -R "malicious-domain.example" /var/log/
```

**Exam-oriented distinctions**

CISSP questions often test subtle differences.

- **Threat intelligence** is about understanding adversaries and using that understanding to improve defense.
- **OPSEC** is about preventing adversaries from learning sensitive information through observation and inference.
- **IOC-based detection** is narrow and can be evaded.
- **TTP-based detection** is more durable and behavior-focused.
- **Collection requirements** should drive what you gather.
- **Source evaluation** should drive how much you trust it.
- **Sharing** must respect legal, ethical, privacy, and classification constraints.

If a question asks what to do first, think in terms of:
- Define the requirement.
- Identify the consumer.
- Determine the needed confidence.
- Choose the right source and handling rules.
- Deliver the right product in the right format.

**Quick comparison of intelligence levels**

| Level | Focus | Typical consumer | Example output |
|---|---|---|---|
| Strategic | Long-term trends and business impact | Executives, risk owners | Quarterly threat landscape brief |
| Operational | Campaigns and adversary plans | IR, SOC leads, threat hunters | Actor profile and campaign summary |
| Tactical | TTPs and detections | SOC, detection engineers | ATT&CK-mapped detection guidance |
| Technical | IOCs and artifacts | SOC, tooling pipelines | Hashes, domains, IPs, YARA/Sigma rules |

**Practical detection engineering example**

A behavior-based Sigma rule can detect suspicious PowerShell usage more effectively than a single hash.

```yaml
title: "Suspicious PowerShell Download Cradle"
logsource:
  product: windows
  category: process_creation
detection:
  selection:
    Image|endswith: '\powershell.exe'
    CommandLine|contains:
      - 'DownloadString'
      - 'IEX'
      - 'Invoke-WebRequest'
  condition: selection
level: high
fields:
  - Image
  - CommandLine
  - ParentImage
```

This kind of rule is useful because it targets behavior associated with multiple campaigns, not just one sample.

**Key takeaways**

- Threat intelligence turns raw observations into decisions.
- Requirements such as PIRs and SIRs should drive collection.
- Source reliability, credibility, and confidence are separate judgments.
- ATT&CK and the Cyber Kill Chain help structure analysis and detection.
- OPSEC is a process for reducing adversary observation and inference.
- Sharing intelligence requires legal, ethical, privacy, and classification controls.
- The best intelligence product is the one that helps the right audience act.
