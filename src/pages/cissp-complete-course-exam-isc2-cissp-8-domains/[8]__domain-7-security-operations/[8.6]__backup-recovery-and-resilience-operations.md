## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Backup, recovery, and resilience fundamentals**

Backup and recovery protect data and services from loss, corruption, ransomware, hardware failure, human error, and site-wide disruption. In CISSP terms, you need to connect business requirements to technical controls: how much data loss is acceptable, how long recovery can take, and what level of service continuity the organization needs.

Two core planning metrics drive most decisions:

- **RPO** (Recovery Point Objective): the maximum acceptable data loss measured in time.
- **RTO** (Recovery Time Objective): the maximum acceptable downtime before service must be restored.

A low RPO usually requires more frequent backups, replication, journaling, or continuous data protection. A low RTO usually requires faster restore methods, warm/hot sites, automation, and prebuilt recovery runbooks.

**Business impact and resilience design**

You select backup and recovery controls based on business impact analysis, not just technical preference. Critical systems with revenue, safety, or regulatory impact need stronger resilience than low-priority systems.

Key resilience concepts include:

- **Fault tolerance**: the system continues operating when a component fails.
- **Redundancy**: duplicate components or services reduce the chance of outage.
- **Single point of failure**: one component whose failure can stop the service.
- **High availability**: design goal that minimizes downtime through redundancy and failover.
- **Resilience**: the ability to absorb disruption and recover service quickly.

Common design patterns include:

- RAID for storage redundancy, while remembering RAID is not a backup.
- Clustering and load balancing for service continuity.
- Replication across availability zones or sites.
- UPS and generator power for short-term continuity.
- Diverse network paths and providers to reduce correlated failure.

A practical resilience review asks:

- What fails if this server, storage array, or link goes down?
- Is there a second path, second node, or second site?
- Does the backup strategy protect against accidental deletion, corruption, and ransomware?
- Can the organization restore within the required RTO and RPO?

**Backup types and what they protect**

Backups differ by scope, speed, and restore complexity. You choose the type based on how much data you can afford to lose and how quickly you need to recover.

- **Full backup**: copies all selected data each time.
  - Simplest to restore.
  - Uses the most storage and backup window.
- **Incremental backup**: copies changes since the last backup of any type.
  - Fastest to run and smallest to store.
  - Restore requires the last full backup plus each incremental in sequence.
- **Differential backup**: copies changes since the last full backup.
  - Larger than incremental, smaller than full.
  - Restore requires the last full backup plus the latest differential.
- **Synthetic full backup**: backup software combines prior backups into a new full image without rereading all source data.
  - Reduces source load.
  - Common in enterprise backup platforms.
- **Continuous data protection**: captures changes nearly continuously or at very short intervals.
  - Supports very low RPO.
  - Often used for critical databases or transaction systems.

Other important backup forms:

- **Image-based backup**: captures an entire system image, including OS, applications, and configuration.
  - Useful for rapid server rebuilds and bare-metal recovery.
- **Bare-metal recovery**: restores a system to dissimilar or empty hardware from an image or recovery media.
  - Important when the original machine is lost or destroyed.
- **System state backup**: captures OS configuration components such as registry, boot files, directory services, and system settings.
  - Common in Windows recovery planning.
- **File-level backup**: protects selected files and folders.
  - Good for user data and less useful for full system recovery.
- **Database backup**: protects database files and transaction logs.
  - Must account for consistency and point-in-time recovery.

**Backup frequency and scheduling strategy**

Backup frequency should follow the business RPO. The shorter the acceptable data loss window, the more frequent the protection mechanism must be.

Typical scheduling patterns:

- **Continuous or near-continuous**
  - Use for very low RPO systems such as financial transactions, healthcare records, or critical operational databases.
  - Often implemented with replication, journaling, or continuous data protection.
- **Hourly**
  - Use when losing up to an hour of data is acceptable.
  - Common for important application data and frequently changing file shares.
- **Daily**
  - Use when one business day of loss is acceptable.
  - Common for general-purpose servers and end-user data.
- **Weekly**
  - Use for lower-value systems or archival data where longer loss windows are acceptable.
  - Often paired with daily incrementals.

A simple selection rule is:

- If the business says “we can lose no more than 15 minutes,” you need replication or continuous protection.
- If the business says “we can lose one business day,” daily backups may be sufficient.
- If the business says “we can rebuild from source systems,” you may only need periodic archival copies.

A common enterprise schedule looks like this:

```text
Critical database:
- Continuous log shipping or replication
- Hourly snapshots
- Daily full backup
- Weekly offsite copy

File server:
- Nightly incremental
- Weekly full
- Monthly archive

Low-priority lab system:
- Weekly full
- 30-day retention
```

**Backup media, storage targets, and the 3-2-1 principle**

The classic **3-2-1 rule** means:

- Keep at least 3 copies of data.
- Store copies on 2 different media or storage types.
- Keep 1 copy offsite or isolated.

Modern variants often add:

- An immutable or write-once copy.
- An offline or air-gapped copy.
- A geographically separate copy for regional disasters.

Common backup targets include:

- Disk-to-disk for fast backup and restore.
- Disk-to-tape for long retention and offline protection.
- Cloud object storage for durability and geographic separation.
- Immutable storage for ransomware resistance.

Tradeoffs matter:

- Disk is fast but often more exposed to online compromise.
- Tape is slower but supports offline retention and low-cost archival.
- Cloud improves geographic resilience but requires strong identity and access controls.

**Database and application backup specifics**

Application-aware backups matter because many systems cannot be safely protected as raw files alone. Databases and transactional applications need consistency guarantees.

Important concepts:

- **Quiescing**: temporarily pausing writes or flushing buffers so the backup captures a consistent state.
- **Crash-consistent backup**: captures data as if power failed suddenly; the application may need recovery on restore.
- **Application-consistent backup**: coordinates with the application to ensure a clean, usable backup state.
- **Point-in-time recovery**: restores data to a specific moment using a full backup plus transaction or redo logs.
- **Log shipping**: copies transaction logs to another system for recovery or warm standby use.

For databases, you usually combine:

- A periodic full backup.
- Frequent differential or incremental backups.
- Continuous transaction log backups.
- Log retention long enough to support the required RPO and recovery window.

Example SQL Server-style backup pattern:

```sql
BACKUP DATABASE Sales
TO DISK = 'D:\Backups\Sales_full.bak'
WITH COMPRESSION, CHECKSUM;

BACKUP LOG Sales
TO DISK = 'D:\Backups\Sales_log.trn'
WITH COMPRESSION, CHECKSUM;
```

Example PostgreSQL-style logical backup:

```bash
pg_dump -Fc -f /backups/appdb_$(date +%F).dump appdb
```

Example MySQL-style backup with consistency considerations:

```bash
mysqldump --single-transaction --routines --triggers appdb > /backups/appdb.sql
```

Practical implications:

- Use application-consistent backups for databases, mail systems, and directory services when possible.
- Use crash-consistent backups only when the application can reliably recover on startup.
- Keep transaction logs if you need point-in-time recovery.
- Test restores, not just backup creation, because a successful backup job does not guarantee recoverability.

**Backup verification and monitoring**

Backup verification is essential because a completed job is not the same as a usable backup. Verification should cover data integrity, media health, and operational alerting.

Verification methods include:

- **Checksum or hash validation**
  - Compare source and backup hashes when supported.
  - Detects corruption during transfer or storage.
- **Media integrity checks**
  - Validate tape, disk, or object storage health.
  - Identify bad sectors, failed cartridges, or unreadable blocks.
- **Automated restore tests**
  - Restore selected files, databases, or VM images to a test environment.
  - Confirms the backup can actually be used.
- **Backup job monitoring and alerting**
  - Alert on failures, missed schedules, unusual duration, or low capacity.
  - Track trends that indicate backup window growth or storage exhaustion.

Useful operational controls:

- Daily review of backup dashboards.
- Alert escalation for failed jobs.
- Periodic checksum verification on archived media.
- Restore sampling from each critical system class.

Example monitoring logic:

```text
If backup job status != SUCCESS:
  send alert to operations
  open incident ticket
  retry once if transient
  escalate if second failure occurs
```

**Retention, rotation, and media lifecycle**

Retention defines how long backups are kept. Rotation defines how media is reused or retired. Both must align with legal, regulatory, operational, and storage requirements.

Common rotation schemes include:

- **Grandfather-Father-Son (GFS)**
  - Daily, weekly, and monthly generations.
  - Good for balancing short-term recovery and long-term retention.
- **Tower of Hanoi**
  - Rotates media in a pattern that reduces the number of tapes needed while preserving multiple restore points.
  - Often referenced in exam prep as a classic media rotation model.
- **FIFO** (first in, first out)
  - Oldest backups are overwritten first.
  - Simple, but may not preserve enough historical recovery points.
- **Harmonic or custom generation schemes**
  - Used when organizations need specific retention windows or regulatory archives.

Example GFS retention policy:

```text
Daily backups: keep 14 days
Weekly backups: keep 8 weeks
Monthly backups: keep 12 months
Year-end archives: keep 7 years
```

Media lifecycle management must include secure disposal:

- Sanitize storage media before reuse or disposal.
- Destroy media when sanitization is not feasible or required by policy.
- Maintain chain of custody for regulated or sensitive backups.
- Document retention expiration and destruction approval.

Secure disposal methods depend on media type:

- **Overwrite or cryptographic erase** for reusable disks and encrypted storage.
- **Degaussing** for magnetic media when appropriate.
- **Shredding, pulverizing, or incineration** for physical destruction.
- **Vendor-certified destruction** when using third-party disposal services.

> If backup media contains regulated data, expired retention does not mean “delete casually.” You still need approved sanitization or destruction procedures.

**Recovery methods and restore scope**

Recovery is not one action; it is a set of restore methods with different speed and completeness.

Common recovery methods:

- **File restore**
  - Recover a single file or folder.
  - Fast and low impact.
- **Volume or partition restore**
  - Recover a logical storage unit.
  - Useful after corruption or accidental deletion.
- **System image restore**
  - Rebuild a server or workstation from an image.
  - Supports bare-metal recovery.
- **Application restore**
  - Recover application data and configuration.
  - May require log replay or service reconfiguration.
- **Full site recovery**
  - Restore services at an alternate site after a major outage.

Restore planning should define:

- Which systems are restored first.
- Which dependencies must come online before applications.
- Who approves the recovery state.
- How you validate service health after restore.

A practical restore order often looks like:

1. Identity and directory services.
2. Core network services such as DNS and DHCP.
3. Storage and virtualization platforms.
4. Databases.
5. Application tiers.
6. End-user and reporting services.

**Disaster recovery sites and failover**

Disaster recovery (DR) sites provide alternate processing capability when the primary site is unavailable. The site type determines cost, readiness, and recovery speed.

Common site types:

- **Hot site**
  - Fully equipped and ready to take over quickly.
  - Lowest RTO, highest cost.
- **Warm site**
  - Partially equipped and requires some setup.
  - Balanced cost and recovery time.
- **Cold site**
  - Basic facility with power and space but little or no preinstalled equipment.
  - Lowest cost, highest RTO.

Failover and failback are distinct:

- **Failover** moves production to the alternate site after an outage or planned maintenance.
- **Failback** returns operations to the primary site after it is repaired and validated.

A sound DR activation process includes:

1. Detect the outage and assess scope.
2. Determine whether the event exceeds the recovery threshold.
3. Declare a disaster when business impact, duration, or risk justifies alternate-site activation.
4. Activate the DR runbook and notify stakeholders.
5. Restore critical services in priority order.
6. Validate business functions and data integrity.
7. Plan failback only after the primary site is stable and tested.

When to declare a disaster depends on business criteria, not emotion. You typically consider:

- Expected outage duration versus RTO.
- Safety, legal, or regulatory impact.
- Availability of staff and facilities.
- Risk of data corruption or further damage.

Example DR runbook structure:

```text
1. Incident declaration
2. Stakeholder notification
3. Infrastructure assessment
4. Alternate site activation
5. Identity/DNS restoration
6. Database recovery
7. Application cutover
8. Business validation
9. Failback planning
10. Post-incident review
```

**Testing and exercising recovery plans**

Recovery plans must be tested regularly because untested plans often fail under real pressure. Testing should involve technical teams and business stakeholders.

Common test types:

- **Tabletop exercise**
  - Walk through the scenario verbally.
  - Good for validating roles, decisions, and communications.
- **Partial restore test**
  - Restore selected files, databases, or services.
  - Confirms backup usability without full disruption.
- **Simulation**
  - Mimics outage conditions without moving production.
  - Useful for validating decision-making and escalation.
- **Parallel test**
  - Run recovery systems alongside production without switching users.
  - Helps validate alternate-site readiness.
- **Full failover test**
  - Move production to the DR site.
  - Best validation, but highest risk and coordination effort.
- **Scheduled DR drill**
  - Planned exercise with business stakeholders.
  - Confirms operational readiness and recovery timing.

Testing should verify:

- RTO and RPO are actually achievable.
- Dependencies are documented and available.
- Contact lists and escalation paths are current.
- Backup media and credentials are accessible.
- Failback steps are understood and rehearsed.

**Practical enterprise backup scenarios**

A realistic backup design often combines multiple methods.

Example 1: Critical database with low RPO

```text
- Continuous log shipping to secondary site
- Hourly snapshots
- Nightly full backup
- Immutable weekly copy to object storage
- Monthly offline archive
- Quarterly restore test
```

Why this works:

- Log shipping and frequent snapshots reduce data loss.
- Immutable and offline copies protect against ransomware.
- Restore tests confirm the process remains usable.

Example 2: Virtualized application server

```text
- Daily image-based backup
- Hourly application-consistent snapshots
- 30-day retention on disk
- 90-day copy to cloud archive
- Bare-metal recovery media stored separately
```

Why this works:

- Image-based backup speeds rebuild.
- Application consistency reduces corruption risk.
- Separate recovery media supports disaster scenarios.

Example 3: File services with moderate RPO

```text
- Nightly incremental backup
- Weekly full backup
- Monthly archive retained for compliance
- Restore sampling every week
```

Why this works:

- The business can tolerate overnight loss.
- Restore sampling catches silent backup failures.

**Configuration examples and operational snippets**

A backup policy should be explicit about schedule, retention, and verification.

Example policy snippet:

```yaml
backup_policy:
  critical_db:
    schedule:
      full: "sunday 01:00"
      incremental: "hourly"
      logs: "every 15 minutes"
    retention:
      daily: 14
      weekly: 8
      monthly: 12
    verification:
      checksum: true
      restore_test: "weekly"
      alert_on_failure: true
```

Example Linux `rsync`-based file backup:

```bash
rsync -aHAX --delete /srv/data/ /backup/data/
```

Example encrypted archive creation:

```bash
tar -czf - /srv/data | gpg --symmetric --cipher-algo AES256 -o /backup/data.tar.gz.gpg
```

Example Windows system state backup concept:

```powershell
wbadmin start systemstatebackup -backuptarget:E: -quiet
```

These examples are not universal standards, but they illustrate the kinds of controls exam questions expect you to recognize: schedule, retention, encryption, verification, and restore readiness.

**Common pitfalls and exam traps**

- Treating RAID as a backup instead of a redundancy control.
- Confusing replication with backup; replication can copy corruption instantly.
- Ignoring application consistency for databases and directory services.
- Designing for RPO without considering RTO, or vice versa.
- Keeping backups online without immutability or offline copies.
- Failing to test restores and DR activation procedures.
- Forgetting secure disposal of expired backup media.
- Overlooking dependencies such as DNS, identity, and storage during recovery.
- Assuming a successful backup job means the data is recoverable.

**CISSP-focused takeaways**

For exam purposes, always tie the control to the business objective:

- Use **RPO** to choose backup frequency and replication strategy.
- Use **RTO** to choose restore method, site type, and automation level.
- Use **application-consistent** backups for transactional systems when possible.
- Use **image-based** and **bare-metal recovery** for fast system rebuilds.
- Use **verification** and **restore testing** to prove recoverability.
- Use **rotation, retention, and secure disposal** to manage media safely.
- Use **failover/failback** planning and DR drills to validate resilience.
