## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Incident response playbook fundamentals**

An **incident response playbook** is a repeatable, documented set of actions for handling a specific incident type. It turns policy into operational steps so you can respond consistently under pressure, preserve evidence, and reduce business impact.

A strong playbook defines:
- **Scope**: what incident types it covers, such as malware, insider threat, data exfiltration, or lost device.
- **Roles**: who leads, who approves, and who communicates.
- **Decision points**: when to isolate systems, notify legal, or escalate externally.
- **Evidence handling**: what to collect, how to preserve it, and who may access it.
- **Exit criteria**: what must be true before recovery and closure.

Playbooks should align with the organization’s incident response policy, business continuity requirements, legal obligations, and regulatory environment. In practice, you use them to reduce improvisation and to ensure that technical actions do not destroy evidence or violate retention rules.

**Incident response phases in CISSP framing**

The standard incident response lifecycle is commonly described as:
1. **Preparation**
2. **Detection and analysis**
3. **Containment**
4. **Eradication**
5. **Recovery**
6. **Post-incident activity**

Each phase has a different goal, and evidence handling changes as the incident progresses.

- **Preparation**: build playbooks, train staff, define contacts, and pre-stage tools.
- **Detection and analysis**: confirm whether an event is an incident, classify severity, and begin evidence preservation.
- **Containment**: stop spread or further loss while minimizing evidence destruction.
- **Eradication**: remove malware, unauthorized accounts, persistence mechanisms, and root causes.
- **Recovery**: restore systems, validate integrity, and monitor for recurrence.
- **Post-incident activity**: lessons learned, reporting, control improvements, and retention review.

> Do not confuse containment with eradication. Containment limits damage; eradication removes the cause.

**Preparation phase**

Preparation is where most incident response quality is won or lost. You create the conditions that let responders act quickly without improvising legal, technical, or communication decisions.

Key preparation activities:
- Maintain an incident response policy and playbooks for common scenarios.
- Define severity levels and escalation thresholds.
- Establish contact lists for legal counsel, HR, privacy, compliance, law enforcement, regulators, and vendors.
- Pre-approve forensic tools and evidence storage locations.
- Train staff on evidence preservation and chain of custody.
- Test procedures through tabletop exercises and simulations.
- Define logging, time synchronization, and retention requirements.

Practical preparation examples:
- Configure centralized logging with time sync via NTP (Network Time Protocol).
- Ensure endpoint detection and response tools can isolate hosts without wiping volatile data.
- Create a secure evidence repository with restricted access and audit logging.
- Prepare templates for incident tickets, evidence logs, and executive notifications.

Example Linux triage commands:
```bash
date -u
hostnamectl
who
w
ps auxf
ss -tulpn
ip addr
journalctl --since "1 hour ago"
```

Example Windows triage commands:
```powershell
Get-Date
hostname
Get-Process | Sort-Object CPU -Descending | Select-Object -First 10
Get-NetTCPConnection | Sort-Object State
Get-WinEvent -LogName Security -MaxEvents 50
```

**Detection and analysis**

Detection and analysis determine whether the event is real, how severe it is, and what evidence must be preserved immediately. This phase often starts with alerts from security tools, user reports, logs, or third parties.

Your goals are to:
- Confirm the incident type and scope.
- Identify affected assets, accounts, and data.
- Preserve volatile evidence before it disappears.
- Establish a timeline of events.
- Determine whether legal, HR, or external notification is required.

Evidence sources during analysis may include:
- Endpoint logs and memory artifacts.
- Network telemetry, firewall logs, proxy logs, DNS logs, and packet captures.
- Cloud audit logs and SaaS activity logs.
- Identity provider logs, such as SSO and MFA events.
- Email headers and message tracking.
- Physical evidence, such as paper records or removable media.

Useful analysis commands:
```bash
# Linux process and network snapshot
ps -ef
lsof -i
netstat -plant
last -a
```

```powershell
# Windows event and service review
Get-Service | Where-Object {$_.Status -eq "Running"}
Get-WinEvent -FilterHashtable @{LogName='System'; StartTime=(Get-Date).AddHours(-4)}
```

**Containment strategy**

Containment is about limiting damage while preserving evidence. You choose between short-term containment and long-term containment depending on business impact and confidence in your analysis.

Short-term containment examples:
- Isolate an endpoint from the network.
- Disable a compromised account.
- Block malicious IP addresses or domains.
- Quarantine suspicious email.
- Suspend a vulnerable integration or API key.

Long-term containment examples:
- Rebuild a compromised server.
- Rotate credentials and keys.
- Segment affected networks.
- Apply compensating controls.
- Move workloads to a clean environment.

Containment decisions should consider:
- Whether the attacker is still active.
- Whether isolation will destroy volatile evidence.
- Whether business operations can tolerate the action.
- Whether the incident involves regulated data or safety-critical systems.

> If you suspect active exfiltration, preserve logs and volatile data before making disruptive changes when feasible.

**Eradication**

Eradication removes the attacker’s foothold and the root cause. This phase is not just “delete malware”; it includes eliminating persistence, closing the vulnerability, and validating that the environment is clean.

Common eradication tasks:
- Remove malware, web shells, scheduled tasks, and unauthorized services.
- Delete rogue accounts, tokens, SSH keys, and API credentials.
- Patch exploited vulnerabilities.
- Reimage compromised hosts when trust cannot be restored.
- Reset passwords and rotate secrets.
- Remove malicious persistence from startup items, registry keys, cron jobs, or cloud automation.

Examples of persistence checks:
```bash
crontab -l
systemctl list-unit-files --type=service
ls -la /etc/cron.*
```

```powershell
Get-ScheduledTask
Get-ItemProperty "HKCU:\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run"
Get-ItemProperty "HKLM:\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run"
```

**Recovery**

Recovery restores services safely and verifies that the incident does not recur. You should restore from known-good backups, validate integrity, and monitor closely after bringing systems back online.

Recovery activities:
- Restore systems from clean backups or golden images.
- Validate file integrity, configuration baselines, and application behavior.
- Re-enable accounts and services in a controlled manner.
- Increase monitoring for recurrence or lateral movement.
- Confirm that business owners accept the restored state.

Recovery is not complete until you verify:
- The original attack path is closed.
- Logging and alerting are functioning.
- Data integrity is intact.
- The system is stable under normal load.

**Post-incident activity**

Post-incident activity captures lessons learned and drives improvement. This phase is essential for CISSP because it connects technical response to governance and continuous improvement.

Typical post-incident tasks:
- Conduct a lessons-learned review.
- Update playbooks, controls, and training.
- Document root cause and impact.
- Review whether notifications were timely and complete.
- Reassess risk and control effectiveness.
- Adjust retention, monitoring, and detection rules.

A good after-action review answers:
- What happened?
- How was it detected?
- What worked well?
- What failed?
- What should change before the next incident?

**Chain of custody essentials**

**Chain of custody** is the documented history of evidence from collection to final disposition. It proves who handled the evidence, when, where, why, and under what conditions. In investigations, the goal is to show that evidence remained authentic, intact, and traceable.

A complete chain of custody record typically includes:
- Unique evidence identifier.
- Description of the item.
- Case number and incident reference.
- Collector name and organization.
- Date, time, and location of collection.
- Method of acquisition.
- Hash values for digital evidence.
- Every transfer of custody.
- Storage location and access controls.
- Final disposition, such as return, destruction, or retention.

Chain of custody supports:
- **Integrity**: evidence has not been altered.
- **Authenticity**: evidence is what it claims to be.
- **Admissibility**: evidence is more likely to be accepted in legal or disciplinary proceedings.

**Evidence admissibility and governing requirements**

Evidence handling is shaped by more than technical best practice. You must consider:
- Organizational policy.
- Applicable law.
- Jurisdiction.
- Regulatory requirements.
- Contractual obligations.
- Internal disciplinary standards.

Different jurisdictions may require different handling, retention, notification, or privacy controls. Evidence that is acceptable for internal HR action may not meet the standard for criminal prosecution or civil litigation. You should preserve evidence in a way that supports the strictest likely use case when feasible.

Important considerations:
- **Admissibility standards** vary by court and jurisdiction.
- **Policy alignment** matters because internal procedures should match documented practice.
- **Legal requirements** may override normal retention or deletion schedules.
- **Cross-border incidents** can trigger conflicting privacy and transfer rules.

> If legal or regulatory action is possible, preserve evidence before routine cleanup or log rotation can destroy it.

**Legal hold and retention/disposition**

A **legal hold** is an instruction to preserve relevant information because it may be needed for litigation, investigation, audit, or regulatory review. When a legal hold is active, normal retention and disposition schedules are suspended for the covered data.

How legal hold affects evidence handling:
- Do not delete, overwrite, or dispose of covered records.
- Suspend routine log rotation or backup expiration for relevant sources if possible.
- Preserve copies of emails, chat logs, tickets, and forensic images.
- Track who received the hold notice and when.
- Reassess scope as the matter evolves.

Disposition decisions should consider:
- Whether the evidence is subject to legal hold.
- Whether retention is required by law or regulation.
- Whether the evidence is needed for internal discipline or insurance.
- Whether the retention period has expired and no hold applies.

**When to involve legal counsel, HR, law enforcement, or regulators**

You should involve the right stakeholders early, but not indiscriminately. The timing depends on the incident type, evidence sensitivity, and legal exposure.

- **Legal counsel**: involve early when there is potential litigation, privacy impact, breach notification, cross-border data transfer, or law enforcement contact.
- **HR**: involve when the incident may involve employee misconduct, insider threat, policy violations, or disciplinary action.
- **Law enforcement**: involve when there is criminal activity, extortion, fraud, theft, or a requirement to preserve evidence for prosecution.
- **Regulators**: involve when breach notification, sector-specific reporting, or statutory deadlines may apply.

Practical rule:
- Escalate to legal before making statements, sharing evidence externally, or changing retention on sensitive records.
- Coordinate with HR before interviewing employees in a disciplinary context.
- Coordinate with law enforcement before imaging or transferring evidence if they will take possession or direct collection.
- Coordinate with privacy/compliance teams when personal data is involved.

**Evidence types and handling examples**

Digital evidence is only one category. Real incidents often include mixed evidence types, and each type needs careful handling.

- **Paper records**
  - Photograph the scene before moving items when appropriate.
  - Use gloves if contamination is a concern.
  - Label each document set and store it in a sealed evidence envelope.
  - Record page counts and any visible markings.

- **Removable media**
  - Treat USB drives, SD cards, and external disks as high-risk evidence.
  - Prevent automatic mounting or execution.
  - Bag and label the device, then image it using write protection when possible.
  - Record serial numbers and capacity.

- **Mobile devices**
  - Preserve power state if volatile data may matter.
  - Place the device in airplane mode or a Faraday bag only if that matches the investigative plan and does not destroy needed evidence.
  - Document lock state, notifications, SIM status, and visible apps.
  - Coordinate with legal because mobile data often includes personal and regulated information.

Example evidence note for a paper record:
```text
Evidence ID: E-2026-0142
Item: Printed invoice packet, 6 pages
Collected by: A. Patel
Date/Time UTC: 2026-04-26 14:12
Location: Finance office, desk drawer
Condition: Stapled, no visible alteration
Transferred to: Secure evidence cabinet C-3
Disposition: Retained under legal hold
```

Example evidence note for a USB device:
```text
Evidence ID: E-2026-0143
Item: Black 64 GB USB flash drive, no label
Serial: 8C1A-77F2
Collected by: A. Patel
Date/Time UTC: 2026-04-26 14:18
Location: Finance office
Method: Seized and bagged; no mounting on collection host
Hash image: SHA-256 pending
Disposition: Stored in evidence locker
```

**Cloud, SaaS, and third-party evidence**

Modern incidents often involve evidence that you do not physically control. Cloud and SaaS (Software as a Service) evidence requires coordination with providers and careful attention to access, retention, and legal process.

Common cloud/SaaS evidence sources:
- Cloud audit logs, such as admin actions and API calls.
- Identity logs from SSO, MFA, and conditional access.
- Object storage access logs.
- SaaS message logs, file access logs, and sharing events.
- Provider support tickets and incident records.
- Snapshots, exports, and configuration history.

Handling considerations:
- Preserve logs quickly because provider retention may be short.
- Export data in a forensically useful format when possible.
- Record tenant IDs, account IDs, timestamps, and time zones.
- Verify whether the provider can attest to log integrity.
- Use contractual and legal channels for third-party evidence requests.

Practical examples:
- In Microsoft 365, preserve mailbox audit logs, message trace, and eDiscovery exports.
- In AWS, preserve CloudTrail, CloudWatch, IAM, and S3 access logs.
- In Google Workspace, preserve admin audit logs, Drive sharing logs, and Gmail logs where available.

Example cloud evidence collection checklist:
- Identify the tenant or account.
- Export relevant logs with UTC timestamps.
- Capture configuration snapshots.
- Preserve API tokens and access history.
- Document provider support case numbers.
- Confirm retention windows and legal hold status.

**Anti-forensics and contamination risks**

**Anti-forensics** refers to actions intended to hide, destroy, alter, or mislead investigators. You should expect anti-forensic behavior in serious incidents and design your playbook to reduce the chance of contamination.

Common anti-forensic techniques:
- Log deletion or tampering.
- Timestamp manipulation.
- File wiping or secure deletion.
- Encryption to block access.
- Use of ephemeral containers or disposable cloud resources.
- Living-off-the-land tools that blend into normal administration.
- Data staging in unusual locations or encrypted archives.

Contamination risks include:
- Booting a compromised system and changing volatile state.
- Mounting disks read-write during collection.
- Using unapproved tools that alter metadata.
- Copying files without preserving timestamps or hashes.
- Allowing unauthorized personnel to access evidence.
- Mixing evidence from different cases.

Mitigations:
- Use write blockers for storage media when appropriate.
- Capture hashes before and after imaging.
- Document every action taken on the evidence.
- Restrict access to trained personnel.
- Keep original evidence untouched whenever possible.

**Chain of custody workflow**

A disciplined workflow helps preserve admissibility and trust in the evidence.

1. **Identify** the evidence and assign a unique ID.
2. **Collect** the item using approved methods.
3. **Document** the item’s condition, location, and collector.
4. **Package** and seal the evidence if physical.
5. **Transfer** custody with signatures or electronic approvals.
6. **Store** the evidence in a secure, access-controlled location.
7. **Analyze** copies, not originals, when possible.
8. **Track** every subsequent handoff.
9. **Dispose or retain** according to policy, law, and legal hold status.

**Sample chain of custody form**

```text
Case Number: IR-2026-041
Incident Type: Suspected data exfiltration
Evidence ID: E-041-A
Item Description: Dell laptop, asset tag FIN-2041
Serial Number: DLX9-8821
Collected From: Finance office, desk 4
Collected By: J. Rivera
Collection Date/Time (UTC): 2026-04-26 13:40
Collection Method: Powered down, photographed, bagged, sealed
Initial Condition: Screen open, logged-in session visible
Hash/Imaging: Full disk image created; SHA-256 recorded
Storage Location: Evidence locker B-12

Custody Log:
1. J. Rivera -> M. Chen
   Date/Time (UTC): 2026-04-26 14:05
   Purpose: Transfer to forensic lab
   Condition: Seal intact

2. M. Chen -> S. Ahmed
   Date/Time (UTC): 2026-04-26 16:20
   Purpose: Analysis copy creation
   Condition: Original retained in locker; working copy created

3. S. Ahmed -> M. Chen
   Date/Time (UTC): 2026-04-26 18:10
   Purpose: Return working copy after analysis
   Condition: Working copy verified; hash matched

4. M. Chen -> Legal Hold Storage
   Date/Time (UTC): 2026-04-27 09:00
   Purpose: Preserve pending investigation
   Condition: Retained under legal hold

Final Disposition: Retained
Disposition Authority: Legal counsel
Disposition Date: Pending
```

**Practical incident workflow example**

A ransomware alert on a file server might follow this sequence:
1. Detect unusual encryption activity and confirm the incident.
2. Preserve logs, memory, and relevant snapshots.
3. Isolate the server from the network.
4. Notify incident response leadership and legal counsel.
5. Determine whether backups are clean and whether lateral movement occurred.
6. Eradicate persistence and rotate credentials.
7. Restore from known-good backups.
8. Monitor for re-infection.
9. Conduct lessons learned and update controls.

**Practical evidence workflow example**

If a manager reports a suspected insider copying files to a USB drive:
- Preserve endpoint logs and USB insertion history.
- Secure the removable media as physical evidence.
- Review access logs for file reads, copies, and uploads.
- Involve HR and legal before interviewing the employee.
- Determine whether a legal hold is needed for email, chat, and file shares.
- Maintain chain of custody for the USB device and any exported logs.

**Exam focus points**

- **Preparation** includes playbooks, training, tools, contacts, and testing.
- **Detection and analysis** focuses on validation, scoping, and evidence preservation.
- **Containment** limits damage without unnecessarily destroying evidence.
- **Eradication** removes the cause and persistence mechanisms.
- **Recovery** restores trusted operations and verifies integrity.
- **Post-incident activity** drives lessons learned and control improvement.
- **Chain of custody** proves evidence integrity and traceability.
- **Legal hold** suspends normal deletion and retention workflows.
- **Admissibility** depends on policy, law, and jurisdiction.
- **Cloud evidence** often requires provider coordination and fast preservation.
- **Anti-forensics** and contamination can invalidate evidence if mishandled.

**Quick reference: what to document**

- Who collected the evidence.
- What was collected.
- When and where it was collected.
- How it was collected.
- Who handled it next.
- Where it was stored.
- Whether hashes were computed.
- Whether legal hold applies.
- When and how it was finally disposed of or retained.
