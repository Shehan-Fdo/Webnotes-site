**Personnel security: purpose and lifecycle**

Personnel security reduces insider risk by controlling who gets access, what they can do, and how access changes over time. In CISSP terms, you are protecting the organization across the full employee lifecycle: pre-employment screening, onboarding, ongoing monitoring, role changes, and termination.

The goal is not just to “trust employees,” but to apply **least privilege**, **need to know**, and **separation of duties** so a single person cannot abuse access without detection or collusion. Strong personnel security also supports legal defensibility, auditability, and consistent enforcement.

- **Pre-employment screening** helps reduce hiring risk before access is granted.
- **Onboarding controls** ensure access is approved, documented, and limited to job needs.
- **Ongoing governance** detects risky behavior and enforces policy over time.
- **Termination controls** remove access quickly and completely.

**Pre-employment screening and legal/privacy constraints**

Background screening is a risk-based control, not a guarantee of trustworthiness. You use it to validate identity, employment history, education, criminal history, credit history where lawful, and other role-relevant factors. The depth of screening should match the sensitivity of the role.

Important limitations apply:

- **Consent** is often required before checks are performed.
- **Jurisdiction-specific laws** may restrict what you can collect, how far back you can search, and how you can use the results.
- **Adverse action** processes may be required if screening results affect hiring decisions.
- **Privacy minimization** matters: collect only what is relevant to the role and retain it only as long as needed.
- **Disparate impact** can arise if screening criteria are not job-related and consistent with business necessity.

A practical approach is to define screening tiers by role sensitivity:

- Standard office roles may require identity verification and employment reference checks.
- Privileged IT, finance, and security roles may require deeper verification, criminal checks where permitted, and enhanced reference validation.
- Roles with access to regulated data may require additional checks aligned to law and contract.

> Screening should be consistent, documented, and legally reviewed. A control that is strong in one country may be unlawful or unusable in another.

**Onboarding, acceptable use, and access authorization**

Onboarding is where policy becomes operational. You should not grant access until the organization has completed identity verification, signed acceptable use and confidentiality agreements, and obtained manager approval for required systems.

Key onboarding controls include:

- **Role-based access control** assignments based on job function.
- **Security awareness** training before or at access activation.
- **Acknowledgment** of policies such as acceptable use, data handling, and remote work.
- **Asset issuance** with inventory tracking for laptops, tokens, badges, and mobile devices.
- **Privileged access** approval through separate workflows, ideally with stronger authentication and logging.

A simple onboarding workflow might look like this:

1. HR creates the hire record and start date.
2. Manager requests access based on the role.
3. Security or IAM approves access according to policy.
4. IT provisions accounts, MFA, and device controls.
5. The employee signs required policies and completes training.
6. Access is reviewed after the probationary period.

**Employee lifecycle governance and insider-threat controls**

Personnel security is strongest when it is continuous, not one-time. CISSP expects you to know governance controls that reduce insider threat and fraud over time.

Important lifecycle controls include:

- **Mandatory vacation** forces a person away from their duties long enough for irregularities to surface.
- **Job rotation** reduces dependency on one individual and makes collusion or concealment harder.
- **Separation of duties** prevents one person from initiating, approving, and reconciling the same transaction.
- **Dual control** requires two authorized people for sensitive actions, such as vault access or key ceremonies.
- **Periodic access reviews** confirm that privileges still match job responsibilities.
- **Behavioral monitoring** can detect unusual access patterns, data exfiltration, or policy violations.

These controls are especially relevant in finance, privileged administration, and environments with high fraud risk. They are not just administrative best practices; they are classic insider-threat mitigations.

**Termination, offboarding, and immediate revocation**

Termination is a high-risk event because access must be removed quickly and comprehensively. Whether the departure is voluntary, involuntary, or due to contract end, you should coordinate HR, management, IT, security, and facilities.

Termination actions should include:

- **Immediate logical access revocation** for accounts, VPN, SSO, email, cloud consoles, and privileged tools.
- **Badge collection and physical access revocation** for doors, parking, lockers, and visitor systems.
- **Device return** for laptops, phones, tokens, smart cards, and removable media.
- **Shared and privileged account coordination** so passwords, secrets, API keys, and service credentials are rotated after departure.
- **Session termination** for active sessions, remote access, and privileged shells.
- **Data ownership transfer** for files, tickets, code repositories, and business records.

For involuntary termination, timing matters. You often disable access before or at the same time as the employee is notified, especially if there is elevated insider risk.

A practical offboarding checklist:

1. HR confirms termination date and type.
2. Security identifies sensitive access and shared credentials.
3. IT disables accounts and revokes tokens.
4. Facilities collects badges and physical keys.
5. Manager transfers ownership of work products and data.
6. Security rotates secrets and reviews logs for suspicious activity.

**Third-party governance and vendor risk management**

Third-party governance extends security beyond your employees to vendors, contractors, cloud providers, processors, and service partners. The core question is: how do you manage risk when another organization touches your data, systems, or operations?

A mature third-party risk management program includes:

- **Due diligence** before onboarding a vendor.
- **Contractual controls** that define security, privacy, and audit rights.
- **Ongoing monitoring** of performance and risk.
- **Offboarding controls** to ensure data return, deletion, and access removal.

You should classify vendors by risk. A payroll processor, managed security provider, or SaaS platform with sensitive data access deserves more scrutiny than a low-risk office supply vendor.

**Vendor due diligence and onboarding workflow**

Due diligence is the process of evaluating a vendor before you trust them with access or data. You are looking for evidence that they can meet your security, privacy, legal, and operational requirements.

Common due diligence artifacts include:

- Security questionnaires and control attestations
- SOC 2 or similar independent audit reports
- Penetration test summaries or remediation evidence
- Privacy impact assessments
- Business continuity and disaster recovery documentation
- Incident response and breach notification procedures
- Subprocessor lists and data flow diagrams
- Insurance certificates, where relevant

A practical vendor assessment workflow:

1. Define the business need and data classification.
2. Determine whether the vendor will process, store, transmit, or merely access data.
3. Assess inherent risk based on data sensitivity, connectivity, and criticality.
4. Review the vendor’s controls and evidence.
5. Decide whether to accept, mitigate, transfer, or reject the risk.
6. Document approvals, exceptions, and compensating controls.

If a vendor fails security requirements, you do not automatically reject them. You can require remediation, limit scope, isolate connectivity, or use contractual and technical compensating controls. If the residual risk remains too high, you should decline the engagement.

**Contractual governance, audit rights, and service review**

Contracts are where security expectations become enforceable. A strong agreement should define what the vendor can do with your data, how long they can keep it, and what happens when the relationship ends.

Important contract terms include:

- **Data ownership** remains with you unless explicitly transferred.
- **Data classification** defines handling requirements and access restrictions.
- **Processing limitations** specify who can process the data and for what purpose.
- **Retention and destruction** requirements define when data must be deleted or returned.
- **Right to audit** allows you to verify compliance, either directly or through independent reports.
- **Breach notification** timelines define how quickly the vendor must notify you.
- **Subcontractor controls** require approval or disclosure of downstream processors.
- **Service review cadence** sets recurring checkpoints for performance and risk.
- **Performance scorecards** track availability, security incidents, remediation, and SLA compliance.

A vendor scorecard can include:

| Metric | Example measure | Why it matters |
|---|---:|---|
| Availability | 99.9% uptime | Confirms operational reliability |
| Security findings | Open critical issues | Shows control maturity |
| Incident response | Time to notify | Measures breach readiness |
| Remediation | Days to close findings | Tracks accountability |
| Compliance | Audit exceptions | Indicates governance gaps |

**Third-party offboarding and data disposition**

Vendor offboarding is often overlooked, but it is critical. When a contract ends, you must ensure the vendor no longer has access to your systems or data and that retention obligations are honored.

Offboarding should address:

- **Account and API revocation** for all vendor users, integrations, and service accounts.
- **Data return** in a usable format if required by contract.
- **Certified destruction** or deletion of data, backups, and replicas when appropriate.
- **Evidence of deletion** such as destruction certificates or signed attestations.
- **Residual access review** for cached credentials, federated trust, and support channels.

If the vendor hosted your data, you should confirm whether backups are deleted immediately or on a delayed schedule. If legal hold or regulatory retention applies, destruction may be deferred, but the decision must be documented.

**Cloud shared responsibility model**

Cloud security depends on the **shared responsibility model**, which divides control ownership between the cloud provider and the customer. The exact split depends on whether you use SaaS, PaaS, or IaaS.

A practical way to think about it:

| Control area | SaaS | PaaS | IaaS |
|---|---|---|---|
| Physical security | Provider | Provider | Provider |
| Hypervisor/host OS | Provider | Provider | Provider |
| Application security | Mostly provider | Shared | Customer |
| Identity and access management | Shared | Shared | Customer-heavy |
| Logging and monitoring | Shared | Shared | Customer-heavy |
| Encryption at rest | Shared | Shared | Shared |
| Key management | Shared | Shared | Customer-heavy |
| Incident response | Shared | Shared | Shared |

Examples:

- In **SaaS**, the provider usually secures the application platform, but you still manage users, MFA, data classification, and configuration.
- In **PaaS**, the provider secures the runtime and platform, while you secure your code, data, identities, and application logic.
- In **IaaS**, you manage the guest OS, middleware, applications, identities, logging, and most configuration, while the provider secures the underlying infrastructure.

Exam-focused examples:

- If a SaaS tenant is breached because an admin account lacked MFA, that is often a customer-side identity control failure.
- If logs are not enabled in a PaaS environment, the provider may offer the capability, but you are responsible for turning it on and retaining the logs.
- If you manage your own keys in IaaS, key rotation and access to the key management system are your responsibility, even if the provider hosts the service.

**Identity, logging, encryption, and key management in cloud**

These controls are common exam traps because responsibility shifts by service model.

- **Identity**: You usually own user lifecycle, privileged access, federation, and MFA policy.
- **Logging**: The provider may generate logs, but you often must configure collection, retention, alerting, and review.
- **Encryption**: The provider may encrypt by default, but you must decide what data to encrypt and how to manage access.
- **Key management**: If you use customer-managed keys, you own rotation, access control, backup, and revocation procedures.

A simple cloud logging example with AWS CLI:

```bash
aws cloudtrail create-trail \
  --name org-trail \
  --s3-bucket-name my-cloudtrail-logs \
  --is-multi-region-trail

aws cloudtrail start-logging --name org-trail
```

A basic identity example for enforcing MFA in a cloud environment:

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "DenyAllExceptIfMFA",
      "Effect": "Deny",
      "Action": "*",
      "Resource": "*",
      "Condition": {
        "BoolIfExists": {
          "aws:MultiFactorAuthPresent": "false"
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

**Supply-chain security and software integrity**

Supply-chain security protects the integrity of hardware, software, updates, and dependencies before they reach production. Attackers often target the build pipeline, package repositories, or update channels because compromising one trusted component can affect many downstream systems.

Key controls include:

- **Code signing** to verify software origin and integrity.
- **Secure update mechanisms** that authenticate patches before installation.
- **Dependency verification** to validate third-party libraries and packages.
- **Repository controls** such as trusted mirrors and package pinning.
- **Build pipeline hardening** with restricted access and audit logging.
- **Artifact provenance** so you can trace what was built, by whom, and from which source.

Practical examples:

- Verify package signatures before installation.
- Use checksums or hashes to confirm downloaded binaries.
- Restrict who can publish to internal package repositories.
- Separate build, test, and release permissions to reduce tampering risk.

Example of verifying a downloaded file hash:

```bash
sha256sum vendor-agent-linux-amd64.tar.gz
```

Example of a signed package verification workflow:

```bash
gpg --verify release.tar.gz.sig release.tar.gz
```

If the signature or hash does not match, you should treat the artifact as untrusted and investigate before deployment.

**Responding when a vendor fails security requirements**

When a vendor does not meet your requirements, your response should be risk-based and documented. The right answer is not always “terminate immediately”; it depends on business criticality, compensating controls, and the severity of the gap.

A practical decision path:

1. Identify the failed control and its impact.
2. Determine whether the issue is remediable.
3. Apply compensating controls such as segmentation, reduced data scope, or temporary monitoring.
4. Set a remediation deadline and require evidence.
5. Escalate to legal, procurement, and business owners if the vendor misses the deadline.
6. Exit the relationship if residual risk remains unacceptable.

Examples:

- If a vendor lacks MFA for administrative access, you may require immediate remediation or restrict them to a segregated support path.
- If a vendor cannot provide audit evidence, you may limit the data they process until assurance improves.
- If a vendor refuses contractual deletion terms, you should treat that as a serious governance gap.

**Practical exam distinctions**

CISSP questions often test whether you choose the most appropriate control at the right time.

- **Background screening** happens before access and is preventive.
- **Mandatory vacation** and **job rotation** help detect concealed fraud over time.
- **Termination** requires immediate access revocation and physical badge collection.
- **Third-party governance** depends on due diligence, contracts, monitoring, and offboarding.
- **Shared responsibility** means cloud security is not fully outsourced.
- **Supply-chain integrity** depends on verifying software origin and update authenticity.

**Quick reference: what to remember**

- Personnel security is a lifecycle process, not a single HR event.
- Legal and privacy rules can limit screening and retention.
- Insider-threat controls include mandatory vacation, job rotation, and separation of duties.
- Termination must remove logical and physical access quickly and rotate shared secrets.
- Vendor governance requires due diligence, contractual enforcement, monitoring, and offboarding.
- Data ownership, classification, retention, and destruction must be defined contractually and operationally.
- Cloud responsibility varies by SaaS, PaaS, and IaaS.
- Supply-chain security relies on code signing, secure updates, and dependency verification.
