## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Security goals and the CIA triad**

The <abbr title="Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability">CIA</abbr> triad is the baseline model for evaluating security controls. In CISSP questions, you often need to identify which objective a control primarily supports, even when it affects all three.

- **Confidentiality** prevents unauthorized disclosure.
  - Examples: encryption, access control lists, data classification, masking, tokenization, physical locks.
  - Best fit when the main risk is exposure of sensitive data.
- **Integrity** prevents unauthorized modification and preserves correctness.
  - Examples: hashes, digital signatures, checksums, file integrity monitoring, input validation, change control.
  - Best fit when the main risk is tampering or corruption.
- **Availability** ensures systems and data are accessible when needed.
  - Examples: redundancy, clustering, load balancing, backups, UPS, failover, DDoS protection.
  - Best fit when downtime is the primary business risk.

A useful exam pattern is to map the control to the dominant objective:

| Control | Primary CIA objective | Why |
|---|---:|---|
| Encryption | Confidentiality | Hides data from unauthorized readers |
| Hashing | Integrity | Detects alteration |
| Digital signature | Integrity, nonrepudiation | Proves origin and detects tampering |
| RAID | Availability | Keeps service running after disk failure |
| Backup | Availability, recovery | Restores data after loss or corruption |
| ACL | Confidentiality | Restricts who can read or use a resource |

> A control can support more than one objective, but CISSP questions usually ask for the *best* answer, not every possible effect.

**Additional security properties and principles**

Beyond CIA, CISSP frequently tests broader security properties that shape design decisions and policy.

- **Privacy** is the proper collection, use, retention, and disclosure of personal data.
  - Privacy is broader than confidentiality because it includes lawful processing and purpose limitation.
  - Example: a company may encrypt customer records but still violate privacy by collecting unnecessary data.
- **Anonymity** means a subject cannot be identified.
  - Example: anonymous web browsing or anonymous reporting systems.
- **Pseudonymity** means a subject uses an identifier that is not their real identity, but actions can still be linked to that pseudonym.
  - Example: a forum handle or employee ID.
- **Nonrepudiation** means a party cannot reasonably deny an action.
  - Example: digitally signed email or signed transaction logs.
- **Possession** is an authentication factor based on something you have.
  - Example: smart card, hardware token, phone-based authenticator, FIDO2 security key.
- **Accountability** means actions can be traced to a specific identity.
  - Example: unique user IDs, audit logs, session recording.
- **Authenticity** means something is genuine and from the claimed source.
  - Example: certificate validation, signed software packages.
- **Authorization** determines what an authenticated identity may do.
- **Least privilege** grants only the access needed to perform a task.
- **Need to know** limits access to information required for a specific job function.
- **Separation of duties** splits critical tasks across multiple people to reduce fraud and error.
- **Separation of privilege** requires more than one condition or approval to complete a sensitive action.
  - Example: two-person approval for wire transfers.
- **Defense in depth** layers controls so one failure does not expose the asset.
- **Fail-secure** means a system defaults to a secure state when it fails.
  - Example: a door lock remains locked on power loss.
- **Fail-open** means a system defaults to an open state when it fails.
  - Example: some fire doors or legacy network bypass designs.
- **Work factor** is the amount of effort required to defeat a control.
  - Example: a 14-character passphrase with MFA has a much higher work factor than a short password alone.

**Authentication factors and access control relationships**

Authentication answers “who are you?” and access control answers “what can you do?” CISSP expects you to distinguish factors and understand how they combine.

- **Something you know**
  - Passwords, PINs, answers to challenge questions.
  - Weakness: can be guessed, phished, reused, or shared.
- **Something you have**
  - Smart card, hardware token, mobile authenticator, cryptographic key.
  - Weakness: can be stolen, cloned, or lost.
- **Something you are**
  - Biometrics such as fingerprint, iris, face, voice.
  - Weakness: false acceptance, false rejection, spoofing, privacy concerns.
- **Something you do**
  - Behavioral biometrics such as typing rhythm, gait, mouse movement.
  - Often used for continuous authentication or fraud detection.
- **Something you are somewhere**
  - Location-based authentication, such as geofencing or trusted network location.
  - Useful for step-up decisions, but location alone is not strong proof of identity.

A common exam trap is confusing possession with knowledge-based one-time passwords. A phone app may store a secret, but the factor is still usually treated as possession because the user must have the device.

**MFA, step-up authentication, SSO, and federation**

- **MFA** (<abbr title="Multi-Factor Authentication">Multi-Factor Authentication</abbr>) uses two or more different factor types.
  - Example: password + hardware token.
  - Stronger than repeated use of the same factor type, such as two passwords.
- **Step-up authentication** increases assurance when risk increases.
  - Example: a user logs in with password and then must provide a biometric or token before approving a wire transfer.
  - Use it for sensitive actions, not just initial login.
- **SSO** (<abbr title="Single Sign-On">Single Sign-On</abbr>) lets a user authenticate once and access multiple systems.
  - Benefit: fewer passwords, better usability, centralized policy.
  - Risk: if the SSO session or identity provider is compromised, many services are exposed.
- **Federation** allows identity to be trusted across organizational boundaries.
  - Example: a company uses an external identity provider for employee access to SaaS applications.
  - Common standards include SAML, OAuth 2.0, and OpenID Connect, each with different roles.
- **Authorization** still happens per application or resource, even when authentication is federated.

A practical scenario:

- A user signs in to a corporate portal with SSO.
- The portal trusts the identity provider through federation.
- When the user opens payroll, the system requests step-up authentication because the action is high risk.
- The access decision then combines identity, role, device posture, and transaction context.

**Access control models and enforcement mechanisms**

Access control models define *how* permissions are assigned and enforced. CISSP often asks you to identify the model from a scenario.

| Model | Core idea | Typical use | Strength | Weakness |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| DAC | Discretionary Access Control | File sharing, general OS permissions | Flexible, user-controlled | Easy to over-share |
| MAC | Mandatory Access Control | Military, highly classified environments | Strong policy enforcement | Rigid, complex |
| RBAC | Role-Based Access Control | Enterprises, job functions | Easy administration | Role explosion |
| ABAC | Attribute-Based Access Control | Cloud, dynamic policy | Fine-grained, context-aware | Policy complexity |
| Rule-based | Rules trigger access decisions | Firewalls, time-based access | Simple automation | Limited flexibility |
| Risk-based | Context and risk score drive access | Adaptive authentication | Responsive to threat level | Needs good telemetry |

**DAC** uses ownership and permissions set by the resource owner.

- Common in Unix/Linux file permissions and many general-purpose systems.
- Example: a user can grant another user access to a file they own.
- Best when collaboration matters more than strict central control.

**MAC** uses centrally enforced labels and clearances.

- Users cannot override policy.
- Example: a document labeled `Secret` can only be accessed by users with the proper clearance and need to know.
- Best when confidentiality is paramount.

**RBAC** assigns permissions to roles, and users inherit permissions through role membership.

- Example: `HR_Manager` can read employee records; `Payroll_Clerk` can update salary data.
- Best when job functions are stable and well-defined.

**ABAC** evaluates attributes of the subject, object, action, and environment.

- Example: allow access if the user is in Finance, the device is managed, the request occurs during business hours, and the data classification is not restricted.
- Best when access must adapt to context.

**ACLs, capabilities, and permissions**

- **ACL** (<abbr title="Access Control List">Access Control List</abbr>) lists which subjects can access an object and how.
  - Common on files, directories, printers, and network resources.
  - Example: `alice: read`, `bob: read/write`.
- **Capabilities** are tokens or references that grant access to an object.
  - The holder of the capability can use the resource without repeatedly checking identity in a central list.
  - Common in some distributed systems and microkernel designs.
- **Permissions** are the specific rights granted, such as read, write, execute, delete, or modify.
  - On Windows, permissions are often combined with inheritance and group membership.
  - On Unix-like systems, permissions include owner/group/other bits and may be extended with ACLs.

A practical Windows example:

```powershell
# View NTFS permissions
Get-Acl C:\Finance\Payroll.xlsx | Format-List

# Grant read access to a group
icacls C:\Finance\Payroll.xlsx /grant "CORP\Finance-Read:(R)"

# Remove an inherited permission
icacls C:\Finance\Payroll.xlsx /inheritance:d
```

A practical Linux example:

```bash
# View permissions
ls -l /srv/finance/report.txt

# Set owner and group
chown alice:finance /srv/finance/report.txt

# Grant group read access
chmod 640 /srv/finance/report.txt

# Add an ACL entry
setfacl -m u:bob:rw /srv/finance/report.txt
getfacl /srv/finance/report.txt
```

**Security models beyond the basics**

CISSP expects you to recognize classic formal models and their purpose.

- **Bell-LaPadula** focuses on confidentiality.
  - Rules: no read up, no write down.
  - Example: a user with `Secret` clearance cannot read `Top Secret`, and cannot write to a lower classification if it could leak data.
- **Biba** focuses on integrity.
  - Rules: no read down, no write up.
  - Example: a high-integrity process should not consume low-integrity input.
- **Clark-Wilson** focuses on integrity through well-formed transactions and separation of duties.
  - Uses constrained data items, transformation procedures, and auditing.
  - Example: accounting systems that require approved transaction workflows.
- **Brewer-Nash / Chinese Wall** prevents conflicts of interest.
  - Access is allowed based on prior access history.
  - Example: a consultant who accesses Company A’s confidential data is then blocked from accessing Company B’s data in the same conflict class.
  - Key idea: the policy changes dynamically as the user touches sensitive datasets.
- **Lattice-based access control** organizes labels and clearances in a partial order.
  - Subjects and objects are placed in a lattice of security levels and categories.
  - Example: `Secret + NATO` may dominate `Confidential + NATO`, but not `Secret + SAP` if categories differ.
  - Useful when multiple dimensions of classification matter.

A quick memory aid:

- **Bell-LaPadula** = confidentiality.
- **Biba** = integrity.
- **Clark-Wilson** = business transaction integrity.
- **Chinese Wall** = conflict of interest.
- **Lattice** = ordered labels and categories.

**Cryptography basics for CISSP**

Cryptography supports confidentiality, integrity, authentication, and nonrepudiation, but each primitive has different strengths.

- **Symmetric cryptography**
  - Same key encrypts and decrypts.
  - Fast and efficient for bulk data.
  - Examples: AES, ChaCha20.
  - Weakness: key distribution is difficult because both parties need the same secret.
- **Asymmetric cryptography**
  - Uses a public/private key pair.
  - Slower than symmetric cryptography.
  - Examples: RSA, ECC, Diffie-Hellman for key exchange.
  - Strength: simplifies key distribution and supports digital signatures.

Typical use pattern:

- Use asymmetric cryptography to establish trust or exchange a session key.
- Use symmetric cryptography for the actual data session.

```text
Client -> Server: hello
Server -> Client: certificate + public key
Client verifies certificate chain
Client and server establish a shared session key
Bulk traffic uses AES or ChaCha20
```

**Hashing, MACs, and signatures**

- **Hashing** creates a fixed-length digest from data.
  - Good for integrity checks and password storage with proper salting and stretching.
  - Not reversible.
- **HMAC** (<abbr title="Hash-based Message Authentication Code">Hash-based Message Authentication Code</abbr>) combines a secret key with a hash.
  - Provides integrity and authenticity.
  - Used when both parties share a secret.
- **Digital signatures** use a private key to sign and a public key to verify.
  - Provide integrity, authenticity, and nonrepudiation.
  - Common in code signing, document signing, and certificate validation.

**Key management lifecycle**

Key management is often more important than the algorithm itself.

1. **Generate** keys using strong randomness.
2. **Distribute** keys securely.
3. **Store** keys in protected hardware or software controls.
4. **Use** keys only for approved purposes.
5. **Rotate** keys on schedule or after compromise.
6. **Backup and escrow** keys when business continuity requires recovery.
7. **Revoke** or retire keys when no longer trusted.
8. **Destroy** keys securely at end of life.

Practical controls include:

- Hardware Security Modules (<abbr title="Hardware Security Module">HSM</abbr>s) for high-value keys.
- Key wrapping to protect keys at rest.
- Separation of duties for key custodians.
- Dual control for sensitive key operations.

**Certificate lifecycle and PKI trust**

Public Key Infrastructure (<abbr title="Public Key Infrastructure">PKI</abbr>) binds identities to public keys through certificates.

- **Enrollment**: a subject requests a certificate.
- **Validation**: the certificate authority verifies identity and request legitimacy.
- **Issuance**: the CA signs and publishes the certificate.
- **Use**: clients validate the certificate chain during TLS or other trust decisions.
- **Renewal**: the certificate is replaced before expiration.
- **Revocation**: the certificate is invalidated before its natural end date.
- **Expiration**: the certificate is no longer valid after the date passes.

Trust chain validation usually checks:

- The certificate is signed by a trusted CA or chains to one.
- The certificate is within its validity period.
- The subject name matches the expected host or identity.
- The key usage and extended key usage are appropriate.
- The certificate has not been revoked.

Revocation is a common exam topic.

- **CRL** (<abbr title="Certificate Revocation List">Certificate Revocation List</abbr>) is a published list of revoked certificates.
  - Advantage: can be cached and checked offline.
  - Weakness: may be stale between updates.
- **OCSP** (<abbr title="Online Certificate Status Protocol">Online Certificate Status Protocol</abbr>) checks certificate status in near real time.
  - Advantage: more current status.
  - Weakness: depends on responder availability and can create privacy concerns.
- **OCSP stapling** lets the server provide a recent OCSP response during the TLS handshake.
  - Reduces client lookup overhead and improves privacy.

> A revoked certificate can still appear unexpired. In exam scenarios, revocation status matters more than the date alone.

**Common cryptographic attack considerations**

- **Replay attack**
  - An attacker captures valid authentication or transaction data and reuses it later.
  - Mitigations: nonces, timestamps, sequence numbers, session tokens, challenge-response.
- **Downgrade attack**
  - An attacker forces the parties to use weaker algorithms or protocols.
  - Mitigations: disable legacy protocols, enforce minimum versions, use secure negotiation.
- **Man-in-the-middle (<abbr title="Man-in-the-Middle">MITM</abbr>)**
  - An attacker intercepts and possibly alters communication between two parties.
  - Mitigations: certificate validation, mutual TLS, pinning where appropriate, authenticated key exchange.
- **Brute force**
  - Tries many keys or passwords until one works.
  - Mitigations: long keys, rate limiting, account lockout, MFA, work factor.
- **Birthday attack**
  - Exploits collision probability in hash functions.
  - Mitigations: use modern hash algorithms with sufficient output length.
- **Side-channel attack**
  - Uses timing, power, or electromagnetic leakage.
  - Mitigations: constant-time code, hardened hardware, shielding.

**Availability, fault tolerance, and resilience**

Availability is not just “the server is up.” It includes the ability to continue service under failure, attack, or maintenance.

- **Fault tolerance** means the system continues operating despite component failure.
  - Example: redundant power supplies, RAID, clustered nodes.
- **Redundancy** means extra components exist to take over if one fails.
  - **Active-active**: multiple nodes serve traffic simultaneously.
  - **Active-passive**: one node serves traffic while another waits.
  - **N+1**: one extra component beyond the minimum required.
- **Load balancing** distributes traffic across multiple systems.
- **Failover** shifts service to a standby system after failure.
- **Backups** support restoration after deletion, corruption, ransomware, or disaster.
- **UPS** (<abbr title="Uninterruptible Power Supply">Uninterruptible Power Supply</abbr>) bridges short power outages and allows graceful shutdown.
- **DDoS protection** preserves availability under volumetric or application-layer attack.

**RTO and RPO**

- **RTO** (<abbr title="Recovery Time Objective">Recovery Time Objective</abbr>) is the maximum acceptable time to restore service.
- **RPO** (<abbr title="Recovery Point Objective">Recovery Point Objective</abbr>) is the maximum acceptable amount of data loss measured in time.

Example:

- RTO = 2 hours means the business can tolerate up to two hours of downtime.
- RPO = 15 minutes means the business can tolerate losing at most 15 minutes of data.

**Resilience vs survivability**

- **Resilience** is the ability to absorb disruption and recover quickly.
- **Survivability** is the ability to continue essential functions during adverse conditions, even if degraded.
- In exam scenarios, resilience often emphasizes recovery speed, while survivability emphasizes continued mission operation under stress.

**Practical hardening examples in enterprise environments**

Hardening is the process of reducing attack surface and enforcing secure defaults.

- **Windows baseline hardening**
  - Disable unnecessary services.
  - Enforce strong password and lockout policies.
  - Use BitLocker for full-disk encryption.
  - Apply Microsoft security baselines or CIS benchmarks.
  - Restrict local administrator use and enable LAPS where appropriate.
- **Group Policy Objects (<abbr title="Group Policy Object">GPO</abbr>s)**
  - Centralize password policy, firewall settings, software restriction, and audit policy.
  - Example: disable legacy SMB versions, enforce screen lock, and control USB storage.
- **Endpoint hardening**
  - Use application allowlisting.
  - Enable host firewall and EDR (<abbr title="Endpoint Detection and Response">Endpoint Detection and Response</abbr>).
  - Remove unused software and browser plugins.
  - Patch operating systems and applications promptly.
- **Network hardening**
  - Segment networks by trust level.
  - Restrict management interfaces.
  - Use secure protocols such as SSH, TLS, and SNMPv3 instead of legacy cleartext services.

A Windows GPO example conceptually looks like this:

```powershell
# Illustrative examples of policy enforcement
Set-ItemProperty -Path "HKLM:\SYSTEM\CurrentControlSet\Control\Lsa" -Name "LimitBlankPasswordUse" -Value 1
Set-NetFirewallProfile -Profile Domain,Public,Private -Enabled True
```

**Scenario-driven exam thinking**

When you face a CISSP question, identify the primary risk, then choose the control that best addresses it.

- If the issue is unauthorized disclosure, think **confidentiality** controls.
- If the issue is tampering or fraud, think **integrity** controls.
- If the issue is downtime or service interruption, think **availability** controls.
- If the issue is identity assurance, think **authentication factors**, MFA, federation, and step-up.
- If the issue is trust in a remote party, think **PKI**, certificate validation, and revocation.
- If the issue is policy enforcement across classifications, think **MAC** or lattice-based models.
- If the issue is conflict of interest, think **Brewer-Nash/Chinese Wall**.
- If the issue is business process integrity, think **Clark-Wilson**.

Mini case studies:

- A finance app must prevent one clerk from approving and paying the same invoice.
  - Best fit: separation of duties, separation of privilege, and audit logging.
- A hospital portal must let doctors access records from managed devices only.
  - Best fit: ABAC with device posture and context checks.
- A classified system must prevent lower-clearance users from reading higher-level data.
  - Best fit: Bell-LaPadula and MAC.
- A cloud service must block access after a user has viewed a competitor’s confidential dataset.
  - Best fit: Brewer-Nash/Chinese Wall.
- A remote workforce needs fewer passwords without weakening control.
  - Best fit: SSO with federation, plus MFA and step-up for sensitive actions.

**Quick review checklist**

- **CIA**: confidentiality, integrity, availability.
- **Authentication factors**: know, have, are, do, where.
- **Access control**: DAC, MAC, RBAC, ABAC, ACLs, capabilities.
- **Security models**: Bell-LaPadula, Biba, Clark-Wilson, Chinese Wall, lattice.
- **Crypto**: symmetric vs asymmetric, hashing, HMAC, signatures, PKI, revocation.
- **Availability**: redundancy, fault tolerance, RTO, RPO, resilience, survivability.
- **Hardening**: baselines, GPOs, endpoint controls, least privilege.
