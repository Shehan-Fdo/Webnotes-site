## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Vendor due diligence in cloud sourcing**

Vendor due diligence is the structured process of evaluating a cloud service provider (CSP) before and during engagement to determine whether the provider can meet your security, privacy, legal, operational, and business requirements. In a CISSP context, you are not just checking a feature list; you are validating whether the provider’s controls, shared responsibility model, and contractual commitments align with your risk appetite and compliance obligations.

Cloud due diligence matters because cloud outsourcing changes the control boundary. You may gain elasticity, resilience, and managed services, but you also inherit dependency risk, concentration risk, and visibility gaps. The goal is to reduce uncertainty before you transfer data, workloads, or trust to the provider.

- **Shared responsibility** defines which controls the provider owns and which remain yours.
- **Risk appetite** determines how much residual risk you can accept after controls are applied.
- **Due diligence** is evidence-based; marketing claims are not sufficient.

**What you evaluate in a cloud provider**

A strong assessment looks at the provider as a business partner and as a control environment. You should examine security architecture, operational maturity, legal terms, and the provider’s ability to support your incident response, audit, and continuity needs.

Key areas include:

- **Security controls**
  - Identity and access management, including MFA, privileged access, and federation support
  - Encryption at rest and in transit, key management options, and customer-managed keys where needed
  - Logging, monitoring, and alerting capabilities
  - Vulnerability management, patching, and secure configuration baselines
- **Compliance and assurance**
  - Independent attestations such as SOC 2, ISO/IEC 27001, PCI DSS, or FedRAMP where relevant
  - Audit reports, bridge letters, and scope limitations
  - Data residency and cross-border transfer controls
- **Operational resilience**
  - Backup, disaster recovery, and recovery time objective (RTO) / recovery point objective (RPO) support
  - Multi-region architecture and failover design
  - Capacity management and service availability commitments
- **Privacy and legal**
  - Data processing terms, subprocessor disclosure, and breach notification timelines
  - Retention, deletion, and e-discovery obligations
  - Jurisdictional issues and government access requests
- **Vendor viability**
  - Financial stability, market position, and roadmap maturity
  - Exit strategy support and portability of data and configurations

**Due diligence lifecycle**

Due diligence is not a one-time questionnaire. It begins before contract signature and continues throughout the relationship. You should treat onboarding, periodic reassessment, and offboarding as separate control points.

1. **Define requirements**
   - Classify the data and workloads you plan to place in the cloud.
   - Identify regulatory, contractual, and internal policy obligations.
   - Set minimum control requirements and unacceptable risks.
2. **Assess the provider**
   - Review security documentation, architecture diagrams, and assurance reports.
   - Validate claims with evidence, not just questionnaires.
   - Compare the provider’s controls to your baseline requirements.
3. **Negotiate protections**
   - Include security obligations, audit rights, breach notification, and service levels in the contract.
   - Clarify responsibilities for logging, incident response, and data deletion.
   - Address subcontractors and geographic restrictions.
4. **Monitor continuously**
   - Reassess the provider after major changes, incidents, or control failures.
   - Track SLA performance, security events, and compliance drift.
   - Update risk treatment decisions as the provider or your business changes.
5. **Exit safely**
   - Confirm data return, deletion, and transition support.
   - Test portability and recovery before termination if possible.
   - Preserve evidence of deletion and handoff.

**Security and assurance artifacts**

You should request artifacts that demonstrate control design and operating effectiveness. The exact set depends on the service model and the sensitivity of the data.

Common evidence includes:

- **SOC reports**
  - SOC 2 Type II is often useful because it covers control design and operating effectiveness over time.
  - Review the scope, exceptions, complementary user entity controls, and subservice organizations.
- **ISO/IEC 27001 certificate**
  - Confirms an information security management system (ISMS), but you still need to review scope and applicability.
- **Penetration test summaries**
  - Useful for understanding recent findings, remediation status, and testing boundaries.
- **Policies and standards**
  - Access control, encryption, logging, incident response, secure development, and retention policies.
- **Architecture and shared responsibility documentation**
  - Clarifies which controls are inherited, shared, or customer-managed.

> A certificate or attestation does not prove the provider is suitable for your use case. Always verify scope, exclusions, and whether the control set matches your data classification and regulatory needs.

**Contractual controls and legal terms**

The contract is where many security expectations become enforceable. In cloud engagements, security teams should work with legal and procurement to ensure the agreement reflects the risk assessment.

Important clauses include:

- **Right to audit**
  - Allows you to verify controls directly or through third-party reports.
  - May be limited in hyperscale environments, so alternative assurance mechanisms are common.
- **Breach notification**
  - Defines timing, content, and communication channels for security incidents.
  - Should align with your regulatory reporting deadlines.
- **Data ownership and deletion**
  - Confirms that you retain ownership of your data and specifies deletion timelines after termination.
- **Subprocessor management**
  - Requires disclosure of subcontractors and notification of material changes.
- **Service levels**
  - Defines uptime, support response, and service credits.
- **Indemnification and liability**
  - Allocates financial responsibility for certain losses, but caps and exclusions often apply.
- **Jurisdiction and governing law**
  - Affects dispute resolution, privacy obligations, and government access issues.

**Practical contract review checklist**

- Confirm the provider states who owns the data.
- Verify breach notification timing is operationally useful, not just legally minimal.
- Ensure deletion includes backups, replicas, and logs where feasible.
- Check whether the provider can support legal hold or e-discovery if required.
- Review whether service credits are the only remedy for outages or security failures.

**Technical validation of cloud controls**

You should validate the provider’s capabilities through technical testing where possible. This is especially important for identity, logging, encryption, and network segmentation.

Examples of practical checks:

```bash
# Verify TLS certificate chain and supported protocol behavior
openssl s_client -connect api.examplecloud.com:443 -servername api.examplecloud.com

# Check DNS resolution and endpoint reachability
nslookup api.examplecloud.com
curl -I https://api.examplecloud.com

# Review local trust and certificate details
openssl x509 -in provider-ca.pem -text -noout
```

For identity federation, confirm that the provider supports your preferred identity provider and strong authentication:

```yaml
# Example SAML federation concept
idp:
  entity_id: https://idp.example.com
  mfa_required: true
  assertion_signing: required
  session_timeout_minutes: 60
```

For logging, verify that events can be exported to your SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) platform:

```bash
# Example: pull cloud audit logs into a central collector
aws logs describe-log-groups
az monitor diagnostic-settings list --resource /subscriptions/<sub-id>/resourceGroups/<rg>/providers/Microsoft.Storage/storageAccounts/<acct>
gcloud logging sinks list
```

**Cloud service model considerations**

The service model changes what you must evaluate and what you can control.

| Model | What the provider typically manages | What you still must verify |
|---|---|---|
| IaaS (Infrastructure as a Service) | Physical hosts, hypervisor, core networking, storage platform | Guest OS hardening, patching, IAM, data protection, workload configuration |
| PaaS (Platform as a Service) | Runtime, middleware, managed scaling, platform patching | Application security, identity, secrets, data governance |
| SaaS (Software as a Service) | Application stack, platform, infrastructure | User access, configuration, data classification, retention, integration security |

In SaaS, due diligence often focuses more on configuration, identity, privacy, and contractual controls. In IaaS, you need deeper technical validation because you retain more operational responsibility.

**Risk domains to assess**

A CISSP-style assessment should map provider risks to business impact and control effectiveness.

- **Confidentiality**
  - Evaluate encryption, access controls, tenant isolation, and key ownership.
- **Integrity**
  - Review change control, logging, code signing, and tamper-evident mechanisms.
- **Availability**
  - Assess redundancy, DDoS protection, failover, and support responsiveness.
- **Privacy**
  - Confirm lawful processing, data minimization, retention, and deletion.
- **Compliance**
  - Ensure the provider can support your obligations under applicable laws and standards.
- **Third-party risk**
  - Examine subcontractors, upstream dependencies, and concentration exposure.

**Questions to ask during assessment**

Use targeted questions to move beyond generic questionnaires.

- How do you isolate tenants at the compute, storage, and network layers?
- Can customers manage their own encryption keys, and what happens if keys are revoked?
- What logs are available, how long are they retained, and can they be exported automatically?
- How do you notify customers of incidents, and what is your internal escalation path?
- Which subprocessors handle customer data, and how are they vetted?
- What is your documented RTO and RPO for the service?
- How do you support secure deletion at termination?
- What happens if your service is acquired, discontinued, or materially changed?

**Evidence review pitfalls**

Not all evidence is equally useful. You should be cautious about over-relying on high-level claims or outdated reports.

- **Scope mismatch**
  - A report may cover only one region, one service, or one business unit.
- **Control exceptions**
  - Findings may be accepted by management but still represent residual risk.
- **Shared responsibility gaps**
  - The provider may secure the platform while leaving critical configuration to you.
- **Stale evidence**
  - Annual reports may not reflect recent incidents or architecture changes.
- **Marketing bias**
  - Security whitepapers are helpful, but they are not independent assurance.

**Example due diligence workflow**

1. Classify the intended workload and data.
2. Map legal and regulatory requirements.
3. Request assurance reports and architecture documentation.
4. Validate identity, logging, encryption, and resilience controls.
5. Review contract terms with legal and procurement.
6. Approve, remediate, or reject based on residual risk.
7. Reassess periodically and after major provider changes.

**Operational monitoring after onboarding**

Once the provider is live, you need ongoing oversight. Continuous monitoring helps you detect drift between the original assessment and current reality.

Useful monitoring activities include:

- Reviewing SLA and uptime reports
- Tracking security advisories and incident notifications
- Revalidating subprocessors and data residency changes
- Testing backup restore and failover procedures
- Monitoring access reviews and privileged account usage
- Reassessing the provider after mergers, acquisitions, or service expansions

A simple internal control record might look like this:

```text
Provider: ExampleCloud
Service: Managed database
Data class: Confidential
Review cadence: Quarterly
Required artifacts: SOC 2 Type II, pen test summary, subprocessor list
Open issues: MFA enforcement for admin console, log export latency
Residual risk: Medium
Decision: Accept with remediation plan
```

**Exit strategy and portability**

Exit planning is part of due diligence, not an afterthought. If you cannot leave a provider cleanly, you may be locked into an unacceptable risk position.

You should confirm:

- Data export formats are usable and documented.
- Configuration and infrastructure definitions can be recreated elsewhere.
- Deletion requests cover primary storage, replicas, backups, and logs as contractually appropriate.
- Transition support is available for a reasonable period.
- Dependencies on proprietary services are understood and accepted.

**Exam focus points**

For CISSP exam purposes, remember that due diligence is about informed risk acceptance, not absolute security. The best answer usually balances business need, control verification, and contractual protection.

- **Due diligence** precedes risk acceptance.
- **Shared responsibility** must be explicit.
- **Independent assurance** is stronger than vendor self-attestation.
- **Contracts** translate security expectations into obligations.
- **Continuous monitoring** is required because cloud services change frequently.
- **Exit planning** reduces lock-in and supports resilience.

**Common mistakes to avoid**

- Assuming a major provider is automatically suitable for all workloads
- Treating a compliance certificate as proof of complete security
- Ignoring data residency, subprocessors, or breach notification terms
- Failing to test logging, restore, or failover capabilities
- Neglecting offboarding and secure deletion requirements

**Practical takeaway**

A strong cloud vendor due diligence program combines governance, technical validation, legal review, and ongoing monitoring. You reduce risk by verifying what the provider actually does, documenting what you require, and ensuring the contract and operating model support your security objectives.
