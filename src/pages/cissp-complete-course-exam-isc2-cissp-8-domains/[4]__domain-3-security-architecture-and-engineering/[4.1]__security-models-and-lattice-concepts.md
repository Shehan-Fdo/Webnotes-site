## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Security models: the exam lens**

Security models are formal ways to describe how a system should make access decisions. They matter because they turn policy into rules you can reason about, test, and enforce. For CISSP, you should know the difference between models that protect **confidentiality**, **integrity**, and **conflict of interest**, and how those models appear in real systems such as **mandatory access control** (MAC) and **multi-level security** (MLS).

A useful way to study them is to separate the *mathematical model* from the *implementation*:

- The model defines the rule set.
- The implementation enforces it in an operating system, database, or application.
- Labels, clearances, and categories are the practical mechanisms that carry the model.

**Core model families**

- **Confidentiality models** focus on preventing unauthorized disclosure.
- **Integrity models** focus on preventing unauthorized or untrusted modification.
- **Conflict-of-interest models** focus on preventing access that could create a business or ethical conflict over time.
- **Access control models** such as DAC, MAC, RBAC, and ABAC are broader categories; security models often sit underneath them and define the decision logic.

**Lattice-based access control**

A **lattice** is a partially ordered set in which every pair of elements has both:
- a **least upper bound** (LUB), also called a **join**
- a **greatest lower bound** (GLB), also called a **meet**

This is more than a general partial order. In a partial order, some pairs may be incomparable and no common join or meet is guaranteed. In a lattice, every pair must have both operations, which makes the structure predictable for security decisions.

In security, lattice elements usually represent labels such as:

- `Unclassified`
- `Confidential`
- `Secret`
- `Top Secret`

and category sets such as:

- `{NATO}`
- `{Crypto}`
- `{NATO, Crypto}`

A label often combines:
- a **classification level**
- a **category set** or **compartment set**

A subject label **dominates** an object label when:
- the subject’s classification is at least as high as the object’s classification, and
- the subject’s category set is a **superset** of the object’s category set

That second point matters: it is not enough to “include NATO” in a vague sense. The subject must contain **all** categories required by the object.

Example:
- Subject: `Secret {NATO, Crypto}`
- Object: `Confidential {NATO}`

The subject dominates the object because:
- `Secret` is higher than `Confidential`
- `{NATO, Crypto}` is a superset of `{NATO}`

If the object were `Confidential {NATO, Nuclear}`, the subject would **not** dominate it because the subject lacks `Nuclear`.

**How lattice concepts support access control**

Lattice-based access control gives you a formal way to compare labels and decide whether access is allowed. It is especially useful when you need:
- strict confidentiality boundaries
- compartmented information
- predictable policy enforcement across many users and objects

In practice, the lattice is the mathematical foundation, while the system’s policy determines which operations are allowed for each direction of flow.

> **Important:** Dominance alone does not authorize every kind of access. Read and write decisions depend on the specific policy layered on top of the lattice, such as Bell-LaPadula or Biba.

**Bell-LaPadula: confidentiality model**

The **Bell-LaPadula (BLP)** model is a classic confidentiality model used to prevent information leakage. It is often associated with military and government-style MLS systems.

Its core rules are:

- **Simple Security Property**: no read up
  - A subject may read an object only if the subject dominates the object.
- **\*-Property**: no write down
  - A subject may write to an object only if the object dominates the subject, or equivalently if the write does not lower confidentiality.

The purpose is to stop a high-clearance subject from leaking sensitive data to a lower-level object.

Example:
- A `Secret` user can read `Confidential` data.
- A `Secret` user cannot write directly to `Unclassified` data if that would expose secret information.

BLP is about **confidentiality**, not integrity. It does not try to ensure that data is trustworthy; it tries to ensure that data does not flow to lower classifications.

**Bell-LaPadula tranquility**

Tranquility describes whether labels can change during system operation.

- **Strong tranquility**
  - Security labels do not change during normal operation.
  - This is rigid and easier to reason about.
- **Weak tranquility**
  - Labels may change, but only in ways that do not violate the security policy.
  - This is more practical in real systems.

For exam purposes, remember:
- strong tranquility = labels stay fixed
- weak tranquility = labels may change under controlled conditions

**Biba: integrity model**

The **Biba** model is the integrity counterpart to Bell-LaPadula. It protects data from being corrupted by less trustworthy subjects.

Its classic rules are:

- **Simple Integrity Property**: no read down
  - A subject should not read data from a lower integrity level if that data could contaminate the subject’s decisions.
- **\*-Integrity Property**: no write up
  - A subject should not write to a higher integrity object.
- **Invocation Property**
  - A subject should not invoke a lower integrity subject if that lower integrity subject could compromise the higher integrity subject.

The invocation property is often tested because it is easy to confuse with the read/write rules. It addresses *calling or using* a lower-integrity component, not just reading or writing data.

Example:
- A high-integrity payroll process should not invoke an untrusted script to calculate salaries.
- A trusted process should not consume low-integrity input without validation.

**Biba variants**

Biba has variants that are commonly mentioned in exam prep:

- **Strict Biba**
  - Enforces the classic no-read-down and no-write-up rules.
- **Low-water mark**
  - Integrity can be lowered when a subject reads lower-integrity data.
  - This is more flexible but can spread contamination.
- **Ring policy**
  - A variant that allows more flexibility by treating integrity levels in a ring-like structure rather than a strict hierarchy.
  - Do not confuse this with hardware protection rings; in Biba, it is a policy variant, not CPU privilege rings.

The key exam idea is that Biba is about preserving trustworthiness, and variants trade strictness for usability.

**Clark-Wilson: commercial integrity model**

The **Clark-Wilson** model is another major integrity model, especially relevant in business systems. It focuses on well-formed transactions and separation of duties rather than pure lattice rules.

Its main ideas are:

- **Constrained Data Items (CDIs)**
  - Data that must be protected by integrity controls.
- **Unconstrained Data Items (UDIs)**
  - Data not yet validated or controlled.
- **Transformation Procedures (TPs)**
  - Authorized programs that can change CDIs.
- **Integrity Verification Procedures (IVPs)**
  - Checks that CDIs remain valid.

Clark-Wilson is useful when you need:
- transaction integrity
- separation of duties
- auditability
- business process control

Compared with Biba:
- Biba is a formal lattice-based integrity model.
- Clark-Wilson is a practical commercial integrity model.

**Brewer-Nash: conflict-of-interest model**

The **Brewer-Nash** model, also called the **Chinese Wall** model, prevents conflicts of interest by changing access decisions based on a user’s access history.

Its key idea is:
- once you access data from one company in a conflict class, your future access is restricted to prevent access to competing companies in that same class

This model is dynamic:
- access depends not only on labels, but also on what you have already accessed

That history-sensitive behavior is the important exam point.

Example:
- A consultant accesses confidential records for Bank A.
- After that, the system may block access to Bank B’s records if Bank A and Bank B are in the same conflict class.

Limitations to remember:
- It can be complex to administer.
- It depends on accurate conflict-class grouping.
- It is harder to scale than simpler static label models.

**MLS and MAC in practice**

**Multi-level security** systems enforce access across multiple classification levels and compartments. **Mandatory access control** means the system, not the user, decides access based on policy.

In practice, MLS/MAC systems use:
- labels on subjects and objects
- trusted administrative processes to assign or change labels
- kernel or policy engine enforcement

Examples of real-world enforcement patterns:
- a government-style document system where `Secret` users can read `Confidential` files but not write to `Unclassified` export folders
- a database with row labels that restrict which records a user can query
- a Linux system using SELinux to enforce label-based policy

SELinux is a Linux MAC framework that can implement formal label-based controls, including MLS-style and MCS-style policies. It is not just “stricter Unix permissions”; it is a policy engine that can confine processes and data flows.

Useful SELinux inspection commands:

```bash
getenforce
sestatus
id -Z
ps -eZ | head
ls -Z /var/www/html
```

Example of a labeled file context view:

```bash
$ ls -Z /var/www/html
system_u:object_r:httpd_sys_content_t:s0 index.html
```

Example of changing a file context label:

```bash
sudo chcon -t httpd_sys_content_t /var/www/html/index.html
```

Example of persistent labeling with `semanage`:

```bash
sudo semanage fcontext -a -t httpd_sys_content_t '/srv/web(/.*)?'
sudo restorecon -Rv /srv/web
```

These commands show the practical difference between:
- a temporary label change with `chcon`
- a policy-managed persistent mapping with `semanage fcontext`

**Government-style label and clearance flow**

A typical MLS environment uses:
- **clearance** for users or subjects
- **classification** for objects
- **compartments** for need-to-know restrictions

A simplified flow looks like this:

1. A user authenticates and receives a clearance, such as `Secret {NATO, Crypto}`.
2. The system checks the object label, such as `Confidential {NATO}`.
3. The system compares dominance and the policy rule for the requested operation.
4. If the operation is a read and the subject dominates the object, access may be allowed.
5. If the operation is a write, the system applies the model’s write rule, such as no write down in Bell-LaPadula.

This is why you should not memorize “dominance means access.” The operation matters.

**Formal lattice operations in security terms**

The join and meet operations are useful for reasoning about combined labels.

- **Join / LUB**
  - the least label that dominates both labels
  - useful when combining requirements
- **Meet / GLB**
  - the greatest label dominated by both labels
  - useful when finding the common lower bound

Example intuition:
- If one object is `Secret {NATO}` and another is `Secret {Crypto}`, the join may be `Secret {NATO, Crypto}`.
- The meet may be a lower label with the intersection of categories, depending on the lattice design.

For CISSP, the key point is not the algebra itself but the security implication:
- lattice structure gives a consistent way to compare labels
- every pair has a predictable upper and lower bound

**Comparison of major models**

| Model | Primary goal | Core rule idea | Typical use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bell-LaPadula | Confidentiality | No read up, no write down | Military/government MLS |
| Biba | Integrity | No read down, no write up | Trusted processing, integrity control |
| Clark-Wilson | Commercial integrity | Well-formed transactions, separation of duties | Business systems, accounting |
| Brewer-Nash | Conflict of interest | Access depends on history | Consulting, finance, legal separation |

Use this table to decide which model fits the scenario:
- secrecy and disclosure risk → Bell-LaPadula
- trustworthiness and contamination risk → Biba
- transaction correctness and auditability → Clark-Wilson
- competing-client conflict prevention → Brewer-Nash

**Exam traps and distinctions**

- **Dominance is not universal authorization**
  - It is a comparison relation used by a policy.
- **Read and write rules differ by model**
  - Bell-LaPadula and Biba reverse each other’s emphasis.
- **Categories matter as much as classification**
  - A higher level without the right compartments may still fail dominance.
- **MLS is an implementation style**
  - The lattice is the formal model behind it.
- **MAC is not discretionary**
  - Users cannot override the policy at will.
- **Brewer-Nash is history-aware**
  - Past access changes future access.
- **Clark-Wilson is not a lattice model**
  - It is a transaction-oriented integrity framework.

**Practical configuration mindset**

When you design or review a label-based system, ask:

- What is the security objective: confidentiality, integrity, or conflict avoidance?
- Are labels static or can they change?
- Are categories used, and if so, how are they assigned?
- Which operations are allowed for each direction of flow?
- Is the system enforcing policy centrally, or can users bypass it?

A simple policy sketch might look like this:

```text
Subject: Secret {NATO, Crypto}
Object:  Confidential {NATO}

Read:
- Allowed if subject dominates object

Write:
- Allowed only if policy permits the flow
- In Bell-LaPadula, writing to lower classification is blocked
```

**Quick memory anchors**

- **Bell-LaPadula** = protect confidentiality
- **Biba** = protect integrity
- **Clark-Wilson** = protect business transaction integrity
- **Brewer-Nash** = prevent conflict of interest
- **Lattice** = ordered labels with join and meet
- **Dominance** = higher or equal level plus superset of categories

**Why this matters on CISSP**

CISSP questions often test whether you can:
- identify the correct model from a scenario
- distinguish confidentiality from integrity
- recognize when categories/compartments affect access
- understand that formal models are implemented through MAC/MLS systems
- avoid confusing policy names with enforcement mechanisms

If a question mentions:
- classified documents, clearances, compartments → think Bell-LaPadula and MLS
- untrusted input, contamination, trusted processes → think Biba
- accounting controls, valid transactions, separation of duties → think Clark-Wilson
- consulting clients, competing organizations, access history → think Brewer-Nash
