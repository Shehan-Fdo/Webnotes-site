**Audit fundamentals**

An audit is a structured, independent review of controls, processes, and records to determine whether they meet defined criteria. In CISSP terms, you care about whether the organization can demonstrate control effectiveness, compliance, and accountability, not just whether a control exists on paper.

Audits support governance by answering three questions:

- Are controls designed appropriately?
- Are controls operating effectively?
- Are exceptions understood, documented, and remediated?

Audit work is evidence-driven. Assertions without verifiable artifacts do not hold up in a formal review, especially when findings may affect legal, regulatory, financial, or security outcomes.

**Audit types and phases**

Audits differ by purpose, scope, and relationship to the organization. CISSP exam questions often test whether you can distinguish the audit type from the audit objective.

- **Internal audits** are performed by the organization or an internal audit function.
  - They assess internal control design and operating effectiveness.
  - They often focus on process improvement, risk reduction, and readiness for external review.
- **External audits** are performed by an independent party outside the organization.
  - They provide greater assurance to regulators, customers, shareholders, or certifying bodies.
  - They are typically more formal and evidence-sensitive.
- **First-party audits** are self-assessments by the organization against its own policies or a standard.
  - They are useful for readiness checks and continuous improvement.
- **Second-party audits** are performed by a customer, partner, or contracting organization.
  - They commonly assess supplier security, privacy, or contractual compliance.
- **Third-party audits** are performed by an independent external auditor.
  - They are used for certification, attestation, or regulatory assurance.

Audit scope also varies by subject matter:

- **Compliance audits** verify adherence to laws, regulations, standards, and internal policy.
- **Operational audits** evaluate efficiency, effectiveness, and process performance.
- **Financial audits** assess the accuracy of financial statements and related controls.
- **Security audits** examine the design and operation of security controls, access management, logging, incident handling, and resilience.

A typical audit lifecycle includes:

1. Define scope, objectives, criteria, and constraints.
2. Establish authority, independence, and access requirements.
3. Plan the audit approach and sampling strategy.
4. Collect and validate evidence.
5. Analyze results and identify exceptions.
6. Report findings with risk impact and recommendations.
7. Track remediation and retain records according to policy.

**Auditor independence, objectivity, and ethics**

Auditor credibility depends on independence and objectivity. If the auditor designed the control, operates the control, or has a personal stake in the outcome, the audit result may be biased or invalid.

- **Independence** means the auditor is free from undue influence and conflicts of interest.
- **Objectivity** means the auditor evaluates evidence fairly and without preconceived conclusions.
- **Ethics** require honesty, confidentiality, due care, and professional skepticism.

Common boundary issues include:

- Auditing your own work or your own team’s controls.
- Reviewing a system you administer or approve.
- Accepting gifts, favors, or pressure from management.
- Allowing scope to be narrowed to hide known weaknesses.

> If independence is compromised, the audit may still be useful as a management review, but it should not be represented as an independent assurance activity.

The CISSP perspective is practical: the auditor should not become the control owner, incident responder, or remediation implementer for the same issue being audited. That separation preserves the integrity of the evidence and the report.

**Audit planning and risk**

Audit planning starts with risk. You prioritize what matters most by understanding how likely a weakness is and how much damage it could cause.

- **Inherent risk** is the risk that exists before controls are applied.
- **Control risk** is the risk that controls fail to prevent or detect an issue.
- **Residual risk** is the remaining risk after controls are implemented and operating.

These risks influence:

- Audit scope and depth
- Sampling size and selection
- Evidence requirements
- Severity of findings
- Escalation thresholds

For example, a privileged access review for a production payment system has higher audit priority than a low-impact internal training portal because the inherent and residual risks are greater.

A useful planning mindset is:

- High inherent risk + weak controls = deeper testing and stronger evidence.
- Low inherent risk + mature controls = narrower testing may be acceptable.
- High residual risk = findings should be escalated quickly and clearly.

**Evidence types and quality**

Audit evidence is any information used to support a conclusion. Strong evidence is relevant, reliable, sufficient, and admissible.

Common evidence sources include:

- Policies, standards, procedures, and control narratives
- System logs, SIEM alerts, and audit trails
- Configuration exports and screenshots
- Tickets, approvals, and change records
- Interview notes and meeting minutes
- Reports from vulnerability scans, penetration tests, or control monitoring
- Physical observations and walkthroughs
- Third-party attestations and contracts

Evidence quality matters more than volume.

- **Relevant** evidence directly supports the control objective.
- **Reliable** evidence comes from a trustworthy source and is hard to alter.
- **Sufficient** evidence is enough to support a conclusion without overreliance on one artifact.
- **Admissible** evidence can be accepted in a formal process and has not been improperly handled.

Examples of stronger evidence:

- Direct system-generated logs with timestamps
- Read-only exports from authoritative systems
- Signed approvals from workflow systems
- Immutable or tamper-evident records

Examples of weaker evidence:

- Verbal claims without corroboration
- Edited screenshots with no context
- Unverified copies of records
- Evidence collected outside approved procedures

**Evidence handling, admissibility, and chain of custody**

When evidence may support disciplinary action, litigation, regulatory action, or forensic investigation, handling must be defensible. The goal is to preserve integrity and prove that the evidence was not altered.

Key concepts:

- **Chain of custody** documents who collected, handled, transferred, stored, and analyzed the evidence.
- **Integrity** is preserved through hashing, access control, and tamper-evident storage.
- **Admissibility** depends on lawful collection, proper handling, and documented provenance.

A defensible evidence record usually includes:

- Unique evidence ID
- Description of the item or artifact
- Source system, location, and owner
- Date and time collected
- Collector identity
- Collection method and tool used
- Hash values for digital artifacts
- Transfer history and storage location
- Access log or custody log
- Retention and disposition instructions

Use hashing to verify that a file has not changed:

```bash
sha256sum firewall-config-backup.tar.gz
sha256sum access-log-2026-04-25.log
```

For disk or memory acquisition, use approved forensic procedures and tools. In higher-risk cases, involve legal counsel early to determine whether the collection must preserve privilege, meet regulatory obligations, or support litigation hold requirements.

> If evidence may be used in court, do not improvise collection steps. Follow organizational policy, involve legal counsel, and preserve chain of custody from the start.

When to involve specialists:

- **Legal counsel** when evidence may be used in litigation, regulatory response, or employment action.
- **Law enforcement** when criminal activity is suspected and reporting is required or strategically appropriate.
- **eDiscovery** teams when large-scale legal review, preservation, or production is needed.
- **Forensic investigators** when you need bit-level acquisition, malware analysis, or artifact preservation.

**Sampling methods and bias**

Audits rarely examine every record. Sampling lets you test a representative subset while managing time and cost. The sample must be defensible and aligned to the risk.

Common sampling methods:

- **Random sampling** gives each item an equal chance of selection.
  - Useful when you want an unbiased general view.
- **Systematic sampling** selects every *n*th item after a random start.
  - Useful for large, ordered populations such as transaction logs.
- **Stratified sampling** divides the population into meaningful groups and samples each group.
  - Useful when risk differs by category, such as privileged vs standard accounts.
- **Judgmental sampling** uses auditor expertise to select items likely to reveal issues.
  - Useful for targeted testing, but it is more subjective and less statistically representative.

Other practical approaches:

- **Attribute sampling** checks whether a control attribute is present or absent.
- **Variable sampling** measures a numeric value, such as transaction amount or downtime.

Sampling risks to manage:

- **Sampling bias** occurs when the sample is not representative.
- **Outliers** can distort conclusions if they are ignored or overemphasized.
- **Population errors** happen when the source list is incomplete or inaccurate.

To reduce bias:

- Define the population clearly before selecting samples.
- Use a documented selection method.
- Avoid cherry-picking only convenient records.
- Reconcile the population count to authoritative sources.
- Investigate outliers rather than discarding them without justification.

A practical example:

- For access reviews, stratify by privilege level and system criticality.
- For change management, use systematic sampling across the audit period.
- For fraud-sensitive transactions, combine random sampling with judgmental selection of high-value items.

**Workpapers and evidence packages**

Workpapers are the audit record. They should allow another qualified reviewer to understand what you tested, what you found, and how you reached your conclusion.

A strong workpaper set includes:

- Audit objective and scope
- Criteria used for evaluation
- Population definition
- Sampling method and rationale
- Evidence list with references
- Test steps performed
- Results and exceptions
- Reviewer sign-off
- Date stamps and version history

A formal evidence package should be organized and traceable:

```text
Audit-2026-04-AccessReview/
├── 01-scope-and-criteria/
├── 02-population-and-sampling/
├── 03-evidence/
│   ├── logs/
│   ├── screenshots/
│   ├── exports/
│   └── approvals/
├── 04-analysis/
├── 05-findings/
└── 06-retention-and-disposition/
```

Good documentation practices:

- Reference each artifact with a unique ID.
- Record the exact query, command, or filter used.
- Preserve original evidence separately from working copies.
- Note any assumptions, limitations, or exceptions.
- Keep a clear audit trail of edits and reviewer comments.

Example of a defensible log collection note:

```text
Evidence ID: EV-LOG-014
Source: SIEM cluster node 3
Collection time: 2026-04-25 14:12 UTC
Collector: J. Rivera
Method: Read-only export via approved admin role
Hash: SHA-256 8f3c...a91d
Storage: WORM repository /case/2026-041
Notes: Export covers 2026-04-24 00:00 UTC to 2026-04-25 00:00 UTC
```

**Testing controls and audit procedures**

Auditors use a mix of inquiry, observation, inspection, and re-performance. The more critical the control, the more you should prefer direct evidence and re-performance over verbal assurance.

Common procedures:

- **Inquiry** asks personnel how a process works.
- **Observation** watches the process being performed.
- **Inspection** reviews records, configurations, or artifacts.
- **Re-performance** repeats the control or test to verify the result.

Examples:

- Verify that privileged access approvals exist in the ticketing system.
- Observe a backup restore test and confirm the restore succeeds.
- Re-perform a password policy check against directory settings.
- Inspect firewall rule changes for proper approval and testing evidence.

A control may be designed well but fail in operation. Audit testing should distinguish:

- **Design effectiveness**: the control, if operated as intended, should prevent or detect the issue.
- **Operating effectiveness**: the control actually works in practice over time.

**Findings, severity, and reporting**

Audit findings should be written so management can act on them and so the conclusion is defensible. A strong finding is specific, evidence-based, and tied to risk.

A common finding structure is:

- **Condition**: what you observed
- **Criteria**: what should have happened
- **Cause**: why the gap exists
- **Effect**: the risk or impact
- **Recommendation**: what should change

Example:

- **Condition**: 12 of 40 privileged accounts lacked quarterly review evidence.
- **Criteria**: Policy requires quarterly access certification for privileged users.
- **Cause**: The review workflow was not enforced after a tool migration.
- **Effect**: Unauthorized access could persist undetected, increasing breach risk.
- **Recommendation**: Restore workflow enforcement, backfill reviews, and assign ownership.

Severity should reflect business impact, likelihood, and exposure. A high-severity finding usually involves:

- Sensitive data
- Privileged access
- Regulatory noncompliance
- Repeated control failure
- Weak compensating controls

Reporting principles:

- Be factual and concise.
- Separate observation from interpretation.
- Avoid blame language.
- Tie each finding to a control objective or requirement.
- Include management response and remediation target dates when available.

**Audit phases and deliverables**

A disciplined audit follows phases that produce traceable deliverables.

1. **Initiation**
   - Define purpose, scope, criteria, and authority.
   - Confirm independence and access.
2. **Planning**
   - Identify risks, controls, and evidence sources.
   - Select sampling methods and test procedures.
3. **Fieldwork**
   - Collect evidence, interview stakeholders, and test controls.
   - Document exceptions and follow-up questions.
4. **Analysis**
   - Compare evidence to criteria.
   - Assess impact, root cause, and severity.
5. **Reporting**
   - Present findings, recommendations, and management responses.
6. **Follow-up**
   - Verify remediation and closure.
   - Escalate unresolved high-risk items.

Deliverables often include:

- Audit charter or engagement letter
- Scope statement
- Risk assessment
- Test plan
- Workpapers
- Findings report
- Remediation tracker
- Closure memo

**Retention, disposition, and secure destruction**

Audit evidence does not disappear when the report is issued. Retention requirements may come from policy, regulation, contract, or legal hold.

Key practices:

- Follow the organization’s retention schedule.
- Preserve evidence longer if litigation, investigation, or regulatory review is pending.
- Store records securely with access controls and logging.
- Destroy evidence securely when retention expires and no hold applies.

Retention considerations:

- Keep original evidence and workpapers for the required period.
- Maintain enough context to reconstruct the audit decision later.
- Protect confidentiality of sensitive logs, credentials, and personal data.
- Use secure destruction methods for digital media and paper records.

Examples of secure destruction:

```bash
shred -u sensitive-export.csv
```

For higher assurance, use approved media sanitization methods appropriate to the storage type and data classification.

**Practical audit examples**

A few common CISSP-relevant scenarios help connect the concepts.

- **Access review audit**
  - Sample privileged accounts across critical systems.
  - Verify approvals, recertification, and timely removal of stale access.
  - Use stratified sampling to separate admins, service accounts, and standard users.
- **Change management audit**
  - Inspect change tickets, testing evidence, and rollback plans.
  - Use systematic sampling across the audit period.
  - Look for emergency changes without post-implementation review.
- **Log management audit**
  - Confirm log sources, retention settings, and alert coverage.
  - Reconcile SIEM ingestion against critical assets.
  - Preserve log exports with hashes and custody records.
- **Vendor security audit**
  - Review contractual controls, attestations, and incident notification clauses.
  - Use second-party audit logic to validate supplier obligations.
  - Escalate gaps that affect shared risk or data handling.

**Exam focus points**

- Distinguish internal, external, first-party, second-party, and third-party audits.
- Match audit type to purpose: compliance, operational, financial, or security.
- Remember that independence and objectivity are essential to credible audit results.
- Use the right sampling method for the population and risk.
- Treat evidence handling as a chain-of-custody problem when legal or forensic use is possible.
- Write findings in condition, criteria, cause, effect, recommendation format.
- Tie severity to risk, not just to policy deviation.
- Retain and dispose of evidence according to policy and legal requirements.
