**Security testing strategy: purpose and scope**

Security testing validates whether controls work as intended and whether residual risk is acceptable. In CISSP terms, it is part of assurance: you test controls, identify weaknesses, and feed results into risk management and continuous improvement.

Security testing is not the same as monitoring or incident response:

- **Security testing** is planned, authorized, and time-bounded.
- **Security monitoring** is continuous detection of events and anomalies.
- **Incident response** reacts to active or suspected compromise.
- **Testing outputs** are findings, evidence, and remediation recommendations.
- **Monitoring outputs** are alerts, logs, and detections.
- **Incident response outputs** are containment actions, eradication steps, and lessons learned.

A good strategy aligns test depth with business risk, system criticality, regulatory obligations, and change cadence. You do not test everything the same way; you choose methods that answer specific assurance questions.

**Governance, ownership, and separation of duties**

Security testing needs clear ownership and authorization. The system owner or business owner usually owns the asset and accepts risk. The security team or independent assessor often plans and executes testing. Auditors validate governance and evidence, but they should not design or run the test they later audit.

Key governance roles:

- **System owner** approves scope for their system and accepts residual risk.
- **Security owner / CISO function** defines policy, minimum testing standards, and escalation paths.
- **Test lead / assessor** designs and executes the test plan.
- **Auditor** independently reviews whether the process followed policy and whether evidence is sufficient.
- **Change manager / operations** coordinates timing to avoid outages.
- **Legal / privacy / compliance** reviews jurisdiction, consent, and data handling constraints.

Separation of duties matters because the person who performs the test should not be the only person who approves it, and the person who approves it should not be the only person who verifies the results. This reduces bias, prevents self-approval, and improves auditability.

A practical approval matrix often includes:

| Activity | Requestor | Approver | Executor | Reviewer |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Internal vulnerability scan | Security team | System owner | Security ops | Security manager |
| Penetration test | Security governance | System owner + legal if needed | Independent tester | Risk owner |
| Phishing simulation | Security awareness | HR + legal + security leadership | Awareness team or vendor | Compliance |
| Physical security test | Facilities/security | Site owner + executive sponsor | Authorized red team | Security leadership |
| Third-party assessment | Vendor management | Risk owner | External assessor | Internal security |

> Testing without explicit authorization can create legal exposure, operational disruption, and evidence that is unusable for audit or remediation.

**Security testing strategy: what to test and when**

A mature strategy uses multiple test types because no single method finds every weakness. CISSP expects you to distinguish between design review, implementation review, and active testing.

Common test types include:

- **Security reviews**: Evaluate policies, architecture, configurations, and procedures against requirements.
- **Code review**: Inspect source code or binaries for insecure logic, injection flaws, secrets, and unsafe APIs.
- **Interface testing**: Validate trust boundaries between systems, APIs, services, and data flows.
- **Acceptance testing**: Confirm security requirements before production release or go-live.
- **Vulnerability scanning**: Identify known weaknesses using automated tools.
- **Penetration testing**: Attempt controlled exploitation to demonstrate impact.
- **Configuration review**: Check hardening, permissions, and baseline compliance.
- **Architecture review**: Assess segmentation, trust zones, identity flows, and resilience.
- **Social engineering testing**: Measure human and process resilience to deception.
- **Physical security testing**: Assess facility controls, badge processes, and tailgating resistance.

Use the right method for the question:

- Use **security reviews** when you need assurance about policy alignment or design quality.
- Use **code review** when you need to find logic flaws before deployment.
- Use **interface testing** when systems exchange data across trust boundaries.
- Use **acceptance testing** when a business owner must sign off before release.
- Use **penetration testing** when you need proof of exploitability and business impact.
- Use **scans** when you need broad coverage and repeatability.

**Penetration testing methodologies and phases**

Penetration testing is a controlled attempt to exploit vulnerabilities to show real-world risk. CISSP commonly distinguishes test styles by how much information the tester has.

| Method | Tester knowledge | Strengths | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Black-box** | Minimal or none | Simulates external attacker realism | Slower discovery, may miss internal issues |
| **Gray-box** | Partial knowledge | Balances realism and efficiency | Can underrepresent unknown attacker behavior |
| **White-box** | Full knowledge | Deep coverage of code, config, and architecture | Less realistic for external attack paths |

Typical phases:

1. **Planning and authorization**
   - Define scope, targets, exclusions, timing, and contacts.
   - Confirm legal approval and rules of engagement.
2. **Reconnaissance**
   - Gather information from public sources, DNS, metadata, and exposed services.
3. **Enumeration and discovery**
   - Identify hosts, ports, users, services, versions, and trust relationships.
4. **Vulnerability analysis**
   - Map findings to likely weaknesses and attack paths.
5. **Exploitation**
   - Attempt controlled exploitation within approved limits.
6. **Post-exploitation**
   - Validate impact, privilege escalation, lateral movement, or data access only if authorized.
7. **Cleanup**
   - Remove accounts, payloads, test data, and temporary changes.
8. **Reporting**
   - Document evidence, impact, likelihood, and remediation guidance.

Practical command examples for discovery and validation:

```bash
nmap -sV -sC -Pn 10.10.10.0/24
nmap --script vuln 10.10.10.15
curl -i https://api.example.com/health
openssl s_client -connect mail.example.com:443 -servername mail.example.com
```

Use these carefully and only within approved scope. A scan is not a penetration test; it is one input to the test process.

**Security reviews, code review, interface testing, and acceptance testing**

Security reviews are often the first line of assurance. They examine whether the design itself supports confidentiality, integrity, and availability.

- **Architecture review**
  - Check trust boundaries, segmentation, identity federation, encryption, and logging.
  - Look for single points of failure and weak administrative paths.
- **Code review**
  - Look for injection, insecure deserialization, hard-coded secrets, weak crypto, and authorization bypass.
  - Review both application code and infrastructure-as-code where possible.
- **Interface testing**
  - Validate API authentication, authorization, input validation, rate limiting, and error handling.
  - Test message queues, file transfers, and service-to-service calls.
- **Acceptance testing**
  - Confirm security requirements are met before production release.
  - Include security acceptance criteria in the definition of done.

Example acceptance criteria:

```text
- MFA is enforced for all privileged users.
- TLS 1.2+ is required for external connections.
- Critical findings are remediated or formally risk-accepted before go-live.
- Logging is enabled for authentication, privilege changes, and admin actions.
```

**Social engineering and physical security testing governance**

Social engineering tests measure whether people and processes resist manipulation. Because these tests can affect trust, morale, and privacy, they require stronger governance than routine technical scans.

Common forms:

- **Phishing simulations**: Test whether users click links, open attachments, or submit credentials.
- **Vishing**: Voice phishing against help desks or employees.
- **Smishing**: SMS-based deception.
- **Physical security testing**: Tailgating, badge cloning attempts, or unauthorized entry attempts.

Governance requirements are stricter because these tests can cross ethical and legal lines:

- Obtain explicit approval from leadership, HR, legal, and security governance when required.
- Define whether you may collect credentials, record calls, or photograph facilities.
- Avoid collecting unnecessary personal data.
- Protect employee privacy and minimize embarrassment.
- Coordinate with incident response so real alerts are not misclassified.
- Decide in advance whether the test is covert or announced.

Special constraints:

- Do not test protected groups or sensitive workflows without explicit review.
- Do not use real customer data in phishing lures.
- Do not bypass safety systems or create physical danger.
- Do not continue a test once it exceeds approved scope.

**Rules of engagement, test plans, and approval artifacts**

A strong security testing program uses written artifacts. These documents make the test repeatable, auditable, and safe.

A sample rules of engagement should include:

- Scope and in-scope assets
- Out-of-scope systems and prohibited actions
- Test window and time zone
- Authorized testers and contacts
- Escalation path for outages or critical findings
- Data handling and retention rules
- Allowed tools and attack intensity
- Cleanup requirements
- Notification requirements for operations, SOC, and help desk
- Stop conditions

Example excerpt:

```text
Rules of Engagement
- Scope: web app, API gateway, and staging database only
- Prohibited: denial-of-service, social media impersonation, and exfiltration of customer PII
- Allowed: authenticated testing, limited privilege escalation attempts, and proof-of-concept exploitation
- Stop condition: any sign of production impact, unexpected data exposure, or service degradation
- Evidence: screenshots, timestamps, logs, and packet captures stored in encrypted repository
```

A test plan usually adds:

- Objectives and success criteria
- Threat model or attack hypotheses
- Test cases and methods
- Tooling and versions
- Schedule and dependencies
- Evidence collection plan
- Reporting format and severity model

An approval matrix helps show who signs off on what:

- **Business owner**: scope and risk acceptance
- **Security leadership**: methodology and safety
- **Legal/privacy**: consent and data restrictions
- **Operations**: maintenance window and rollback readiness
- **Auditor**: later verifies that approvals existed and were followed

**Evidence handling, chain of custody, and auditability**

Evidence must be trustworthy if it will support remediation, legal review, or audit. Chain of custody documents who collected evidence, when, where, how, and who accessed it afterward.

Evidence types include:

- Screenshots of successful proof-of-concept exploitation
- Logs from target systems and security tools
- Packet captures
- Command output
- Hashes of files or payloads
- Test notes and timestamps
- Copies of relevant configurations
- Ticket references and remediation records

Good handling practices:

- Store evidence in an access-controlled repository.
- Encrypt evidence at rest and in transit.
- Record timestamps in a consistent time zone, preferably UTC.
- Hash files to detect tampering.
- Limit access to need-to-know personnel.
- Preserve original evidence and work from copies when possible.
- Document every transfer or review of evidence.

Example chain-of-custody record:

```text
Evidence ID: PT-2026-014-SCR-03
Collected by: A. Tester
Collected at: 2026-04-26T14:22:10Z
Source: web01.example.com
Type: screenshot
Hash: SHA-256 9f2c...a81d
Stored in: encrypted vault / pentest/2026-04
Access granted to: test lead, security manager, auditor
```

**Metrics, reporting, and continuous improvement**

Testing governance is not complete until results are measured and acted on. Metrics help leadership understand risk trends and remediation performance.

Useful metrics include:

- **KPI**: Key Performance Indicator, such as percentage of critical findings remediated on time.
- **KRI**: Key Risk Indicator, such as number of internet-facing critical exposures.
- Mean time to remediate by severity
- Reopen rate for fixed findings
- Recurrence rate of the same weakness
- Coverage rate by asset class or business unit
- Trend of high-risk findings over time
- Phishing click rate or report rate, if ethically approved

Reporting should answer:

- What was tested?
- What was found?
- What is the business impact?
- What is the likelihood and severity?
- What is the remediation owner and due date?
- What residual risk remains?

A good report separates executive summary from technical detail:

- **Executive summary**: business impact, top risks, and decisions needed
- **Technical findings**: evidence, reproduction steps, and affected assets
- **Remediation plan**: fixes, owners, and deadlines
- **Appendix**: logs, screenshots, and methodology

Feed results into risk management by updating risk registers, control assessments, and remediation tracking. Trend reporting helps show whether the organization is improving or repeatedly failing in the same areas.

**Legal, privacy, and regulatory considerations**

Testing must respect consent, jurisdiction, and data handling rules. A test that is technically successful can still be unacceptable if it violates law or policy.

Consider:

- Written authorization before testing begins
- Jurisdictional limits if systems or users span multiple countries
- Privacy laws that restrict collection of personal data
- Sector regulations that require notice, retention, or reporting
- Contractual limits with customers, partners, or cloud providers
- Rules for handling regulated data such as health, payment, or student records

Practical safeguards:

- Minimize collection of personal data.
- Mask or redact sensitive data in reports.
- Use test accounts and synthetic data where possible.
- Avoid storing secrets in tickets or chat logs.
- Confirm whether third-party systems may be tested at all.

> If a test may affect regulated data, get legal and compliance review before execution, not after.

**Independent validation and team exercises**

Independent validation increases confidence because it reduces bias and confirms that controls work under different assumptions.

Common forms:

- **Third-party assessment**: External assessor evaluates controls or performs testing.
- **Red team**: Simulates a real adversary with stealth and objective-driven tactics.
- **Blue team**: Defends, detects, and responds.
- **Purple team**: Collaborative exercise where red and blue teams share findings to improve detection and response.

How they differ from standard penetration tests:

- A penetration test usually focuses on finding and proving vulnerabilities within a defined scope.
- A red team exercise focuses on achieving a goal, such as access to sensitive data, while evading detection.
- A purple team exercise emphasizes learning and control improvement rather than stealth.
- A third-party assessment adds independence and credibility for governance or compliance.

Use independent validation when you need external assurance, regulatory confidence, or a fresh perspective on recurring weaknesses.

**Practical governance example: a simple testing workflow**

1. Define the business objective and risk question.
2. Identify scope, exclusions, and data sensitivity.
3. Obtain approvals from the system owner, security leadership, and legal or privacy as needed.
4. Write the rules of engagement and test plan.
5. Coordinate with operations, SOC, and incident response.
6. Execute the test using approved methods.
7. Collect and protect evidence with chain of custody.
8. Report findings with severity, impact, and remediation guidance.
9. Track remediation to closure.
10. Retest and update risk records.

Example command set for a controlled validation workflow:

```bash
# Discover exposed services
nmap -sV -Pn 192.0.2.10

# Validate TLS configuration
testssl.sh https://app.example.com

# Check HTTP headers
curl -I https://app.example.com

# Review local logs after a controlled test
journalctl -u nginx --since "2026-04-26 14:00" --until "2026-04-26 15:00"
```

**Exam focus and common distinctions**

For CISSP, remember these contrasts:

- **Testing vs monitoring**: testing is periodic and planned; monitoring is continuous and operational.
- **Testing vs incident response**: testing seeks assurance; incident response seeks containment and recovery.
- **Scan vs penetration test**: scans identify possible weaknesses; penetration tests attempt controlled exploitation.
- **Internal vs external perspective**: black-box, gray-box, and white-box describe tester knowledge.
- **Technical vs social testing**: phishing and physical tests require extra approvals and privacy care.
- **Assessment vs audit**: assessment improves security; audit verifies compliance and evidence.

If you can explain who authorizes testing, who performs it, how evidence is protected, and how results drive remediation, you are covering the governance core of this topic.
