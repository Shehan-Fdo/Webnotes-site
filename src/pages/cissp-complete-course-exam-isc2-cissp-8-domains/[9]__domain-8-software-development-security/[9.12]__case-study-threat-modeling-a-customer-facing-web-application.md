## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**Threat modeling purpose and scope**

Threat modeling is a structured way to identify what can go wrong, how an attacker might do it, and what controls reduce risk before you build or release a system. For a customer-facing web application, the goal is not only to find technical flaws, but also to prioritize risks based on business impact, data sensitivity, regulatory exposure, and operational dependencies.

A good model answers four questions:

- What are you protecting?
- Who might attack it and why?
- Where are the trust boundaries?
- What controls and residual risks remain?

For CISSP purposes, threat modeling sits at the intersection of **security architecture**, **risk management**, **software development security**, and **security assessment and testing**. It is most effective when done early, repeated often, and tied to change management and release decisions.

**Common methodologies and when to use them**

Several methodologies help structure the analysis. You do not need to use only one; many teams combine them.

- **STRIDE** focuses on threat categories:
  - Spoofing
  - Tampering
  - Repudiation
  - Information disclosure
  - Denial of service
  - Elevation of privilege
- **PASTA** (Process for Attack Simulation and Threat Analysis) is risk-centric and maps business objectives to attack scenarios.
- **DREAD** is a scoring approach for prioritizing threats, though many organizations now prefer more modern risk scoring because DREAD can be subjective.
- **Attack trees** break attacker goals into subgoals and paths, which is useful for visualizing multiple ways to compromise a target.
- **Cyber Kill Chain** helps you think about attacker phases from reconnaissance through actions on objectives.
- **MITRE ATT&CK** is useful for mapping observed or plausible tactics and techniques to real-world adversary behavior.

> Use STRIDE to enumerate threats, PASTA to connect threats to business risk, attack trees to explore paths, and the kill chain to understand attacker progression.

**Case study scenario: customer-facing web application**

Assume a retail company exposes a web application that lets customers browse products, create accounts, log in, save payment methods, place orders, and view order history. The app includes:

- A public web front end
- An API layer
- An authentication service
- A database for customer profiles and orders
- A payment processor integration
- A content delivery network (CDN)
- A CI/CD pipeline that builds and deploys the application
- Third-party JavaScript libraries and backend packages

The app processes **personally identifiable information** (PII), such as names, email addresses, shipping addresses, and possibly phone numbers. If it stores or transmits payment card data, **PCI DSS** scope becomes a major concern. If it serves customers in regulated regions, **GDPR** and similar privacy laws affect data minimization, retention, breach handling, and lawful processing.

**Step-by-step threat modeling workflow**

Use a repeatable process so the output is actionable and auditable.

1. **Define scope and business objectives**
   - Identify the application boundaries, major features, and business goals.
   - Clarify what data is in scope, what is out of scope, and what assumptions you are making.
   - Determine whether the app handles PII, payment data, health data, or other regulated information.

2. **Identify assets and data flows**
   - List critical assets such as customer accounts, session tokens, order records, API keys, and build credentials.
   - Map how data moves between browser, CDN, web tier, API, identity provider, database, and third parties.
   - Note where data is stored, encrypted, cached, logged, or exported.

3. **Draw trust boundaries**
   - Mark transitions between untrusted and trusted zones.
   - Typical boundaries include:
     - Internet to CDN
     - CDN to web application
     - Web application to internal API
     - Application to database
     - Application to payment processor
     - Developer workstation to CI/CD pipeline
     - CI/CD pipeline to production environment
   - Each boundary is a place where authentication, authorization, validation, and logging matter.

4. **Enumerate threats**
   - Apply STRIDE to each component and data flow.
   - Add attack trees for high-value assets such as account takeover or payment fraud.
   - Consider supply chain, privacy, and availability threats, not just injection and authentication issues.

5. **Assess risk**
   - Rate likelihood and impact using your organization’s risk method.
   - Increase priority for threats involving regulated data, public exposure, or high business impact.
   - Consider compensating controls and existing safeguards.

6. **Select controls and treatments**
   - Prevent, detect, respond, or transfer the risk where appropriate.
   - Prefer layered controls such as MFA, input validation, WAF, secrets management, and monitoring.
   - Document residual risk if you cannot fully eliminate the threat.

7. **Validate and integrate**
   - Review the model during design reviews.
   - Revisit it when requirements change, new dependencies are added, or major releases are planned.
   - Use it as a release gate for high-risk changes.

**Example architecture and trust boundaries**

A simple text view helps you reason about the system:

```text
Customer Browser
   |
   |  Internet (untrusted)
   v
CDN / WAF
   |
   |  TLS termination or pass-through
   v
Web Front End
   |
   |  Internal API calls
   v
API Gateway / Application Services
   |
   |  AuthN/AuthZ, business logic
   +----> Identity Provider
   |
   +----> Payment Processor (third party)
   |
   +----> Database
   |
   +----> Logging / SIEM
```

Key trust boundaries in this design:

- The browser is fully untrusted.
- The CDN/WAF is a control point, not a trust anchor by itself.
- The application tier must not trust client-supplied data.
- The database should only accept connections from approved application identities.
- Third-party services introduce external dependency and availability risk.
- The CI/CD pipeline is a privileged path into production and must be protected like a production asset.

**Threats by component**

Use component-level analysis to avoid missing obvious attack paths.

- **Browser/client**
  - Cross-site scripting (XSS) can steal sessions or alter transactions.
  - Cross-site request forgery (CSRF) can trigger unwanted actions if anti-CSRF controls are weak.
  - Session theft can occur through insecure storage, weak cookies, or malware on the endpoint.
- **CDN/WAF**
  - Misconfiguration can expose origin servers directly.
  - Bypass rules may allow malicious traffic to reach the app.
  - Cache poisoning can serve attacker-controlled content to users.
- **Authentication service**
  - Credential stuffing can lead to account takeover.
  - Weak password reset flows can be abused for impersonation.
  - Session fixation or token replay can bypass intended controls.
- **Application/API layer**
  - Injection flaws can expose or alter data.
  - Broken access control can let users access other users’ orders or profiles.
  - Insecure deserialization or unsafe object binding can lead to remote code execution or privilege escalation.
- **Database**
  - Excessive privileges can magnify the impact of a compromise.
  - Unencrypted backups can expose PII.
  - Poor segregation of duties can allow unauthorized data extraction.
- **Payment integration**
  - Tampering with payment parameters can alter amounts or recipients.
  - Webhook spoofing can fake payment success.
  - Dependency on the payment provider creates availability and integrity concerns.
- **Logging and monitoring**
  - Sensitive data in logs can create privacy and compliance issues.
  - Log tampering can hide attacker activity.
  - Missing correlation IDs can slow incident response.
- **CI/CD pipeline**
  - Compromised build agents can inject malicious code.
  - Stolen signing keys can produce trusted but malicious releases.
  - Poisoned dependencies can enter the build through package registries.

**Threats using STRIDE**

STRIDE is a practical way to ensure coverage across threat categories.

- **Spoofing**
  - Fake customer login attempts using stolen credentials.
  - Forged service-to-service requests if mutual authentication is absent.
  - Spoofed webhook calls from a payment provider if signatures are not verified.
- **Tampering**
  - Manipulated request parameters to change order totals or shipping addresses.
  - Modified JavaScript bundles if integrity controls are missing.
  - Altered build artifacts in the pipeline.
- **Repudiation**
  - Users deny placing an order if logs are incomplete.
  - Administrators deny changing access if privileged actions are not audited.
  - Payment disputes become harder to resolve without immutable records.
- **Information disclosure**
  - Sensitive data leaks through verbose error messages, logs, or misconfigured storage.
  - IDOR (Insecure Direct Object Reference) exposes another customer’s records.
  - Secrets leak from environment variables, source code, or CI logs.
- **Denial of service**
  - Login endpoints are flooded with credential stuffing or bot traffic.
  - Expensive search or report queries exhaust resources.
  - Third-party dependency outages degrade checkout availability.
- **Elevation of privilege**
  - A low-privilege user exploits broken authorization to gain admin access.
  - A compromised CI runner gains deployment rights.
  - A vulnerable library leads to server-side code execution.

**Other threat lenses beyond STRIDE**

For CISSP completeness, use additional methods when the system is high value or complex.

- **PASTA**
  - Start with business objectives and risk appetite.
  - Model attacker profiles and attack scenarios.
  - Quantify business impact where possible.
- **DREAD**
  - Score damage, reproducibility, exploitability, affected users, and discoverability.
  - Use cautiously because scores can vary by assessor.
- **Attack trees**
  - Example goal: steal customer payment data.
  - Branches may include phishing admin users, exploiting SQL injection, compromising backups, or abusing logs.
- **Cyber Kill Chain**
  - Reconnaissance: identify exposed login and API endpoints.
  - Weaponization: prepare phishing pages or exploit payloads.
  - Delivery: send malicious links or automated login attempts.
  - Exploitation: exploit a vulnerability or stolen credential.
  - Installation: establish persistence in the app or pipeline.
  - Command and control: maintain access.
  - Actions on objectives: exfiltrate data or commit fraud.

**Data classification and privacy considerations**

Threat prioritization changes when the application handles regulated or sensitive data. A customer-facing web app often processes multiple data classes at once.

- **PII**
  - Names, emails, addresses, phone numbers, and account identifiers require protection against unauthorized disclosure.
  - Minimize collection and retention to reduce exposure.
- **PCI DSS**
  - Payment card data requires strict segmentation, logging, access control, and secure handling.
  - If possible, use tokenization or a hosted payment page to reduce scope.
- **GDPR**
  - Personal data processing must follow principles such as data minimization, purpose limitation, and storage limitation.
  - Support rights such as access, deletion, and correction where applicable.
  - Consider lawful basis, breach notification timelines, and cross-border transfer issues.
- **Other privacy obligations**
  - Local breach notification laws may require rapid reporting.
  - Contractual obligations with enterprise customers may impose retention or encryption requirements.

These obligations affect prioritization. For example, a low-likelihood leak of customer addresses may still rank high because it creates legal, reputational, and notification costs.

**Supply chain and dependency risks**

Modern web applications depend on many external components, and attackers often target the weakest link.

- **Third-party libraries**
  - Vulnerable open-source packages can introduce remote code execution, XSS, or data leakage.
  - Typosquatting and dependency confusion can trick build systems into pulling malicious packages.
- **CI/CD pipeline compromise**
  - Attackers may target source control, build servers, artifact repositories, or deployment credentials.
  - A compromised pipeline can distribute malicious code to every customer.
- **Software update integrity**
  - Verify package signatures and checksums where supported.
  - Protect artifact repositories and enforce provenance controls.
  - Use code signing for releases and validate signatures before deployment.
- **Secrets exposure**
  - API keys, signing keys, and database credentials must not live in source code or build logs.
  - Rotate secrets if exposure is suspected.
- **Vendor and SaaS risk**
  - Outages or breaches at identity providers, analytics platforms, or payment processors can affect availability and confidentiality.
  - Review vendor security posture and contractual commitments.

Practical controls include dependency scanning, software composition analysis (SCA), pinned versions, lockfiles, artifact signing, and least-privilege service accounts.

**Secure SDLC integration points**

Threat modeling is most useful when embedded into the software development life cycle (SDLC), not treated as a one-time exercise.

- **Requirements phase**
  - Identify security, privacy, and compliance requirements early.
  - Define data classification and retention rules.
- **Design reviews**
  - Review architecture diagrams, trust boundaries, and data flows before implementation.
  - Require threat modeling for new features that handle sensitive data or new integrations.
- **Implementation**
  - Translate threats into secure coding requirements and test cases.
  - Use code review checklists for authentication, authorization, input validation, and logging.
- **Change management**
  - Reassess threats when dependencies, APIs, or infrastructure change.
  - Treat major auth, payment, or data-flow changes as security-impacting changes.
- **Release gates**
  - Block release if critical threats lack mitigation or approved risk acceptance.
  - Verify that security tests, SAST, DAST, and dependency scans meet policy.
- **Operations**
  - Feed incidents, alerts, and penetration test findings back into the model.
  - Update the threat register when new attack patterns emerge.

**Worked example: building a threat model end to end**

Consider an e-commerce checkout flow.

1. **Define the asset**
   - The primary asset is customer order data, including PII and payment-related information.
   - Secondary assets include session tokens, pricing rules, and inventory data.

2. **Map the flow**
   - Customer logs in.
   - Customer adds items to cart.
   - Customer submits shipping and payment details.
   - Application sends payment request to processor.
   - Application stores order and confirmation.

3. **Identify trust boundaries**
   - Browser to CDN/WAF
   - CDN/WAF to web app
   - Web app to auth service
   - Web app to payment processor
   - Web app to database
   - Developer tools to CI/CD pipeline

4. **Enumerate threats per component**
   - Browser: XSS steals session cookies.
   - Web app: broken access control exposes another user’s order history.
   - Payment integration: webhook spoofing marks unpaid orders as paid.
   - Database: SQL injection exposes customer records.
   - CI/CD: malicious dependency injects backdoor code into checkout logic.

5. **Prioritize**
   - High priority: account takeover, payment fraud, PII exposure, and pipeline compromise.
   - Medium priority: cache poisoning, log leakage, and denial of service.
   - Lower priority: cosmetic tampering with non-sensitive content.

6. **Select controls**
   - Enforce MFA for admins and risk-based step-up authentication for customers.
   - Use parameterized queries and server-side authorization checks.
   - Verify webhook signatures and use idempotency keys for payment calls.
   - Sign builds and restrict deployment permissions.
   - Apply CSP (Content Security Policy), secure cookies, and anti-CSRF tokens.

7. **Document residual risk**
   - If a third-party payment provider outage can still stop checkout, record the dependency risk and business impact.
   - If some customer data must remain in logs for fraud detection, define retention, masking, and access controls.

**Example threat register**

A threat register makes the model actionable. It should be concise, traceable, and owned.

**Risk treatment plan and residual risk acceptance**

A threat model should lead to decisions, not just observations.

- **Avoid**
  - Remove a risky feature or data collection point.
  - Example: do not store cardholder data if a hosted payment page can handle it.
- **Mitigate**
  - Add controls that reduce likelihood or impact.
  - Example: add MFA, WAF rules, and secure coding fixes.
- **Transfer**
  - Shift some risk through insurance or a vendor contract, though accountability remains.
  - Example: use a managed payment provider with contractual security obligations.
- **Accept**
  - Formally accept residual risk when the cost of further reduction exceeds the benefit.
  - Acceptance should be documented, time-bound, and approved by the right authority.

A strong acceptance record includes:

- The threat description
- The affected asset
- The residual likelihood and impact
- The compensating controls in place
- The business justification
- The approver and review date

**Practical controls and examples**

Use layered controls across the stack.

```nginx
server {
    listen 443 ssl http2;
    server_name shop.example.com;

    add_header Content-Security-Policy "default-src 'self'; object-src 'none'; frame-ancestors 'none'" always;
    add_header X-Content-Type-Options "nosniff" always;
    add_header Referrer-Policy "no-referrer" always;

    location / {
        proxy_pass http://app_backend;
        proxy_set_header Host $host;
        proxy_set_header X-Forwarded-For $proxy_add_x_forwarded_for;
    }
}
```

Useful control themes include:

- **Authentication**
  - MFA for privileged users
  - Strong password policy and secure recovery flows
  - Short-lived tokens and secure session handling
- **Authorization**
  - Server-side object-level checks
  - Least privilege for services and admins
  - Separate duties for development, operations, and security
- **Input and output handling**
  - Validate on the server
  - Encode output to prevent XSS
  - Use allowlists where practical
- **Cryptography**
  - Encrypt data in transit with TLS
  - Encrypt sensitive data at rest
  - Protect keys in a KMS (Key Management Service) or HSM (Hardware Security Module)
- **Monitoring**
  - Centralize logs
  - Alert on anomalous login, payment, and admin activity
  - Preserve evidence for investigations
- **Resilience**
  - Rate limit abuse-prone endpoints
  - Use circuit breakers and graceful degradation for third-party failures
  - Test backups and recovery

**How to document the final output**

The deliverables of threat modeling should be easy to review and maintain.

- **Architecture diagram**
  - Shows components, data flows, and trust boundaries.
- **Threat register**
  - Lists threats, severity, owners, and status.
- **Risk treatment plan**
  - Maps each threat to mitigation, transfer, avoidance, or acceptance.
- **Security requirements**
  - Converts threats into implementable controls and testable criteria.
- **Residual risk acceptance**
  - Records what remains and who approved it.
- **Verification evidence**
  - Includes test results, scan reports, and review notes.

A concise documentation set helps auditors, developers, and managers understand why a control exists and whether it is still needed.

**Exam-focused takeaways**

- Threat modeling is proactive risk analysis, not just vulnerability scanning.
- Trust boundaries are often more important than component names.
- Customer-facing apps require special attention to PII, PCI DSS, and privacy obligations.
- Supply chain and CI/CD compromise can be as dangerous as direct application exploits.
- Threat modeling works best when integrated into design reviews, change management, and release gates.
- The output must be actionable: threats, priorities, treatments, owners, and residual risk.

**Quick review checklist**

- Have you identified all sensitive data types?
- Have you drawn every trust boundary?
- Have you considered STRIDE plus at least one broader methodology?
- Have you included supply chain and pipeline threats?
- Have you tied the model to SDLC checkpoints?
- Have you documented residual risk acceptance?
