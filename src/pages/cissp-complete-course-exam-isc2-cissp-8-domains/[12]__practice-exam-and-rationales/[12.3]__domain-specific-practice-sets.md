## **Course:** CISSP Complete Course (Exam ISC2 CISSP – 8 Domains)

**How to use these practice sets**

These notes are organized by CISSP domain and focus on the kinds of distinctions the exam tests: ownership vs. custody, policy vs. procedure, prevention vs. detection, and governance vs. operations. The goal is not memorization alone; it is to help you choose the best answer when multiple options are technically plausible.

- Treat each domain as a decision-making framework.
- Look for the control objective first, then the implementation detail.
- Prefer answers that align with business risk, least privilege, and defense in depth.

**Domain 1: Security and Risk Management**

This domain is heavily about governance. You need to distinguish policy-level decisions from operational execution, and understand how risk is identified, analyzed, treated, and monitored. CISSP questions often test whether you choose the most appropriate management action rather than the fastest technical fix.

**Core governance concepts**

- **Security governance** aligns security with business goals, legal obligations, and risk appetite.
- **Due care** means taking reasonable steps to protect assets.
- **Due diligence** means actively investigating and verifying that protections are appropriate and effective.
- **Security policy** states management intent; standards are mandatory specifics; procedures are step-by-step instructions; guidelines are recommended but not mandatory.
- **Roles and responsibilities** should be clearly assigned to avoid conflicts and gaps.

**Common roles**

- **Data owner** classifies data and defines protection requirements.
- **Data custodian** implements and maintains the controls that protect data.
- **Data user** follows policy and handles data appropriately.
- **System owner** is accountable for the system’s operation and risk decisions.
- **Security administrator** manages security controls and enforcement.

**Risk analysis**

Risk analysis helps you decide what to protect first and how much to spend. CISSP expects you to know both qualitative and quantitative methods.

- **Qualitative analysis** uses subjective scales such as high, medium, and low.
- **Quantitative analysis** uses numeric values and financial estimates.
- **SLE (Single Loss Expectancy)** = asset value × exposure factor.
- **ARO (Annualized Rate of Occurrence)** = expected frequency per year.
- **ALE (Annualized Loss Expectancy)** = SLE × ARO.

Example:

```text
Asset value = $500,000
Exposure factor = 20%
SLE = $100,000
ARO = 0.25
ALE = $25,000
```

Use quantitative analysis when you need financial justification for controls. Use qualitative analysis when data is incomplete or the business decision depends on relative risk ranking.

**Risk treatment options**

- **Avoid** the activity that creates the risk.
- **Mitigate** by applying controls to reduce likelihood or impact.
- **Transfer** by shifting financial impact, often through insurance or contracts.
- **Accept** when the residual risk is within tolerance.

> Risk is never eliminated completely; you manage it to an acceptable level.

**Legal, regulatory, and privacy concepts**

- **Compliance** means meeting external obligations such as laws, regulations, and contracts.
- **Privacy** focuses on how personally identifiable information (PII) is collected, used, shared, retained, and deleted.
- **Data minimization** means collecting only what you need.
- **Purpose limitation** means using data only for the stated purpose.
- **Retention** and **disposal** rules must match legal and business requirements.
- **Data sovereignty** means data is subject to the laws of the country where it resides or is processed.

**Business continuity governance**

- **Business continuity planning (BCP)** keeps essential business functions operating during disruption.
- **Disaster recovery (DR)** restores IT services after a major outage.
- **Continuity of operations (COOP)** is often used in government contexts, but the broader idea is maintaining essential functions during disruption.
- **Business impact analysis (BIA)** identifies critical processes, dependencies, and acceptable downtime.

**Awareness and training**

Security awareness reduces human error and social engineering success.

- **Awareness** reminds users what threats look like.
- **Training** teaches specific skills and procedures.
- **Education** builds deeper knowledge for roles such as security analysts and administrators.
- Use phishing simulations, role-based training, and policy acknowledgments to reinforce behavior.

**Domain 2: Asset Security**

Asset security focuses on protecting information throughout its lifecycle: creation, storage, use, sharing, archival, and destruction. The exam often asks you to choose the right handling method for the data type and media involved.

**Information classification**

Classification determines how data is labeled, handled, and protected.

- Common criteria include sensitivity, criticality, legal impact, and business value.
- Classification should be based on the impact of unauthorized disclosure, modification, or loss.
- Labels must be consistent with handling rules.

Typical classification examples:

- Public
- Internal
- Confidential
- Restricted

**Data ownership and lifecycle**

- The **data owner** defines classification and protection requirements.
- The **data custodian** enforces storage, backup, and access controls.
- The lifecycle includes creation, use, storage, sharing, archival, and destruction.
- Reclassification may be needed when business value or legal requirements change.

**Data remanence and sanitization**

Data remanence is the residual representation of data that remains after deletion.

- **Clear** uses logical techniques to protect against simple non-invasive recovery.
- **Purge** makes recovery infeasible using state-of-the-art laboratory techniques.
- **Destroy** physically renders media unusable.

Choose sanitization based on media type and sensitivity. For example, a decommissioned SSD may require vendor-approved secure erase or cryptographic erase rather than file shredding.

> `shred` is not a universal solution for whole disks, SSDs, or journaling filesystems. Use media-appropriate sanitization methods.

**Media handling**

- **Magnetic disks** may support overwrite-based sanitization, but modern storage layers can complicate guarantees.
- **Solid-state drives (SSDs)** use wear leveling, so logical overwrites may not reach all cells.
- **Optical media** may require physical destruction or approved destruction methods.
- **Paper records** still need secure storage, transport, and destruction.

**Cryptographic storage considerations**

Encryption protects confidentiality at rest, but key management determines whether the protection is meaningful.

- Store keys separately from encrypted data when possible.
- Protect keys with access control, rotation, backup, and recovery procedures.
- Use strong algorithms and approved modes.
- Protect against key exposure in logs, memory dumps, and configuration files.

Example disk encryption setup:

```bash
# Example: LUKS on Linux
cryptsetup luksFormat /dev/nvme0n1p3
cryptsetup open /dev/nvme0n1p3 securedata
mkfs.ext4 /dev/mapper/securedata
mount /dev/mapper/securedata /mnt/securedata
```

**Privacy and data sovereignty**

- Minimize collection and retention of PII.
- Apply masking, tokenization, or pseudonymization when full values are not needed.
- Know where data is stored, replicated, and backed up.
- Cross-border transfers may trigger legal and contractual obligations.

**Domain 3: Security Architecture and Engineering**

This domain tests how you choose secure design principles, models, and controls. It often includes architecture tradeoffs, hardware trust, physical protections, and cryptographic design.

**Security design principles**

- **Least privilege** gives only the access required.
- **Separation of duties** splits critical tasks among multiple people.
- **Defense in depth** layers controls so one failure does not expose the asset.
- **Fail-safe defaults** deny access unless explicitly allowed.
- **Complete mediation** checks every access attempt.
- **Economy of mechanism** keeps designs simple and easier to verify.
- **Open design** assumes the design can be known; security depends on the key or secret, not obscurity.

**Security models**

These models help you reason about confidentiality and integrity.

- **Bell-LaPadula** focuses on confidentiality.
  - No read up
  - No write down
- **Biba** focuses on integrity.
  - No read down
  - No write up
- **Clark-Wilson** emphasizes well-formed transactions and separation of duties.
- **Brewer-Nash** prevents conflicts of interest by restricting access based on prior access history.

Use these models as exam clues. If a question emphasizes secrecy, think Bell-LaPadula. If it emphasizes integrity of business transactions, think Clark-Wilson.

**Hardware, firmware, and trusted computing**

- **Trusted Platform Module (TPM)** stores cryptographic material and supports measured boot.
- **Secure boot** verifies boot components before execution.
- **UEFI** can enforce boot integrity more flexibly than legacy BIOS.
- **Firmware** attacks can persist below the operating system and are difficult to detect.
- **Hardware root of trust** establishes a trusted starting point for system integrity.

**Physical and environmental security**

Physical controls protect people, equipment, and facilities.

- **Deterrent** controls discourage attacks.
- **Preventive** controls stop or reduce attacks.
- **Detective** controls identify events.
- **Corrective** controls restore normal operation.

Examples:

- Badges, guards, locks, mantraps, CCTV, and alarms
- Fire suppression systems
- HVAC and humidity control
- UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply) and generators
- Raised floors and cable management in data centers

**Cryptography basics**

- **Symmetric encryption** uses one key for encryption and decryption.
- **Asymmetric encryption** uses a public/private key pair.
- **Hashing** provides integrity, not confidentiality.
- **Digital signatures** provide integrity, authenticity, and nonrepudiation.
- **HMAC (Hash-based Message Authentication Code)** combines a hash with a secret key for integrity and authentication.

Authenticated encryption is common in modern systems.

- **GCM (Galois/Counter Mode)** provides confidentiality and integrity.
- Never reuse a nonce/IV with the same key in GCM.
- Reuse can break security catastrophically.

Example TLS configuration concept:

```nginx
ssl_protocols TLSv1.2 TLSv1.3;
ssl_prefer_server_ciphers on;
ssl_ciphers 'TLS_AES_256_GCM_SHA384:TLS_CHACHA20_POLY1305_SHA256';
```

**Domain 4: Communication and Network Security**

This domain covers secure network design, protocol selection, segmentation, and traffic protection. The exam often asks you to identify the right protocol, port, or architecture for a scenario.

**Secure network architecture**

- Segment networks by trust level and business function.
- Use **DMZ (demilitarized zone)** for public-facing services.
- Place sensitive systems behind internal segmentation controls.
- Use firewalls, proxies, and gateways to enforce policy.
- Apply zero trust principles where every request is authenticated and authorized.

**Common protocol and port associations**

| Service | Protocol/Port | Notes |
|---|---:|---|
| DNS | UDP/TCP 53 | TCP used for zone transfers and large responses |
| DHCP | UDP 67/68 | Server/client assignment |
| HTTP | TCP 80 | Unencrypted web traffic |
| HTTPS | TCP 443 | HTTP over TLS |
| SSH | TCP 22 | Secure remote administration |
| SMTP | TCP 25 | Mail transfer |
| IMAP | TCP 143 / 993 | 993 is IMAPS |
| POP3 | TCP 110 / 995 | 995 is POP3S |
| LDAP | TCP/UDP 389 / 636 | 636 is LDAPS |
| RDP | TCP 3389 | Remote desktop |
| NTP | UDP 123 | Time synchronization |
| IKE/ISAKMP | UDP 500 | Key exchange for IPsec |
| IPsec ESP | IP protocol 50 | Encapsulating Security Payload |
| IPsec AH | IP protocol 51 | Authentication Header |

**VPN types**

- **Site-to-site VPN** connects networks.
- **Remote-access VPN** connects individual users to a network.
- **IPsec** is common for network-layer protection.
- **TLS VPN** often protects application or remote-access sessions.
- Choose based on trust boundary, performance, and client support.

**Wireless security**

- Prefer **WPA3** where available.
- Use strong authentication and unique credentials.
- Disable legacy protocols when possible.
- Monitor for rogue access points and evil twins.
- Use 802.1X with RADIUS for enterprise authentication.

**Network monitoring and detection**

- **IDS (Intrusion Detection System)** alerts on suspicious activity.
- **IPS (Intrusion Prevention System)** can block or drop traffic.
- **NIDS** monitors network traffic.
- **HIDS** monitors host activity.
- **NetFlow** and packet capture support traffic analysis and incident response.

**NAT and security**

Network Address Translation changes address mappings between networks.

- NAT conserves public IP addresses.
- NAT can complicate inbound connectivity and attribution.
- NAT is not a security control by itself.
- Do not confuse address translation with access control.

**DNS and DHCP security**

- Protect DNS with zone transfer restrictions, split-horizon design, and DNSSEC where appropriate.
- Secure DHCP with port security, DHCP snooping, and trusted switch ports.
- Monitor for rogue DNS or DHCP servers.

**Domain 5: Identity and Access Management**

IAM is about proving identity, granting the right access, and removing access when it is no longer needed. CISSP questions often test lifecycle management and the difference between authentication, authorization, and accounting.

**Core IAM concepts**

- **Identification** is claiming an identity.
- **Authentication** is proving the claim.
- **Authorization** is determining what the identity can do.
- **Accounting** records what the identity did.

**Authentication factors**

- **Something you know**: password, PIN
- **Something you have**: token, smart card, phone
- **Something you are**: biometric
- **Something you do**: behavioral pattern
- **Somewhere you are**: location
- **Something you inherit** is sometimes used as a broader biometric category

**MFA and biometrics**

- **MFA (Multi-Factor Authentication)** uses two or more different factor types.
- Biometrics should be evaluated by false acceptance rate and false rejection rate.
- Biometrics are not secrets; they are identifiers with error rates.
- Use liveness detection where spoofing is a concern.

**Account lifecycle management**

- Provision accounts based on approved business need.
- Use joiner-mover-leaver processes.
- Disable or remove access promptly when employment or role changes.
- Review privileged accounts more frequently.
- Revoke shared credentials where possible.

**Provisioning and deprovisioning**

- Automate onboarding and offboarding when feasible.
- Tie access to HR events and role changes.
- Remove stale accounts and orphaned entitlements.
- Re-certify access periodically.

Example Linux account management:

```bash
# Create a user and add to a group
useradd -m -s /bin/bash analyst01
passwd analyst01
usermod -aG secops analyst01

# Disable an account
usermod -L analyst01
chage -E 0 analyst01
```

**Authorization models**

- **DAC (Discretionary Access Control)** lets the owner decide access.
- **MAC (Mandatory Access Control)** uses labels and centralized policy.
- **RBAC (Role-Based Access Control)** assigns access by role.
- **ABAC (Attribute-Based Access Control)** uses attributes such as device, location, and time.

Choose the model that matches the business need. RBAC is common for enterprise administration. ABAC is useful when context matters.

**Federation and SSO**

- **SSO (Single Sign-On)** reduces repeated logins.
- **Federation** allows trust across organizations or domains.
- **SAML** is common for enterprise web federation.
- **OpenID Connect (OIDC)** is an authentication layer built on OAuth 2.0.
- **OAuth 2.0** is for delegated authorization, not authentication by itself.

> OAuth 2.0 alone does not authenticate a user. If a question asks how a user logs in, look for OIDC, SAML, or another authentication mechanism.

**Privileged access management**

- Use separate admin accounts.
- Require stronger authentication for privileged actions.
- Log and review privileged sessions.
- Use just-in-time access where possible.
- Rotate secrets and service account credentials.

**Domain 6: Security Assessment and Testing**

This domain focuses on how you verify that controls work and whether they meet policy, compliance, and risk requirements. Auditing, testing, and vulnerability management are related but not identical.

**Assessment and testing types**

- **Vulnerability assessment** identifies weaknesses.
- **Penetration testing** attempts to exploit weaknesses to demonstrate impact.
- **Security testing** validates controls and configurations.
- **Audit** checks compliance against policy, standards, or regulations.
- **Code review** examines source, binaries, or bytecode for defects and vulnerabilities.

**Static and dynamic testing**

- **Static analysis** examines code, binaries, or bytecode without execution.
- **Dynamic analysis** evaluates behavior while the software runs.
- **Fuzzing** sends malformed or unexpected input to find crashes and logic flaws.
- **Regression testing** confirms that fixes did not break existing functionality.

**Testing by phase**

- During development, use code review, static analysis, and unit tests.
- Before release, use integration, system, and acceptance testing.
- In production, use monitoring, vulnerability scanning, and controlled validation.

**Vulnerability management workflow**

1. Discover assets and vulnerabilities.
2. Validate findings and remove false positives.
3. Prioritize by risk, exposure, and business impact.
4. Remediate, mitigate, or accept with approval.
5. Verify fixes and track exceptions.

**Audit evidence and handling**

- Preserve evidence integrity.
- Maintain chain of custody when evidence may be used in legal proceedings.
- Record who collected, handled, transferred, and stored evidence.
- Use hashes to verify that evidence has not changed.

Example hashing workflow:

```bash
sha256sum evidence.img > evidence.img.sha256
sha256sum -c evidence.img.sha256
```

**Metrics and control validation**

- **KPI (Key Performance Indicator)** measures performance against a goal.
- **KRI (Key Risk Indicator)** signals increasing risk.
- **MTTR (Mean Time to Repair/Recover)** measures restoration speed.
- **False positive rate** affects analyst workload and trust in tools.

Use metrics to show whether controls are effective, not just whether they exist.

**Domain 7: Security Operations**

This domain covers day-to-day protection, monitoring, incident response, recovery, and operational resilience. The exam often tests the order of actions and the tradeoff between speed, evidence preservation, and business continuity.

**Logging and monitoring**

- Log authentication events, privilege changes, configuration changes, and security alerts.
- Centralize logs in a SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) platform.
- Protect logs from tampering and unauthorized access.
- Synchronize time with NTP so events can be correlated accurately.

Example syslog forwarding:

```bash
# rsyslog forwarding example
*.* @@loghost.example.com:514
```

**Incident response lifecycle**

A common sequence is:

1. Preparation
2. Detection and analysis
3. Containment
4. Eradication
5. Recovery
6. Lessons learned

Containment may be short-term or long-term. If ransomware is actively spreading, isolating the host can be the right first containment step. If the situation is unclear, preserve evidence and confirm the event before taking disruptive action when possible.

**Forensics and chain of custody**

- Preserve volatile evidence when appropriate.
- Document every action taken on evidence.
- Use write blockers for storage media when imaging.
- Keep originals untouched and analyze copies when possible.
- Maintain chain of custody for admissibility and accountability.

**Backups and restore strategy**

- **Full backup** copies all selected data.
- **Incremental backup** copies changes since the last backup.
- **Differential backup** copies changes since the last full backup.
- Test restores regularly; a backup that cannot be restored is not useful.

**Recovery objectives**

- **RTO (Recovery Time Objective)** is the maximum acceptable downtime.
- **RPO (Recovery Point Objective)** is the maximum acceptable data loss measured in time.
- Lower RTO and RPO usually require more cost and complexity.

**Change management**

- Evaluate impact before making changes.
- Use approvals, testing, rollback plans, and maintenance windows.
- Track emergency changes and review them after implementation.
- Poor change control is a common cause of outages.

**Physical security operations**

- Use visitor logs, escort procedures, and badge management.
- Review camera footage and alarm events.
- Protect media during transport and disposal.
- Apply environmental monitoring for temperature, humidity, smoke, and water leaks.

**Domain 8: Software Development Security**

This domain focuses on building secure software and controlling the development lifecycle. CISSP questions often ask where a control belongs in the SDLC (Software Development Life Cycle) and how to reduce defects before release.

**Secure SDLC**

- Define security requirements early.
- Perform threat modeling during design.
- Use secure coding standards during implementation.
- Test security before release.
- Review dependencies and deployment pipelines.
- Monitor and patch after release.

**Common vulnerability classes**

- **Injection** flaws such as SQL injection and command injection
- **Cross-site scripting (XSS)**, where malicious script runs in a victim’s browser
- **Cross-site request forgery (CSRF)**, where a victim is tricked into sending an unwanted request
- **Broken authentication** and session management
- **Insecure deserialization**
- **Security misconfiguration**
- **Sensitive data exposure**
- **Broken access control**

**Secure coding practices**

- Validate input on the server side.
- Encode output based on context.
- Use parameterized queries.
- Avoid hard-coded secrets.
- Handle errors without leaking sensitive details.
- Use least privilege for application service accounts.

Example parameterized query:

```python
cursor.execute(
    "SELECT id, email FROM users WHERE username = %s",
    (username,)
)
```

**API security**

- Authenticate API clients and users appropriately.
- Use tokens with limited scope and lifetime.
- Enforce rate limiting and input validation.
- Protect secrets in headers and environment variables.
- Log access without exposing tokens or sensitive payloads.

**Containers and cloud-native security**

- Build minimal images.
- Scan images for vulnerabilities.
- Run containers with least privilege.
- Avoid running as root when possible.
- Restrict network access between services.
- Treat orchestration credentials as highly sensitive.

Example container hardening:

```dockerfile
FROM python:3.12-slim
WORKDIR /app
COPY requirements.txt .
RUN pip install --no-cache-dir -r requirements.txt
COPY . .
USER 10001
CMD ["python", "app.py"]
```

**Supply chain security**

- Verify third-party libraries and dependencies.
- Pin versions where appropriate.
- Use code signing and integrity checks.
- Review build pipelines and artifact repositories.
- Protect against dependency confusion and malicious updates.

**Release and rollback governance**

- Use version control and change approval.
- Tag releases and keep rollback artifacts.
- Test rollback procedures before production use.
- Separate development, test, and production environments.

**High-yield exam traps**

- **Authentication vs. authorization**: proving identity is not the same as granting access.
- **Policy vs. standard**: policy states intent; standard is mandatory detail.
- **Audit vs. test**: audit checks compliance; testing validates control behavior.
- **Clear vs. purge**: clear protects against simple recovery; purge aims to defeat state-of-the-art recovery.
- **NAT vs. firewall**: NAT translates addresses; it does not enforce security policy by itself.
- **OAuth vs. OIDC**: OAuth is authorization; OIDC adds authentication.
- **IDS vs. IPS**: IDS alerts; IPS can block.
- **RTO vs. RPO**: RTO is time to restore service; RPO is acceptable data loss window.

**Scenario practice mindset**

When you read a CISSP question, ask:

- What is the primary goal: confidentiality, integrity, availability, or governance?
- Is the question asking for the best first step, the best control, or the best long-term solution?
- Does the answer need to preserve evidence, minimize business disruption, or satisfy compliance?
- Is the control preventive, detective, corrective, or deterrent?

**Quick review checklist**

- Know the major roles, responsibilities, and risk terms.
- Distinguish data handling methods by media and sensitivity.
- Match security models to confidentiality or integrity goals.
- Memorize common ports and protocol purposes.
- Understand IAM lifecycle, federation, and privileged access.
- Separate assessment, audit, and testing concepts.
- Know incident response order, backup types, and recovery metrics.
- Map secure coding and SDLC controls to the right phase.
