## **Course:** Building Wealth Online with Tech: A Complete Guide

**Value-based pricing overview**

- **Value-based pricing** sets price from the customer’s perceived value.
- It focuses on what customers think the offering is worth, not just production cost.
- Harvard Business School describes value as the difference between customer appreciation and what they pay.
- This approach aims to capture more of the value a product creates.

**Why it matters**

- Pricing based on value can improve profit margins.
- It can help you avoid leaving money on the table.
- It puts the customer at the center of pricing decisions.
- It can support stronger customer loyalty when you communicate value clearly.

**How customers judge value**

- Customers compare perceived value against the asking price.
- Value is not only a feature list.
- Value depends on how much benefit the customer expects to receive.
- Perception can vary across markets and customer groups.

**Common challenges**

- Value-based pricing is often misunderstood.
- It can create confusion if you treat it as a simple formula.
- It is harder to use when you cannot measure customer value clearly.
- Surveys and competitor benchmarks may not fully capture real willingness to pay in complex markets.

**Practical implementation**

1. Identify the value your product creates for the customer.
2. Estimate how customers perceive that value.
3. Set a price that reflects that perceived value.
4. Test whether customers are willing to pay that price.
5. Adjust based on observed response and outcomes.

**Useful cautions**

> Value-based pricing is not just a price level; it is a pricing philosophy that depends on understanding customer value.

- Do not rely only on cost-plus thinking.
- Do not assume demographics alone explain willingness to pay.
- Do not ignore how usage and outcomes shape value in software and SaaS contexts.
- Do not treat pricing as separate from product positioning.

**Where it fits best**

- It is common in SaaS and software pricing discussions.
- It works well when you can connect price to measurable customer outcomes.
- It is useful when your product creates clear differentiation.
- It is strongest when you can explain and prove the value you deliver.

**Key takeaway**

- Value-based pricing helps you align price with customer benefit.
- The main task is not choosing a number.
- The main task is understanding and proving value.
