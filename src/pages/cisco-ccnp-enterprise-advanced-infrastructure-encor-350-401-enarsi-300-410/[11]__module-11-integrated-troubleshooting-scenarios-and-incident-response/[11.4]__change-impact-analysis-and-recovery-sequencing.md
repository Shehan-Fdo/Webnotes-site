## **Course:** Cisco CCNP Enterprise Advanced Infrastructure (ENCOR 350-401 & ENARSI 300-410)

**Change-impact analysis overview**

Change-impact analysis is the process of predicting what a proposed change can affect before you implement it. In enterprise networks, you use it to reduce outage risk, define rollback options, and decide whether the change belongs in a normal window, an emergency window, or a freeze period.

A strong analysis answers four questions:

- What depends on the device, link, or service you plan to change?
- What fails if the change goes wrong?
- How will you detect the failure quickly?
- How will you restore service in the correct order?

This topic is highly relevant to CCNP-style troubleshooting because many failures are not caused by the change itself, but by an incomplete understanding of dependency chains.

**Core goals of impact analysis**

You evaluate both the direct and indirect effects of a change. Direct effects are the intended configuration or software modifications. Indirect effects are the downstream consequences on routing, first-hop redundancy, security policy, application reachability, and management access.

Typical goals include:

- Protecting **availability** by identifying critical dependencies before implementation.
- Limiting **blast radius** by scoping the change to a site, VLAN, VRF, or device group.
- Preserving **management access** so you can recover the device if the data plane fails.
- Defining **rollback** steps that are fast enough to use during the maintenance window.
- Establishing validation checks that prove the service is actually restored, not just that the config was accepted.

**Inputs you should gather before a change**

You need enough information to understand the current state and the expected state after the change. In practice, that means collecting topology, configuration, operational, and business data.

- **Topology data**
  - Upstream and downstream neighbors
  - Redundant links, EtherChannels, and alternate paths
  - HSRP/VRRP/GLBP roles and tracked objects
  - Routing adjacencies and redistribution points
- **Configuration data**
  - Interface addressing and VLAN membership
  - ACLs, route maps, prefix lists, and policy-based routing
  - OSPF, EIGRP, BGP, and static routes
  - NAT, QoS, and security features
- **Operational data**
  - Interface counters and error rates
  - CPU, memory, and temperature
  - Syslog history and recent flaps
  - SNMP, NetFlow, IP SLA, and telemetry baselines
- **Business data**
  - Critical applications and their dependency order
  - Maintenance window length
  - Change freeze periods and approval requirements
  - Stakeholder contact list and escalation path

**Change window, freeze, and approval workflow**

Formal change-impact analysis is not complete until you account for governance. A technically safe change can still be rejected if it violates a freeze period or lacks approval.

Use a structured workflow:

1. Identify whether the change is normal, standard, emergency, or prohibited during a freeze.
2. Confirm the maintenance window and expected service impact.
3. Obtain approval from the change advisory process or the designated authority.
4. Notify stakeholders who depend on the affected service.
5. Record the implementation and rollback plan before the window opens.

A change freeze usually applies during peak business periods, major events, or incident recovery. If you must proceed during a freeze, document the business justification and the risk acceptance decision.

**Stakeholder communication**

Communication is part of the technical plan because it reduces confusion during partial outages and recovery. You should tell stakeholders what may fail, what will remain available, and how long validation may take.

Include:

- The maintenance start and end time
- The expected user impact
- The services at risk
- The rollback trigger
- The escalation contact
- The validation criteria for declaring success

For example, if you are changing a WAN edge route policy, application owners need to know that reachability may shift between paths and that transient loss of sessions may occur.

**Dependency mapping and blast radius**

Dependency mapping identifies what must work for the target service to function. This is the foundation of recovery sequencing.

Common dependency chains include:

- User access depends on access switch ports, VLANs, STP, gateway redundancy, routing, and DNS.
- A branch site depends on WAN transport, edge routing, NAT, and upstream reachability.
- A data center application depends on load balancers, front-end services, application servers, databases, and storage.
- Management access depends on OOB, console, SSH, AAA, routing to the management subnet, and ACLs.

A useful rule is to trace the service from the user outward and from the device inward. That helps you identify both the symptom and the root dependency.

**Monitoring and telemetry during impact analysis**

You should use monitoring sources before, during, and after the change to confirm the blast radius and validate restoration. These sources help you distinguish a real outage from a transient convergence event.

Useful telemetry includes:

- **SNMP** for interface status, CPU, memory, and environmental alarms
- **NetFlow** for traffic shifts and unexpected drops in flow volume
- **Syslog** for link flaps, adjacency resets, and security events
- **IP SLA** for reachability and latency checks across critical paths
- **Streaming telemetry** for near-real-time state and counters
- **NMS dashboards** for service health, alarms, and trend baselines

Example validation commands on Cisco IOS/IOS XE:

```bash
show logging
show interfaces status
show interfaces counters errors
show ip route
show ip ospf neighbor
show ip bgp summary
show standby brief
show etherchannel summary
show access-lists
show policy-map interface
```

These commands do not replace telemetry, but they help you confirm whether the device state matches the monitoring view.

**Recovery sequencing principles**

Recovery sequencing is the order in which you restore components after a failed change or outage. The correct order depends on dependencies, not on a fixed rule like “front end before back end” or “core before access” in every case.

Use these principles:

- Restore the **foundation first**: power, physical links, management access, and underlay reachability.
- Restore **control-plane dependencies** next: routing adjacencies, FHRP, STP, and AAA if they block access.
- Restore **service dependencies** in the order required by the application or traffic flow.
- Validate each layer before moving to the next one.
- Avoid restoring a dependent component before its upstream dependency is stable.

A practical decision rule is: if component B cannot function until component A is healthy, restore A first.

**Prioritization matrix for simultaneous failures**

When multiple dependencies fail at once, use a simple prioritization matrix to decide what to restore first.

| Priority | Restore first when... | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | It restores management access or prevents further damage | Console access, OOB management, upstream routing, power |
| 2 | It restores the control plane or gateway function | HSRP/VRRP, routing neighbors, STP root, ACLs blocking admin access |
| 3 | It restores shared transport for many services | WAN edge, core links, EtherChannel, trunking |
| 4 | It restores application-specific dependencies | Load balancer, app server, database, DNS, NAT policy |
| 5 | It restores optimization or noncritical features | QoS tuning, secondary paths, telemetry refinements |

This matrix is useful in exam scenarios because the best answer is often the one that restores the largest number of dependent services with the least risk.

**Management plane access loss**

Loss of management access is a critical recovery scenario because it can prevent you from fixing the original problem. Always plan for a way back in.

Protect management access by using:

- Console access or remote console server
- Out-of-band management network
- Separate management VRF or dedicated management interface
- Local fallback credentials if AAA fails
- Pre-staged rollback commands or scripts

If a change may affect SSH, AAA, or the management route, validate access before you commit the change. If you lose access, recover the management path before attempting deeper service restoration.

**Configuration backup and restore methods**

You should always have a configuration recovery method before making changes. The exact method depends on platform and software version, but the concepts are consistent.

Common approaches include:

- Saving the running configuration to startup configuration
- Copying configs to a TFTP, FTP, SCP, or SFTP server
- Using archive and rollback features where supported
- Using configuration replace to restore a known-good state
- Reverting to a previous image or startup config if the platform supports it

Useful commands:

```bash
copy running-config startup-config
copy startup-config tftp:
copy running-config scp:
show archive
configure replace flash:backup-config force
rollback configuration last 1
```

> Be careful with rollback features: they can revert more than the single line you intended, so verify the scope before you apply them.

A practical backup workflow is:

1. Save the current config.
2. Export a copy off-box.
3. Record the exact change set.
4. Confirm the rollback method works on the platform.
5. Verify that the backup includes any required secrets or that you can re-enter them safely.

**End-to-end change plan example**

Consider a branch router where you must change the WAN route policy and adjust HSRP tracking on the LAN gateway. The goal is to prefer the primary WAN link while preserving failover.

Pre-checks:

```bash
show ip route
show ip ospf neighbor
show ip bgp summary
show standby brief
show interfaces status
show logging | last 20
```

Implementation plan:

```bash
conf t
route-map WAN-PRIMARY permit 10
 set local-preference 200
!
interface GigabitEthernet1/0/0
 standby 10 priority 110
 standby 10 preempt
 standby 10 track GigabitEthernet1/0/2 20
end
write memory
```

Validation:

```bash
show standby brief
show ip route
show ip bgp
show interfaces GigabitEthernet1/0/2
show logging
```

Rollback plan:

```bash
conf t
no route-map WAN-PRIMARY
interface GigabitEthernet1/0/0
 no standby 10 track GigabitEthernet1/0/2 20
 no standby 10 preempt
 standby 10 priority 100
end
write memory
```

This example shows the full lifecycle: pre-check, change, validation, and rollback. In a real deployment, you would also confirm that the tracked object is a meaningful dependency, such as a different uplink or a monitored object whose failure should reduce gateway priority.

**Recovery sequencing decision framework**

Use a repeatable framework when you must restore service after a failed change.

1. Identify the symptom.
   - Determine whether the issue is loss of management, loss of routing, loss of gateway redundancy, ACL blocking, or application failure.
2. Find the dependency chain.
   - Ask what must be healthy before the next layer can work.
3. Restore the highest-priority dependency first.
   - Start with access, transport, or control-plane elements.
4. Validate at each layer.
   - Confirm adjacency, route installation, gateway state, and traffic flow.
5. Move to the next dependent service.
   - Do not skip ahead until the upstream layer is stable.
6. Document the final state.
   - Record what changed, what was restored, and what remains at risk.

A simple prioritization matrix can help:

- If you cannot reach the device, restore management access first.
- If the device is reachable but traffic is blackholed, restore routing or gateway function first.
- If routing is stable but a service is down, restore the application dependency next.
- If the service is restored but performance is poor, tune QoS or path selection last.

**Targeted verification commands for recovery**

During recovery, use commands that match the suspected failure domain.

- **Routing**
  - `show ip route`
  - `show ip protocols`
  - `show ip ospf neighbor`
  - `show ip bgp summary`
  - `show ip bgp`
- **First-hop redundancy**
  - `show standby brief`
  - `show vrrp brief`
  - `show glbp brief`
- **Layer 2 and STP**
  - `show spanning-tree`
  - `show spanning-tree vlan <vlan-id>`
  - `show etherchannel summary`
  - `show interfaces trunk`
- **Security and ACLs**
  - `show access-lists`
  - `show ip access-lists`
  - `show platform hardware qfp active feature acl statistics` 
- **Interface health**
  - `show interfaces`
  - `show interfaces counters errors`
  - `show interfaces description`
- **Policy and forwarding**
  - `show route-map`
  - `show policy-map interface`
  - `show ip cef`
  - `show ip nat translations`

If a command is platform-specific, use the equivalent supported on your device. For exam purposes, focus on the intent of the verification rather than memorizing one exact syntax for every platform.

**ACL and policy change impact**

ACLs and route maps are common sources of unintended outages because they can block traffic silently. When you change them, validate both the match logic and the hit counts.

Example ACL review:

```bash
show ip access-lists
show access-lists
```

What to check:

- Sequence order
- Implicit deny behavior
- Source and destination direction
- Port matches
- Existing hit counts
- Whether the ACL is applied inbound or outbound

For validation, use a real client test from a workstation or a network test host rather than relying only on device-side assumptions. For example, use `curl`, `nc`, or an application-specific test to confirm TCP/443 reachability from the user path.

**FHRP and gateway recovery**

First-hop redundancy protocol changes can affect default gateway availability. If you alter HSRP, VRRP, or GLBP, verify the active and standby roles, tracked objects, and preemption behavior.

Example HSRP verification:

```bash
show standby brief
show standby
```

Important considerations:

- Track a meaningful dependency, such as an uplink or upstream next hop, not the same interface used only as the local gateway interface.
- Confirm that priority changes cause the intended failover.
- Verify that preemption does not create unnecessary flapping.
- Check that the virtual IP remains reachable during the transition.

**Routing and next-hop recovery**

Routing changes often fail because the next hop or neighbor reachability is not stable. BGP does not depend on OSPF adjacency directly, but it does depend on reachability to the BGP neighbor or next hop when those addresses are learned through an IGP or static route.

Useful checks:

```bash
show ip route
show ip ospf neighbor
show ip bgp summary
show ip bgp
show ip cef <prefix>
```

If you use loopbacks for peering, verify that the IGP carries the loopback route before expecting BGP to establish or prefer the correct path.

**Data center and application recovery sequencing**

Application recovery follows dependency order, not a fixed front-end-first or database-first rule. Restore the component that the next layer needs.

Typical patterns:

- If the application server cannot start without the database, restore the database first.
- If the load balancer health checks fail because the app tier is down, restore the app tier before the front end can pass traffic.
- If DNS is broken, many services appear down even when the servers are healthy.
- If storage is unavailable, database and application recovery may both fail.

A practical sequence is:

1. Restore shared infrastructure such as power, storage, and network reachability.
2. Restore identity, DNS, and database services if they are upstream dependencies.
3. Restore application servers and middleware.
4. Restore load balancers, reverse proxies, or front-end services.
5. Validate user transactions end to end.

**Documentation and post-change review**

After recovery, document what happened while the details are fresh. This improves future change-impact analysis and helps you refine the recovery order.

Record:

- The original change request
- The observed failure mode
- The dependency that caused the outage
- The commands used to verify the issue
- The rollback or recovery action taken
- The final validation results
- Any follow-up actions, such as monitoring or config cleanup

A post-change review should answer whether the impact analysis was accurate, whether the rollback was sufficient, and whether the recovery order matched the actual dependency chain.

**Exam-focused takeaways**

- Change-impact analysis is about predicting blast radius before implementation.
- Recovery sequencing is driven by dependency order, not by a fixed generic rule.
- Protect management access first when a change can isolate the device.
- Use telemetry and targeted show commands to confirm both failure and restoration.
- Keep a tested backup and rollback method ready before the maintenance window.
- Communicate the change, the freeze status, and the expected impact to stakeholders.
- Validate each layer before moving to the next one in the recovery chain.
