## **Course:** Cisco CCNP Enterprise Advanced Infrastructure (ENCOR 350-401 & ENARSI 300-410)

**Cisco SD-WAN control-plane overview**

Cisco SD-WAN separates the **control plane** from the **data plane** so you can scale routing, policy, and transport selection independently. The main building blocks are:

- **vBond** orchestrates onboarding and helps controllers and edges discover each other.
- **vSmart** distributes policy and OMP information.
- **vManage** provides centralized management, monitoring, and configuration.
- **WAN Edge** devices build data-plane tunnels and exchange routes through OMP.

A useful mental model is:

```text
WAN Edge ----control connections----> vBond / vSmart / vManage
WAN Edge ----data-plane tunnels-----> WAN Edge
```

The exam often tests whether you can distinguish:

- **Control connections**: secure sessions to controllers for onboarding and policy exchange.
- **OMP peering**: the route-distribution protocol between WAN Edges and vSmart.
- **Transport tunnels**: encrypted or cleartext data-plane tunnels between WAN Edges over the underlay.

**OMP fundamentals**

**Overlay Management Protocol (OMP)** is the routing and policy distribution protocol in Cisco SD-WAN. It carries overlay reachability and attributes so WAN Edges can learn how to reach prefixes and transport endpoints without running a full mesh of traditional routing protocols across the WAN.

OMP primarily advertises three route types:

- **Service routes**: prefixes from service VPNs, such as user LAN subnets.
- **TLOC routes**: transport locator information used to reach a WAN Edge over a specific transport.
- **vSmart routes**: routes that represent reachability to vSmart controllers in the overlay.

OMP is not just “route exchange.” It also carries attributes that influence best-path selection and forwarding behavior. Important attributes include:

- **Preference**: higher preference is more desirable for a route type or route instance.
- **Site ID**: identifies the site and helps prevent loops and control route propagation.
- **Origin**: indicates where the route came from, such as connected, static, OSPF, BGP, or OMP.
- **Originator**: the system IP of the device that originally advertised the route.
- **Tag**: policy metadata used for filtering and route control.
- **Path ID**: distinguishes multiple paths for the same prefix and supports multipath behavior.
- **TLOC-related attributes**: tie a service route to the transport locator that can reach it.

A practical exam point: a route can be present in OMP but still not be installed into the forwarding table if policy, preference, or reachability conditions prevent it from being usable.

**How OMP route selection works**

OMP best-path selection is more specific than a simple “site and origin” comparison. The exact decision process depends on route type, but in general you should think in terms of:

- **Route type**
- **Preference**
- **Site ID**
- **Originator**
- **Origin**
- **TLOC reachability and policy**
- **Path ID and multipath eligibility**

For service routes, the route must be associated with a reachable TLOC before it is useful for forwarding. For TLOC routes, the device must consider whether the transport is allowed, reachable, and preferred by policy.

A common exam trap is assuming that “the route exists in OMP” automatically means “traffic can use it.” In reality, the route may be:

- filtered by control policy,
- excluded by data policy,
- not installed because the associated TLOC is unreachable,
- or present but not preferred.

**TLOCs explained**

A **Transport Locator (TLOC)** identifies how to reach a WAN Edge over a specific transport. The canonical TLOC tuple is:

- **System IP**
- **Color**
- **Encapsulation**

The **system IP** identifies the device in the overlay. The **color** identifies the transport or transport class, such as `biz-internet`, `public-internet`, `mpls`, or `lte`. The **encapsulation** identifies the tunnel encapsulation used on that transport, typically `ipsec` or `gre` depending on design and platform support.

> Site ID is associated with the device and matters for policy and loop prevention, but it is not part of the TLOC identifier.

TLOCs are important because they are the anchor for tunnel formation and route reachability. If a WAN Edge advertises a service route, remote sites need a TLOC path to reach that prefix.

**TLOC attributes and behavior**

TLOCs can be influenced by several attributes and settings:

- **Preference**: used to prefer one TLOC over another when multiple transports exist.
- **Weight**: can influence load sharing or selection in some designs.
- **Restrict**: limits which peers can use the TLOC.
- **Encapsulation**: must match what the peer can negotiate or support.
- **Color**: often used in policy to classify transports and steer traffic.

A TLOC may be advertised on multiple transports from the same WAN Edge. For example, a branch with MPLS and internet can advertise two TLOCs with the same system IP but different colors.

Example conceptual mapping:

| Device | System IP | Color | Encapsulation |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Branch-1 | 10.255.1.1 | mpls | ipsec |
| Branch-1 | 10.255.1.1 | biz-internet | ipsec |

This means the same edge can be reached through different underlay paths, and policy can choose which one to prefer.

**Transport tunnels and the underlay**

Transport tunnels are the data-plane tunnels that carry user traffic between WAN Edges. They are built over the underlay transport and are associated with TLOCs.

Typical design flow:

1. The WAN Edge brings up underlay connectivity in **VPN 0**.
2. The device establishes control connections to controllers.
3. The device advertises its TLOCs and service routes through OMP.
4. Remote edges learn the TLOCs and build data-plane tunnels as needed.

The tunnel itself is not the same thing as the control connection. Control connections are for onboarding and route exchange; transport tunnels carry application traffic.

A simple topology example:

```text
          MPLS underlay                    Internet underlay
Branch A -------------------\            /------------------- Branch B
                             \          /
                              \        /
                               vSmart / vBond
                              /        \
                             /          \
Branch A -------------------/            \------------------- Branch B
          IPsec/GRE data tunnels between TLOCs
```

**VPN segmentation model**

Cisco SD-WAN uses VPNs to segment traffic and routing domains. The most important ones for exam prep are:

- **VPN 0**: transport VPN, used for WAN transport interfaces and controller reachability.
- **VPN 512**: management VPN, used for local/out-of-band management access.
- **Service VPNs**: user or application VRFs, such as VPN 10, VPN 20, and so on.

VPN segmentation gives you:

- route isolation,
- policy control,
- separate forwarding tables,
- and cleaner operational boundaries.

**VPN 0 use cases**

VPN 0 is where you place transport-facing interfaces. It is commonly used for:

- internet uplinks,
- MPLS uplinks,
- LTE uplinks,
- and the control-plane reachability needed for vBond, vSmart, and vManage.

A typical IOS XE SD-WAN-style concept is:

```bash
vpn 0
 interface GigabitEthernet0/0/0
  ip address 203.0.113.2/30
  tunnel-interface
   color biz-internet
   encapsulation ipsec
   allow-service all
```

Use this as a conceptual example, not a universal copy-paste template. Exact syntax varies by platform and release, and some deployments restrict which services are allowed on a transport interface.

Key points:

- The transport interface lives in **VPN 0**.
- The tunnel interface is tied to a **color** and **encapsulation**.
- The WAN Edge uses this transport to reach controllers and peers.

**VPN 512 use cases**

VPN 512 is the **management VPN**. It is used for local management access such as:

- SSH to the device,
- SNMP polling,
- out-of-band management,
- local reachability to management systems.

It is not the normal path for overlay controller reachability in standard designs. In most deployments, controller reachability uses VPN 0. However, special designs may use management connectivity for limited administrative access or bootstrap-related workflows.

**Service VPN use cases**

Service VPNs carry user and application traffic. They are where you place:

- LAN-facing interfaces,
- routing protocols such as OSPF, EIGRP, BGP, or static routes,
- application subnets,
- and segmentation boundaries.

Example conceptual service VPN configuration:

```bash
vpn 10
 interface GigabitEthernet0/1
  ip address 10.10.10.1/24
  no shutdown
 router ospf 10
  redistribute connected
```

This is intentionally simplified. In real deployments, service VPN routing often requires additional interface, VRF, and platform-specific context. The exam focus is on the relationship between the service VPN, its routes, and how those routes are advertised into OMP.

**Route advertisement and import behavior**

Routes learned in a service VPN can be advertised into OMP. Remote WAN Edges then import those OMP routes into the appropriate local VPN RIB/FIB if policy allows it.

Think of the flow like this:

```text
LAN prefix -> service VPN RIB -> OMP advertisement -> remote OMP table -> remote VPN RIB/FIB -> forwarding
```

Important implications:

- A prefix can exist in OMP but not be installed in the local VPN forwarding table.
- Policy can block advertisement, import, or forwarding independently.
- The same prefix can be segmented into different VPNs to keep tenants or applications isolated.

**Route leaking and segmentation**

Route leaking lets you share routes between VPNs when business requirements demand controlled connectivity. This is useful for:

- shared services,
- centralized security,
- voice or application dependencies,
- and hub-and-spoke designs.

Common approaches include:

- **Localized route leaking** on the WAN Edge.
- **Centralized policy-based leaking** through vSmart.
- **Service insertion** designs where traffic is steered through a firewall or other service.

Example conceptual localized leaking:

```bash
vpn 10
 route-target export 10:10
 route-target import 10:20

vpn 20
 route-target export 10:20
 route-target import 10:10
```

The exact syntax and capabilities vary by platform and release, but the exam concept is that route targets and policy determine which VPNs can exchange routes.

A practical scenario:

- VPN 10 contains user devices.
- VPN 20 contains a shared printer or application server.
- You leak only the required prefixes, not the entire routing table.

That preserves segmentation while enabling controlled access.

**Policy types that affect OMP and TLOC behavior**

Cisco SD-WAN policy is a major part of how OMP and TLOCs behave. The main policy categories are:

- **Control policy**: influences route and TLOC advertisement, acceptance, and preference in the control plane.
- **Data policy**: influences forwarding decisions for traffic flows.
- **App-aware routing policy**: steers traffic based on application performance metrics such as loss, latency, and jitter.

These policies can affect:

- which TLOCs are advertised,
- which routes are accepted,
- which transport is preferred,
- and whether traffic is steered to a backup path.

Examples of policy use:

- Prefer MPLS for voice traffic and internet for bulk traffic.
- Block specific prefixes from being advertised to a branch.
- Prevent a low-trust site from learning sensitive routes.
- Fail over to LTE when SLA thresholds are violated.

**Best-path and forwarding examples**

Consider a branch with two transports:

- MPLS with color `mpls`
- Internet with color `biz-internet`

If both are up, the WAN Edge may advertise two TLOCs. Remote sites can then choose the preferred path based on:

- TLOC preference,
- policy,
- application requirements,
- and tunnel health.

If MPLS fails:

- the MPLS TLOC becomes unreachable or ineligible,
- OMP may still retain the route briefly depending on timers,
- forwarding shifts to the remaining eligible TLOC,
- and BFD detects tunnel health changes quickly.

This is why OMP and tunnel state must be interpreted together. A route can still exist while the preferred tunnel is down.

**Verification commands**

Use verification commands to separate control-plane state from forwarding state.

Common commands include:

```bash
show sdwan omp routes
show sdwan omp routes detail
show sdwan omp tlocs
show sdwan omp peers
show sdwan bfd sessions
show sdwan control connections
show ip route vpn 10
show ip route vpn 0
show sdwan tunnel statistics
```

What to look for:

- **OMP routes**: confirm the prefix is learned and from which site/originator.
- **OMP TLOCs**: confirm the transport locator is advertised and reachable.
- **BFD sessions**: confirm tunnel liveliness and loss/latency/jitter.
- **Control connections**: confirm controller sessions are up.
- **VPN routes**: confirm the route is installed in the local routing table.

Example interpretation:

- If `show sdwan omp routes detail` shows a prefix but `show ip route vpn 10` does not, policy or import may be blocking installation.
- If OMP sees the route but `show sdwan bfd sessions` is down, the tunnel may not be usable for forwarding.
- If control connections are down, route exchange and tunnel management may be impaired.

**Troubleshooting workflow**

When traffic does not pass in an SD-WAN overlay, troubleshoot in layers.

1. Verify underlay reachability in **VPN 0**.
2. Verify controller control connections.
3. Verify OMP peering and route exchange.
4. Verify TLOC advertisement and tunnel formation.
5. Verify BFD state and tunnel health.
6. Verify VPN route installation and policy.
7. Verify application or data policy behavior.

Useful questions:

- Is the transport interface up and reachable?
- Is the correct color configured?
- Is the encapsulation compatible?
- Is the remote TLOC advertised and eligible?
- Is a control policy filtering the route?
- Is a data policy steering traffic elsewhere?
- Is the route installed in the correct VPN?

> A common failure is not “color mismatch” in the abstract, but rather that the remote TLOC is not reachable, not eligible, or not permitted by policy.

**Real-world scenario: dual-transport branch**

A branch has:

- MPLS in VPN 0 with color `mpls`
- Internet in VPN 0 with color `biz-internet`
- User LAN in VPN 10
- Guest LAN in VPN 20

Behavior:

- Normal traffic uses MPLS for business applications.
- Guest traffic may be forced to internet by data policy.
- If MPLS degrades, app-aware routing can move voice to internet or LTE.
- If a shared service exists in a hub, only selected prefixes are leaked into VPN 10 or VPN 20.

This design shows why OMP, TLOCs, transport tunnels, and VPN segmentation are tightly linked:

- OMP distributes the reachability.
- TLOCs identify the transport endpoints.
- Tunnels carry the traffic.
- VPNs isolate and control the routing domains.

**Exam-focused distinctions**

| Concept | What it is | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| OMP | Overlay route and policy protocol | Distributes service routes, TLOCs, and controller reachability |
| TLOC | Transport locator tuple | Identifies how to reach a WAN Edge over a specific transport |
| Transport tunnel | Data-plane tunnel | Carries user traffic between edges |
| VPN 0 | Transport VRF | Hosts WAN uplinks and controller reachability |
| VPN 512 | Management VRF | Hosts local management access |
| Service VPN | Tenant/application VRF | Segments user traffic and routing |

**Practical configuration mindset**

When you design or troubleshoot, keep the layers separate:

- **Underlay**: IP reachability on the physical transport.
- **Transport identity**: TLOC tuple and color.
- **Overlay control**: OMP and controller sessions.
- **Forwarding**: VPN RIB/FIB installation and tunnel health.
- **Segmentation**: service VPNs and route leaking policy.

That layered view helps you avoid common exam mistakes, especially when a route exists in one table but not another.

**Key takeaways**

- OMP carries **service routes**, **TLOC routes**, and **vSmart routes**.
- A TLOC is identified by **system IP, color, and encapsulation**.
- **Site ID** is important for policy and loop prevention, but it is not part of the TLOC identifier.
- **VPN 0** is for transport and controller reachability in standard designs.
- **VPN 512** is for management access.
- **Service VPNs** isolate user and application traffic.
- A route in OMP is not automatically a usable forwarding path.
- Policy can affect route advertisement, import, and tunnel selection at multiple layers.
