## **Course:** Cisco CCNP Enterprise Advanced Infrastructure (ENCOR 350-401 & ENARSI 300-410)

**Cisco SD-WAN policy model**

Cisco SD-WAN uses policy to control how routes, TLOCs, services, and traffic are exchanged and forwarded. The exam focus is usually on the difference between **centralized policy** and **localized policy**, and on how application-aware routing uses measured transport quality to steer traffic.

Centralized policy is authored in vManage, then distributed through the control plane. In practical terms:

- You define policy objects and rules in vManage.
- vManage compiles and orchestrates the policy.
- vSmart controllers receive the centralized policy and distribute the resulting control information to WAN Edge devices through OMP.
- WAN Edge devices enforce forwarding decisions for traffic and route advertisement behavior.

> Enforcement happens on the WAN Edge, not on vSmart. vSmart is the control-plane distribution point.

**Centralized policy vs localized policy**

Centralized policy is the main exam topic for enterprise SD-WAN designs because it gives you a single place to define intent across many sites. Localized policy is configured on the device itself and is useful when you need site-specific behavior that should not be centrally orchestrated.

| Policy type | Where you configure it | What it affects | Typical use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Centralized policy | vManage | OMP route/TLOC/service control, traffic steering, app-aware routing | Enterprise-wide intent |
| Localized policy | WAN Edge device | Local forwarding and route handling on that device | Site-specific exceptions |

Centralized policy is usually preferred when you want consistent behavior across many branches. Localized policy is useful when a single site needs a unique exception, but it is harder to scale and audit.

**Centralized policy building blocks**

Cisco SD-WAN centralized policy is built from reusable objects and policy definitions. The exact GUI workflow is vManage-driven, but the exam expects you to understand the structure:

- **Lists** define match objects.
  - `site-list`
  - `vpn-list`
  - `prefix-list`
  - `tloc-list`
  - `app-list`
  - `data-prefix-list`
  - `color-list` in some designs or releases
- **Policy definitions** describe what to do when a match occurs.
  - control policy
  - data policy
  - application-aware routing policy
- **Sequences** define ordered match/action logic inside a policy section.
- **Policy attachment** binds the policy to a site, VPN, or device group depending on policy type.

A useful mental model is:

1. Build reusable match objects.
2. Reference those objects in a policy definition.
3. Order sequences carefully.
4. Attach the policy at the correct scope.
5. Verify the resulting control-plane or forwarding behavior.

**Common centralized policy objects**

A few object types appear repeatedly in exam questions:

- **site-list**: matches one or more site IDs.
- **vpn-list**: matches one or more VPNs/VRFs.
- **prefix-list**: matches IP prefixes.
- **tloc-list**: matches transport locators, usually by color, system IP, and encapsulation.
- **app-list**: matches applications by application name or category.
- **data-prefix-list**: matches source or destination prefixes for data policy.

These are match objects, not the policy outcome themselves. For example, a site-list does not create segmentation by itself; it is simply a selector used inside a policy.

**Control policy fundamentals**

Control policy influences what OMP advertises or accepts. OMP, the Overlay Management Protocol, is the control protocol used between vSmart and WAN Edge devices to exchange routes, TLOCs, and service information.

Control policy commonly does the following:

- accept or reject OMP routes
- accept or reject TLOCs
- control route advertisement behavior
- influence which routes are propagated to which sites
- filter or leak routes between VPNs in a controlled way

Control policy does **not** act like a routing protocol metric policy. It is not about “preferring” a route in the classic IGP sense. Instead, it controls distribution and visibility of overlay information.

A simplified control-policy workflow looks like this:

```text
Match object -> policy sequence -> action -> OMP advertisement/acceptance -> WAN Edge routing table impact
```

**Example: centralized control policy concept**

The following is a simplified representation of how a control policy is structured. Syntax varies by release and vManage workflow, so treat this as conceptual rather than copy-paste production CLI.

```text
policy
  lists
    site-list BRANCHES
      site-id 101
      site-id 102
    !
    prefix-list CORP-SUBNETS
      ip-prefix 10.10.0.0/16
    !
  !
  control-policy CONTROL-BRANCH-FILTER
    sequence 10
      match route
        site-list BRANCHES
        prefix-list CORP-SUBNETS
      action accept
    !
    sequence 20
      action reject
    !
  !
apply-policy
  site-list BRANCHES
    control-policy CONTROL-BRANCH-FILTER
```

What matters for the exam:

- The policy is built from lists and sequences.
- The first matching sequence is often decisive for that policy section, but you should always read the specific policy type and action behavior carefully.
- A final reject sequence is common to prevent unintended matches from being advertised.

**Data policy fundamentals**

Data policy influences forwarding behavior for user traffic. It is applied to traffic flows rather than to OMP route exchange.

Common data-policy actions include:

- set next-hop
- set TLOC
- set DSCP
- service insertion
- accept or drop traffic
- steer traffic to a specific transport or tunnel path

Data policy is not the place to think about local-preference as a normal SD-WAN action. That is a routing-policy concept in other contexts, not a standard SD-WAN data-policy action.

A simplified data-policy example:

```text
policy
  lists
    app-list VIDEO
      app-name youtube
      app-name teams
    !
    vpn-list USERS
      vpn 10
    !
  !
  data-policy BRANCH-STEERING
    vpn-list USERS
      sequence 10
        match app-list VIDEO
        action set tloc color biz-internet
      !
      sequence 20
        action accept
    !
  !
apply-policy
  site-list BRANCHES
    data-policy BRANCH-STEERING
```

Interpretation:

- Traffic matching the application list is steered to a specific transport.
- Nonmatching traffic is accepted by the later sequence.
- The policy is attached to the relevant sites or VPN scope.

**Application-aware routing overview**

Application-aware routing, often called **app-route**, uses transport measurements to choose the best path for a specific application. It is one of the most tested SD-WAN features because it combines policy, SLA, and transport selection.

The key idea is:

- WAN Edge devices probe each available transport path.
- The probes are SD-WAN-specific BFD-based measurements between edges over each color/TLOC pair.
- The measured values produce SLA statistics such as latency, jitter, and loss.
- The app-route policy compares those values to thresholds and selects the best eligible path.

This is not generic BFD alone. It is the SD-WAN app-route/BFD mechanism used to evaluate transport quality for application steering.

**App-route components**

Application-aware routing typically uses these elements:

- **Application match**
  - identifies which traffic the policy applies to
  - often based on app-list or application category
- **SLA class**
  - defines acceptable thresholds
  - usually includes latency, jitter, and loss
- **Preferred path**
  - the primary transport or color
- **Backup path**
  - alternate transport if the preferred path fails or violates SLA
- **Fallback behavior**
  - defines what happens when no path meets SLA

A practical design pattern is:

- voice traffic gets strict SLA thresholds
- video traffic gets moderate thresholds
- best-effort traffic uses looser thresholds or no app-route steering

**SLA class behavior**

An SLA class defines the performance envelope for a flow. The policy compares measured transport values against the class.

Typical parameters:

- latency in milliseconds
- jitter in milliseconds
- loss in percentage

Example conceptual SLA class:

```text
sla-class VOICE-SLA
  latency 150
  jitter 30
  loss 1
```

How to think about it:

- If a path meets the SLA, it is eligible.
- If multiple paths meet the SLA, the policy chooses according to preference order or best available path logic.
- If no path meets the SLA, fallback behavior determines whether you stay on the preferred path, use the best available path, or take another configured action.

**Preferred and backup path logic**

App-aware routing is not just “pick the lowest latency path.” It is policy-driven.

Common behavior patterns:

- **Preferred path first**
  - use the primary transport if it meets SLA
- **Backup path on failure**
  - move to the secondary transport if the primary fails or violates SLA
- **Best available path**
  - choose the path that best satisfies the SLA when the preferred path is not acceptable
- **Stay on preferred path**
  - continue using the preferred transport even if no path currently meets SLA, depending on the configured fallback mode

The exact fallback options and names can vary by release, but the exam concept is consistent: fallback behavior is explicit and policy-driven, not automatic magic.

**Example: app-aware routing policy concept**

```text
policy
  lists
    app-list REALTIME
      app-name voice
      app-name video
    !
    sla-class REALTIME-SLA
      latency 150
      jitter 30
      loss 1
    !
  !
  app-route-policy BRANCH-APP-ROUTE
    sequence 10
      match app-list REALTIME
      action
        sla-class REALTIME-SLA
        preferred-color biz-internet
        backup-color mpls
        fallback best-available
    !
    sequence 20
      action accept
  !
apply-policy
  site-list BRANCHES
    app-route-policy BRANCH-APP-ROUTE
```

This is simplified, but it captures the exam-relevant structure:

- match the application
- bind the SLA class
- define preferred and backup transports
- define fallback behavior

**How app-route decisions are made**

The decision process is usually:

1. The edge identifies the application flow.
2. The edge checks the configured app-route policy.
3. The edge evaluates available transports using SD-WAN BFD probes.
4. The edge compares measured values to the SLA class.
5. The edge selects the eligible path based on preference and fallback rules.

If the preferred path violates SLA, the edge can move to a backup path if one is eligible. If no path meets SLA, the configured fallback mode determines the result.

**Before and after behavior example**

Before policy:

- voice traffic may use any available tunnel
- a branch may send traffic over a path with high jitter
- users experience poor call quality

After policy:

- voice traffic is matched by app-list
- the edge checks SLA on each transport
- the edge selects the transport that meets the voice SLA
- if the primary path degrades, traffic shifts to the backup path

This is the practical value of app-aware routing: you align application needs with transport quality.

**Policy attachment and hierarchy**

Policy attachment is a common exam trap because the policy must be attached at the right scope.

General hierarchy:

- **Centralized policy** is created in vManage.
- **Control policy** affects OMP route/TLOC/service exchange.
- **Data policy** affects traffic forwarding.
- **App-route policy** affects application path selection.
- The policy is then attached to the relevant site list, VPN list, or device scope depending on the policy type and design.

Important distinctions:

- A control policy can influence what routes are learned or advertised.
- A data policy can steer or drop traffic.
- An app-route policy can select the transport path for a specific application.
- The same site can receive multiple centralized policy components.

**Practical vManage workflow**

A typical workflow in vManage is:

1. Create the match lists.
2. Create the policy definition.
3. Add sequences and actions.
4. Attach the policy to the correct site list or VPN scope.
5. Activate the policy.
6. Verify the resulting behavior on the WAN Edge.

This workflow matters because many failures come from attachment mistakes rather than match logic.

**Verification and troubleshooting**

Verification is where many exam questions become platform-specific. Command availability differs between IOS XE SD-WAN and vEdge software, so focus on the intent of the command rather than memorizing one exact syntax for every release.

Useful verification goals:

- confirm the policy is attached
- confirm the policy is active
- confirm the match sequence is being hit
- confirm OMP advertisements changed as expected
- confirm app-route selected the intended transport
- confirm SLA thresholds are being met or violated

Common verification areas include:

- policy status in vManage
- OMP route and TLOC tables
- app-route statistics
- BFD session health
- active path selection for a given application flow

Representative commands may include:

```bash
show sdwan omp routes
show sdwan omp tlocs
show sdwan app-route stats
show sdwan app-route sla-class
show sdwan bfd sessions
show sdwan policy from-vsmart
```

Use these carefully:

- exact syntax can vary by platform and release
- some commands are more common on IOS XE SD-WAN than on older vEdge images
- some outputs are more useful in vManage than on the CLI

**What to look for in outputs**

When troubleshooting centralized policy:

- check whether the policy is installed on the device
- check whether the expected sequence counter increments
- check whether routes are being accepted or rejected as intended
- check whether a TLOC or prefix is missing because of a control-policy filter
- check whether a VPN route is present but not used because of a data-policy or app-route decision

When troubleshooting app-aware routing:

- check whether BFD probes are up for each transport
- check whether latency, jitter, and loss are within SLA
- check whether the application is matched by the correct app-list
- check whether the preferred path is actually eligible
- check whether fallback behavior is causing the observed path selection

**Troubleshooting patterns**

A few common failure patterns show up repeatedly:

- **Wrong match object**
  - the app or prefix is not in the list
- **Wrong sequence order**
  - an earlier sequence matches first and prevents the intended action
- **Wrong attachment scope**
  - the policy is attached to the wrong site list or VPN
- **Transport not eligible**
  - the path is down or does not meet SLA
- **Policy not activated**
  - the policy exists in vManage but is not pushed or applied
- **Release-specific command confusion**
  - the verification command differs from the one you expected

> If traffic does not behave as expected, verify attachment and sequence order before assuming the match criteria are wrong.

**Exam-relevant distinctions**

A few distinctions are especially important for CCNP-level questions:

- **Centralized policy** is orchestrated centrally and distributed through the SD-WAN control plane.
- **Localized policy** is configured on the device and is better for site-specific exceptions.
- **Control policy** affects OMP route and TLOC exchange.
- **Data policy** affects traffic forwarding decisions.
- **App-aware routing** uses transport measurements to steer application traffic based on SLA.
- **Site lists and prefix lists** are match objects, not segmentation mechanisms by themselves.
- **Segmentation** in SD-WAN is primarily achieved with VPNs/VRFs plus policy.

**Practical design example**

Consider a branch with two transports:

- MPLS
- Internet

You want:

- voice over the transport with the best real-time performance
- business-critical apps to prefer MPLS
- general web traffic to use Internet

A design approach is:

- create an `app-list` for voice and video
- define an SLA class with tight latency, jitter, and loss thresholds
- create a control policy if you need to control route visibility between sites
- create a data policy if you need to steer specific traffic classes
- create an app-route policy for real-time applications
- attach the policy to the branch site list

Result:

- voice traffic follows the best eligible path
- business apps use the preferred transport when available
- noncritical traffic remains on the cheaper or default path

**Quick reference**

- **OMP**: control protocol between vSmart and WAN Edge devices for routes, TLOCs, and service information.
- **TLOC**: Transport Locator; identifies a WAN transport path using system IP, color, and encapsulation.
- **SLA**: Service Level Agreement; in app-route, the threshold set for latency, jitter, and loss.
- **BFD**: Bidirectional Forwarding Detection; used by SD-WAN to probe transport health and derive app-route metrics.
- **vSmart**: control-plane controller that distributes centralized policy and OMP information.
- **vManage**: management and policy orchestration interface.

**What to remember for the exam**

- Centralized policy is built from lists, sequences, and policy definitions.
- vManage is the authoring and orchestration point, but WAN Edge devices enforce forwarding decisions.
- Control policy changes OMP advertisement and acceptance behavior.
- Data policy changes traffic forwarding behavior.
- App-aware routing uses SD-WAN BFD probes to measure transport quality per application flow.
- SLA classes define the acceptable path quality for an application.
- Fallback behavior matters when no path meets SLA.
- Verification often requires checking both policy installation and transport health.
