## **Course:** Cisco CCNP Enterprise Advanced Infrastructure (ENCOR 350-401 & ENARSI 300-410)

**SD-WAN architecture overview**

Cisco SD-WAN separates **management**, **control**, and **data** functions so you can scale policy and path selection independently of the underlay. The fabric is built around edge devices and controllers that each have distinct roles.

- **WAN Edge** devices forward user traffic and build overlay tunnels across the underlay.
- **vBond** acts as the orchestrator for initial discovery, authentication, and controller introduction.
- **vSmart** distributes control information using **OMP** (Overlay Management Protocol).
- **vManage** provides centralized management, monitoring, and policy orchestration rather than acting as a standard control-plane peer.

A useful mental model is:

- Underlay = transport reachability
- Overlay = encrypted tunnels and control exchange
- Orchestration = trust establishment and controller discovery
- Management = configuration, monitoring, and policy workflow

**Core fabric components**

| Component | Primary role | Exam focus |
|---|---|---|
| WAN Edge | Builds tunnels, advertises routes, forwards traffic | TLOCs, OMP, BFD, transport colors |
| vBond | Authenticates devices and introduces them to controllers | ZTP, certificate trust, discovery |
| vSmart | Control-plane policy and route distribution | OMP routes, centralized policy |
| vManage | GUI/API management and orchestration | Templates, monitoring, policy push |

**Control-plane and data-plane separation**

The control plane is not the same as the data plane. In Cisco SD-WAN, the control plane establishes trust and distributes reachability information, while the data plane carries user packets through encrypted tunnels.

- **Control connections** are used for authentication, discovery, and OMP exchange.
- **Data tunnels** carry application traffic between WAN Edge devices.
- **BFD** (Bidirectional Forwarding Detection) runs over overlay tunnels to measure path liveliness and quality.

> Do not treat vManage as a normal adjacency in the same sense as vBond or vSmart. It manages the fabric, but it is not the primary peer for route exchange.

**Controller roles and interaction flow**

A typical onboarding sequence is:

1. The WAN Edge boots with bootstrap information, organization name, system IP, site ID, and controller reachability.
2. The device reaches **vBond** first, usually through a public or reachable transport address.
3. vBond validates identity and helps the edge discover the correct **vSmart** and **vManage** controllers.
4. The edge forms secure control connections to **vSmart** and, for management, to **vManage**.
5. The edge establishes overlay tunnels to other WAN Edge devices and begins exchanging routes through OMP.

This sequence is important because a failure early in the chain often prevents later stages from appearing healthy.

**DTLS and TLS in control connections**

Cisco SD-WAN control connections commonly use **DTLS** (Datagram Transport Layer Security) or **TLS** (Transport Layer Security), depending on transport and platform behavior.

- **DTLS** is UDP-based and is often associated with control connections over transports where UDP is preferred.
- **TLS** is TCP-based and is often used where TCP transport is required or selected.
- The exact transport behavior can vary by release and deployment design, so memorize the role of the protocol rather than a single oversimplified port story.

Practical exam takeaway:

- Focus on the fact that control connections are secure and authenticated.
- Remember that the data plane is not “just UDP control traffic”; it is an encrypted overlay tunnel system, commonly IPsec-based.
- Do not confuse control transport with user data transport.

**Bootstrap and trust establishment**

A new edge must prove identity before it can join the fabric. Trust is usually established through certificates and organization metadata.

Common onboarding inputs include:

- **organization-name**: must match across controllers and edges
- **system-ip**: unique identifier for the device in the fabric
- **site-id**: identifies the physical site for policy and route behavior
- **vbond** address: initial discovery target
- **device certificate** or **bootstrap certificate**: used for authentication

Example bootstrap-style information:

```text
system-ip 10.10.10.11
site-id 101
organization-name ACME-SDWAN
vbond 203.0.113.10
```

> An organization-name mismatch is a classic failure mode. If the edge and controllers do not share the same org name, authentication and control establishment fail even if IP reachability exists.

**ZTP and certificate workflows**

**ZTP** (Zero Touch Provisioning) lets you onboard devices with minimal manual configuration. The device contacts a discovery service or preconfigured controller path, retrieves bootstrap details, and then authenticates into the fabric.

Common trust models include:

- **Enterprise CA**: your internal certificate authority signs device certificates.
- **Cisco-signed certificates**: devices ship with factory trust anchors.
- **Manual bootstrap**: used in labs or constrained deployments.

What matters for exam prep:

- The device must trust the controller certificate chain.
- The controller must trust the device identity.
- The organization name and certificate chain must align.
- Discovery can fail even when routing works if trust is broken.

Useful verification commands on a WAN Edge:

```bash
show sdwan control local-properties
show sdwan control connections
show sdwan certificate status
show sdwan certificate installed
show sdwan omp peers
```

**TLOCs and transport identity**

A **TLOC** (Transport Locator) identifies how a WAN Edge is reachable in the overlay. It is one of the most tested SD-WAN concepts.

A TLOC is commonly described by:

- **system IP**: the device identity
- **color**: the transport type or path classification
- **encapsulation**: how the tunnel is built, such as IPsec
- **preference**: influences path preference
- **weight**: influences load sharing among equal candidates

The **color** is especially important because it maps the transport interface to a logical path type such as internet, MPLS, or LTE. Colors help the fabric understand which underlay path a tunnel uses.

Example conceptual mapping:

| Transport | Example color | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Internet broadband | `biz-internet` | Common public transport |
| MPLS | `mpls` | Private WAN transport |
| LTE/5G | `lte` | Backup or branch resilience |
| Generic public internet | `public-internet` | Often used in lab examples |

TLOC attributes influence path selection:

- **Preference** is used to prefer one TLOC over another.
- **Weight** helps distribute traffic when multiple paths are eligible.
- **Encapsulation** must match what both sides support.
- **Color** must align with policy and transport intent.

**OMP fundamentals**

**OMP** is the control protocol used between WAN Edge devices and vSmart controllers. It distributes overlay reachability and policy-relevant information.

OMP commonly carries:

- **OMP routes**: prefixes learned from edges or redistributed into the fabric
- **TLOC routes**: reachability to transport locators
- **Service routes**: routes for services such as centralized services or VPN-specific reachability
- **Policy information**: attributes used by centralized policy to influence propagation and selection

Key exam points:

- OMP is the main route exchange mechanism in Cisco SD-WAN.
- vSmart is the controller that distributes OMP information.
- vBond does not act as the primary route exchange controller.
- OMP decisions are influenced by **site ID**, **VPN**, route attributes, and policy.

Route propagation behavior depends on:

- **VPN ID**: routes stay within the correct VPN unless policy permits otherwise
- **site ID**: helps prevent unnecessary route reflection loops and supports split-horizon behavior
- **centralized policy**: can control advertisement, preference, and import/export behavior
- **local policy**: can still affect how a device treats routes and traffic locally

**OMP route types and behavior**

A practical way to think about OMP is that it advertises both reachability and how to reach the transport.

- **OMP routes** represent prefixes and service reachability.
- **TLOC routes** represent the transport endpoint and tunnel identity.
- **Service routes** can represent service insertion or centralized service reachability in some designs.

Why this matters:

- A prefix alone is not enough; the fabric also needs the TLOC that tells it how to reach the advertising edge.
- If a TLOC is missing or unreachable, the prefix may exist in control-plane tables but not be usable in forwarding.
- If policy suppresses a route, the prefix may never appear on remote edges even though the control session is healthy.

**BFD and overlay liveliness**

**BFD** monitors tunnel liveliness and path quality across the overlay. It is not a replacement for OMP, but it complements it.

- OMP tells the fabric what routes and TLOCs exist.
- BFD tells the fabric whether a specific tunnel path is healthy enough for forwarding.
- Path loss, delay, and jitter can influence preferred forwarding decisions when multiple tunnels exist.

A simple operational view:

- OMP = control reachability and route distribution
- BFD = fast path health detection
- Data plane = actual packet forwarding over the selected tunnel

**Control connections versus data tunnels**

| Aspect | Control connection | Data tunnel |
|---|---|---|
| Purpose | Authentication, discovery, OMP exchange | User traffic forwarding |
| Main peers | vBond, vSmart, vManage | Other WAN Edge devices |
| Protocol role | DTLS/TLS secure control sessions | Encrypted overlay tunnel, commonly IPsec-based |
| Health signal | Control session state | BFD and tunnel status |
| Impact of failure | No route exchange or onboarding | Traffic loss or path failover |

This distinction is critical for troubleshooting. A device can have a healthy control connection and still fail to forward traffic if the data tunnel or path policy is broken.

**Policy types and where they apply**

Cisco SD-WAN uses both centralized and localized policy concepts.

- **Centralized policy** is pushed from vManage and enforced through vSmart and the fabric control plane.
- **Localized policy** is applied on the device itself and affects local forwarding or route handling.

Common exam-relevant effects:

- Centralized policy can control route advertisement between VPNs and sites.
- Centralized policy can influence preferred TLOCs and application-aware routing behavior.
- Local policy can still override or refine behavior on a specific edge.

When troubleshooting, always ask:

- Is the route missing because it was never learned?
- Was it learned but filtered by policy?
- Was it advertised but not installed due to VPN or site-ID mismatch?
- Is the tunnel healthy but the route suppressed?

**Practical verification workflow**

Use a layered approach when checking a fabric issue.

1. Verify basic identity and trust.
2. Verify control connections.
3. Verify OMP peers and route tables.
4. Verify TLOC reachability and BFD status.
5. Verify policy and VPN membership.

Useful commands on an edge:

```bash
show sdwan control connections
show sdwan control local-properties
show sdwan omp peers
show sdwan omp routes
show sdwan omp tlocs
show sdwan bfd sessions
show sdwan vpn 0 interface
show sdwan vpn 10 route
```

Useful controller-side checks:

```bash
show control connections
show omp peers
show omp routes
show certificate status
show orchestrator connections
```

If you use vManage, also check:

- device inventory status
- certificate status
- template attachment state
- centralized policy activation
- transport and tunnel health dashboards

**Common failure modes and what they usually mean**

- **No control connection to vBond**
  - Check underlay reachability, DNS, NAT, firewall rules, and bootstrap values.
  - Confirm the device can reach the discovery address.

- **Certificate or org-name failure**
  - Check certificate validity, trust chain, and organization-name consistency.
  - Look for authentication failures in control logs.

- **Control up, no OMP routes**
  - Check vSmart reachability, OMP peer state, VPN membership, route advertisement, and centralized policy.
  - Confirm the route is not filtered by site-ID or import/export policy.

- **OMP up, no traffic**
  - Check BFD sessions, TLOC reachability, tunnel encapsulation, and application policy.
  - Confirm the correct color is allowed and preferred.

- **One transport path fails**
  - Check underlay loss, color-specific reachability, NAT traversal, and path preference.
  - Verify whether the fabric should fail over automatically or keep the path as standby.

**Troubleshooting example**

Suppose a branch edge can ping the vBond public IP, but it never appears fully onboarded in vManage.

Likely checks:

```bash
show sdwan control connections
show sdwan certificate status
show sdwan control local-properties
show sdwan omp peers
```

What you might infer:

- If the control connection to vBond is missing, discovery or trust is failing.
- If the certificate status is invalid, the device may not authenticate to the controllers.
- If vBond is up but vSmart is missing, controller discovery may be incomplete or blocked.
- If OMP peers are down, the edge cannot exchange routes even if the transport is reachable.

A practical symptom-to-cause mapping:

- **Reachability exists, onboarding fails** → trust, org-name, or certificate issue
- **Onboarding succeeds, routes missing** → OMP, VPN, or policy issue
- **Routes exist, traffic fails** → BFD, tunnel, color, or forwarding issue

**Configuration concepts to remember**

Even though exact syntax varies by platform and template workflow, the key onboarding concepts are consistent.

- Define the device identity and site information.
- Point the device to vBond for discovery.
- Ensure the correct certificate chain is installed.
- Attach transport and service templates or equivalent configuration.
- Confirm the device forms control connections before expecting routes.

Example conceptual template values:

```text
system-ip 10.10.10.11
site-id 101
organization-name ACME-SDWAN
vbond 203.0.113.10
vpn 0 transport
vpn 10 service
```

> Template-based deployments are common in production. Do not rely on a lab-style CLI snippet as if it were the only valid IOS XE SD-WAN workflow.

**Exam-focused distinctions**

- **vBond** = discovery, authentication, orchestration
- **vSmart** = OMP control distribution and policy enforcement
- **vManage** = management, monitoring, and orchestration interface
- **OMP** = route and TLOC exchange
- **BFD** = tunnel liveliness and performance monitoring
- **TLOC** = transport identity for a tunnel path
- **Color** = transport classification and policy selector

**Quick comparison: OMP and BFD**

| Feature | OMP | BFD |
|---|---|---|
| Layer of concern | Control plane | Data path health |
| Main purpose | Distribute routes and TLOCs | Detect tunnel/path failure quickly |
| Controller involvement | Yes, through vSmart | Indirect; runs over overlay tunnels |
| Typical failure symptom | Missing routes or peers | Traffic loss or path failover |
| Exam clue | “Why is the route not present?” | “Why is the tunnel not usable?” |

**What to memorize**

- The onboarding chain starts with **vBond**, not vManage.
- **vSmart** is the main controller for OMP route exchange.
- **vManage** manages and orchestrates; it is not the primary route peer.
- **TLOC attributes** such as color, encapsulation, preference, and weight influence path selection.
- **BFD** validates tunnel health, while **OMP** distributes reachability.
- **VPN ID**, **site ID**, and **policy** all affect whether routes appear and are used.
