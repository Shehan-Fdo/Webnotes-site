## **Course:** Cisco CCNP Enterprise Advanced Infrastructure (ENCOR 350-401 & ENARSI 300-410)

**Overview**

Cisco SD-Access (Software-Defined Access) onboarding and fabric extension cover how you bring devices into a managed fabric, assign them the right role, and connect the fabric to external networks or other sites. In practice, this means you must understand both the **workflow** in Catalyst Center and the **data-plane/control-plane behavior** on the devices themselves.

The exam focus is usually on:
- How devices are discovered, claimed, provisioned, and assigned to fabric roles.
- How fabric nodes differ from transit or external devices.
- How border nodes, control-plane nodes, and edge nodes support overlay connectivity.
- How to extend the fabric to external networks, shared services, and other sites without breaking policy or routing.

**Core onboarding concepts**

Device onboarding is the process of moving a switch, wireless controller, or related device from a generic managed state into a design-specific role. In Cisco environments, Catalyst Center typically orchestrates this using discovery, inventory, image management, credentials, and provisioning workflows.

Common onboarding methods include:
- **Plug and Play (PnP)** for zero-touch or near-zero-touch deployment.
- **Zero Touch Provisioning (ZTP)** on supported platforms and software trains.
- **Manual claim and provisioning** when devices are already reachable and you want to assign them to a site or fabric role.
- **Template-based provisioning** for settings that are not fully automated by the fabric workflow.

A practical onboarding sequence often looks like this:
1. Discover the device or let it contact Catalyst Center through PnP/ZTP.
2. Add the device to inventory and validate reachability.
3. Assign the device to a site hierarchy.
4. Apply image, credentials, and provisioning settings as needed.
5. Convert or assign the device to a fabric role if it participates in SD-Access.

> **Important:** Catalyst Center can manage images and credentials, but fabric onboarding is not simply “push an image and credentials.” The device role, site placement, underlay readiness, and fabric design must all be correct before the node can participate in the fabric.

**Catalyst Center onboarding workflows**

Catalyst Center is the orchestration point for onboarding. It uses device credentials, discovery, and provisioning policies to prepare devices for fabric use. The exact workflow depends on whether the device is new, already in service, or being repurposed.

Typical workflows:
- **PnP workflow**
  - Device boots with a factory or minimal configuration.
  - It reaches the PnP server or cloud-assisted onboarding path.
  - Catalyst Center assigns the intended image and base configuration.
  - The device is then provisioned into the correct site and role.

- **ZTP workflow**
  - The device obtains basic network connectivity and contacts the provisioning service.
  - It downloads bootstrap information and joins the management workflow.
  - You still need to validate that the device is compatible with the intended fabric design.

- **Claim and provision workflow**
  - You add the device to inventory using discovery or manual claim.
  - You assign it to a site.
  - You provision it with the required settings and then convert it to a fabric node if needed.

Practical CLI checks on the device:
```bash
show version
show ip interface brief
show cdp neighbors detail
show lldp neighbors detail
show running-config | section aaa
show running-config | section interface
```

**Fabric node roles and what they do**

SD-Access fabric roles are not interchangeable. You should know what each role does and where policy is enforced.

| Role | Primary purpose | Typical placement | Key notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Edge node | Connects endpoints to the fabric | Access layer | Enforces access policy, maps endpoints into VN/VRF and SGT context |
| Control-plane node | Maintains endpoint-to-location mapping | Central or resilient site location | Runs LISP control-plane functions for endpoint registration |
| Border node | Connects fabric to external networks or other fabric domains | Fabric edge | Handles route exchange, route leaking, and boundary policy |
| Wireless controller integration | Extends fabric to wireless clients | Usually Catalyst 9800 WLC | APs are not fabric nodes, but the controller participates in fabric workflows |

The most important distinction is that **edge nodes** are where endpoint attachment and policy enforcement happen, while **border nodes** are where the fabric meets external routing domains.

**Fabric node prerequisites**

Before onboarding a device into a fabric, validate the design and platform constraints. These are common exam and real-world failure points.

- **Supported platform and software**
  - Confirm the switch or controller model supports the intended SD-Access role.
  - Verify the IOS XE release is compatible with the Catalyst Center version and fabric feature set.
- **Underlay readiness**
  - Ensure IP reachability between fabric nodes.
  - Confirm routing adjacency, loopback reachability, and MTU support.
- **MTU**
  - VXLAN encapsulation adds overhead, so the underlay must support a larger MTU than plain Ethernet in many designs.
  - If MTU is too small, overlay traffic may fail or fragment unexpectedly.
- **Time and identity**
  - NTP, DNS, and AAA should be stable.
  - Certificates and trust relationships matter for management and controller workflows.
- **Management access**
  - SSH, SNMP, NETCONF/RESTCONF, and related management services may be required depending on the workflow.

Useful verification commands:
```bash
show ip route
ping <loopback-ip> source <interface>
traceroute <remote-loopback-ip> source <interface>
show interfaces | include MTU
show ntp status
show clock
```

**Access authentication during onboarding**

When you onboard access switches, you often need 802.1X and MAC Authentication Bypass (MAB) so endpoints can be authenticated and authorized consistently. The switch acts as the **authenticator**: it enforces the result, tracks session state, and decides which method to try next based on local policy and server response.

A common access-port design includes:
- 802.1X for capable endpoints.
- MAB for devices that cannot do 802.1X.
- Fallback behavior if the authentication server is unavailable or the endpoint fails 802.1X.
- Host mode selection based on whether one or multiple devices may connect.

Example access-port configuration:
```cisco
interface GigabitEthernet1/0/10
 description User access port
 switchport mode access
 authentication port-control auto
 authentication order dot1x mab
 authentication priority dot1x mab
 authentication event fail action next-method
 authentication event server dead action authorize vlan 999
 authentication host-mode single-host
 mab
 dot1x pae authenticator
 spanning-tree portfast
```

Notes:
- `authentication event fail action next-method` helps the switch try the next method when the first one fails.
- `authentication host-mode single-host` is common for a single user device; other designs may use multi-auth or multi-domain.
- `authentication event server dead action authorize vlan 999` is a design choice, not a universal requirement.
- You should align the access policy with the intended user, voice, printer, or IoT design.

A useful verification sequence:
```bash
show authentication sessions interface gi1/0/10 details
show dot1x interface gi1/0/10 details
show mab all
show access-session interface gi1/0/10
```

**Fabric wireless onboarding**

Fabric wireless extends SD-Access to wireless clients through a wireless controller, typically a **Catalyst 9800 WLC**. Access points are not fabric nodes in the same sense as switches, but the controller integrates with the fabric so wireless clients can be placed into the correct virtual network and policy context.

High-level flow:
- APs join the Catalyst 9800 controller using standard wireless join behavior.
- The controller is integrated with the fabric site and fabric edge design.
- Wireless client traffic is mapped into the correct VN/VRF and policy context.
- The fabric edge or controller workflow ensures the client is placed into the intended overlay segment.

What to remember for exams:
- The controller is the fabric-aware component.
- APs are managed by the controller and are not usually treated as fabric nodes.
- Wireless clients still need identity, segmentation, and policy mapping.
- The design must align with the fabric site and border architecture.

Useful checks on the WLC:
```bash
show wireless summary
show ap summary
show wireless client summary
show fabric summary
show fabric ap summary
show fabric client summary
```

**Fabric site design and hierarchy**

Fabric design starts with the site hierarchy in Catalyst Center. Site boundaries matter because they define where fabric domains begin and end, where borders are placed, and how policy and routing are extended.

Key ideas:
- A **fabric site** is the administrative and operational boundary for a fabric domain.
- Border placement is usually at the edge of the fabric site where external connectivity is needed.
- Multiple fabric sites may exist in a larger enterprise, often with inter-site routing and shared services considerations.
- Site hierarchy influences provisioning, policy inheritance, and device assignment.

Practical design guidance:
- Keep the fabric domain aligned with a logical campus or building group when possible.
- Place border nodes where external routing, shared services, or inter-fabric connectivity is required.
- Avoid mixing unrelated routing domains inside a single fabric site unless the design explicitly calls for it.

**Border node types and use cases**

Border nodes are not all the same. In Cisco SD-Access terminology, you should distinguish between border behaviors and choose the right one for the design.

| Border type | Main use case | Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| External border | Connects the fabric to external networks such as WAN, Internet, or data center | Exchanges routes between fabric VRFs and external routing domains |
| Internal border | Connects fabric domains or fabric sites within the enterprise | Supports inter-fabric or intra-enterprise route exchange |
| Control-plane border | Extends control-plane reachability for endpoint registration in specific designs | Helps with LISP control-plane connectivity across boundaries |

Selection depends on:
- Whether the destination is another fabric, a shared services network, or a non-fabric external domain.
- Whether you need route exchange, route leaking, or control-plane extension.
- Whether the design is single-site, multi-site, or campus-to-data-center.

> **Important:** Border nodes can enforce policy at the fabric boundary and perform route leaking or route control, but they do not apply endpoint policy in the same way edge nodes do.

**Fabric extension and route leaking**

Fabric extension is the process of connecting the fabric to external networks or other fabric domains while preserving segmentation. The main challenge is to exchange routes without collapsing all traffic into a single flat network.

Common extension patterns:
- **L3 border** for routed connectivity and route exchange.
- **L2 border** when you must extend a Layer 2 segment for a specific use case.
- **Fusion router** when you need a dedicated routing device to connect fabric VRFs to external networks or shared services.
- **Shared services VRF** for services such as DNS, DHCP, AD, or management that must be reachable from multiple segments.

Route leaking concepts:
- Routes are exchanged between VRFs in a controlled way.
- Default routes may be propagated from the border to fabric VRFs or from the fabric to upstream networks depending on design.
- Route-maps and redistribution policies control what is imported or exported.
- Transit VRFs may be used to carry traffic between domains without exposing all prefixes.

A simplified route-control example:
```cisco
vrf definition CORP
 address-family ipv4
 exit-address-family

vrf definition SHARED-SERVICES
 address-family ipv4
 exit-address-family

route-map CORP-TO-EXT permit 10
 match ip address prefix-list CORP-PFX

ip prefix-list CORP-PFX seq 10 permit 10.10.0.0/16 le 24
```

A more realistic border routing example uses VRF-aware routing and controlled redistribution:
```cisco
vrf definition CORP
 rd 65000:10
 address-family ipv4
 exit-address-family

vrf definition EXTERNAL
 rd 65000:20
 address-family ipv4
 exit-address-family

interface GigabitEthernet1/0/1
 description To fabric underlay
 ip address 10.0.0.2 255.255.255.252

interface GigabitEthernet1/0/2
 description To external network
 vrf forwarding EXTERNAL
 ip address 172.16.0.2 255.255.255.252

router bgp 65000
 address-family ipv4 vrf CORP
  redistribute connected
  redistribute static route-map CORP-TO-EXT
 exit-address-family
 address-family ipv4 vrf EXTERNAL
  neighbor 172.16.0.1 remote-as 65100
  neighbor 172.16.0.1 activate
 exit-address-family
```

This example is intentionally simplified. In real SD-Access border deployments, Catalyst Center templates, fabric control-plane integration, and route-leak policy are more involved than a standalone VRF/BGP snippet.

**L3 border, L2 border, and fusion router**

These designs solve different problems.

- **L3 border**
  - Preferred in many campus designs.
  - Keeps routing explicit and scalable.
  - Works well for external connectivity and route control.

- **L2 border**
  - Used when a Layer 2 extension is required.
  - Can be useful for legacy dependencies or specific service attachment needs.
  - Should be used carefully because it can extend failure domains.

- **Fusion router**
  - Acts as a routing hub between fabric VRFs and external networks.
  - Useful when you want to keep the border function simpler or separate policy domains.
  - Often appears in designs with shared services or complex route control.

A practical rule:
- Use **L3 border** when you want clean segmentation and scalable route exchange.
- Use **L2 border** only when a business or technical requirement forces Layer 2 extension.
- Use a **fusion router** when the design needs a dedicated routing intermediary.

**Catalyst Center provisioning and fabric conversion**

Catalyst Center usually drives the fabric conversion workflow. The device is discovered, claimed, assigned to a site, and then provisioned with the correct role and fabric settings.

Typical steps:
1. Add the device to inventory.
2. Validate credentials, reachability, and image compliance.
3. Assign the device to the correct site.
4. Provision the device with the intended role.
5. Convert the device into an edge, border, or control-plane node as required.
6. Verify the fabric state and endpoint registration.

Useful Catalyst Center-style checks:
- Inventory status and sync state.
- Device compliance and image status.
- Site assignment and provisioning status.
- Fabric role assignment and node health.
- Assurance alarms related to onboarding or border connectivity.

**Verification commands for fabric and border health**

Verification should cover underlay, control-plane, and policy behavior. Do not rely on a single command.

Common switch-side commands:
```bash
show cdp neighbors
show lldp neighbors
show ip route
show ip ospf neighbor
show bgp ipv4 unicast summary
show vrf
show vrf detail
show running-config | section fabric
show running-config | section lisp
show lisp session
show lisp eid-table
show lisp map-cache
show authentication sessions
```

On some platforms and releases, additional SD-Access-specific commands may include:
```bash
show fabric summary
show fabric control-plane
show fabric border
show fabric edge
show fabric multicast
```

For border troubleshooting, focus on:
- Underlay reachability to neighbors and loopbacks.
- VRF route presence and next-hop resolution.
- Redistribution and route-map behavior.
- LISP control-plane health where applicable.
- MTU consistency across the path.

**Troubleshooting workflow**

When onboarding or extension fails, isolate the problem by layer.

1. **Check management reachability**
   - Confirm SSH, DNS, NTP, and AAA.
   - Verify the device is reachable from Catalyst Center.

2. **Check underlay health**
   - Confirm routing adjacencies.
   - Verify loopback reachability.
   - Confirm MTU and interface status.

3. **Check fabric role and provisioning**
   - Confirm the device is assigned to the correct site.
   - Verify the intended role is applied.
   - Check for image or template mismatches.

4. **Check control-plane and overlay state**
   - Validate LISP or fabric control-plane status where applicable.
   - Confirm endpoint registration and route exchange.

5. **Check border route exchange**
   - Verify VRF routes are present.
   - Confirm route redistribution and route-leak policy.
   - Check for missing default routes or filtered prefixes.

Example troubleshooting commands:
```bash
show logging | include LISP|VXLAN|BGP|AAA|AUTH
show ip route vrf CORP
show ip route vrf EXTERNAL
show bgp ipv4 unicast vrf CORP
show bgp ipv4 unicast vrf EXTERNAL
show lisp session
show lisp map-cache
show authentication sessions interface gi1/0/10 details
```

> **Important:** SD-Access uses LISP control-plane reachability and VXLAN data-plane encapsulation. Do not confuse this with generic EVPN/NVE operational models when studying verification commands.

**Practical design example**

Consider a campus with:
- One fabric site for the main building.
- Edge switches in access closets.
- A pair of border nodes at the campus edge.
- A Catalyst 9800 controller for wireless.
- Shared services in a separate VRF.

A workable design flow is:
- Onboard the access switches with PnP or claim them in Catalyst Center.
- Assign them to the campus site and convert them to edge nodes.
- Onboard the border pair and define external route exchange.
- Integrate the Catalyst 9800 controller for fabric wireless.
- Leak only the required shared-services routes into the user VRFs.
- Verify endpoint registration, route exchange, and client connectivity.

This design keeps segmentation intact while allowing users, wireless clients, and services to communicate where policy permits.

**Exam focus summary**

- Know the difference between **onboarding** and **fabric conversion**.
- Understand how **PnP**, **ZTP**, and manual claim/provisioning fit into Catalyst Center workflows.
- Distinguish **edge**, **border**, and **control-plane** roles from transit devices.
- Understand border subtypes and when to use **external**, **internal**, or **control-plane** border behavior.
- Be able to explain **route leaking**, shared services, and default route propagation at a high level.
- Remember that fabric wireless depends on controller integration, not APs becoming fabric nodes.
- Verify both underlay and overlay health before troubleshooting policy or endpoint issues.
