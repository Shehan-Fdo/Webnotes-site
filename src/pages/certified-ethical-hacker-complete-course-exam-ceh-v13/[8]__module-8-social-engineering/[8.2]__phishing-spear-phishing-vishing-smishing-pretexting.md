## **Course:** Certified Ethical Hacker Complete Course (Exam CEH v13)

**Overview**

Social engineering attacks exploit trust, urgency, curiosity, and authority rather than technical flaws. In CEH-style questions, you should be able to distinguish the delivery channel, the target selection method, and the attacker’s objective. The most common family is **phishing**, but exam questions often extend into **spear phishing**, **whaling**, **CEO fraud**, **vishing**, **smishing**, and **pretexting**.

A useful mental model is:

- **Channel**: email, voice, SMS, social media, web, or in-person
- **Targeting**: broad, role-based, or highly specific
- **Goal**: credential theft, malware delivery, payment fraud, data exfiltration, or access escalation

**Phishing Fundamentals**

Phishing is a deceptive message that impersonates a trusted entity to trick a user into revealing credentials, approving a payment, or opening malicious content. It usually relies on urgency or fear, such as account suspension, invoice problems, or security alerts.

Common phishing traits include:

- **Spoofed sender identity** using lookalike domains or display-name abuse
- **Credential harvesting** through fake login pages
- **Malware delivery** through attachments or links
- **Brand impersonation** of banks, cloud services, HR portals, or delivery companies
- **Time pressure** that reduces user verification

Typical phishing delivery mechanisms include:

- **Link-based phishing** that sends users to a fake portal
- **Attachment-based phishing** that delivers malware through documents, archives, or scripts
- **Clone phishing** that copies a legitimate email and swaps the original link or attachment for a malicious one
- **QR phishing** where a malicious QR code redirects to a fake site
- **Business email compromise** patterns that impersonate internal staff for invoice or payroll fraud

> A common exam trap is confusing phishing with pharming. **Phishing** manipulates the user through a fake message, while **pharming** manipulates name resolution or traffic routing so the user reaches a malicious site even when they type the correct address.

**Spear Phishing, Whaling, and CEO Fraud**

**Spear phishing** is targeted phishing aimed at a specific person, team, or organization. The attacker customizes the lure using job role, recent projects, internal terminology, or public information from social media and company websites.

Why it works:

- The message feels relevant and credible
- The attacker can reference real coworkers, vendors, or events
- The target is more likely to bypass normal skepticism

**Whaling** is spear phishing aimed at high-value executives or decision-makers. The goal is often access to sensitive data, wire transfers, or privileged approvals.

**CEO fraud** is a common whaling variant where the attacker impersonates a chief executive or another senior leader to pressure finance, HR, or legal staff into transferring money or releasing information. It often uses urgency, secrecy, and authority.

Indicators of spear phishing and whaling:

- **Personalized context** that still contains subtle inconsistencies
- **Unexpected requests** for payment, password reset, or document review
- **Reply-to mismatch** between the visible sender and the actual mailbox
- **Domain lookalikes** such as swapped letters, added hyphens, or extra subdomains
- **Conversation hijacking** where an attacker replies inside a compromised thread

**Vishing**

**Vishing** is voice phishing conducted over phone calls, VoIP, or callback scams. The attacker impersonates help desk staff, bank support, law enforcement, delivery services, or internal IT.

Common vishing goals:

- Obtain passwords, one-time codes, or MFA approvals
- Convince the victim to install remote access software
- Redirect payments or reset account recovery details
- Extract sensitive business or personal information

Practical indicators of vishing:

- **Caller ID spoofing** that displays a trusted number
- **Scripted urgency** and pressure to act immediately
- **Authority cues** such as badge numbers, case IDs, or fake escalation paths
- **Request for secrets** that legitimate support should not ask for
- **Push to move off-record** to a personal number or external chat app

A strong organizational defense is a **challenge-response verification script** for help desks and finance teams. For example, staff should verify identity using multiple independent factors, such as employee ID, manager callback, registered extension, or ticket number, before resetting credentials or approving sensitive actions.

Example help desk verification flow:

1. Ask the caller for a ticket number or case reference.
2. Call back using the number stored in the directory, not the number provided by the caller.
3. Verify at least two non-public attributes, such as department and manager name.
4. Deny password resets, MFA changes, or payment requests if verification fails.
5. Escalate suspicious calls to security operations.

**Smishing**

**Smishing** is phishing delivered by SMS, messaging apps, or mobile push-style text messages. It works well because mobile users often see shortened links, limited sender context, and urgent notifications on small screens.

Common smishing themes:

- Package delivery alerts
- Bank fraud warnings
- Voicemail or missed-call notifications
- Account verification prompts
- Fake two-factor authentication or login alerts

Smishing indicators:

- **Shortened URLs** that hide the destination
- **Urgent language** that pushes immediate action
- **Unexpected attachments** or app-install prompts
- **Phone-number spoofing** or unfamiliar sender IDs
- **Requests to reply with codes** or personal data

**Pretexting**

**Pretexting** is the creation of a believable story or role to manipulate a target into revealing information or granting access. Unlike generic phishing, pretexting often involves live interaction and a carefully maintained persona.

Examples of pretexts:

- A contractor needing temporary access
- A vendor confirming invoice details
- A new employee requesting onboarding help
- A security analyst verifying an incident
- A delivery driver asking for building access

Pretexting is effective because it uses social norms:

- People want to be helpful
- People defer to authority
- People avoid slowing down business processes
- People assume internal requests are legitimate

A good defense is to require **policy-based verification** rather than relying on politeness or familiarity. For example, a receptionist should not grant access based only on a convincing story; they should follow badge checks, visitor logs, escort rules, and manager approval.

**Pharming and Related Adjacent Concepts**

**Pharming** redirects users to malicious destinations without relying on a deceptive message. It can involve DNS poisoning, hosts file tampering, malicious router settings, or compromised recursive resolvers.

How it differs from phishing:

- **Phishing**: the user is tricked by a message
- **Pharming**: the user is silently redirected at the network or name-resolution layer

Why this matters for exams:

- A user may type the correct URL and still land on a fake site
- DNS inspection, resolver integrity, and certificate validation become important
- Browser warnings may appear if the attacker cannot present a valid certificate

**Attack Lifecycle and Delivery Patterns**

A typical social engineering attack follows a sequence:

1. Reconnaissance on the target’s role, contacts, and habits
2. Pretext creation using believable context
3. Delivery through email, voice, SMS, or messaging
4. Credential capture, malware execution, or payment diversion
5. Follow-up to maintain access or escalate the fraud

Common payloads and outcomes:

- **Credential harvesting** for email, VPN, or cloud portals
- **Malware installation** through documents, archives, or fake updates
- **Session theft** by capturing MFA codes or approving push prompts
- **Financial fraud** through invoice redirection or gift-card scams
- **Data theft** from shared drives, HR systems, or executive mailboxes

**Detection and Analysis**

When investigating suspected phishing, look for both message-level and infrastructure-level indicators.

Email header checks:

- Compare the visible sender with the `From`, `Reply-To`, and `Return-Path` fields
- Inspect `Received` lines for unexpected relays or geographic anomalies
- Check authentication results such as SPF, DKIM, and DMARC
- Look for display-name spoofing, where the name appears trusted but the domain is not

Example header clues:

```text
From: "Microsoft Security" <security-alerts@micros0ft-support.example>
Reply-To: helpdesk@attacker.example
Return-Path: bounce@attacker.example
Authentication-Results: spf=fail; dkim=none; dmarc=fail
```

URL and domain analysis:

- Watch for homoglyphs, extra words, and misleading subdomains
- Decode percent-encoding or punycode when needed
- Compare the visible link text with the actual destination
- Check for URL shorteners that hide the final domain

Examples of suspicious URLs:

```text
https://login.example.com.security-check.attacker.example
https://examp1e.com
https://xn--exmple-cua.com
https://bit.ly/3fakeLink
```

DNS and network checks:

```bash
dig +short example.com
nslookup example.com
whois example.com
curl -I https://example.com
```

Mobile and voice indicators:

- SMS sender IDs that do not match the claimed organization
- Missed-call scams that prompt a premium-rate callback
- VoIP calls with inconsistent caller ID or repeated number changes
- Requests to read back one-time passwords or approve MFA prompts

**Defensive Controls**

Effective defense combines technology, process, and user training.

Technical controls:

- **Email filtering** with attachment sandboxing and URL rewriting
- **SPF, DKIM, and DMARC** to reduce spoofed email acceptance
- **Web filtering** and DNS security to block known malicious domains
- **Endpoint protection** to detect malicious attachments and scripts
- **MFA** with phishing-resistant methods where possible
- **Conditional access** based on device health, location, and risk

Process controls:

- **Out-of-band verification** for payment changes and password resets
- **Least privilege** so a compromised account has limited impact
- **Dual approval** for wire transfers and sensitive HR actions
- **Call-back procedures** using trusted directory numbers
- **Incident reporting** workflows for suspicious messages and calls

User awareness controls:

- Teach users to inspect sender identity and destination URLs
- Train staff to verify urgent requests through a second channel
- Encourage reporting instead of deleting suspicious messages
- Reinforce that security teams do not ask for passwords or MFA codes

**Practical Verification Examples**

Use these checks during triage or awareness exercises:

- Confirm the sender domain against the organization’s approved domain list
- Hover over links or inspect them on mobile before tapping
- Verify invoice changes by calling a known vendor contact
- Check whether a “security alert” asks for credentials through email, which is a red flag
- Validate SMS claims by opening the official app or typing the known URL manually

Example of a safe verification workflow for a payment request:

1. Receive the request by email.
2. Pause before acting on urgency.
3. Call the requester using a number already stored in the company directory.
4. Confirm the request in a separate channel.
5. Require manager or finance approval before transfer.

**Exam Focus Points**

For CEH-style questions, remember these distinctions:

- **Phishing** is broad and message-based
- **Spear phishing** is targeted and personalized
- **Whaling** targets executives
- **CEO fraud** impersonates senior leadership for financial fraud
- **Vishing** uses voice calls
- **Smishing** uses SMS or messaging apps
- **Pretexting** relies on a fabricated story or role
- **Pharming** redirects traffic rather than tricking the user with a message

If a question mentions a fake support call, callback scam, or identity verification over the phone, the best answer is usually **vishing** or **pretexting** depending on whether the emphasis is the channel or the fabricated story. If it mentions a fake invoice from a “CEO” to finance, think **whaling** or **CEO fraud**. If the victim types the correct URL but still reaches a malicious site, think **pharming**.

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**

- Confusing **display-name spoofing** with a trusted sender domain
- Assuming a valid-looking login page is safe without checking the URL and certificate
- Treating all phone-based fraud as phishing instead of vishing or pretexting
- Ignoring attachment-based malware delivery in favor of link-only examples
- Forgetting that social engineering often combines multiple tactics in one campaign

**Hands-On Lab Ideas**

You can practice detection safely in a lab by creating mock messages and analyzing them.

Example email triage checklist:

```bash
# Inspect headers exported from a suspicious email
grep -Ei 'from:|reply-to:|return-path:|received:|authentication-results:' suspicious.eml

# Check the destination of a suspicious URL
curl -I 'https://example.test/login'
```

Example awareness test scenario:

- A fake finance email requests an urgent wire transfer
- The sender name matches the CEO, but the domain is external
- The message includes a shortened link and a PDF attachment
- The correct response is to verify through a known internal channel and report the message

**Summary**

These attacks succeed because they exploit human trust and process gaps. For the exam, focus on the delivery channel, the targeting style, and the attacker’s objective. Then match the scenario to the correct term and the most appropriate defense.
