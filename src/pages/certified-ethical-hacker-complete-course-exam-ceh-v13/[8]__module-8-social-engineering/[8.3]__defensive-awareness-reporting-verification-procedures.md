## **Course:** Certified Ethical Hacker Complete Course (Exam CEH v13)

**Defensive awareness in incident handling**

Defensive awareness is the discipline of noticing suspicious activity early, validating it carefully, and escalating it through the right channels without destroying evidence or leaking sensitive details. In CEH-style workflows, you are not only looking for compromise; you are also deciding whether a signal is merely an <abbr title="security event management signal">event</abbr>, a higher-confidence <abbr title="security alert">alert</abbr>, or a confirmed <abbr title="security incident">incident</abbr>.

A practical defensive workflow usually follows this pattern:

- Detect a signal from logs, EDR, SIEM, email gateway, IDS/IPS, or user report.
- Triage the signal to determine whether it is benign, suspicious, or confirmed malicious.
- Preserve evidence before making changes that could erase volatile data.
- Escalate according to severity, scope, and business impact.
- Document everything in a ticket, case record, or incident management system.

The goal is not to “prove” compromise immediately. The goal is to reduce uncertainty quickly while keeping the environment and evidence intact.

**Event, alert, and incident classification**

A common exam trap is treating every security signal as an incident. In practice, organizations distinguish between several levels of confidence and impact.

- **Event**: A logged occurrence with no immediate proof of malicious activity.
  - Example: A user logs in from a new device.
  - Example: A firewall denies an inbound connection.
- **Alert**: A rule, correlation, or analyst review indicates suspicious behavior that needs investigation.
  - Example: Multiple failed logins followed by success from an unusual geolocation.
  - Example: EDR flags a PowerShell process with encoded commands.
- **Incident**: A confirmed or highly probable security compromise that requires response actions.
  - Example: Malware execution with persistence and outbound command-and-control traffic.
  - Example: Unauthorized data access confirmed by logs and endpoint artifacts.

You distinguish them by asking:

- Is there evidence of malicious intent or impact?
- Is the behavior explainable by normal business activity?
- Do multiple sources support the same conclusion?
- Does the activity violate policy, confidentiality, integrity, or availability?

A useful triage mindset is to start broad and narrow down:

1. Identify the source and context of the signal.
2. Correlate with other telemetry.
3. Check whether the behavior matches a known baseline.
4. Decide whether to close, monitor, escalate, or declare an incident.

**Roles and responsibilities in incident response**

Incident handling works best when responsibilities are explicit. The exact titles vary by organization, but the functions are consistent.

- **SOC analyst**: Monitors alerts, performs first-line triage, enriches indicators, and escalates validated cases.
- **Incident handler / responder**: Leads containment, eradication, and recovery tasks; coordinates technical response.
- **Incident manager**: Owns coordination, prioritization, timelines, and stakeholder updates.
- **System owner**: Understands the affected system’s business purpose, dependencies, and acceptable downtime.
- **Legal**: Advises on regulatory obligations, evidence handling, retention, disclosure, and potential law enforcement involvement.
- **HR**: Becomes involved when employee misconduct, insider threat, or policy violations are suspected.
- **PR / communications**: Manages external messaging and public statements to avoid inconsistent or premature disclosure.
- **Forensics specialist**: Preserves and analyzes evidence when deeper investigation or legal defensibility is required.
- **IT operations / platform team**: Implements containment actions such as isolation, account resets, patching, or restoration.

A strong response process uses a clear escalation matrix so the analyst knows who to contact, when to contact them, and what information to include.

**Communication protocols and confidentiality**

Incident communications must be controlled. During an active investigation, you should share only what recipients need to know to perform their role. Unauthorized disclosure can tip off an attacker, create legal exposure, or spread misinformation.

Key communication rules:

- Use approved channels such as the incident ticket, secure chat, or designated bridge line.
- Limit distribution lists to the response team and approved stakeholders.
- Mark sensitive updates as confidential when required by policy.
- Avoid speculation; report observed facts, timestamps, and evidence references.
- Do not post incident details in public channels, general team chats, or informal messages.
- Coordinate external communication through legal and PR, not ad hoc technical staff.

> If the incident may involve regulated data, insider activity, or law enforcement interest, treat communications as sensitive from the start.

A practical update should include:

- What happened, in one sentence.
- What systems or users are affected.
- What is known, what is unconfirmed, and what is being investigated.
- What action has already been taken.
- What decision or approval is needed next.

**Initial reporting and evidence preservation**

The first responder must balance containment with preservation. If you isolate a host too aggressively, you may destroy volatile evidence such as active network connections, memory-resident malware, or running processes. If you do nothing, the attacker may continue to exfiltrate data or move laterally.

Preservation priorities:

- Capture volatile evidence first when feasible:
  - Running processes
  - Network connections
  - Logged-in users
  - Memory image
  - ARP and routing state
- Record the system state before changes:
  - Hostname
  - IP address
  - Logged-in user
  - Time source and timezone
  - Open sessions and services
- Use containment methods that minimize evidence loss:
  - Network quarantine via switch port, NAC, or EDR isolation
  - Account disablement when credential abuse is suspected
  - Firewall blocks for known malicious destinations

When to involve forensics:

- The incident may become a legal or disciplinary matter.
- You need chain-of-custody discipline.
- The evidence includes memory, disk images, or removable media.
- The root cause is unclear and deeper reconstruction is required.

A common mistake is rebooting a system before collecting volatile data. Rebooting can erase memory artifacts, active malware, and transient logs.

**Verification methods and artifact analysis**

Verification is the process of confirming whether a signal is real, what it affects, and how far it has spread. Good verification uses multiple artifacts rather than a single indicator.

Common verification methods include:

- **Packet capture review**: Inspect traffic for suspicious destinations, unusual protocols, beaconing patterns, or data exfiltration.
- **Endpoint timeline analysis**: Reconstruct process execution, file creation, registry changes, logons, and persistence events over time.
- **Sandbox detonation**: Execute a suspicious file or URL in an isolated environment to observe behavior such as process spawning, registry edits, network callbacks, or file drops.
- **Email header analysis**: Examine sender path, Received headers, SPF/DKIM/DMARC results, reply-to mismatches, and suspicious routing.
- **Log correlation**: Compare endpoint, authentication, DNS, proxy, firewall, and application logs to confirm the sequence of events.
- **Memory analysis**: Look for injected code, suspicious modules, network sockets, or hidden processes.
- **File reputation and hash checking**: Compare hashes against threat intelligence or internal allowlists.

Useful artifacts to inspect:

- DNS queries for newly registered or rare domains
- Proxy logs for unusual user agents or large outbound transfers
- Authentication logs for impossible travel or repeated failures
- EDR telemetry for suspicious parent-child process chains
- Registry or startup entries for persistence
- Scheduled tasks, services, cron jobs, or launch agents
- Email attachments, URLs, and embedded scripts

Example endpoint triage commands on Windows:

```powershell
Get-Process | Sort-Object CPU -Descending | Select-Object -First 10
netstat -ano
tasklist /v
wevtutil qe Security /c:20 /f:text
```

Example Linux triage commands:

```bash
ps auxf
ss -tulpn
lsof -i
journalctl -xe --no-pager
last -a | head
```

Example hash and file inspection:

```bash
sha256sum suspicious.bin
file suspicious.bin
strings -a suspicious.bin | head -50
```

**False positives, false negatives, and detection quality**

Verification is not only about confirming alerts; it is also about improving detection quality. A false positive is benign activity that looks malicious. A false negative is malicious activity that the control fails to detect.

Handling false positives:

- Document why the alert was benign.
- Tune the rule, threshold, or exception list if the pattern is expected.
- Preserve the rationale so future analysts do not repeat the same investigation.

Handling false negatives:

- Compare the incident timeline against what the control should have seen.
- Identify missing telemetry, disabled logging, or blind spots.
- Add detections for missed indicators such as parent-child process chains, rare outbound domains, or suspicious authentication patterns.
- Validate that sensors, parsers, and forwarding pipelines are functioning correctly.

Improving detection quality often means combining signals. For example, a single failed login may be normal, but failed logins plus new device enrollment plus unusual geolocation may justify escalation.

**Reporting formats, ticket fields, and escalation matrices**

Organizations usually record incidents in a ticketing or case management system. The exact format varies, but the fields are often similar.

Typical incident ticket fields:

- Ticket ID
- Date and time detected
- Reporter or detection source
- Affected asset, user, or application
- Incident category
- Severity or priority
- Status
- Summary of observed behavior
- Indicators of compromise
- Actions taken
- Evidence references
- Owner and assignee
- Escalation path
- Business impact
- Containment status
- Recovery status
- Closure notes and lessons learned

A severity matrix helps standardize response. For example:

| Severity | Typical impact | Example response |
|---|---|---|
| Low | Limited scope, no confirmed compromise | Monitor, document, and tune detection |
| Medium | Suspicious activity with possible impact | Investigate, collect evidence, notify owner |
| High | Confirmed compromise or sensitive system affected | Contain, escalate, coordinate response |
| Critical | Major outage, active exfiltration, or regulated data exposure | Immediate executive and legal escalation |

An escalation matrix defines who gets notified based on severity, asset type, and business impact. For example:

- Low: SOC queue and system owner
- Medium: SOC analyst, incident handler, system owner
- High: Incident manager, system owner, legal if needed
- Critical: Incident manager, legal, PR, executive leadership, forensics

**Playbooks and runbooks**

Playbooks and runbooks turn response knowledge into repeatable action. A **playbook** describes the response strategy for a scenario such as phishing, ransomware, or credential theft. A **runbook** gives the step-by-step operational tasks to execute that strategy.

Why they matter:

- They reduce hesitation during high-pressure incidents.
- They standardize evidence collection and escalation.
- They help new analysts act consistently.
- They support auditability and post-incident review.

A phishing playbook may include:

1. Confirm the message is malicious using headers, URLs, and attachment analysis.
2. Search for other recipients and delivery success.
3. Quarantine the message and block indicators.
4. Reset credentials if a user interacted with the payload.
5. Preserve evidence and update the incident record.

A ransomware runbook may include:

1. Isolate affected endpoints.
2. Preserve memory and disk evidence where possible.
3. Disable compromised accounts.
4. Block known indicators at network and email controls.
5. Coordinate restoration from clean backups.

**Practical triage workflow**

A disciplined triage workflow helps you move from signal to decision without skipping evidence preservation.

1. Receive the alert or user report.
2. Identify the asset, user, time window, and detection source.
3. Check whether the activity matches expected behavior.
4. Correlate with logs, endpoint telemetry, and network data.
5. Preserve volatile evidence if compromise is plausible.
6. Classify the case as event, alert, or incident.
7. Escalate using the severity matrix and playbook.
8. Record actions, timestamps, and evidence references.
9. Continue monitoring for recurrence or lateral movement.

Example triage notes in a ticket:

```text
Summary: EDR detected encoded PowerShell on WS-1042.
Observed: Parent process winword.exe spawned powershell.exe with -enc argument.
Correlated: DNS lookup to rare domain and outbound HTTPS to new IP.
Action: Host isolated via EDR; memory capture requested; user account disabled pending review.
Status: Escalated to incident handler as High severity.
```

**Real-world reporting and escalation example**

A user reports that their mailbox sent messages they did not write. The SOC analyst should not immediately assume account takeover, but should verify quickly.

Possible checks:

- Review email headers for spoofing or forwarding abuse.
- Check sign-in logs for impossible travel or unfamiliar devices.
- Look for mailbox rules that auto-forward or hide messages.
- Search for similar messages sent to other recipients.
- Confirm whether the user reused credentials elsewhere.

If evidence supports compromise, the analyst escalates and documents:

- Affected account and mailbox
- Time of suspicious access
- Indicators such as IP, user agent, and rule changes
- Containment actions such as password reset and session revocation
- Notification path to the account owner, incident manager, and legal if data exposure is possible

**Evidence handling and chain of custody**

When evidence may be used for disciplinary, legal, or regulatory purposes, you need a defensible chain of custody. That means you can show who collected the evidence, when, where, how, and who handled it afterward.

Good evidence handling includes:

- Labeling evidence with unique identifiers
- Recording collection time and collector identity
- Storing evidence in approved secure locations
- Limiting access to authorized personnel
- Hashing files before and after transfer when appropriate
- Documenting every handoff

Common evidence types:

- Disk images
- Memory captures
- Log exports
- Packet captures
- Email messages and headers
- Screenshots of relevant system state
- Cloud audit logs and API activity records

**How verification maps to response procedures**

Verification is not a separate activity from response; it feeds the response workflow. The result of verification determines whether you close the case, monitor it, or activate a playbook.

- If the signal is a benign event, you document and close it.
- If the signal is an alert with unresolved risk, you continue investigation and monitoring.
- If the signal is a confirmed incident, you execute the appropriate runbook and escalate.

This mapping matters because it prevents overreaction to noise and underreaction to real compromise. It also ensures that containment, eradication, and recovery happen in the right order.

**Quick reference for CEH-style exam thinking**

- Distinguish **event**, **alert**, and **incident** before escalating.
- Preserve volatile evidence before making disruptive changes.
- Use multiple artifacts to verify compromise.
- Follow approved communication channels and confidentiality rules.
- Escalate according to severity, business impact, and playbooks.
- Document actions, timestamps, and evidence references in the ticket.
- Involve legal, HR, PR, and forensics when the situation requires it.
- Improve detections after the incident by analyzing false positives and false negatives.
