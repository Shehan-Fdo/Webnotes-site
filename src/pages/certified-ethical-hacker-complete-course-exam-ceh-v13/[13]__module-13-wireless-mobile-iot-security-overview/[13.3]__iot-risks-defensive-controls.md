## **Course:** Certified Ethical Hacker Complete Course (Exam CEH v13)

**IoT security overview**

<abbr title="Internet of Things">IoT</abbr> systems combine embedded devices, sensors, gateways, mobile apps, cloud services, and APIs. The security challenge is not just the device itself; it is the full ecosystem. A weak link in firmware, wireless pairing, cloud authorization, or a companion app can expose the entire deployment.

IoT risk is amplified by scale and diversity:
- Devices often ship with minimal local controls and limited logging.
- Many deployments prioritize availability and convenience over hardening.
- Long device lifecycles make patching, certificate rotation, and inventory management difficult.
- Physical access is often realistic, especially in homes, retail, healthcare, industrial, and smart-building environments.

A useful mental model is to assess IoT across four layers:
- **Device layer**: firmware, storage, debug ports, sensors, local services.
- **Network layer**: Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, Zigbee, Z-Wave, cellular, MQTT, CoAP, AMQP.
- **Application layer**: mobile apps, web portals, APIs, cloud dashboards.
- **Operational layer**: provisioning, updates, monitoring, incident response, vendor support.

**Common IoT attack surface**

IoT devices are frequently attacked through exposed services, weak trust boundaries, and poor lifecycle controls rather than only through password guessing.

- **Default credentials** remain common, especially on consumer and small-business devices.
- **Open management interfaces** such as Telnet, SSH, HTTP admin panels, UPnP, or vendor-specific ports can expose control functions.
- **Insecure companion apps** may leak tokens, use weak local storage, or trust invalid certificates.
- **Cloud/API abuse** can allow unauthorized device control, telemetry access, or account takeover if authorization is weak.
- **Insecure pairing and onboarding** can let an attacker join a device before it is properly enrolled.
- **Exposed debug ports** such as UART, JTAG, or SWD can reveal firmware, secrets, or shell access.
- **Insecure update channels** can allow malicious firmware or downgrade attacks.

> If a device is reachable, manageable, or updateable, it is part of the attack surface.

**Typical IoT vulnerabilities**

These weaknesses are common in CEH-relevant scenarios and often appear together.

- **Insecure data storage**
  - Sensitive data may be stored in plaintext on flash, SD cards, or local databases.
  - Tokens, Wi-Fi credentials, and telemetry caches may be recoverable from the device or companion app.
- **Lack of encryption at rest**
  - Logs, recordings, health data, and configuration files may remain readable after device theft or teardown.
- **Poor authorization**
  - APIs may authenticate users but fail to enforce object-level access control.
  - One tenant may access another tenant’s devices or telemetry.
- **Insecure OTA update mechanisms**
  - Over-the-air updates may lack signature verification, anti-rollback protection, or secure transport.
  - Attackers may inject malicious firmware or force vulnerable versions.
- **Hardcoded backdoors**
  - Vendor debug accounts, hidden service endpoints, or undocumented commands may bypass normal access control.
- **Weak input validation**
  - Web interfaces and APIs may be vulnerable to injection, command execution, or buffer overflow in embedded code.
- **Insecure defaults**
  - Services may ship enabled by default, with broad permissions and no rate limiting.
- **Insufficient logging**
  - Devices may not record authentication failures, configuration changes, or firmware events.

**IoT-specific attack vectors**

IoT attacks often target the ecosystem around the device rather than the device alone.

- **Insecure web/mobile companion apps**
  - Mobile apps may store bearer tokens in plaintext, use weak local encryption, or expose secrets in logs.
  - Web dashboards may rely on predictable identifiers or weak session handling.
  - Reverse engineering can reveal API endpoints, hidden functions, and hardcoded keys.
- **Exposed management interfaces**
  - Admin panels, SSH, Telnet, SNMP, RTSP, and vendor RPC services may be reachable from untrusted networks.
  - Remote management features may be enabled without VPN, allowlist, or MFA.
- **Cloud/API abuse**
  - Attackers may enumerate device IDs, abuse weak API keys, or exploit missing authorization checks.
  - Excessive permissions in cloud IAM (Identity and Access Management) can expose fleets of devices.
- **Default/open services**
  - Services such as HTTP, Telnet, FTP, SMB, UPnP, or multicast discovery may be left open.
  - Discovery protocols can reveal device type, firmware version, and network topology.
- **Wireless abuse**
  - Weak pairing, replayable commands, or poor key management can affect Bluetooth Low Energy (<abbr title="Bluetooth Low Energy">BLE</abbr>), Zigbee, Z-Wave, and Wi-Fi devices.
- **Physical compromise**
  - Attackers may use teardown, port probing, or fault injection to extract secrets or bypass protections.

**Protocol considerations and secure configuration**

Different IoT protocols have different security expectations. In exams and real deployments, focus on authentication, encryption, authorization, and broker or gateway hardening.

**MQTT**

<abbr title="Message Queuing Telemetry Transport">MQTT</abbr> is lightweight and common for telemetry. It is secure only when transport and authorization are configured correctly.

- Use **TLS** for broker connections, typically on port `8883`.
- Require client certificates or strong token-based authentication.
- Restrict publish/subscribe permissions by topic.
- Disable anonymous access.
- Enforce per-device credentials and rotate them regularly.
- Apply rate limits and connection limits to reduce abuse.

Example broker hardening with Mosquitto:
```conf
listener 8883
protocol mqtt

allow_anonymous false
password_file /etc/mosquitto/passwd
acl_file /etc/mosquitto/acl

cafile /etc/mosquitto/ca.crt
certfile /etc/mosquitto/server.crt
keyfile /etc/mosquitto/server.key
require_certificate true
tls_version tlsv1.2
```

Example ACL:
```conf
user device01
topic readwrite sensors/device01/#
topic read sensors/common/status

user device02
topic readwrite sensors/device02/#
```

Useful validation commands:
```bash
mosquitto_sub -h broker.example.com -p 8883 --cafile ca.crt -u device01 -P 'StrongPass!' -t 'sensors/device01/#' -v
mosquitto_pub -h broker.example.com -p 8883 --cafile ca.crt -u device01 -P 'StrongPass!' -t 'sensors/device01/temp' -m '22.4'
```

**CoAP**

<abbr title="Constrained Application Protocol">CoAP</abbr> is common in constrained environments and often runs over UDP. Security typically relies on <abbr title="Datagram Transport Layer Security">DTLS</abbr> or OSCORE.

- Use DTLS or <abbr title="Object Security for Constrained RESTful Environments">OSCORE</abbr> for message protection.
- Restrict observe relationships and resource exposure.
- Validate tokens and authorization for each resource.
- Avoid exposing management resources without authentication.
- Consider gateway termination if end-device crypto is too limited.

Example secure CoAP client test:
```bash
coap-client -m get coaps://sensor.example.com/.well-known/core --identity device01 --key secretkey
```

**AMQP**

<abbr title="Advanced Message Queuing Protocol">AMQP</abbr> is often used in backend integration and cloud messaging.

- Use TLS on the broker endpoint.
- Require strong authentication, preferably certificate-based or managed identity.
- Restrict virtual host and queue permissions.
- Separate device ingestion queues from administrative queues.
- Monitor for unusual queue depth, publish rates, and consumer failures.

Example RabbitMQ TLS and access pattern:
```bash
rabbitmqctl add_user device01 StrongPass!
rabbitmqctl set_permissions -p /iot device01 "^telemetry\." "^telemetry\." "^telemetry\."
```

**Other relevant protocols**

- **Zigbee**
  - Secure joining, unique network keys, and controlled trust center behavior matter.
  - Weak commissioning can allow rogue devices into the mesh.
- **Z-Wave**
  - Use secure inclusion and avoid legacy insecure pairing modes.
- **BLE**
  - Prefer authenticated pairing and avoid “Just Works” for sensitive functions.
  - Watch for replay, downgrade, and weak bonding storage.
- **RFID/NFC**
  - Protect against cloning, relay attacks, and unauthorized reads where applicable.
- **Wi-Fi**
  - Use WPA2-Enterprise or WPA3 where possible.
  - Segment IoT SSIDs from corporate and guest networks.

**Defensive controls for IoT**

A strong IoT defense uses layered controls because no single control is sufficient.

- **Asset inventory**
  - Track device model, firmware, serial number, owner, location, and network segment.
  - Unknown devices should be treated as suspicious until validated.
- **Network segmentation**
  - Place IoT devices on dedicated VLANs or microsegments.
  - Restrict east-west traffic and limit outbound internet access.
- **Zero trust principles**
  - Authenticate and authorize every connection.
  - Do not trust internal network location alone.
- **Least privilege**
  - Give devices only the ports, topics, APIs, and cloud permissions they need.
- **Secure boot**
  - Verify bootloader and firmware integrity at startup.
- **Signed firmware**
  - Reject unsigned or tampered images.
- **Certificate management**
  - Use unique device identities and rotate certificates before expiration.
- **Patch and vulnerability management**
  - Maintain a firmware update schedule and track vendor advisories.
- **Monitoring**
  - Collect logs from brokers, gateways, cloud services, and network sensors.
- **Application allowlisting**
  - Restrict which binaries, scripts, or services can run on gateways and edge nodes.
- **Data minimization**
  - Collect only necessary telemetry and retain it only as long as needed.

**Secure boot and firmware integrity**

Secure boot helps ensure that only trusted code runs on the device.

- The boot chain should verify each stage before execution.
- Root of trust may be in hardware, a secure element, or a trusted bootloader.
- Firmware images should be signed with a private key held offline or in a hardened build pipeline.
- Anti-rollback controls prevent attackers from reinstalling older vulnerable firmware.

Example firmware signature verification workflow:
```bash
openssl dgst -sha256 -verify vendor_pubkey.pem -signature firmware.sig firmware.bin
```

Practical workflow:
1. Verify the vendor signature before deployment.
2. Compare the firmware hash against the release manifest.
3. Confirm the version is not older than the approved baseline.
4. Stage the update on a test device before fleet rollout.
5. Record the update result and preserve logs.

**Secure OTA update workflow**

Over-the-air updates are a major risk if they are not authenticated and validated.

- Use TLS for transport security.
- Sign update packages and verify signatures on-device.
- Bind updates to a trusted vendor certificate or public key.
- Require integrity checks before installation.
- Support rollback only to approved versions.
- Log update attempts, failures, and version changes.

Example update validation logic:
```pseudo
if verify_tls_connection() and verify_signature(update_package) and
   hash_matches_manifest(update_package) and
   version >= minimum_allowed_version:
    install(update_package)
else:
    reject(update_package)
```

**Privacy and data leakage**

IoT telemetry often contains more than operational data. It can reveal behavior, routines, location, health status, occupancy, or industrial processes.

- **Telemetry leakage**
  - Sensor readings can expose when people are home, when equipment is active, or how a facility operates.
- **Metadata exposure**
  - Device names, MAC addresses, SSIDs, and topic names can reveal identity and environment details.
- **Cloud retention risk**
  - Long retention periods increase the impact of account compromise or insider misuse.
- **Third-party sharing**
  - Analytics, support, and integration services may receive more data than necessary.

Defensive measures:
- Minimize collection and retention.
- Encrypt data in transit and at rest.
- Mask or pseudonymize identifiers where possible.
- Review vendor privacy settings and data-sharing defaults.
- Separate operational telemetry from personally identifiable information (<abbr title="Personally Identifiable Information">PII</abbr>).

**Malware and botnets**

IoT malware is a major risk because many devices are always on, internet-connected, and poorly maintained. Botnets often recruit devices through default credentials, exposed services, or unpatched vulnerabilities.

Common patterns:
- **Scanning and exploitation**
  - Malware scans for Telnet, SSH, HTTP admin panels, and known CVEs.
- **Persistence**
  - It may modify startup scripts, firmware, or configuration files.
- **Command and control**
  - Infected devices receive instructions from a remote server or peer network.
- **Abuse**
  - Devices may be used for distributed denial-of-service (<abbr title="Distributed Denial of Service">DDoS</abbr>), proxying, spam, or credential attacks.

Defensive controls:
- Block unnecessary inbound access from the internet.
- Disable Telnet and other legacy services.
- Use egress filtering to reduce command-and-control reachability.
- Monitor for unusual DNS, outbound connections, CPU spikes, or process changes.
- Compare firmware hashes and startup configuration against a known-good baseline.
- Use network intrusion detection to flag scanning, beaconing, and anomalous protocol use.

Example detection ideas:
```bash
# Look for suspicious outbound connections from a gateway
ss -tunap

# Check for unexpected listening services
ss -lntup

# Review recent auth and service events
journalctl -u mosquitto --since "24 hours ago"
```

**Physical security controls**

Physical access can defeat many logical controls, so operational environments need tamper resistance and tamper evidence.

- **Secure enclosure**
  - Use locked cabinets, tamper-resistant screws, and protected mounting.
- **Tamper detection**
  - Use switches, seals, or sensors that trigger alerts when a case is opened.
- **Debug port protection**
  - Disable or fuse off JTAG, UART, and SWD in production where possible.
  - Cover ports or place them behind internal access panels.
- **Environmental controls**
  - Protect devices from power cycling, temperature abuse, and unauthorized resets.
- **Asset labeling**
  - Track serial numbers and physical location to detect swaps or theft.

> A device with exposed debug access is often only one teardown away from compromise.

**Monitoring and detection**

IoT monitoring must combine network, host, and cloud visibility.

- **Network indicators**
  - Unexpected DNS lookups, beaconing, scanning, or protocol misuse.
- **Device indicators**
  - Reboots, firmware changes, config drift, or disabled security settings.
- **Cloud indicators**
  - New API keys, unusual geolocation, mass device commands, or privilege changes.
- **Broker indicators**
  - Topic flooding, unauthorized subscriptions, retained message abuse, or connection storms.

Useful controls:
- NetFlow or packet capture on IoT segments.
- IDS/IPS signatures for known IoT malware and scanning behavior.
- Broker audit logs for publish/subscribe anomalies.
- Cloud audit logs for IAM changes and API access.
- Baseline comparisons for firmware, services, and configuration.

**Incident response for IoT compromise**

IoT incident response must preserve evidence while limiting spread and restoring service safely.

1. **Identify and scope**
   - Determine which devices, accounts, topics, APIs, and cloud tenants are affected.
   - Check whether the compromise is local, network-based, or cloud-based.
2. **Contain**
   - Isolate the device or segment it from critical systems.
   - Revoke tokens, rotate credentials, and disable exposed management interfaces.
   - Block malicious outbound destinations at the firewall or DNS layer.
3. **Preserve evidence**
   - Capture logs from the device, gateway, broker, mobile app backend, and cloud provider.
   - Record timestamps, firmware versions, hashes, and network connections.
   - If feasible, image flash storage or preserve a forensic copy before rebooting.
4. **Coordinate with vendors and cloud providers**
   - Request firmware advisories, known indicators of compromise, and account audit data.
   - Confirm whether the issue is a product defect, misconfiguration, or active intrusion.
5. **Eradicate**
   - Remove malicious firmware or configuration changes.
   - Reflash from a trusted image and re-enroll the device.
   - Replace compromised keys and certificates.
6. **Recover**
   - Restore service gradually and monitor for reinfection.
   - Validate telemetry, access control, and update channels.
7. **Lessons learned**
   - Update hardening baselines, segmentation rules, and procurement requirements.

Practical preservation checklist:
- Export broker logs and cloud audit logs.
- Save `syslog`, `journalctl`, and application logs.
- Capture `tcpdump` or SPAN traffic if possible.
- Record firmware hash and device serial number.
- Photograph physical state if tampering is suspected.
- Document who handled the device and when.

Example collection commands:
```bash
journalctl --since "2026-04-25" > device-journal.log
tcpdump -i eth0 -w iot-segment.pcap
sha256sum firmware.bin > firmware.sha256
```

**Hardening checklist**

Use this as a practical baseline for CEH-style scenarios and real deployments.

- Change default credentials and disable unused accounts.
- Disable Telnet, UPnP, and unnecessary services.
- Enforce TLS/DTLS for device-to-cloud communication.
- Use unique per-device credentials and certificates.
- Restrict MQTT topics, CoAP resources, and AMQP queues.
- Segment devices from user and server networks.
- Sign and verify firmware updates.
- Protect debug ports and physical access.
- Log authentication, configuration, and update events.
- Monitor for botnet behavior, scanning, and beaconing.
- Review privacy settings and minimize telemetry retention.

**Exam focus reminders**

- IoT risk is usually a combination of weak device security, weak network controls, and weak cloud authorization.
- Default credentials are important, but they are only one part of the attack surface.
- Be ready to explain how insecure apps, exposed interfaces, cloud APIs, and update mechanisms create compromise paths.
- Know the difference between confidentiality, integrity, and availability impacts in telemetry-heavy environments.
- Defensive controls should be layered: secure boot, signed updates, segmentation, monitoring, and incident response.
