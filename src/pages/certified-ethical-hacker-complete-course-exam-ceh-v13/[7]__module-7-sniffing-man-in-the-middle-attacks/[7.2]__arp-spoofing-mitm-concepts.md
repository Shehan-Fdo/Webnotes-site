## **Course:** Certified Ethical Hacker Complete Course (Exam CEH v13)

**ARP fundamentals and why spoofing works**

<abbr title="Address Resolution Protocol">ARP</abbr> maps IPv4 addresses to <abbr title="Media Access Control">MAC</abbr> addresses on a local network. Hosts use ARP because Ethernet frames need a destination MAC address, while applications usually know only the destination IP address.

ARP is a stateless protocol in the sense that a host often accepts ARP replies without strong validation. That design choice makes ARP efficient on trusted local networks, but it also creates an attack surface for cache poisoning.

- **ARP request**: A broadcast asking, “Who has this IP?”
- **ARP reply**: A unicast or broadcast response stating, “This IP is at this MAC.”
- **ARP cache**: A temporary table that stores IP-to-MAC mappings.
- **ARP poisoning**: Injecting false mappings into a victim’s cache.

A typical local communication flow is:

1. The host checks its ARP cache for the next-hop IP.
2. If no entry exists, it broadcasts an ARP request.
3. The legitimate device replies with its MAC address.
4. The host stores the mapping for a limited time.

**ARP cache aging and refresh behavior**

ARP entries do not last forever. Operating systems age them out after a timeout, and they may refresh them when traffic continues or when a new ARP reply is received. This matters for poisoning because the attacker must keep sending forged replies to maintain the false mapping.

- **Aging** reduces stale entries and limits how long a poisoned entry persists without reinforcement.
- **Refresh traffic** can overwrite a poisoned entry if the legitimate device responds and the host accepts the new mapping.
- **Persistence** of poisoning usually depends on repeated spoofed ARP replies at intervals shorter than the cache timeout.

In practice, if the attacker stops sending forged replies, the victim may recover naturally as the cache ages out or is refreshed by legitimate ARP traffic.

**How ARP spoofing enables man-in-the-middle**

<abbr title="Man-in-the-Middle">MITM</abbr> attacks place the attacker between two communicating parties so traffic can be observed, modified, or relayed. ARP spoofing is a common MITM technique on switched Ethernet networks because it can redirect traffic at Layer 2 without changing IP routing.

A common pattern is to poison both sides:

- The victim is told the gateway’s IP belongs to the attacker’s MAC.
- The gateway is told the victim’s IP belongs to the attacker’s MAC.

This causes both directions of traffic to flow through the attacker, who then forwards packets to avoid breaking connectivity.

```text
Victim  <----->  Attacker  <----->  Gateway
   IP A              MAC X            IP G
```

If the attacker does not forward traffic, the result is usually a denial of service rather than a stealthy MITM.

**Gratuitous ARP and abuse cases**

A **gratuitous ARP** is an ARP announcement sent without being asked, often to announce a host’s own IP-to-MAC mapping or to detect duplicate IP addresses. It is legitimate in many environments, especially after interface changes, failover, or IP takeover events.

Attackers commonly abuse gratuitous ARP because many hosts and switches will accept or propagate the announcement quickly.

- It can rapidly poison caches by advertising a false mapping.
- It can help an attacker “refresh” poisoned entries before they age out.
- It is often used in bursts to keep victims synchronized to the attacker’s MAC.

Because gratuitous ARP is normal in some operational scenarios, defenders should correlate it with other signals rather than treating every announcement as malicious.

**Proxy ARP and why it matters**

With **proxy ARP**, a router or Layer 3 device answers ARP requests on behalf of another host or subnet. This can make remote systems appear locally reachable even though they are not on the same Layer 2 segment.

Proxy ARP can be confused with spoofing because both involve a device answering ARP requests for an IP it does not “own” in the usual sense. The difference is intent and configuration:

- **Proxy ARP** is a legitimate routing behavior used to simplify connectivity in some networks.
- **ARP spoofing** is unauthorized and aims to redirect traffic for interception or disruption.

Proxy ARP can also complicate detection because a valid proxy response may look similar to a poisoned mapping if you only inspect ARP tables. In CEH-style questions, remember that routers normally prevent direct ARP interaction across subnets, but proxy ARP is an exception that can blur that boundary.

**Layer 2 scope, VLAN boundaries, and router limits**

ARP spoofing is primarily effective inside the same <abbr title="Layer 2">L2</abbr> broadcast domain, usually a single switch segment or VLAN. ARP is not routed across subnets, so a host on one subnet cannot normally poison ARP caches on another subnet through a router alone.

Key implications:

- **Switches** forward broadcasts within the VLAN, allowing ARP requests and replies to reach local hosts.
- **Routers** separate broadcast domains, so they stop ordinary ARP traffic from crossing subnet boundaries.
- **VLANs** create logical L2 boundaries; an attacker generally needs access to the same VLAN as the target.
- **Cross-subnet attacks** usually require another foothold, such as compromised internal access, misconfiguration, proxy ARP, or a separate attack path.

This is why ARP spoofing is a local-network attack, not an Internet-wide one.

**Switch behavior and why the attack works on switched networks**

Switches learn MAC addresses by observing source MACs on incoming frames and building a forwarding table. They do not inherently validate whether an ARP reply is truthful. If the attacker is on the same switch and VLAN, the switch will forward the forged frames like any other traffic.

Important limitations and opportunities:

- A switch does not normally verify IP-to-MAC truthfulness at Layer 2.
- Port security can limit the number of MAC addresses learned on a port.
- Dynamic ARP Inspection (DAI) can validate ARP packets against trusted bindings.
- DHCP snooping can provide those trusted bindings for DAI.

**CEH comparison: ARP spoofing vs DNS spoofing vs MAC flooding**

These attacks are often tested together because they all can support interception, redirection, or denial of service, but they operate at different layers.

| Attack | Primary layer | Main effect | Typical goal | Key limitation |
|---|---:|---|---|---|
| ARP spoofing | Layer 2 | Poison IP-to-MAC mappings | MITM or local redirection | Usually limited to same VLAN |
| DNS spoofing | Layer 7 | Poison name resolution | Redirect users to fake hosts | Depends on DNS trust path |
| MAC flooding | Layer 2 | Overfill switch CAM table | Force flooding of frames | Often causes broad noise/DoS |

Practical distinctions:

- **ARP spoofing** targets local host-to-host or host-to-gateway traffic.
- **DNS spoofing** targets how names resolve to IPs, often affecting broader application access.
- **MAC flooding** tries to make a switch behave like a hub by exhausting its Content Addressable Memory (CAM) table, which can expose traffic to sniffing but is noisy and easier to detect.

In exam scenarios, ARP spoofing is the most direct path to a stealthy local MITM, while MAC flooding is more of a switch-table exhaustion technique and DNS spoofing is a name-resolution attack.

**Attack workflow and traffic relay**

A successful ARP MITM usually follows this sequence:

1. Identify the victim, gateway, and local subnet.
2. Poison the victim’s ARP cache with the attacker’s MAC for the gateway IP.
3. Poison the gateway’s ARP cache with the attacker’s MAC for the victim IP.
4. Enable IP forwarding so packets continue to flow.
5. Optionally capture, modify, or filter traffic.
6. Maintain poisoning with repeated ARP replies.

On Linux, packet forwarding can be enabled temporarily:

```bash
sudo sysctl -w net.ipv4.ip_forward=1
```

To make it persistent on many Linux systems:

```bash
echo 'net.ipv4.ip_forward=1' | sudo tee -a /etc/sysctl.conf
sudo sysctl -p
```

A common lab tool is `arpspoof` from the `dsniff` suite:

```bash
sudo arpspoof -i eth0 -t 192.168.1.50 192.168.1.1
sudo arpspoof -i eth0 -t 192.168.1.1 192.168.1.50
```

This sends forged ARP replies to both targets so traffic is redirected through the attacker.

**Verification and monitoring commands**

You should know how to inspect ARP state and confirm whether poisoning is occurring.

```bash
ip neigh show
arp -a
```

Useful Linux checks:

```bash
ip addr show
ip route show
tcpdump -i eth0 arp
tcpdump -i eth0 host 192.168.1.50
```

Windows checks:

```powershell
arp -a
Get-NetNeighbor
route print
```

What to look for:

- The gateway IP mapping changes to an unexpected MAC.
- Multiple IPs map to the same attacker MAC.
- ARP replies appear frequently from a host that should not be answering for that IP.
- Traffic continues only if forwarding is enabled on the attacker.

**Defensive controls and hardening**

The strongest defenses combine switch enforcement, host validation, and monitoring.

- **Static ARP entries**: Useful for critical systems, but hard to scale and maintain.
- **Dynamic ARP Inspection (DAI)**: Validates ARP packets against trusted bindings.
- **DHCP snooping**: Builds IP-to-MAC-to-port bindings that DAI can trust.
- **Port security**: Limits MAC learning on access ports.
- **Segmentation**: Reduces the number of hosts sharing a broadcast domain.
- **IDS/IPS signatures**: Detect suspicious ARP reply patterns or poisoning bursts.
- **Switch log correlation**: Helps identify repeated MAC moves, port anomalies, or DAI drops.

A common enterprise pattern is DHCP snooping plus DAI on access switches:

```text
DHCP Snooping -> learns valid bindings
DAI -> checks ARP packets against bindings
Port Security -> limits MAC abuse
```

Example Cisco-style configuration:

```cisco
ip dhcp snooping
ip dhcp snooping vlan 10
interface GigabitEthernet0/1
 ip dhcp snooping trust
!
interface GigabitEthernet0/2
 ip arp inspection trust
!
ip arp inspection vlan 10
```

Verification commands:

```cisco
show ip dhcp snooping binding
show ip arp inspection
show ip arp inspection statistics
show port-security interface gigabitEthernet0/2
show mac address-table
```

If DAI is active, you may see dropped ARP packets for invalid bindings. That is a strong indicator of attempted poisoning or misconfigured hosts.

**Detection methods and incident clues**

Detection is easier when you correlate multiple signals instead of relying on one command.

- **Static ARP monitoring**: Compare current ARP tables against known-good mappings.
- **IDS/IPS alerts**: Look for repeated unsolicited ARP replies or duplicate IP-to-MAC claims.
- **Switch logs**: Correlate ARP inspection drops, MAC flaps, and port-security violations.
- **Endpoint telemetry**: Watch for sudden gateway MAC changes or duplicate neighbor entries.
- **Packet capture**: Confirm whether a host is sending forged replies or gratuitous ARP bursts.

Common indicators include:

- The default gateway MAC changes unexpectedly on multiple hosts.
- Two different IPs resolve to the same MAC address.
- ARP replies arrive without a matching request.
- The same MAC appears on multiple switch ports in a short time.
- Users report intermittent connectivity, certificate warnings, or session resets during interception.

**Practical lab example**

A simple lab topology looks like this:

```text
[Victim PC] ---\
                [Switch] --- [Gateway/Router]
[Attacker] ----/
```

In a controlled lab, you can observe the effect of poisoning with packet capture:

```bash
sudo tcpdump -i eth0 -n arp
```

Then compare neighbor tables before and after the attack:

```bash
ip neigh show
arp -a
```

If you stop the spoofing process, the victim may eventually recover as the ARP cache ages out or refreshes from legitimate traffic. If the attacker keeps sending forged replies, the poisoned state persists.

**Exam-focused takeaways**

- ARP spoofing works because ARP lacks strong authentication.
- It is most effective on the same Layer 2 broadcast domain or VLAN.
- Routers normally block ARP across subnets, but proxy ARP can blur that boundary.
- Poisoning persists only while the attacker keeps refreshing the cache before aging expires.
- Gratuitous ARP is legitimate but often abused to accelerate poisoning.
- DAI, DHCP snooping, port security, and segmentation are core defenses.
- ARP spoofing, DNS spoofing, and MAC flooding are different attacks that can all appear in MITM-related questions.

**Quick comparison of defensive priorities**

1. Prevent unauthorized ARP trust with DAI and DHCP snooping.
2. Reduce blast radius with VLAN segmentation.
3. Limit abuse with port security and static bindings where practical.
4. Detect anomalies with IDS/IPS, switch logs, and neighbor-table monitoring.

> In CEH-style scenarios, the best answer is often the one that combines Layer 2 scope, cache poisoning behavior, and switch-based defenses rather than treating ARP spoofing as a generic “sniffing” technique.
