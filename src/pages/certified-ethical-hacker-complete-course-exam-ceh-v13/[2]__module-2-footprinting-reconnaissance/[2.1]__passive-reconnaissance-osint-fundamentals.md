## **Course:** Certified Ethical Hacker Complete Course (Exam CEH v13)

**Passive reconnaissance overview**

Passive reconnaissance is the process of collecting information about a target without directly interacting with its systems in a way that is likely to be detected. In CEH contexts, this usually means using publicly available or third-party sources to build a target profile before any active scanning begins. The goal is to reduce noise, avoid triggering alerts, and identify high-value leads for later validation.

**Open Source Intelligence (OSINT)** is the broader discipline of collecting and analyzing information from publicly accessible sources. Passive recon is a subset of OSINT, but the two overlap heavily in practice. You use OSINT to answer questions such as:

- What domains, subdomains, and IP ranges are associated with the target?
- Which technologies, people, and vendors are visible externally?
- What metadata, documents, or images reveal internal details?
- Which third-party services expose useful records or historical context?

A useful mental model is: passive recon finds *clues*, while active recon confirms *facts*.

**Legal and ethical boundaries**

For CEH preparation, you should treat scope control as a first-class requirement. Passive collection can still cross legal or ethical lines if you access restricted content, bypass access controls, or collect data outside the authorized engagement scope.

- Stay within the written scope, rules of engagement, and authorization.
- Avoid credential harvesting, impersonation, or deceptive contact unless explicitly approved.
- Do not attempt to evade privacy controls or access non-public data through unauthorized means.
- Treat personal data carefully; collect only what is relevant to the assessment.
- Document sources and timestamps so findings are defensible and reproducible.

> Passive does not mean unrestricted. Publicly visible data can still be sensitive, and collection methods must remain lawful and authorized.

**Reconnaissance workflow**

A practical passive recon workflow usually moves from broad to specific:

1. Identify the organization, brands, subsidiaries, and known domains.
2. Enumerate public DNS and registration data.
3. Collect technology and infrastructure hints from third-party sources.
4. Pivot into people, documents, images, and social platforms.
5. Correlate findings into a target map for later validation.

This approach helps you avoid premature assumptions. For example, a company may use multiple domains for marketing, support, and cloud services, each with different exposure patterns.

**WHOIS and domain registration intelligence**

WHOIS is a standard passive recon source for domain registration details. It can reveal registrar information, registration dates, name servers, and sometimes administrative contacts. In modern environments, however, WHOIS data is often partially redacted because of privacy protection services and GDPR-style masking.

Key WHOIS details you may extract:

- **Registrar** name and abuse contact
- **Creation** and expiration dates
- **Name servers** associated with the domain
- **Registrant** organization, if not redacted
- **Status** flags such as `clientTransferProhibited`
- **Historical WHOIS** records from third-party archives, when available

Limitations matter:

- Privacy protection can hide registrant names, emails, and phone numbers.
- Some records are intentionally incomplete or stale.
- Historical WHOIS may show prior ownership or old infrastructure, but it is not always authoritative.
- Registrar data can differ from actual operational ownership, especially for resellers or managed domains.

Example WHOIS lookup:

```bash
whois example.com
```

Example output fields to interpret:

```text
Registrar: Example Registrar, Inc.
Creation Date: 2012-05-14
Name Server: ns1.exampledns.net
Name Server: ns2.exampledns.net
Registrant Organization: REDACTED FOR PRIVACY
```

When you see privacy protection, do not stop the analysis. Pivot to name servers, registration dates, and historical records. Those often reveal infrastructure patterns even when identity fields are hidden.

**DNS-focused passive intelligence**

DNS is one of the richest passive recon sources because it exposes how an organization publishes services to the internet. You should look beyond the main A record and examine the broader DNS footprint.

Useful DNS record types include:

- **A/AAAA** records for IPv4 and IPv6 addresses
- **MX** records for mail delivery hosts
- **NS** records for authoritative name servers
- **CNAME** records for aliases and hosted services
- **TXT** records for verification, SPF, DKIM, and DMARC hints
- **SRV** records for service discovery in some environments

Example lookups:

```bash
dig example.com A
dig example.com AAAA
dig example.com MX
dig example.com NS
dig example.com TXT
```

A practical interpretation pattern:

- **MX** records often reveal mail providers such as Microsoft 365 or Google Workspace.
- **NS** records can expose DNS hosting providers and sometimes shared infrastructure.
- **TXT** records may reveal SPF sender policies, verification tokens, or cloud service integrations.
- **CNAME** chains often point to SaaS platforms, content delivery networks, or hosted application endpoints.

Example:

```bash
dig example.com MX +short
```

```text
10 mail.example.com.
20 altmail.example.net.
```

This tells you the organization may operate its own mail host or use a hybrid mail setup. You can then inspect the mail host names for additional clues.

**Passive DNS and historical DNS intelligence**

Passive DNS is a historical view of DNS resolution data collected by third parties. It helps you see how domains and IPs have changed over time, which is especially useful when current DNS records are sparse or heavily protected.

Passive DNS can reveal:

- Past A record mappings for a domain
- Subdomains that previously resolved
- IP addresses shared across multiple domains
- Infrastructure migrations and hosting changes
- Potentially related domains in the same environment

This is valuable because current DNS often shows only the present state, while passive DNS shows the evolution of the target’s footprint.

Example use case:

- A domain currently resolves to a CDN.
- Passive DNS shows it previously pointed to an on-premises IP range.
- That historical IP range may still be relevant for legacy services, forgotten subdomains, or archived assets.

Passive DNS is not a single tool; it is a category of third-party intelligence sources. In practice, you may use commercial platforms, threat intelligence portals, or DNS history services.

**Subdomain enumeration from passive sources**

Passive subdomain enumeration uses third-party data rather than direct brute-force probing. This is often safer and faster during early recon.

Common passive sources include:

- Certificate Transparency (CT) logs
- Passive DNS datasets
- Search engine indexes
- Public code repositories
- Archived web content
- Security intelligence platforms

CT logs are useful, but they are not the only source. A mature recon workflow combines multiple sources because each one has blind spots.

Example workflow:

```bash
# Example: collect subdomains from a passive source list
cat domains.txt | while read d; do
  subfinder -d "$d" -silent
done
```

If you use tools such as `subfinder`, `amass`, or similar recon utilities, prefer passive modes first when you want to minimize interaction with target infrastructure.

**Practical DNS pivot example**

Suppose you discover these records:

```bash
dig example.com NS +short
```

```text
ns1.cloudflare.com.
ns2.cloudflare.com.
```

```bash
dig example.com MX +short
```

```text
10 example-com.mail.protection.outlook.com.
```

Interpretation:

- DNS is hosted behind Cloudflare.
- Email is handled by Microsoft 365.
- The organization likely uses multiple cloud services, which may create separate administrative surfaces and verification records.

From there, you can inspect TXT records for SPF and DMARC:

```bash
dig example.com TXT +short
```

```text
"v=spf1 include:spf.protection.outlook.com -all"
"google-site-verification=abc123..."
```

This may reveal external services used for email and web verification.

**Image and EXIF metadata**

Images are a powerful passive OSINT source because they can contain embedded metadata. EXIF (Exchangeable Image File Format) data may include camera model, timestamps, software used, and sometimes GPS coordinates. Not every image contains useful metadata, but when it does, it can expose location or workflow details.

Common metadata fields:

- **Camera** make and model
- **Creation** and modification timestamps
- **GPS** latitude and longitude
- **Software** used to edit or export the image
- **Author** or copyright fields, if present

Example inspection:

```bash
exiftool image.jpg
```

Example geolocation-related output:

```text
GPS Latitude                    : 37 deg 46' 30.12" N
GPS Longitude                   : 122 deg 25' 10.44" W
Create Date                     : 2025:03:14 09:22:11
Software                        : Adobe Photoshop 25.0
```

If GPS data exists, you can map it to a location. If timestamps are present, they may help correlate with events, office hours, or travel patterns. Be careful: metadata can be stripped, altered, or misleading.

Useful follow-up commands:

```bash
exiftool -gps:all -a -G1 -s image.jpg
strings image.jpg | head
```

**Document metadata and file artifacts**

Office documents, PDFs, spreadsheets, and presentations often contain metadata that reveals authors, departments, software versions, and internal paths. This is a classic passive recon source because documents are frequently published without sanitization.

Potentially useful fields:

- **Author** and last modified by
- **Company** or organization name
- **Template** names
- **Internal file paths**
- **Revision** history
- **Embedded comments** or tracked changes

Example:

```bash
exiftool report.pdf
```

You may also inspect Office documents with tools such as `oletools` or `strings` when appropriate.

Example findings:

- `Author: Jane Smith`
- `Company: Example Corp`
- `Last Saved By: finance-admin`
- `Template: \\fileserver\templates\quarterly.potx`

These details can support people mapping, department identification, and internal naming conventions.

**People intelligence and social engineering context**

OSINT often includes people intelligence, which means mapping employees, roles, relationships, and public-facing identities. In CEH-style questions, this is important because attackers frequently use public employee data to craft convincing phishing or impersonation attempts. Your job in an assessment is to understand exposure, not to misuse it.

Useful people-intelligence techniques include:

- **Employee mapping** from company websites, press releases, and professional profiles
- **Org chart reconstruction** from job titles, reporting lines, and department clues
- **Username correlation** across platforms using consistent handles or naming patterns
- **Role identification** such as help desk, finance, HR, IT, or executives
- **Vendor and contractor mapping** to identify third-party trust relationships

A common pattern is to correlate usernames across services:

- `j.smith` on one platform
- `jsmith` on another
- `johnsmith` in a code repository
- `john.smith@example.com` in a document author field

This can reveal naming conventions and likely email formats.

Example social pivot workflow:

1. Find a public employee profile with a job title.
2. Search for the same name on other platforms.
3. Correlate usernames, profile photos, and employer references.
4. Identify likely email patterns and internal teams.
5. Use the result to understand exposure, not to contact or deceive.

A practical example of a safe pivot:

- A LinkedIn profile shows `Senior Network Engineer`.
- A GitHub profile with the same handle references the same employer.
- A conference speaker bio lists a corporate email format.
- A document author field matches the same name.

This triangulation helps you infer the organization’s naming and communication patterns.

**Social media and public platform pivoting**

Social platforms can expose job roles, office locations, event attendance, technology stacks, and internal culture. The value comes from correlation, not from any single post.

What to look for:

- Employee announcements and promotions
- Conference photos and badge images
- Job postings with technology requirements
- Public issue trackers and developer discussions
- Company pages that list subsidiaries or regional offices

Example search patterns:

```text
site:linkedin.com "Example Corp" "security engineer"
site:github.com "Example Corp" "example.com"
site:x.com "Example Corp" "new office"
```

You can also search for usernames across platforms:

```text
"jdoe" "Example Corp"
"jdoe" "github"
"jdoe" "linkedin"
```

Be cautious with false positives. Common names and reused handles can create misleading correlations, so you should validate with multiple independent clues.

**Search engine dorks and indexing clues**

Search engines remain a core passive recon source because they index public content that organizations may not realize is exposed. Search operators help you narrow results to specific file types, domains, and phrases.

Useful operators:

- `site:` to restrict to a domain
- `filetype:` to find documents
- `intitle:` to search page titles
- `inurl:` to search URL patterns
- Quoted strings for exact phrases

Examples:

```text
site:example.com filetype:pdf
site:example.com intitle:index of
site:example.com "confidential"
site:example.com filetype:xls OR filetype:xlsx
```

These queries can surface documents, directory listings, and public references that reveal internal naming conventions or exposed assets.

**Archived content and web history**

Web archives and cached content can reveal pages that no longer exist on the live site. This is useful when organizations remove pages after a rebrand, incident, or infrastructure change.

Archived content may show:

- Old subdomains
- Deprecated login portals
- Legacy contact pages
- Historical product names
- Prior employee lists or team pages

This is especially useful when combined with passive DNS, because archived pages may reference hostnames that no longer resolve.

**Third-party technology fingerprinting**

Passive recon also includes identifying technologies from public-facing artifacts without probing aggressively. You can infer stacks from page source, headers, scripts, and public documentation.

Examples of passive clues:

- HTML comments and asset paths
- JavaScript bundle names
- Publicly visible error pages
- CDN and analytics identifiers
- Public job postings mentioning tools and platforms

Example command:

```bash
curl -s https://example.com | grep -Ei 'generator|wp-content|react|vue|angular'
```

This is not a substitute for active fingerprinting, but it can guide later validation.

**Practical worked example: WHOIS to DNS to people**

Imagine you are assessing `example.com`.

1. Run WHOIS:
   ```bash
   whois example.com
   ```
2. Note the registrar, creation date, and name servers.
3. Query DNS:
   ```bash
   dig example.com NS +short
   dig example.com MX +short
   dig example.com TXT +short
   ```
4. Observe that mail is hosted in Microsoft 365 and DNS is on Cloudflare.
5. Search for public employee profiles and job postings mentioning those services.
6. Correlate a document author name with a public profile and a likely email format.

This chain gives you a passive map of ownership, infrastructure, and personnel without touching internal systems.

**Practical worked example: passive DNS and subdomains**

Suppose passive DNS shows historical resolution for `vpn.example.com` and `dev.example.com`.

- `vpn.example.com` previously pointed to `203.0.113.10`
- `dev.example.com` previously pointed to `203.0.113.20`
- Current DNS only exposes the main website

Interpretation:

- The organization may have retired or moved services.
- Old subdomains may still appear in archives, certificates, or third-party datasets.
- Those names are worth documenting for later validation in an authorized phase.

You can then compare with CT logs or passive subdomain sources to see whether the names still appear elsewhere.

**Practical worked example: social media pivoting**

A public employee profile shows a systems administrator at `Example Corp`.

- The profile lists a conference talk on Microsoft 365 administration.
- A GitHub account with the same handle contains scripts referencing `examplecorp.local`.
- A PDF author field from a public document shows the same name.

From this, you can infer:

- The person likely works in IT operations.
- The organization may use Microsoft 365.
- The handle is reused across platforms, which helps correlate identities.

This is useful for understanding exposure, but you should not use it to impersonate the person or contact them outside the engagement rules.

**Common OSINT sources and what they reveal**

| Source | Typical value | Key limitation |
|---|---|---|
| WHOIS | Registrar, dates, name servers | Privacy redaction, stale data |
| Passive DNS | Historical host-to-IP mappings | Coverage varies by provider |
| DNS records | MX, NS, TXT, CNAME clues | Only current published state |
| Search engines | Indexed files and pages | Index may be incomplete |
| Social platforms | People, roles, events | False positives, privacy settings |
| Images | EXIF, GPS, timestamps | Metadata may be stripped |
| Documents | Authors, paths, templates | Sanitization may remove clues |
| Archives | Historical pages and assets | Snapshots may be outdated |

**Operational tips for CEH exam scenarios**

- Start with passive sources before active scanning.
- Correlate at least two independent clues before treating a finding as reliable.
- Distinguish between current records and historical records.
- Remember that privacy protection is common in WHOIS and social platforms.
- Treat metadata as evidence, not proof, until corroborated.
- Use scope control to avoid collecting irrelevant personal data.

**Common pitfalls**

- Assuming WHOIS privacy means no useful data exists.
- Treating a single social profile as definitive identity proof.
- Ignoring MX, NS, and TXT records while focusing only on A records.
- Overlooking image GPS data because only document metadata was checked.
- Confusing passive DNS history with current authoritative DNS.
- Using OSINT findings outside the authorized assessment scope.

**Exam-oriented takeaways**

For CEH v13, passive recon and OSINT are about building a target picture without direct interaction. You should know how to:

- Use WHOIS and understand its limitations.
- Query DNS records and interpret MX, NS, TXT, and CNAME data.
- Leverage passive DNS and historical records.
- Extract metadata from documents and images, including EXIF geolocation.
- Map people and usernames across public platforms.
- Apply legal, ethical, and scope constraints throughout the process.

The strongest exam answers usually emphasize that passive recon is low-noise, broad in coverage, and highly dependent on correlation across multiple public sources.
