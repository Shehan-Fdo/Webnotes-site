## **Course:** Certified Ethical Hacker Complete Course (Exam CEH v13)

**Attack surface mapping overview**

Attack surface mapping is the process of identifying every externally reachable point where an attacker could interact with an organization’s systems, people, or services. In CEH terms, you use reconnaissance to build a defensible inventory of assets, exposures, and relationships before deeper testing begins.

The goal is not just to “find hosts.” You want to understand:
- What exists
- How it is exposed
- Who owns it
- Why it matters to the business
- What evidence supports the observation

A good recon report turns raw observations into decision-ready intelligence. It separates confirmed facts from hypotheses, avoids overclaiming, and preserves evidence so the client can verify what you found.

**Core concepts and scope**

Attack surface mapping covers more than IP addresses and open ports. It includes internet-facing infrastructure, cloud services, third-party dependencies, exposed credentials, public documents, and human-facing assets such as employee email patterns or social media profiles.

Common scope areas include:
- **Network perimeter**: public IP ranges, VPN gateways, firewalls, DNS, mail, and web services
- **Web applications**: login portals, APIs, admin panels, file upload endpoints, and hidden paths
- **Cloud exposure**: public storage buckets, permissive identity and access management (IAM) policies, exposed management consoles, and SaaS shadow IT
- **People and process**: employee naming conventions, job roles, contact patterns, and public documents
- **Third parties**: vendors, outsourced services, CDN endpoints, and partner portals

Reconnaissance reporting is different from full vulnerability reporting:
- Recon reporting documents **exposure and intelligence**
- Vulnerability reporting documents **confirmed weaknesses, impact, and remediation**

In a recon-only report, you should avoid:
- Claiming exploitation when you only observed exposure
- Listing unverified CVEs unless you validated them
- Including intrusive proof-of-concept payloads that were not authorized
- Recommending fixes for issues you did not confirm, unless you clearly label them as likely next steps

**Reconnaissance workflow**

A practical workflow helps you move from broad discovery to validated reporting.

1. Define scope and rules of engagement.
2. Enumerate domains, subdomains, IP ranges, and cloud assets.
3. Identify services, technologies, and exposed interfaces.
4. Correlate findings into asset groups and business context.
5. Capture evidence and preserve timestamps.
6. Rank exposure by criticality and likelihood.
7. Write findings with clear remediation and ownership.

A simple workflow diagram can look like this:

```text
Scope -> Discovery -> Validation -> Correlation -> Evidence -> Reporting
```

**Common discovery techniques**

Use multiple techniques because each one reveals different parts of the attack surface.

- **DNS enumeration**
  - Query `A`, `AAAA`, `MX`, `NS`, `TXT`, and `CNAME` records
  - Look for subdomain patterns and delegated zones
  - Identify cloud-hosted endpoints and third-party services

- **Port and service discovery**
  - Scan public IPs for exposed services
  - Confirm service banners and TLS certificates
  - Note unusual ports, management interfaces, and legacy protocols

- **Web enumeration**
  - Enumerate directories, virtual hosts, parameters, and API endpoints
  - Inspect robots files, sitemap files, and JavaScript references
  - Review login pages, admin panels, and file upload features

- **OSINT (Open Source Intelligence)**
  - Search public documents, code repositories, and social profiles
  - Correlate employee names, email formats, and vendor references
  - Identify leaked secrets, exposed metadata, and forgotten assets

- **Cloud and SaaS discovery**
  - Search for public buckets, shared documents, and tenant-specific portals
  - Identify misconfigured IAM roles, public snapshots, and exposed dashboards
  - Detect shadow IT by finding unsanctioned SaaS domains or file-sharing services

**Useful CLI examples**

These commands are common in recon workflows and help produce evidence for reporting.

```bash
# DNS and subdomain discovery
dig example.com any
dig +short ns example.com
dig +short mx example.com
amass enum -d example.com
subfinder -d example.com

# Passive and active host discovery
nmap -Pn -sS -sV -O -p 1-1000 203.0.113.0/24
masscan 203.0.113.0/24 -p80,443,22,3389 --rate 1000

# Web content discovery
whatweb https://app.example.com
ffuf -u https://app.example.com/FUZZ -w wordlist.txt -mc all
gobuster dir -u https://app.example.com -w wordlist.txt

# TLS and certificate inspection
openssl s_client -connect app.example.com:443 -servername app.example.com
```

When you use active scanning, note the scan rate, date, source IP, and any rate-limiting or blocking behavior. Those details matter in the report because they affect confidence and reproducibility.

**OSINT tooling and interpretation**

CEH-style recon often expects familiarity with OSINT tools and how to interpret their output.

- **Google dorking**
  - Use search operators to find exposed files, login pages, and indexed documents
  - Examples include `site:`, `filetype:`, `intitle:`, and `inurl:`
  - Treat results as leads until you verify them directly

```text
site:example.com filetype:pdf
site:example.com intitle:"index of"
site:example.com inurl:admin
```

- **Maltego**
  - Use it to visualize relationships between domains, people, emails, infrastructure, and organizations
  - Good for linking subdomains to certificates, social accounts, and third-party services
  - Report the graph as an intelligence aid, not as proof by itself

- **Recon-ng**
  - Modular framework for collecting domain, host, and contact data
  - Useful for consolidating passive recon sources into a repeatable workflow
  - Report module outputs with timestamps and source attribution where possible

- **theHarvester**
  - Collects emails, subdomains, hosts, and names from public sources
  - Interpret results as a starting point for validation
  - Distinguish between discovered names and confirmed employee identities

Example interpretation:
- If theHarvester returns `10 emails`, report that as “publicly discoverable email addresses associated with the target domain.”
- If Maltego links a subdomain to a cloud provider, verify with DNS and HTTP headers before treating it as confirmed.
- If Recon-ng finds a contact record, note whether it came from a public source, a breach dataset, or a search engine cache.

**Cloud-specific attack surface mapping**

Cloud environments expand the attack surface because assets are elastic, distributed, and often managed by multiple teams.

Look for:
- **Exposed storage**
  - Public object storage buckets
  - Misconfigured ACLs (access control lists)
  - Shared folders with anonymous read access
- **IAM misconfigurations**
  - Overly permissive roles
  - Publicly assumable roles
  - Weak trust policies
  - Long-lived access keys in public repositories
- **SaaS shadow IT**
  - Unapproved file-sharing platforms
  - Personal collaboration tools used for business data
  - Forgotten tenant instances and trial accounts
- **Management exposure**
  - Public dashboards
  - Metadata endpoints
  - Admin consoles reachable from the internet

A cloud finding should include the service type, region or tenant if known, exposure level, and business impact. For example, a public bucket containing marketing assets is lower risk than a bucket containing backups or customer data, even though both are exposed.

**Evidence collection and preservation**

Recon reporting depends on evidence quality. Capture enough detail so another analyst can verify the finding later.

Common evidence types include:
- **Screenshots** of web pages, dashboards, error messages, and exposed listings
- **Packet captures** showing service banners, redirects, or suspicious traffic
- **Raw scan outputs** from tools such as `nmap`, `masscan`, `ffuf`, or `amass`
- **Log excerpts** from proxy logs, server logs, WAF logs, or authentication logs
- **HTML source** and response headers
- **DNS query output** and certificate details
- **Downloaded public documents** or metadata extracts

Preserve evidence carefully:
- Record timestamps in UTC when possible
- Keep original filenames and tool output
- Hash files with `sha256sum` to support integrity checks
- Store evidence in a controlled location with access restrictions
- Avoid modifying original artifacts; work on copies

Example integrity workflow:

```bash
sha256sum nmap-scan.txt screenshot1.png > evidence-hashes.txt
```

> If evidence may contain sensitive data, treat it as confidential client material and limit distribution to authorized stakeholders only.

**Legal, ethical, and chain-of-custody considerations**

Reconnaissance often touches sensitive data even when you do not exploit anything. Handle it as if it could be reviewed in an audit or legal process.

Key practices:
- Follow the agreed scope and authorization
- Minimize collection of personal data unless it is necessary
- Mark sensitive artifacts as confidential
- Maintain chain of custody for collected evidence
- Record who collected the evidence, when, where, and how
- Do not alter evidence after collection
- Use secure storage and access controls for reports and artifacts

Chain of custody is especially important when:
- You collect screenshots of exposed data
- You download public documents that may contain metadata
- You capture packet traces or logs that could be disputed
- You identify leaked credentials or secrets

A simple chain-of-custody record can include:
- Evidence ID
- Description
- Source URL or host
- Collector
- Date and time
- Hash value
- Storage location
- Access history

**What to include in a recon-only report**

A recon-only report should focus on exposure, intelligence, and prioritization.

Include:
- Scope and methodology
- Asset inventory and discovery sources
- Confirmed exposures and observations
- Evidence references
- Risk ranking or prioritization
- Business context and ownership where known
- Recommended next steps for validation or hardening

Do not include:
- Unverified exploit steps
- Payloads or destructive actions
- Full vulnerability exploitation chains unless explicitly authorized
- Excessive raw data dumps that obscure the findings
- Sensitive personal data unless it is necessary and approved

A recon report is strongest when it answers:
- What is exposed?
- How was it discovered?
- Why does it matter?
- Who should act on it?
- What should happen next?

**Attack surface inventory fields**

A structured inventory makes reporting easier and supports remediation tracking.

Common fields:
- **Asset name**: hostname, IP, bucket name, SaaS tenant, or application name
- **Asset type**: server, web app, API, storage, identity, email, or third-party service
- **Exposure type**: internet-facing, public read, authenticated only, partner-only, or internal
- **Business owner**: team, department, or service owner
- **Asset criticality**: high, medium, low, or business-specific tier
- **Discovery source**: DNS, scan, OSINT, cloud inventory, or manual validation
- **Evidence reference**: screenshot ID, scan file, log excerpt, or packet capture
- **Risk rating**: qualitative or numeric
- **Remediation status**: open, in progress, mitigated, accepted, or false positive
- **Last verified**: timestamp of the most recent confirmation

Example inventory row:

| Asset | Type | Exposure | Owner | Criticality | Evidence | Status |
|---|---|---:|---|---|---|---|
| `app.example.com` | Web app | Internet-facing | Web Platform Team | High | Screenshot-03, `nmap.txt` | Open |
| `s3.example-bucket` | Storage | Public read | Marketing | Medium | `aws-cli-output.txt` | In progress |
| `vpn.example.com` | VPN gateway | Internet-facing | IT Operations | High | `tls-capture.pcap` | Open |

**Reporting structure**

A clear report structure helps stakeholders move from discovery to action.

Typical recon report outline:
1. Title page and classification
2. Executive summary
3. Scope and methodology
4. Attack surface overview
5. Key findings
6. Evidence appendix
7. Risk and prioritization summary
8. Remediation recommendations
9. Conclusion

**Sample executive summary**

The organization has a broad internet-facing attack surface that includes public web applications, remote access services, cloud-hosted storage, and third-party SaaS dependencies. Reconnaissance identified multiple exposed assets that are not consistently tracked in the asset inventory, including subdomains, public documents, and at least one cloud storage endpoint with permissive access. Several assets lack clear business ownership, which slows remediation and increases the chance of shadow IT persistence. The most urgent actions are to validate ownership, restrict unnecessary exposure, and centralize monitoring for newly discovered assets.

**Sample recon report outline with filled-in content**

1. **Title**
   - Attack Surface Mapping and Reconnaissance Report for Example Corp

2. **Executive summary**
   - Public-facing assets were discovered across web, cloud, and remote access services.
   - Two assets appear to be unmanaged or weakly governed.
   - Evidence suggests incomplete inventory coverage and possible shadow IT.

3. **Scope**
   - `example.com`, associated subdomains, public IP ranges, and approved cloud tenants
   - No exploitation performed
   - No authenticated testing beyond permitted access

4. **Methodology**
   - Passive DNS and OSINT collection
   - Limited active scanning of approved ranges
   - Web enumeration and certificate review
   - Cloud exposure checks for public storage and identity misconfiguration

5. **Key findings**
   - Public storage bucket accessible without authentication
   - Admin portal indexed by search engines
   - Multiple subdomains not present in the official inventory
   - Employee email pattern easily inferred from public sources

6. **Evidence appendix**
   - Screenshot of public bucket listing
   - `nmap` output for exposed services
   - `ffuf` results for hidden paths
   - Log excerpt showing repeated login attempts
   - Packet capture of TLS handshake and redirect chain

7. **Prioritization**
   - High: public storage with sensitive content
   - Medium: unmanaged subdomains
   - Low: public marketing assets with no sensitive data

8. **Recommendations**
   - Update asset inventory
   - Assign owners to unknown assets
   - Restrict public access where unnecessary
   - Monitor for new subdomains and cloud resources

**Sample finding format**

A recon finding should be concise, evidence-backed, and actionable.

- **Finding title**: Publicly accessible cloud storage bucket
- **Description**: A storage bucket is readable without authentication and contains marketing files and internal naming conventions.
- **Evidence**: Screenshot, bucket listing output, and HTTP response headers
- **Impact**: Information disclosure and potential reconnaissance aid for attackers
- **Recommendation**: Restrict public access, review bucket policy, and confirm data classification
- **Status**: Open
- **Owner**: Marketing Operations

**Practical examples of recon evidence**

Examples of evidence you may reference in a report:
- A screenshot showing a directory listing or login page
- A packet capture showing a service banner or redirect
- Raw `nmap` output listing open ports and service versions
- Log excerpts showing exposed endpoints or repeated access attempts
- DNS output showing delegated subdomains or third-party services
- Search engine results showing indexed documents or admin pages

Example raw scan excerpt:

```text
PORT    STATE SERVICE VERSION
80/tcp  open  http    Apache httpd 2.4.57
443/tcp open  ssl/http nginx 1.24.0
8443/tcp open  ssl/http admin console
```

Example web enumeration output:

```text
/robots.txt
/sitemap.xml
/admin/
/api/v1/
/uploads/
```

**Prioritization and risk framing**

Recon findings should be prioritized by exposure and business relevance, not just by technical novelty.

Consider:
- Internet exposure
- Sensitivity of the data or service
- Ease of discovery by an attacker
- Presence of authentication or access controls
- Business criticality
- Whether the asset is known and managed
- Whether the exposure is temporary or persistent

A simple risk statement can be:
- “This asset is publicly reachable and contains information that reduces attacker effort.”
- “This service is exposed but appears to contain only low-sensitivity content.”
- “This cloud resource is publicly readable and may reveal internal naming conventions or data structure.”

**Remediation guidance in recon reports**

Even without full vulnerability testing, you can recommend practical next steps.

Common recommendations:
- Remove unnecessary public exposure
- Enforce authentication and least privilege
- Update the asset inventory and assign ownership
- Restrict cloud storage policies and IAM permissions
- Monitor for new subdomains and exposed services
- Review indexed documents and public repositories
- Centralize logging and alerting for externally reachable assets

Keep recommendations aligned with what you observed. If you did not validate a weakness, phrase the recommendation as a control improvement rather than a confirmed fix.

**Common mistakes to avoid**

- Confusing discovery with exploitation
- Reporting every open port as a vulnerability
- Omitting timestamps and evidence references
- Failing to identify ownership or business context
- Using vague language like “seems bad” instead of specific observations
- Including too much raw output without interpretation
- Ignoring cloud and SaaS assets because they are not traditional hosts

**Quick reference checklist**

- Confirm scope and authorization
- Collect evidence with timestamps
- Preserve original artifacts and hashes
- Distinguish observations from conclusions
- Include asset criticality, exposure type, owner, and remediation status
- Separate recon findings from vulnerability findings
- Protect confidential data and maintain chain of custody
- Provide an executive summary and a clear next-step plan

**Exam-focused takeaway**

For CEH v13, remember that attack surface mapping is about building a complete, evidence-backed picture of what an attacker can see from the outside. Reconnaissance reporting should be ethical, structured, and actionable. The strongest reports combine technical detail, business context, and careful evidence handling so the client can prioritize remediation without confusion.
