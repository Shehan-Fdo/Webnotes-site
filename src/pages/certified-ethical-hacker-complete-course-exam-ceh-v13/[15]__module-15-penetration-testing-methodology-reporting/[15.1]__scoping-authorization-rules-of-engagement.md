## **Course:** Certified Ethical Hacker Complete Course (Exam CEH v13)

**Why scoping matters**

Scoping is the process of defining exactly what you are allowed to test, how you may test it, when you may test it, and what you must avoid. In ethical hacking, scope is not a formality; it is the boundary that separates authorized security work from illegal activity. A clear scope reduces risk to production systems, protects third parties, and gives both the tester and the client a shared understanding of acceptable activity.

A strong engagement begins with three pillars:

- **Authorization**: written permission to test.
- **Scope**: the assets, targets, and boundaries of the assessment.
- **Rules of Engagement**: the operational rules that govern how testing is performed.

**Core legal and ethical principles**

Ethical hacking depends on explicit permission. Verbal approval is not enough for a real engagement because it is difficult to prove, easy to misunderstand, and often insufficient for legal defense. You should always ensure the authorization is documented, signed, and tied to a specific engagement.

Key principles include:

- **Need-to-know**: only the people who must know about the test should receive details.
- **Least privilege**: testers should receive only the access required to complete the work.
- **Non-disruption**: testing should avoid unnecessary downtime, data loss, or service impact.
- **Accountability**: every action should be attributable to a named tester and a defined approval.

> If you do not have written authorization, do not test. Even a technically harmless scan can be unauthorized if it is outside the approved scope.

**Authorization documents**

Authorization is usually established through formal documents that define the relationship between the client and the tester. Common documents include:

- **Master Services Agreement (MSA)**: the overarching legal contract between organizations.
- **Statement of Work (SOW)**: the specific project description, deliverables, and timeline.
- **Rules of Engagement (RoE)**: the operational rules for the assessment.
- **Letter of Authorization (LoA)**: written proof that the tester is allowed to act on behalf of the client.
- **Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA)**: confidentiality agreement covering sensitive information.

These documents often work together. For example, an MSA may establish the legal relationship, an SOW may define the project, and an RoE may specify the exact testing behavior.

**What scope should define**

A useful scope is precise enough to prevent ambiguity. It should identify what is in scope, what is out of scope, and what conditions apply during testing.

Typical scope elements include:

- **Targets**: IP addresses, hostnames, URLs, applications, cloud accounts, wireless networks, or physical locations.
- **Testing type**: external, internal, web application, wireless, social engineering, physical, or red team.
- **Time window**: approved dates, hours, and maintenance windows.
- **Source restrictions**: approved tester IPs, VPN endpoints, or geographic locations.
- **Impact limits**: whether denial-of-service (DoS) testing, exploitation, or payload delivery is allowed.
- **Data handling**: what data may be collected, stored, encrypted, or shared.
- **Exclusions**: systems, subnets, third parties, or business units that are off limits.

A scope that says “test the network” is too vague. A better scope lists exact ranges, domains, and applications, such as:

```text
In scope:
- 203.0.113.10/32
- 198.51.100.0/24
- app.example.com
- vpn.example.com

Out of scope:
- 198.51.100.128/25
- third-party SaaS tenants
- production database clusters
```

**Rules of Engagement**

The Rules of Engagement define how the assessment is conducted. They translate legal permission into operational behavior. RoE should be specific enough that the tester can act confidently without improvising risky decisions.

Common RoE topics include:

- **Allowed techniques**: vulnerability scanning, manual validation, exploitation, password attacks, phishing simulation, or physical entry testing.
- **Prohibited actions**: destructive payloads, persistence, data exfiltration, or lateral movement beyond scope.
- **Escalation path**: who to contact if a critical issue, outage, or unexpected exposure is discovered.
- **Emergency stop**: how the client can pause or terminate testing immediately.
- **Communication cadence**: daily updates, status calls, or incident notifications.
- **Evidence handling**: screenshots, logs, packet captures, and proof-of-concept artifacts.
- **Cleanup requirements**: removal of accounts, agents, files, scheduled tasks, or test data after the engagement.

A practical RoE often includes a contact matrix:

| Role | Responsibility | Contact method |
|---|---|---|
| Client sponsor | Approves scope and business decisions | Email, phone |
| Technical contact | Handles technical questions and outages | Chat, phone |
| Tester lead | Coordinates the assessment team | Email, secure messaging |
| Incident contact | Receives urgent security notifications | Phone, pager |

**Operational planning**

Before testing begins, you should confirm the environment and reduce ambiguity. This is especially important when the target includes production systems, cloud services, or third-party dependencies.

Useful planning checks include:

- Confirm the exact asset inventory and ownership.
- Verify maintenance windows and blackout periods.
- Identify monitoring tools that may trigger alerts.
- Determine whether rate limits or lockout thresholds exist.
- Agree on safe test accounts and test data.
- Validate backup and rollback procedures for high-risk tests.

For example, if you are testing a web application with login controls, you should know whether account lockout is enabled and whether the client wants you to avoid triggering it:

```bash
# Example validation workflow
nmap -sV -Pn 203.0.113.10
curl -I https://app.example.com
ffuf -u https://app.example.com/FUZZ -w wordlist.txt -rate 50
```

These commands are only appropriate if the RoE allows scanning and content discovery. If the client prohibits aggressive enumeration, you must reduce intensity or stop.

**Authorization in practice**

Written authorization should be easy to verify during the engagement. Keep a copy of the approval documents and ensure the scope is accessible to the team. If the engagement changes, obtain updated approval before continuing.

A simple authorization checklist:

1. Confirm the client identity and asset ownership.
2. Review the signed MSA, SOW, RoE, and LoA.
3. Verify the in-scope targets and exclusions.
4. Confirm the test window and escalation contacts.
5. Record any special restrictions, such as no DoS or no social engineering.
6. Obtain written approval for any scope change before testing it.

**Common scope boundaries**

Different engagement types require different boundaries. You should tailor the scope to the assessment objective.

- **External testing**: focuses on internet-facing assets such as web apps, VPNs, mail gateways, and DNS.
- **Internal testing**: assumes network access inside the organization and may include segmentation testing.
- **Web application testing**: targets specific URLs, APIs, authentication flows, and backend integrations.
- **Wireless testing**: covers SSIDs, access points, encryption settings, and rogue device detection.
- **Social engineering**: requires explicit approval for phishing, vishing, smishing, or pretexting.
- **Physical testing**: requires clear permission for site access, badge testing, lock bypass attempts, or tailgating simulations.

Each of these can create different legal and operational risks. For example, a wireless assessment may affect neighboring tenants if the environment is shared, and a phishing simulation may trigger HR or legal concerns if employees are not informed in advance.

**Handling changes during the engagement**

Scope changes happen frequently. New assets may be discovered, business priorities may shift, or the client may ask for additional testing. You should treat every change as a formal update, not an informal request.

Best practices for scope changes:

- Document the requested change.
- Assess the risk and impact.
- Obtain written approval from the authorized client contact.
- Update the RoE and test plan.
- Notify the team before proceeding.

If a tester discovers an interesting but out-of-scope system, the correct response is to stop and request approval. Do not “just take a quick look.” That behavior can invalidate the engagement and create legal exposure.

**Evidence collection and reporting**

Evidence should support findings without exceeding the approved handling rules. Collect only what you need to prove the issue and protect sensitive data carefully.

Good evidence practices include:

- Capture screenshots that show the vulnerability and context.
- Save command output in a secure location.
- Redact secrets, tokens, and personal data when possible.
- Encrypt stored evidence at rest and in transit.
- Limit access to the report and raw artifacts.

Example of a concise proof-of-concept note:

```text
Target: https://app.example.com/login
Issue: SQL injection suspected in username parameter
Evidence: Error-based response observed with a single quote
Impact: Potential authentication bypass or data exposure
Status: Confirmed only within approved test account and scope
```

**Safety and risk management**

Ethical hacking should minimize business disruption. Some tests can cause instability even when performed correctly, especially on fragile systems or poorly configured applications.

Use caution with:

- **Denial-of-service testing**: may overwhelm services or shared infrastructure.
- **Password spraying**: may lock accounts or trigger security controls.
- **Exploit validation**: may crash services or alter data.
- **Privilege escalation**: may create persistent changes if not carefully controlled.
- **Cloud testing**: may incur cost, trigger autoscaling, or affect shared resources.

When in doubt, choose the least disruptive method that still validates the issue. For example, a safe validation may use a benign payload rather than a destructive one.

**Practical RoE template elements**

A complete RoE often includes the following sections:

- Engagement name and dates
- Client and tester contacts
- In-scope and out-of-scope assets
- Allowed and prohibited techniques
- Test windows and blackout periods
- Notification and escalation procedures
- Data retention and destruction requirements
- Reporting format and delivery method
- Emergency stop conditions
- Signatures and approval dates

A minimal template might look like this:

```text
Engagement: External Web Application Assessment
Scope: app.example.com, api.example.com
Allowed: authenticated scanning, manual testing, limited exploitation validation
Prohibited: DoS, persistence, data exfiltration, social engineering
Window: Mon-Fri 22:00-04:00 UTC
Escalation: security@example.com, +1-555-0100
Evidence: encrypted storage only
Stop condition: client request or service instability
```

**Exam focus points**

For CEH v13, focus on the relationship between the documents and the operational boundaries they create. You should be able to distinguish between scope, authorization, and RoE.

- **Scope** tells you what you may test.
- **Authorization** tells you that you are allowed to test.
- **Rules of Engagement** tell you how to test safely and responsibly.
- **Out-of-scope activity** is not permitted even if it seems useful.
- **Written approval** is essential for legal and ethical testing.

**Key takeaways**

Scoping and authorization are the foundation of every legitimate security assessment. Clear boundaries protect the client, the tester, and the integrity of the engagement. The best assessments are not the most aggressive ones; they are the ones that stay within approved limits, collect useful evidence, and deliver actionable results without unnecessary risk.
