## **Course:** Certified Ethical Hacker Complete Course (Exam CEH v13)

**Why reporting matters in ethical hacking**

A penetration test is only valuable if the findings are communicated clearly, accurately, and in a way the client can act on. In CEH v13, reporting is not an afterthought; it is the deliverable that turns technical discovery into business risk, remediation priorities, and measurable improvement.

A strong report usually contains three layers:

- **Executive summary** for leadership and non-technical stakeholders
- **Technical report** for engineers, administrators, and security teams
- **Retesting results** to verify remediation and close the loop

The best reports are evidence-based, reproducible, and scoped to the agreed rules of engagement. They avoid hype, separate facts from interpretation, and explain impact in terms the audience understands.

**Executive summary**

The executive summary is a concise, non-technical overview of the assessment. Its purpose is to help decision-makers understand the overall security posture without reading exploit details.

It should answer:

- What was tested
- Why the assessment was performed
- What the overall risk level is
- Which issues matter most
- What business impact may result
- What should happen next

Keep the language accessible. Avoid exploit names, payload details, or command output unless they are needed to explain business impact. Instead, translate technical findings into risk statements.

Typical executive summary content includes:

- **Assessment scope**: systems, applications, networks, or users tested
- **Methodology summary**: reconnaissance, scanning, exploitation, validation, and post-exploitation where authorized
- **High-level findings**: number and severity of issues
- **Risk posture**: overall exposure and likely consequences
- **Remediation priorities**: what to fix first and why
- **Limitations**: exclusions, time constraints, or inaccessible assets

A useful executive summary often uses plain business language:

- “An externally reachable web application allows unauthorized access to sensitive data.”
- “Weak password policy increases the likelihood of account compromise.”
- “Missing patch management exposes critical servers to known exploits.”

A simple structure works well:

1. State the objective of the assessment.
2. Summarize the overall security posture.
3. Highlight the most important risks.
4. Explain business impact in plain language.
5. Recommend next actions and ownership.

**Technical report**

The technical report is the detailed, evidence-driven record of what you tested and what you found. It should be precise enough that another qualified tester can reproduce the issue and that defenders can validate the fix.

A technical report typically includes:

- **Scope and authorization**
  - Client name, engagement dates, and approved targets
  - In-scope and out-of-scope assets
  - Testing windows and constraints
  - Rules of engagement and authorization references

- **Methodology**
  - Reconnaissance and enumeration
  - Vulnerability identification
  - Exploitation attempts
  - Privilege escalation, lateral movement, or persistence if authorized
  - Validation and cleanup

- **Findings**
  - Vulnerability title
  - Severity rating
  - Affected asset or application
  - Description and root cause
  - Evidence and reproduction steps
  - Impact
  - Remediation guidance
  - References to standards or advisories when relevant

- **Appendices**
  - Tool output
  - Screenshots
  - Packet captures
  - Logs
  - Hashes or checksums
  - Timeline of activity

A good finding is specific and testable. For example:

- **Finding title**: SQL injection in login parameter
- **Affected asset**: `https://app.example.com/login`
- **Root cause**: unsanitized user input passed to a database query
- **Evidence**: request/response pairs, error messages, or proof of data extraction
- **Impact**: unauthorized data access or account compromise
- **Fix**: parameterized queries, input validation, and least-privilege database accounts

Use consistent severity ratings. Many teams map findings to a qualitative scale such as Low, Medium, High, and Critical, often informed by CVSS (Common Vulnerability Scoring System). If you include CVSS, explain the score components briefly so the reader understands why the issue is severe.

A practical finding template:

```markdown
### Finding: Weak TLS Configuration

- Severity: High
- Affected asset: `mail.example.com:443`
- Description: The server supports deprecated cipher suites and TLS 1.0.
- Evidence: Scanner output and handshake verification show legacy protocol support.
- Impact: Increased risk of downgrade attacks and weak encryption exposure.
- Recommendation: Disable TLS 1.0/1.1, remove weak ciphers, and enforce modern cipher suites.
```

**Evidence collection and reproducibility**

Evidence is the backbone of the report. Without it, findings are hard to trust and harder to fix.

Capture enough detail to support validation:

- Exact target IPs, hostnames, URLs, and ports
- Timestamped commands and outputs
- Screenshots of successful exploitation or misconfiguration
- Request and response samples for web issues
- Logs showing authentication failures, privilege changes, or service behavior
- Version numbers and patch levels
- Hashes for files or payloads when relevant

When documenting commands, include the context needed to reproduce safely:

```bash
nmap -sV -sC -Pn -p 22,80,443 192.0.2.10
curl -i https://app.example.com/login
nikto -h https://app.example.com
```

For web testing, request/response evidence is often more useful than a screenshot alone:

```http
POST /login HTTP/1.1
Host: app.example.com
Content-Type: application/x-www-form-urlencoded

username=admin'--&password=test
```

```http
HTTP/1.1 500 Internal Server Error
...
```

That kind of evidence shows the exact input, the observed behavior, and why the issue matters.

> Do not include live secrets, production credentials, or destructive payloads in a report unless the engagement explicitly requires them and the client approves their inclusion.

**Writing effective remediation guidance**

Remediation should be actionable, prioritized, and realistic. Avoid vague advice like “improve security.” Instead, tell the client what to change and in what order.

Good remediation guidance usually includes:

- **Immediate containment**: disable exposed services, rotate credentials, block malicious access
- **Root-cause fix**: patch software, correct configuration, redesign insecure logic
- **Compensating controls**: WAF (Web Application Firewall), segmentation, MFA (Multi-Factor Authentication), monitoring
- **Verification steps**: how to confirm the fix worked

Examples:

- Replace hardcoded credentials with a secrets manager.
- Enforce MFA for administrative accounts.
- Patch the vulnerable package to a fixed version.
- Restrict management interfaces to a trusted subnet.
- Add server-side input validation and parameterized database queries.

When possible, tie remediation to ownership:

- Application team for code changes
- Infrastructure team for host hardening
- Identity team for authentication controls
- Security operations for monitoring and alerting

**Retesting**

Retesting verifies whether the client fixed the issues identified in the original assessment. It is a separate activity from the initial test and should be scoped clearly.

Retesting answers three questions:

- Was the vulnerability actually fixed?
- Did the fix introduce any new issues?
- Is the asset now protected against the original attack path?

Retesting is important because a patch or configuration change may only partially solve the problem. For example, a vulnerable service might be updated but still exposed through an alternate port, or a web fix might block one payload while leaving the underlying flaw intact.

A retest process usually follows these steps:

1. Review the original finding and remediation plan.
2. Confirm the asset, version, or configuration change.
3. Re-run the original validation safely.
4. Test for bypasses or alternate attack paths.
5. Record the result as fixed, partially fixed, or unresolved.
6. Update the report with evidence and timestamps.

Retesting evidence should be as clear as the original evidence. Show the before-and-after state:

- Original vulnerable behavior
- Remediated configuration or version
- Validation that the issue no longer reproduces
- Any residual risk or follow-up actions

A concise retest note might look like this:

```markdown
### Retest Result: Weak TLS Configuration

- Original status: TLS 1.0 and weak ciphers enabled
- Remediation applied: TLS 1.0/1.1 disabled; strong cipher suites enforced
- Validation: `nmap --script ssl-enum-ciphers -p 443 mail.example.com` no longer reports deprecated protocols
- Result: Fixed
```

**Common report quality issues**

Poor reports create confusion and delay remediation. Watch for these problems:

- **Ambiguous findings**: “Server is insecure” without a specific issue
- **Missing evidence**: no screenshots, logs, or reproduction steps
- **Unclear severity**: ratings that do not match the actual impact
- **Overly technical executive summaries**: leadership cannot act on them
- **No remediation path**: findings without fix guidance
- **Inconsistent terminology**: different names for the same asset or issue
- **Unverified retest claims**: stating a fix worked without testing it

A report should be internally consistent. The executive summary, technical findings, and retest results must all align with the same scope, dates, and asset names.

**Practical report workflow**

A repeatable workflow helps you produce reliable deliverables:

1. Collect evidence during testing with timestamps and asset identifiers.
2. Organize findings by severity and affected system.
3. Draft the executive summary after the technical analysis is complete.
4. Write each finding with description, impact, evidence, and remediation.
5. Review for accuracy, scope alignment, and terminology consistency.
6. Deliver the report securely to authorized stakeholders.
7. Retest after remediation and update the original findings.

This workflow reduces omissions and makes the report easier to defend during review meetings.

**Example technical finding format**

```markdown
### Finding: Default Credentials on Administrative Interface

- Severity: Critical
- Affected asset: `192.0.2.25:8443`
- Description: The administrative portal accepts vendor default credentials.
- Evidence: Successful login with documented default account during authorized testing.
- Impact: Full administrative access to the management interface.
- Recommendation: Change default credentials, disable unused default accounts, and enforce MFA.
- Retest status: Pending
```

**What exam questions often test**

CEH-style questions on this topic usually focus on the purpose and audience of each report type, the importance of evidence, and the meaning of retesting.

Remember these distinctions:

- **Executive summary**: business-focused, concise, non-technical
- **Technical report**: detailed, reproducible, evidence-based
- **Retesting**: verification that remediation actually resolved the issue

If you keep the audience, evidence, and validation goals clear, you can produce reports that are both professional and operationally useful.
