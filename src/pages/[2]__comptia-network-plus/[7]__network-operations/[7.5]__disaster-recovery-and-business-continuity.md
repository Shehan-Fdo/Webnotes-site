## **Course:** Comptia Network Plus

**Disaster recovery planning**

- A **disaster recovery plan (DRP)** defines how you respond to an outage or major problem.
- The DRP covers the steps to restore critical IT systems, networks, and data.
- Use the DRP to prepare for events such as natural disasters, hardware failures, cyberattacks, and human error.
- Keep the plan organization-wide so recovery actions stay coordinated.

**Business continuity focus**

- **Business continuity** keeps essential operations running during and after disruption.
- Disaster recovery supports continuity by restoring IT services.
- Plan for both immediate response and longer-term recovery.
- Use continuity planning to reduce downtime and data loss.

**Backups and replication**

- Backups create copies of data for later restoration.
- Offsite data replication keeps data available outside the primary site.
- Cloud-based alternatives can replace on-site systems during recovery.
- Third-party services can add recovery support when internal resources are limited.

**Recovery site options**

- A remote recovery site can take over operations after a disaster.
- A cloud environment can host replacement services when on-site systems fail.
- Choose a site option that matches the organization’s recovery needs.
- Test the site setup before an actual outage.

**Recovery metrics**

- **RTO** means recovery time objective.
- **RPO** means recovery point objective.
- Use RTO to define how quickly you must restore service.
- Use RPO to define how much data loss is acceptable.
- Track metrics such as **MTBF**, **MTTF**, and **MTTR** to measure reliability and recovery performance.

**Testing and maintenance**

- Test disaster recovery procedures before an incident occurs.
- Verify that backups, replication, and recovery sites work as expected.
- Update the DRP when systems or requirements change.
- Regular testing improves confidence in the recovery process.

**Planning priorities**

- Identify the services that matter most to the organization.
- Restore critical systems first.
- Match recovery methods to the type of disruption.
- Keep documentation current so recovery steps stay usable.
