## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Professional (Exam SAP-C02)

**Why this topic matters**

Hybrid and multi-Region architectures often look simple on a whiteboard but become expensive in production because of data transfer, replication, health checks, routing, and duplicated infrastructure. In SAP-C02 case studies, you are usually choosing the least-cost design that still meets recovery time objective (<abbr title="Recovery Time Objective">RTO</abbr>), recovery point objective (<abbr title="Recovery Point Objective">RPO</abbr>), latency, compliance, and operational constraints.

**Core decision framework**

Before comparing services, identify the dominant cost driver:

- **Traffic path**: internet, private backbone, inter-Region, or on-premises.
- **Statefulness**: stateless apps are cheaper to duplicate than stateful systems.
- **Recovery target**: lower RTO and RPO usually increase cost.
- **Scale pattern**: steady traffic, bursty traffic, or rare disaster recovery.
- **Governance**: cross-account, cross-Region, and regulated data movement can add controls and storage overhead.

A useful exam habit is to ask: “What is the cheapest architecture that still meets the recovery objective?” Not “What is the most resilient architecture?”

**Multi-AZ versus multi-Region tradeoff**

Multi-AZ is usually the first resiliency step because it gives high availability within one Region with lower complexity than multi-Region. Multi-Region improves geographic resilience and can reduce blast radius, but it almost always increases cost because you duplicate more infrastructure and pay for inter-Region data movement.

- **Multi-AZ**
  - Lower cost than multi-Region.
  - Better for high availability within a Region.
  - Usually simpler for databases, load balancers, and failover.
  - Does not protect against Region-wide outages.

- **Multi-Region**
  - Higher cost due to duplicated stacks, replication, and routing.
  - Better for disaster recovery and geographic latency.
  - Requires careful data consistency planning.
  - Often justified only when business impact of a Region outage is high.

For exam scenarios, choose multi-AZ when the requirement is availability, and choose multi-Region when the requirement is regional disaster recovery, regulatory separation, or global latency.

**Hidden cost drivers in hybrid connectivity**

Hybrid designs often fail cost reviews because the connectivity layer is more expensive than the application layer.

- **NAT Gateway**
  - Charges accrue for hourly usage and data processed.
  - Becomes expensive when many private subnets send large volumes of outbound traffic to the internet.
  - Often replaced by VPC endpoints for AWS services to avoid NAT traversal.
  - A common optimization is to route S3 and DynamoDB traffic through gateway endpoints instead of NAT.

- **Transit Gateway (<abbr title="Transit Gateway">TGW</abbr>)**
  - Adds attachment charges and data processing charges.
  - Scales well for many VPCs and on-premises connections.
  - Usually cheaper operationally than many full-mesh VPC peering links at large scale.
  - Can become costly if you move large east-west traffic volumes through it unnecessarily.

- **VPC peering**
  - No hourly attachment fee, but data transfer charges still apply.
  - Works well for a small number of VPCs.
  - Does not support transitive routing.
  - Becomes operationally awkward and expensive to manage at scale because each pair needs routing and security coordination.

- **Direct Connect (<abbr title="AWS Direct Connect">DX</abbr>)**
  - Useful for predictable private connectivity and reduced internet variability.
  - Costs include port hours, data transfer, and possibly colocation/provider charges.
  - Often paired with VPN as backup rather than as a standalone DR strategy.
  - The cheapest option depends on traffic volume and whether you need private routing, consistent latency, or hybrid failover.

- **Site-to-Site VPN**
  - Lower setup cost than DX.
  - Uses the internet, so latency and jitter are less predictable.
  - Good for backup connectivity or smaller environments.
  - Can be part of a failover design with DX.

**Choosing between VPC peering, Transit Gateway, and Direct Connect Gateway**

Use the simplest option that meets scale and routing needs.

| Option | Best fit | Cost pattern | Key limitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| VPC peering | Few VPCs, simple point-to-point connectivity | No central hub fee, but data transfer still applies | No transitive routing |
| Transit Gateway | Many VPCs, centralized routing, shared services | Attachment and data processing charges | Can add cost if used for unnecessary hairpinning |
| Direct Connect Gateway | Multiple VPCs or Regions over DX | DX port and data charges plus associated routing components | Requires DX design and routing discipline |

A common exam answer is:
- Use **VPC peering** for a small, stable topology.
- Use **Transit Gateway** for hub-and-spoke scale.
- Use **Direct Connect Gateway** when you need DX connectivity to multiple VPCs or Regions.

**DNS failover cost and behavior**

Amazon Route 53 is often used for DNS-based failover because it is simple and low operational overhead, but DNS is not instant failover. Recovery speed depends on TTL (<abbr title="Time To Live">TTL</abbr>), resolver caching, and health check timing.

- **TTL impact**
  - Lower TTL values reduce how long clients may keep stale records.
  - Lower TTL can increase DNS query volume and cost.
  - Very low TTLs do not guarantee immediate client-side cutover because recursive resolvers may still cache responses.

- **Health check timing**
  - Route 53 health checks detect endpoint failure based on configured intervals and thresholds.
  - Faster detection usually means more checks and potentially more cost.
  - Health checks should match the actual endpoint behavior, especially for HTTPS and hostname-based targets.

- **Caching reality**
  - Clients and recursive resolvers may continue using cached answers until TTL expiry.
  - DNS failover is therefore best for non-zero RTOs where a short delay is acceptable.
  - For near-immediate traffic shifting, consider load balancer or accelerator-based approaches.

> DNS failover is not instantaneous. If the business expects sub-minute recovery, you must account for TTL, resolver caching, and health check detection time.

**Route 53 failover pattern**

A typical active/passive design uses a primary endpoint and a secondary endpoint with health checks.

```bash
aws route53 create-health-check \
  --caller-reference "$(date +%s)" \
  --health-check-config '{
    "Type":"HTTPS",
    "FullyQualifiedDomainName":"app.example.com",
    "Port":443,
    "ResourcePath":"/health",
    "RequestInterval":30,
    "FailureThreshold":3
  }'
```

Practical notes:

- Use the hostname that clients actually resolve when possible.
- Ensure the certificate and server name indication (<abbr title="Server Name Indication">SNI</abbr>) behavior matches the endpoint.
- For an Application Load Balancer, validate the health endpoint path and TLS configuration carefully.
- If you need alias records, use the correct hosted zone ID for the specific AWS target and Region rather than assuming a universal value.

Example failover record set pattern:

```json
{
  "Comment": "Primary and secondary failover records",
  "Changes": [
    {
      "Action": "UPSERT",
      "ResourceRecordSet": {
        "Name": "app.example.com",
        "Type": "A",
        "SetIdentifier": "primary",
        "Failover": "PRIMARY",
        "AliasTarget": {
          "HostedZoneId": "ZXXXXXXXXXXXXX",
          "DNSName": "primary-alb-123.us-east-1.elb.amazonaws.com",
          "EvaluateTargetHealth": true
        }
      }
    },
    {
      "Action": "UPSERT",
      "ResourceRecordSet": {
        "Name": "app.example.com",
        "Type": "A",
        "SetIdentifier": "secondary",
        "Failover": "SECONDARY",
        "AliasTarget": {
          "HostedZoneId": "ZYYYYYYYYYYYYY",
          "DNSName": "secondary-alb-456.us-west-2.elb.amazonaws.com",
          "EvaluateTargetHealth": true
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

**Global Accelerator cost and use case**

AWS Global Accelerator is useful when you want static anycast IPs, fast edge-based routing, and health-based failover without relying on DNS propagation. It is especially useful for TCP and UDP workloads or when you want to avoid client-side DNS caching delays.

- **Strengths**
  - Provides static IP addresses.
  - Uses AWS edge locations to route users to healthy endpoints.
  - Improves failover behavior compared with DNS-only designs.
  - Works well for global applications with latency-sensitive ingress.

- **Cost considerations**
  - You pay for accelerator hours and data transfer.
  - It is not a generic replacement for all outbound internet egress optimization.
  - It is usually justified when faster failover, static IPs, or global ingress performance matter more than the added cost.

- **When to prefer it**
  - You need fast failover for public endpoints.
  - You need fixed IPs for allowlists.
  - You want to reduce the operational complexity of DNS-based traffic shifting.

A correct workflow is to create the accelerator, capture the returned ARN, then use that ARN when creating listeners and endpoint groups.

```bash
ACCEL_ARN=$(aws globalaccelerator create-accelerator \
  --name prod-accel \
  --ip-address-type IPV4 \
  --enabled \
  --query 'Accelerator.AcceleratorArn' \
  --output text)

LISTENER_ARN=$(aws globalaccelerator create-listener \
  --accelerator-arn "$ACCEL_ARN" \
  --protocol TCP \
  --port-ranges FromPort=443,ToPort=443 \
  --query 'Listener.ListenerArn' \
  --output text)

aws globalaccelerator create-endpoint-group \
  --listener-arn "$LISTENER_ARN" \
  --endpoint-group-region us-east-1 \
  --endpoint-configurations EndpointId=arn:aws:elasticloadbalancing:us-east-1:123456789012:loadbalancer/app/prod-alb/50dc6c495c0c9188,Weight=128
```

**S3 cross-Region cost patterns**

Amazon S3 is often central in multi-Region designs because it is easy to replicate, archive, and restore. The cost model depends on storage, requests, replication, and retrieval.

- **Cross-Region Replication (<abbr title="Cross-Region Replication">CRR</abbr>)**
  - Replication adds request costs and inter-Region data transfer.
  - You also pay for storage in the destination Region.
  - Useful for compliance, DR, and low-RPO requirements.
  - Can be expensive for high-churn buckets with many small objects.

- **Replication Time Control (<abbr title="Replication Time Control">RTC</abbr>)**
  - Adds a premium for predictable replication timing.
  - Useful when you need an SLA-like replication window.
  - Not needed for every DR workload.
  - Choose it only when the business value of predictable replication exceeds the extra cost.

- **Request costs versus storage costs**
  - Many small objects can generate significant PUT, GET, and replication request charges.
  - Large archives may be dominated by storage rather than request cost.
  - Lifecycle policies can reduce storage cost by moving data to cheaper classes.
  - Versioning and delete markers can increase storage footprint if not managed.

Example replication configuration concept:

```json
{
  "Role": "arn:aws:iam::123456789012:role/s3-replication-role",
  "Rules": [
    {
      "Status": "Enabled",
      "Priority": 1,
      "DeleteMarkerReplication": { "Status": "Disabled" },
      "Filter": { "Prefix": "critical/" },
      "Destination": {
        "Bucket": "arn:aws:s3:::dr-bucket-us-west-2",
        "StorageClass": "STANDARD"
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

**Hybrid data movement options**

Not all data transfer should happen over always-on network links. AWS provides services that change the cost profile depending on data volume, frequency, and urgency.

- **AWS DataSync**
  - Managed service for online data transfer.
  - Good for recurring file transfers, migrations, and synchronization.
  - Often cheaper operationally than building custom transfer scripts.
  - Useful when you need scheduled or incremental movement.

- **AWS Storage Gateway**
  - Bridges on-premises environments with AWS storage.
  - Useful for hybrid backup, file, and volume use cases.
  - Can reduce the need to keep all data on-premises.
  - Cost depends on gateway type and storage consumption.

- **AWS Snowball**
  - Physical data transfer for large one-time migrations or disconnected environments.
  - Often cheaper and faster than network transfer for very large datasets.
  - Useful when bandwidth is limited or transfer windows are short.
  - Not ideal for continuous synchronization.

Decision rule:
- Use **DataSync** for recurring online transfers.
- Use **Storage Gateway** for hybrid storage integration.
- Use **Snowball** for bulk offline migration.

**Backup and disaster recovery cost considerations**

Cross-account and cross-Region backup strategies are common in enterprise case studies because they improve governance and blast-radius isolation.

- **AWS Backup**
  - Centralizes backup policy management.
  - Supports cross-account and cross-Region copy jobs.
  - Adds backup storage and copy costs.
  - Helps enforce retention and compliance with less operational overhead.

- **Cross-account backup**
  - Improves separation of duties.
  - Protects against accidental deletion in the source account.
  - Often required for security and audit controls.
  - May require additional IAM, KMS (<abbr title="Key Management Service">KMS</abbr>), and vault access policy design.

- **Cross-Region backup**
  - Improves disaster recovery posture.
  - Adds storage and transfer costs.
  - Should be reserved for data that justifies the extra resilience.
  - Not every dataset needs cross-Region copies.

A practical governance pattern is to keep production backups in a dedicated backup account with restricted access and copy only the datasets that have a real recovery requirement.

**Direct Connect plus VPN failover architecture**

A common hybrid DR design uses Direct Connect for primary private connectivity and VPN as backup. This gives predictable performance during normal operation and a fallback path if the DX circuit fails.

```text
On-premises
   |
   +-- Direct Connect --> Direct Connect Gateway --> Transit Gateway --> VPCs
   |
   +-- Site-to-Site VPN --------------------------> Transit Gateway --> VPCs
```

Operational points:

- Use BGP (<abbr title="Border Gateway Protocol">BGP</abbr>) to advertise routes over both paths.
- Prefer DX routes with more specific prefixes or lower path cost where appropriate.
- Keep VPN configured and tested so failover is not theoretical.
- Validate route propagation in Transit Gateway route tables.

Example route propagation and association workflow:

```bash
aws ec2 create-transit-gateway-route-table --transit-gateway-id tgw-0123456789abcdef0

aws ec2 associate-transit-gateway-route-table \
  --transit-gateway-route-table-id tgw-rtb-0123456789abcdef0 \
  --transit-gateway-attachment-id tgw-attach-0123456789abcdef0

aws ec2 enable-transit-gateway-route-table-propagation \
  --transit-gateway-route-table-id tgw-rtb-0123456789abcdef0 \
  --transit-gateway-attachment-id tgw-attach-0123456789abcdef0
```

**When to use Direct Connect Gateway**

Direct Connect Gateway is useful when you need to connect a DX circuit to multiple VPCs, often across Regions, without building separate point-to-point designs for each VPC.

- Use it when:
  - You have multiple VPCs or Regions to reach from on-premises.
  - You want centralized DX routing.
  - You need to reduce routing complexity compared with many separate connections.

- Avoid it when:
  - You only have one small VPC and simple connectivity needs.
  - The added routing abstraction does not justify the operational overhead.
  - A VPN or simpler peering design meets the requirement at lower cost.

**Cost-aware architecture patterns**

The following patterns show how to think like an exam architect.

- **Pattern: low-cost DR for a mostly static web app**
  - Primary Region runs the app.
  - Secondary Region keeps minimal infrastructure warm.
  - S3 CRR replicates only critical assets.
  - Route 53 failover handles DNS-based cutover.
  - This is cheaper than active-active, but recovery is slower.

- **Pattern: global low-latency ingress**
  - Use Global Accelerator or CloudFront depending on protocol and caching needs.
  - Choose Global Accelerator for non-HTTP protocols or static IP requirements.
  - Choose CloudFront when caching and content delivery reduce origin load and egress.
  - Do not use Global Accelerator just because it sounds “more global.”

- **Pattern: enterprise hybrid hub**
  - Use Transit Gateway as the central routing hub.
  - Attach VPCs and DX/VPN to the hub.
  - Use VPC endpoints to reduce NAT traffic.
  - This is usually more scalable than many VPC peering links.

- **Pattern: bulk migration**
  - Use Snowball for very large initial loads.
  - Use DataSync for incremental catch-up.
  - Avoid paying prolonged network transfer costs for one-time migrations.
  - This is often the cheapest path when bandwidth is constrained.

**Exam-style decision cues**

Look for these clues in the question stem:

- **“Lowest cost with acceptable RTO”**
  - Prefer multi-AZ, Route 53 failover, or warm standby over active-active multi-Region.
  - Avoid overbuilding with duplicate full stacks.

- **“Private connectivity with predictable latency”**
  - Consider Direct Connect, possibly with VPN backup.
  - Use Transit Gateway if many networks must connect.

- **“Many VPCs and shared services”**
  - Prefer Transit Gateway over VPC peering.
  - Use peering only for a small number of links.

- **“Large data migration”**
  - Consider Snowball or DataSync before continuous network transfer.
  - Evaluate request and replication costs for S3-based designs.

- **“Fast global failover”**
  - Consider Global Accelerator before DNS-only failover.
  - Use Route 53 when DNS delay is acceptable and cost simplicity matters.

**Common mistakes to avoid**

- Assuming the cheapest design is always the one with the fewest AWS services.
- Ignoring data transfer charges while focusing only on compute.
- Using VPC peering for a hub-and-spoke topology that should use Transit Gateway.
- Choosing multi-Region active-active when the requirement only needs regional failover.
- Forgetting that DNS caching delays recovery even when health checks fail quickly.
- Replicating all data cross-Region when only a subset is needed for DR.
- Treating Direct Connect as a universal cost saver without modeling port, transfer, and routing costs.

**Practical cost checklist**

Before finalizing a design, verify:

1. Which traffic crosses AZ, Region, or on-prem boundaries.
2. Whether NAT Gateway can be reduced with VPC endpoints.
3. Whether TGW is justified by scale or whether peering is enough.
4. Whether S3 replication is required for all objects or only critical prefixes.
5. Whether DNS failover timing meets the RTO after TTL and caching.
6. Whether backup copies need cross-account or cross-Region isolation.
7. Whether DX, VPN, DataSync, or Snowball is the cheapest transfer path for the data volume.

**Summary**

Hybrid and multi-Region cost drivers are usually dominated by data movement, not by the headline service itself. In SAP-C02 case studies, the best answer is rarely “use everything.” It is usually the smallest architecture that satisfies recovery, latency, and governance requirements while minimizing duplicated infrastructure and unnecessary transfer charges.
