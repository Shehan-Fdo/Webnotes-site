## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Professional (Exam SAP-C02)

**Cost optimization strategy overview**

AWS cost optimization for compute usually starts with three questions: how steady is the workload, how much capacity must be guaranteed, and how much flexibility do you need for future changes. Savings Plans and Reserved Instances (RIs) reduce cost for predictable usage, while right-sizing reduces waste before you commit to discounts.

A strong SAP-C02 answer usually distinguishes between:

- **Commitment-based discounts** for steady usage
- **Capacity guarantees** for workloads that must launch at a specific time or place
- **Elastic scaling** for bursty or uncertain demand
- **Measurement and recommendation tools** that help you choose the right mix

**Savings Plans fundamentals**

Savings Plans are a **dollar-per-hour commitment**. You commit to a fixed hourly spend for a term, and AWS applies the discount automatically to eligible usage up to that hourly commitment. This is different from RIs, which are tied more directly to instance attributes and capacity scope.

Key mechanics:

- You commit to an hourly amount for a term, commonly 1 or 3 years.
- AWS applies the discount to eligible usage as it runs.
- If eligible usage is below the hourly commitment, you still pay for the unused portion.
- If eligible usage exceeds the commitment, the excess is billed at normal rates or other applicable rates.
- The commitment is owned by the purchasing account, but eligible usage can be covered across linked accounts in the same billing family or AWS Organization, depending on the plan type and billing setup.

Savings Plans are best when you want flexibility and predictable savings without locking into a specific instance family or size in every case.

**Savings Plans types and when to use them**

There are two main Savings Plans types you should know for exam scenarios:

| Type | Scope | Flexibility | Typical use case |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Compute Savings Plans** | Broad compute usage | Highest flexibility | Mixed EC2, Fargate, and Lambda usage; changing instance families or Regions |
| **EC2 Instance Savings Plans** | Specific EC2 instance family in a Region | Less flexible, usually deeper discount | Stable EC2 fleets with known family and Region |

Compute Savings Plans generally provide the most operational flexibility. EC2 Instance Savings Plans typically offer a deeper discount than Compute Savings Plans, but only when your usage matches the chosen family and Region.

Practical guidance:

- Choose **Compute Savings Plans** when you expect architecture changes, instance family changes, or a mix of EC2, Fargate, and Lambda.
- Choose **EC2 Instance Savings Plans** when you have a stable EC2 baseline in one family and Region.
- Use **On-Demand** for burst capacity above your committed baseline.
- Reassess commitments when traffic patterns, architecture, or instance families change.

**Savings Plans coverage and billing behavior**

Coverage means AWS applies the commitment to eligible usage first, then bills any remaining usage separately. This is important for exam questions about partial utilization and overcommitment.

Typical behavior:

- Eligible usage is matched against the hourly commitment.
- The commitment is consumed as usage accrues during the hour.
- If the hour’s eligible usage is less than the commitment, the unused commitment is still charged.
- If the hour’s eligible usage is greater than the commitment, only the covered portion receives the discount.

Example scenario:

```text
Hourly Savings Plan commitment: $10/hour
Eligible usage in an hour: $7/hour
Result: $7 is discounted, $3 is unused but still charged

Hourly Savings Plan commitment: $10/hour
Eligible usage in an hour: $14/hour
Result: $10 is discounted, $4 is billed separately
```

This is why overcommitting is a real risk. You want the commitment to match a stable baseline, not peak demand.

**Reserved Instances fundamentals**

Reserved Instances are a discount model tied to specific service attributes. For EC2, RIs are commonly used to reduce cost for predictable workloads, but the discount application depends on scope, platform, tenancy, Region, and instance attributes.

Important distinctions:

- RIs are not a general compute commitment like Savings Plans.
- EC2 RIs are more constrained by instance family, Region, and other attributes.
- Some RI types provide capacity reservation benefits in addition to pricing discounts.
- RI discounts are applied differently from Savings Plans and can be more restrictive in exchange for potentially better savings.

For exam purposes, think of RIs as a more specific instrument and Savings Plans as a more flexible commitment.

**EC2 Reserved Instance scope and flexibility**

EC2 RIs come in scope options that affect how the discount applies:

- **Regional RIs**
  - Apply to matching usage in a Region.
  - Often support **instance size flexibility** for many Linux/UNIX platforms within the same instance family.
  - Do not guarantee capacity.
- **Zonal RIs**
  - Apply to a specific Availability Zone.
  - Provide a capacity reservation in that AZ for matching instances.
  - Are useful when you need both discounting and capacity assurance.

Important exam nuance:

- Instance size flexibility is not universal.
- It is commonly available for Regional Standard RIs on many Linux/UNIX platforms.
- Windows, SQL Server, and some other platform or tenancy combinations have restrictions.
- Always match the RI’s platform, tenancy, Region, and family to the workload.

**Standard vs Convertible Reserved Instances**

Standard and Convertible RIs solve different problems.

| RI type | Main advantage | Main limitation | Best fit |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Standard RI** | Usually deeper discount | Less flexibility | Stable, long-lived workloads |
| **Convertible RI** | Can exchange for another Convertible RI | Lower discount than Standard RI | Workloads likely to change over time |

Standard RI notes:

- Standard RIs are the better choice when the workload is stable and you want maximum discount.
- Some Standard RIs may be eligible for sale on the RI Marketplace, but only certain EC2 Standard Reserved Instances qualify.
- Convertible RIs are not sold on the RI Marketplace.

Convertible RI notes:

- You can exchange Convertible RIs for other Convertible RIs.
- The new reservation must satisfy AWS exchange rules at the time of exchange.
- Exchanges are useful when you need to change instance family, platform, or other attributes without losing all commitment value.
- Do not assume you can freely reduce commitment value below the original equivalent; the exchange must meet AWS’s value and eligibility rules.

**RI eligibility and matching rules**

Reserved Instance discounts only apply when the workload matches the reservation attributes closely enough.

Common matching dimensions:

- **Region**
- **Availability Zone** for zonal reservations
- **Instance family**
- **Instance type**
- **Platform** such as Linux, Windows, or SQL Server
- **Tenancy** such as default, dedicated instance, or dedicated host
- **Scope** and size flexibility rules

Exam traps:

- A Linux RI does not discount a Windows instance.
- A shared-tenancy RI does not automatically apply to dedicated tenancy.
- A Region mismatch prevents discount application.
- A Zonal RI can help with capacity, but it is less flexible than a Regional RI.
- A Convertible RI exchange does not mean unlimited freedom to reshape the reservation.

**Savings Plans vs Reserved Instances**

Use this comparison to choose the right tool in scenario questions.

| Feature | Savings Plans | Reserved Instances |
|---|---|---|
| Commitment model | Dollar-per-hour | Instance or capacity-oriented discount model |
| Flexibility | Higher | Lower |
| Best for | Evolving compute usage | Stable, known workloads |
| Coverage behavior | Applies to eligible usage until hourly commitment is consumed | Applies when instance attributes match reservation rules |
| Capacity guarantee | No | Zonal RIs can reserve capacity |
| Cross-service coverage | Compute Savings Plans can cover EC2, Fargate, and Lambda eligible usage | EC2 RIs are EC2-specific |

A practical rule of thumb:

- Use **Savings Plans** for broad, flexible savings.
- Use **RIs** when you know the exact EC2 shape and want to optimize more aggressively.
- Use **Zonal RIs** when capacity assurance matters.
- Use **On-Demand** for unpredictable or bursty demand.

**Common mixed-purchase strategy**

Many real workloads use a layered approach:

- Buy a Savings Plan for the steady baseline.
- Keep On-Demand for burst capacity.
- Add RIs only where the workload is highly stable and the instance attributes are unlikely to change.

Example topology:

```text
Baseline steady usage  -> Savings Plan
Known fixed EC2 fleet  -> EC2 Instance RI or Standard RI
Burst traffic spikes    -> On-Demand
Capacity-sensitive AZ   -> Zonal RI
```

This approach reduces risk because you avoid overcommitting the entire workload.

**Capacity Reservations and host-related capacity planning**

Capacity Reservations are about ensuring that EC2 capacity is available in a specific Availability Zone when you need it. They are primarily a capacity assurance feature, not a general discount mechanism.

Key points:

- You reserve EC2 capacity in a specific AZ.
- Matching On-Demand instances can consume that reserved capacity.
- You pay for the reserved capacity while it is reserved.
- Capacity Reservations are useful for launch certainty, disaster recovery readiness, and tightly controlled deployment windows.

Do not confuse this with billing discounts:

- Savings Plans and RIs reduce compute cost.
- Capacity Reservations ensure capacity exists.
- They solve different problems, even though they can be used together.

For advanced scenarios, Dedicated Hosts can also matter:

- A **Dedicated Host** gives you physical server-level isolation and visibility.
- Dedicated Host reservations can reduce cost for steady dedicated-host usage.
- They are relevant when licensing, compliance, or tenancy requirements drive the architecture.
- They are not the same as standard EC2 RIs or Savings Plans.

**Right-sizing principles**

Right-sizing means matching resource size to actual demand before you commit to long-term discounts. If you right-size first, you avoid paying for waste for the entire term.

Common right-sizing signals:

- Low average CPU utilization with large headroom
- Low memory pressure and low swap usage
- Network and disk metrics far below provisioned capacity
- Frequent scale-in opportunities in Auto Scaling groups
- Lambda functions with excess memory allocation relative to runtime behavior

Right-sizing is not just about reducing instance size. Sometimes increasing memory or changing architecture reduces total cost.

**EC2 right-sizing examples**

For EC2, use CloudWatch metrics and workload behavior to decide whether to resize.

Useful metrics:

- `CPUUtilization`
- `NetworkIn` and `NetworkOut`
- `DiskReadOps` and `DiskWriteOps`
- `StatusCheckFailed`
- Memory metrics if you publish them via the CloudWatch agent

Practical examples:

- If average CPU stays below 10–15% and memory is also low, you may be overprovisioned.
- If CPU is low but memory pressure is high, a smaller CPU instance may not help; you may need a different family with better memory characteristics.
- If network throughput is the bottleneck, changing instance family may matter more than changing size.

Example CloudWatch Agent snippet for memory visibility:

```json
{
  "metrics": {
    "metrics_collected": {
      "mem": {
        "measurement": [
          "mem_used_percent"
        ]
      }
    }
  }
}
```

**Auto Scaling right-sizing**

Auto Scaling helps you right-size by adjusting capacity to demand instead of permanently overprovisioning.

Best practices:

- Set scaling policies based on meaningful workload metrics, not only CPU.
- Use target tracking when the workload has a stable relationship between demand and a metric.
- Use step scaling when demand changes in larger jumps.
- Review minimum, desired, and maximum capacity regularly.

Example target tracking policy concept:

```json
{
  "TargetValue": 50.0,
  "PredefinedMetricSpecification": {
    "PredefinedMetricType": "ASGAverageCPUUtilization"
  }
}
```

Interpretation:

- If average utilization rises above target, scale out.
- If it falls below target, scale in.
- This reduces the need to buy excess reserved capacity for peak load.

**ECS and Fargate right-sizing**

For Amazon ECS, right-sizing often means adjusting task CPU and memory reservations to match actual usage.

What to look for:

- Tasks that consistently use far less CPU or memory than requested
- Services that scale out before tasks are saturated
- Container memory limits that trigger restarts or throttling
- Overly large task definitions that waste Fargate spend

Practical guidance:

- Reduce task CPU and memory if metrics show sustained headroom.
- Increase memory if tasks are OOM-killed or GC-heavy.
- Use service autoscaling to absorb burst demand.
- Consider Compute Savings Plans if you run a mix of EC2, ECS on Fargate, and Lambda.

**Lambda right-sizing**

Lambda cost is driven by memory size and execution duration, so right-sizing memory can reduce total cost even if the configured memory value increases.

Why this happens:

- More memory also allocates more CPU.
- Faster execution can reduce billed duration enough to offset the higher memory setting.
- The optimal setting is often not the smallest memory size.

Practical approach:

- Test several memory settings.
- Compare duration, billed duration, and total cost.
- Look for the point where higher memory reduces runtime enough to lower total cost.

Example CLI invocation for a test run:

```bash
aws lambda invoke \
  --function-name my-function \
  --payload '{"action":"benchmark"}' \
  response.json
```

**Compute Optimizer and recommendation tools**

AWS Compute Optimizer helps identify underutilized or overprovisioned resources. It is useful for EC2, Auto Scaling groups, Lambda, and some other resource types, but recommendations are only as good as the data available.

Important limitations and inputs:

- It needs sufficient historical utilization data before recommendations become reliable.
- Sparse or newly created resources may not produce strong recommendations.
- It works best when workloads have stable patterns.
- It should be combined with application knowledge, not used blindly.

Use recommendation tools for:

- Identifying overprovisioned instances
- Finding candidate instance families or sizes
- Validating whether a workload can be downsized before buying commitments
- Estimating savings from a proposed change

**Cost Explorer recommendations and usage analysis**

AWS Cost Explorer helps you analyze spend and estimate commitment purchases.

Use it to:

- Review historical usage patterns
- Estimate Savings Plan or RI purchase amounts
- Compare current spend against a proposed commitment
- Identify underutilized commitments

Practical CLI examples:

```bash
aws ce get-cost-and-usage \
  --time-period Start=2026-04-01,End=2026-05-01 \
  --granularity MONTHLY \
  --metrics UnblendedCost
```

```bash
aws ce get-savings-plans-utilization \
  --time-period Start=2026-04-01,End=2026-05-01 \
  --granularity MONTHLY
```

```bash
aws ce get-reservation-utilization \
  --time-period Start=2026-04-01,End=2026-05-01 \
  --granularity MONTHLY
```

Use recommendation outputs carefully:

- They estimate based on historical patterns.
- They do not guarantee future savings.
- They can be wrong if your workload is changing.
- They are most useful when you have a stable baseline and want to size a commitment.

**How to think about commitment lifecycle**

Commitments should be reviewed regularly, not treated as one-time purchases.

A practical lifecycle:

1. Measure current usage and identify the stable baseline.
2. Right-size resources to remove waste.
3. Buy a commitment that matches the baseline.
4. Monitor utilization and coverage.
5. Reassess when architecture, traffic, or instance families change.

Risks to watch:

- **Undercommitment**: you leave savings on the table.
- **Overcommitment**: you pay for unused commitment.
- **Wrong scope**: the discount does not apply because the workload does not match the reservation or plan.
- **Stale assumptions**: the workload changes after the purchase.

**Exam scenario patterns**

Common SAP-C02 patterns include:

- A stable EC2 fleet with no expected changes: choose a Standard RI or EC2 Instance Savings Plan depending on flexibility needs.
- A mixed EC2, Fargate, and Lambda environment: choose a Compute Savings Plan.
- A workload that must launch in a specific AZ during failover: consider a Zonal RI or Capacity Reservation.
- A workload with low utilization and high cost: right-size first, then commit.
- A workload with seasonal spikes: commit to the baseline and leave bursts On-Demand.
- A workload likely to change instance family soon: prefer Compute Savings Plans or Convertible RIs.

**Practical decision guide**

- Use **Compute Savings Plans** when flexibility matters most.
- Use **EC2 Instance Savings Plans** when EC2 usage is stable and family-specific.
- Use **Standard RIs** when the workload is fixed and you want strong savings.
- Use **Convertible RIs** when you expect the workload to evolve but still want commitment-based savings.
- Use **Capacity Reservations** when launch capacity is the primary requirement.
- Use **right-sizing** before any long-term commitment.

**Quick reference**

- **Savings Plans** = hourly dollar commitment, flexible coverage, unused commitment still billed.
- **RIs** = more specific discounting, stronger matching rules, can include capacity benefits in zonal cases.
- **Right-sizing** = reduce waste before buying commitments.
- **Compute Optimizer** = recommendation engine, best with enough historical data.
- **Cost Explorer** = analysis and recommendation tool for spend, coverage, and utilization.
