## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Professional (Exam SAP-C02)

**Overview**

In multi-account AWS environments, you need two things at the same time: clear visibility into spend across accounts and a controlled way to buy and govern shared services. The exam expects you to understand how AWS Organizations, consolidated billing, Cost and Usage Reports (CUR), AWS Budgets, and procurement controls work together.

The core design goal is simple: let teams move fast in their own accounts while central finance, platform, or procurement teams retain oversight, chargeback/showback capability, and purchasing control.

**AWS Organizations and consolidated billing**

AWS Organizations is the foundation for multi-account governance. It lets you group accounts into an organization, apply policies, and centralize billing. With consolidated billing, usage from member accounts rolls up to the organization’s payer context for invoicing and discounts, while each account still retains its own resources, tags, and operational boundaries.

Key points:

- **Management account** is the administrative root of the organization.
- **Payer account** is the account responsible for billing and payment setup in the organization.
- **Member accounts** consume services and contribute usage to the consolidated bill.
- **Cost allocation** still depends on account IDs, tags, and usage dimensions, not just the invoice total.

Use consolidated billing when you want:

- Shared volume discounts and tiered pricing across accounts.
- One place to manage payment instruments and billing preferences.
- Easier chargeback or showback across business units.
- Centralized procurement of reserved capacity and savings plans.

> Be careful not to equate “management account” with “the only billing account” in every setup. In practice, the payer context and payment responsibility are what matter for billing operations.

**Cost visibility building blocks**

A strong cost visibility design uses multiple layers. No single report is enough for all audiences.

- **AWS Cost Explorer** provides interactive analysis of spend trends, service mix, linked account costs, and tag-based views.
- **Cost and Usage Report (CUR)** provides the most detailed billing dataset for downstream analytics.
- **AWS Budgets** provides threshold-based alerts and can trigger actions.
- **AWS Cost Categories** help group costs by business logic such as application, team, environment, or product line.
- **Resource tags** provide the primary attribution mechanism for chargeback and showback.

A practical pattern is:

- Use account structure for coarse separation.
- Use tags for application and ownership attribution.
- Use Cost Categories for business reporting.
- Use CUR for authoritative analytics and reconciliation.

**Cost and Usage Report (CUR)**

CUR is the most detailed billing export. It is commonly delivered to Amazon S3 and queried with Amazon Athena, Amazon Redshift, or other analytics tools. For exam purposes, remember that CUR schema details depend on the options you enable, such as resource IDs, hourly granularity, and whether you use the newer CUR format.

Typical uses:

- Build chargeback dashboards.
- Reconcile invoices with internal cost centers.
- Analyze usage by account, service, region, tag, or line item type.
- Detect anomalies and unused resources.

A common Athena pattern is to query CUR data in S3. Exact column names vary by CUR version and configuration, so verify the schema before writing production queries.

```sql
SELECT
  line_item_usage_account_id,
  product_product_name,
  SUM(line_item_unblended_cost) AS cost
FROM cur_db.cur_table
WHERE bill_billing_period_start_date = DATE '2026-04-01'
GROUP BY 1, 2
ORDER BY cost DESC;
```

Practical notes:

- Use `line_item_usage_account_id` to group by consuming account.
- Use `line_item_unblended_cost` for raw cost analysis.
- Use `line_item_blended_cost` only when your reporting model intentionally uses blended pricing.
- Confirm whether your CUR includes the columns you need before relying on a query pattern.

**Chargeback and showback**

Chargeback means you allocate actual costs to the consuming team or business unit. Showback means you report costs without directly billing the internal consumer.

Use chargeback when:

- Finance requires internal billing.
- Teams own their budgets and must be accountable for spend.
- You need strong incentives for cost optimization.

Use showback when:

- You want transparency without internal invoicing.
- Teams are still maturing in cost ownership.
- You need a softer governance model before formal chargeback.

Common attribution methods:

- **Account-based** allocation for dedicated workloads.
- **Tag-based** allocation for shared accounts.
- **Usage-based** allocation for shared platform services.
- **Split cost allocation** for shared resources such as NAT gateways, shared clusters, or central logging.

**AWS Budgets**

AWS Budgets lets you define cost, usage, reservation, and savings plans thresholds. It is useful for alerting and governance, but it is not just a passive notification tool.

Budgets can:

- Send alerts by email, SNS, or chat integrations.
- Track actual or forecasted spend.
- Monitor usage metrics such as EC2 instance hours or data transfer.
- Trigger **Budgets Actions** to apply controls when thresholds are breached.

Budgets Actions can help enforce policy by attaching IAM policies, applying service control policy-related controls through organizational workflows, or otherwise restricting usage depending on the action type and setup. The exact enforcement path depends on the budget action and your organization design.

Example budget definition pattern:

```json
{
  "BudgetName": "prod-monthly-budget",
  "BudgetType": "COST",
  "TimeUnit": "MONTHLY",
  "BudgetLimit": {
    "Amount": "5000",
    "Unit": "USD"
  },
  "CostFilters": {
    "LinkedAccount": ["111122223333"]
  },
  "CostTypes": {
    "IncludeTax": true,
    "IncludeSubscription": true,
    "UseBlended": false,
    "IncludeRefund": false,
    "IncludeCredit": true,
    "IncludeUpfront": true,
    "IncludeRecurring": true,
    "IncludeOtherSubscription": true,
    "IncludeSupport": true,
    "IncludeDiscount": true,
    "UseAmortized": false
  }
}
```

Important caveats:

- Budget API structures vary by operation and budget type.
- `CostFilters` keys and values must match supported dimensions.
- Validate the payload against the specific AWS Budgets API or CLI command you use.

Example CLI creation flow:

```bash
aws budgets create-budget \
  --account-id 123456789012 \
  --budget file://budget.json
```

**Budgets Actions and enforcement**

Budgets alone do not stop spend automatically, but they can participate in enforcement workflows. This distinction matters on the exam.

Typical enforcement patterns:

- Alert at 50%, 80%, and 100% of budget.
- Trigger an action to deny launching additional resources.
- Apply a restrictive IAM policy to a role or user.
- Use organizational controls to reduce blast radius.

A practical governance model is:

1. Detect overspend risk with Budgets.
2. Notify owners and finance.
3. Trigger an action or workflow if the threshold is exceeded.
4. Review whether the control should be temporary or permanent.

**AWS Cost Categories**

Cost Categories let you map costs into business-friendly groupings. They are especially useful when account structure does not match reporting structure.

Examples:

- Map multiple accounts to one product line.
- Group tags like `Environment=Prod` and `Environment=Production` into one category.
- Separate shared platform costs from application costs.

Why they matter:

- They simplify dashboards for executives.
- They reduce the need for complex spreadsheet logic.
- They help standardize reporting across teams.

**Tagging strategy**

Tags are essential for cost allocation, but they only work well when you enforce consistency.

Recommended tags:

- `Owner`
- `Application`
- `Environment`
- `CostCenter`
- `BusinessUnit`

Best practices:

- Enforce tags at provisioning time.
- Use AWS Organizations tag policies where appropriate.
- Audit untagged resources regularly.
- Treat tags as part of the control plane, not an afterthought.

Common pitfalls:

- Inconsistent tag keys such as `costcenter` vs `CostCenter`.
- Missing tags on shared infrastructure.
- Assuming tags alone solve shared-cost allocation.

**Centralized procurement patterns**

Centralized procurement means one team controls purchasing decisions for shared AWS commitments and marketplace or enterprise agreements, while workloads remain distributed across accounts.

Common centralized procurement items:

- **Reserved Instances** for predictable EC2 usage.
- **Savings Plans** for flexible compute commitments.
- **Enterprise Discount Programs** or negotiated pricing structures.
- **AWS Marketplace** subscriptions and software procurement.
- Shared support plans and central vendor management.

Why centralize procurement:

- Better discount utilization across the organization.
- Reduced duplication of commitments in separate accounts.
- Stronger negotiation leverage.
- Easier governance over third-party software purchases.

A common operating model is:

- Platform or finance team owns commitments.
- Application teams consume capacity across linked accounts.
- Reporting shows which teams benefit from shared commitments.
- Finance reviews utilization and coverage regularly.

**Reserved Instances and Savings Plans**

Reserved Instances and Savings Plans are both commitment-based discount mechanisms, but they differ in flexibility.

| Feature | Reserved Instances | Savings Plans |
|---|---|---|
| Scope | Often service-specific | Broader compute flexibility |
| Commitment | Instance attributes or spend | Spend-based commitment |
| Flexibility | Lower | Higher |
| Best for | Stable, known workloads | Variable but steady compute spend |

Exam-relevant guidance:

- Use Reserved Instances when you have stable, predictable instance patterns and want specific reservation characteristics.
- Use Savings Plans when you want broader flexibility across instance families, sizes, or compute services, depending on the plan type.
- Centralize commitment purchasing when multiple accounts can benefit from pooled usage.

**AWS Marketplace and software procurement**

AWS Marketplace can simplify software procurement, but it also introduces governance needs.

Consider:

- Who is allowed to subscribe.
- Whether subscriptions should be centralized or delegated.
- How software spend is tracked across accounts.
- Whether procurement requires approval workflows.

Good controls include:

- Restricting Marketplace subscription permissions.
- Reviewing vendor terms centrally.
- Tagging Marketplace-related resources and subscriptions.
- Monitoring recurring software charges in CUR.

**Shared services and cost allocation**

Shared services such as networking, logging, security tooling, and CI/CD platforms often create the hardest cost allocation problems.

Examples of shared-cost sources:

- NAT gateways.
- Transit Gateway attachments and data processing.
- Centralized logging and SIEM ingestion.
- Shared EKS clusters or shared container platforms.
- Central security tooling and scanning services.

Allocation approaches:

- Split by account usage.
- Split by traffic volume.
- Split by number of users, requests, or workloads.
- Allocate a fixed platform fee plus variable usage.

Choose the method that best matches the business value model. The exam often tests whether you can distinguish technical allocation from business allocation.

**Practical architecture pattern**

A common enterprise design looks like this:

- One organization with a management account and a payer context.
- Separate accounts for production, non-production, security, logging, and shared services.
- CUR exported to a central S3 bucket.
- Athena and QuickSight used for reporting.
- Budgets configured per account, application, and environment.
- Central procurement team manages Savings Plans and Marketplace subscriptions.
- Tag policies and SCPs enforce governance.

Example topology:

```text
AWS Organizations
├── Management / Payer context
├── Security account
├── Logging account
├── Shared services account
├── Prod application accounts
└── Dev/Test application accounts
```

This model gives you:

- Isolation of workloads.
- Centralized financial control.
- Better auditability.
- Easier scaling as the organization grows.

**CLI and reporting examples**

List organization accounts:

```bash
aws organizations list-accounts
```

Describe a budget:

```bash
aws budgets describe-budget \
  --account-id 123456789012 \
  --budget-name prod-monthly-budget
```

Query CUR in Athena:

```sql
SELECT
  line_item_usage_account_id AS account_id,
  SUM(line_item_unblended_cost) AS total_cost
FROM cur_db.cur_table
WHERE bill_billing_period_start_date >= DATE '2026-04-01'
GROUP BY 1
ORDER BY total_cost DESC;
```

Create a Cost Explorer report view by account and service in the console or via API, then use it to compare spend trends across linked accounts.

**Exam traps and how to avoid them**

- Do not assume the management account is always the only billing entity; focus on payer and consolidated billing behavior.
- Do not assume CUR schema columns are identical in every setup; verify the export configuration.
- Do not treat Budgets as a hard stop by default; remember Budgets Actions and governance workflows.
- Do not use region-deny SCPs as a universal answer without considering global services and exceptions.
- Do not rely on tags alone for shared-cost allocation; combine tags, accounts, and cost categories.

> A region-restriction SCP can be useful, but global services and some control-plane operations may require exceptions. Test policies carefully before broad rollout.

**Decision guide**

Use this quick mental model during the exam:

- Need invoice consolidation and discount pooling? Use Organizations with consolidated billing.
- Need detailed cost analytics? Use CUR and Athena.
- Need executive-friendly grouping? Use Cost Categories.
- Need alerts or thresholds? Use Budgets.
- Need enforcement after threshold breach? Use Budgets Actions plus IAM or organizational controls.
- Need internal billing? Use chargeback.
- Need transparency without billing? Use showback.
- Need better pricing leverage? Centralize procurement of commitments and marketplace subscriptions.

**Summary**

Multi-account cost visibility is about making spend understandable across accounts, teams, and services. Centralized procurement is about buying shared capacity and software in a controlled way that maximizes discounts and governance. The strongest designs combine Organizations, CUR, Cost Explorer, Budgets, Cost Categories, tagging, and centralized commitment management.
