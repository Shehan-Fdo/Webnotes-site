## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Professional (Exam SAP-C02)

**Decision framework overview**

A strong architecture decision in AWS is rarely about maximizing one dimension. For SAP-C02, you are expected to choose designs that meet performance, resilience, and cost goals together, while respecting operational complexity and business constraints. The best answer is usually the one that satisfies the stated requirement with the least unnecessary overhead.

Use a structured evaluation:
- **Performance**: latency, throughput, concurrency, and scaling behavior.
- **Resilience**: fault tolerance, recovery time, recovery point, and blast-radius reduction.
- **Cost**: direct service cost, data transfer, operational overhead, and overprovisioning.
- **Operational fit**: how much automation, monitoring, and maintenance the design requires.

A practical decision often starts with the workload profile:
- Is traffic steady, spiky, or unpredictable?
- Is the workload read-heavy, write-heavy, or mixed?
- Can the application tolerate eventual consistency or brief degradation?
- Is the business optimizing for lowest latency, highest availability, or lowest spend?

**Core trade-off principles**

AWS architecture questions often test whether you can identify the cheapest design that still meets the requirement, or the most resilient design that does not overshoot the need.

- **Performance improvements** often increase cost through larger instances, more replicas, caching, or cross-Region replication.
- **Resilience improvements** often increase cost through multi-AZ, multi-Region, backups, warm standby, or active-active patterns.
- **Cost reductions** often reduce headroom, redundancy, or speed unless you add automation and careful scaling controls.

A useful mental model is:
- Optimize for the stated business objective first.
- Preserve enough headroom for peak demand.
- Add resilience only to the level required by the recovery objective.
- Avoid paying for always-on capacity when elastic capacity can meet the same need.

**Performance decision factors**

Performance is not just raw speed. It includes how well the system behaves under load and how quickly it recovers from load spikes.

Common performance levers:
- **Caching** to reduce repeated reads and lower latency.
- **Horizontal scaling** to increase throughput.
- **Read replicas** to offload read traffic from primaries.
- **Partitioning/sharding** to distribute hot keys or large datasets.
- **Content delivery** to move static content closer to users.
- **Asynchronous processing** to decouple user-facing latency from backend work.

Examples of when to use them:
- Use **Amazon CloudFront** for global content delivery and edge caching.
- Use **ElastiCache** when repeated reads or session lookups dominate.
- Use **Aurora read replicas** or database read replicas when read traffic exceeds primary capacity.
- Use **SQS** or **SNS** to buffer bursts and smooth downstream load.
- Use **Auto Scaling** when demand varies and you want to avoid fixed overprovisioning.

Practical example:
```bash
aws autoscaling put-scaling-policy \
  --auto-scaling-group-name web-asg \
  --policy-name cpu-target-50 \
  --policy-type TargetTrackingScaling \
  --target-tracking-configuration '{
    "PredefinedMetricSpecification": {
      "PredefinedMetricType": "ASGAverageCPUUtilization"
    },
    "TargetValue": 50.0
  }'
```

This kind of policy improves performance under load while controlling cost by scaling only when needed.

**Resilience decision factors**

Resilience is the ability to continue operating despite failures. In AWS, that usually means designing for component failure, AZ failure, and sometimes Region failure.

Key resilience concepts:
- **High availability (HA)**: the system remains available during common failures.
- **Fault tolerance**: the system continues with little or no interruption even when components fail.
- **Disaster recovery (DR)**: the system can be restored after a major outage.
- **Recovery Time Objective (RTO)**: maximum acceptable downtime.
- **Recovery Point Objective (RPO)**: maximum acceptable data loss.

Typical resilience patterns:
- **Multi-AZ** for automatic failover within a Region.
- **Multi-Region** for regional disaster recovery or global availability.
- **Backups and snapshots** for restore-based recovery.
- **Pilot light** for minimal always-on recovery infrastructure.
- **Warm standby** for faster recovery with partial capacity running.
- **Active-active** for the highest availability and lowest failover time, usually at the highest cost.

When choosing a pattern:
- Use **Multi-AZ** when you need strong availability but do not need regional failover.
- Use **pilot light** when RTO and RPO are moderate and cost matters.
- Use **warm standby** when faster recovery is required and you can pay for partial duplication.
- Use **active-active** when downtime is extremely expensive and the application can support global traffic management.

Critical warning:
> Multi-AZ improves availability within a Region, but it does not protect you from a full-Region outage. If the requirement explicitly mentions regional disaster recovery, you need a multi-Region strategy.

Example of a resilient database posture:
- Primary database in one AZ.
- Synchronous standby in another AZ.
- Automated failover through the managed service.
- Regular backups to support point-in-time recovery.

For Amazon RDS, enabling Multi-AZ is a common choice when the goal is availability rather than read scaling.

```bash
aws rds modify-db-instance \
  --db-instance-identifier prod-db \
  --multi-az \
  --apply-immediately
```

**Cost decision factors**

Cost is more than the hourly price of a service. You should consider the full cost profile:
- Compute hours
- Storage and IOPS
- Data transfer, especially cross-AZ and cross-Region
- Managed service premiums
- Operational labor
- Idle capacity and overprovisioning

Cost optimization techniques:
- **Right-size** instances and storage.
- **Use Auto Scaling** to match demand.
- **Choose managed services** when they reduce operational burden enough to justify the premium.
- **Use Reserved Instances or Savings Plans** for steady-state workloads.
- **Use Spot Instances** for fault-tolerant batch or stateless workloads.
- **Reduce data transfer** by keeping chatty components close together.
- **Use lifecycle policies** for infrequently accessed data.

Examples:
- Use **S3 Intelligent-Tiering** when access patterns are uncertain.
- Use **S3 lifecycle transitions** for archival data.
- Use **Graviton-based instances** when compatible, because they often improve price-performance.
- Use **CloudFront** to reduce origin load and data transfer from the source.

A simple cost-aware S3 lifecycle example:
```json
{
  "Rules": [
    {
      "ID": "archive-old-objects",
      "Status": "Enabled",
      "Filter": {
        "Prefix": ""
      },
      "Transitions": [
        {
          "Days": 30,
          "StorageClass": "STANDARD_IA"
        },
        {
          "Days": 90,
          "StorageClass": "GLACIER"
        }
      ]
    }
  ]
}
```

**How to compare options in exam scenarios**

When multiple answers seem plausible, compare them against the stated requirement in this order:
1. **Does it meet the hard requirement?**
   - Availability target
   - Latency target
   - Recovery objective
   - Compliance or data residency constraint
2. **Does it avoid unnecessary complexity?**
   - Prefer simpler designs when they satisfy the need.
3. **Does it scale efficiently?**
   - Favor elastic scaling over fixed capacity.
4. **Does it minimize total cost?**
   - Include transfer, storage, and operations.
5. **Does it preserve future flexibility?**
   - Choose designs that can evolve without replatforming.

A common exam trap is selecting the most resilient architecture when the question only asks for improved availability. Another trap is choosing the cheapest option that fails the stated RTO or performance target.

**Performance versus resilience trade-offs**

Some design choices improve one dimension while hurting another.

- **Caching** improves performance and can reduce cost, but it may introduce stale data and invalidation complexity.
- **Multi-AZ** improves resilience, but it increases cost and may add write latency in some systems.
- **Cross-Region replication** improves disaster recovery, but it adds latency, transfer cost, and operational complexity.
- **Active-active** improves availability and latency for global users, but it is expensive and harder to operate.
- **Asynchronous processing** improves responsiveness, but it can increase eventual consistency and complicate error handling.

Use these patterns when the trade-off matches the business need:
- Choose **cache-first** when reads dominate and slight staleness is acceptable.
- Choose **synchronous replication** when data loss is unacceptable and latency impact is tolerable.
- Choose **asynchronous replication** when lower latency and lower cost matter more than immediate consistency.
- Choose **decoupling** when user experience matters more than immediate backend completion.

**Resilience versus cost trade-offs**

Resilience usually costs money because you pay for redundancy, duplication, or standby capacity.

- **Single-AZ** is cheaper but less resilient.
- **Multi-AZ** is a common middle ground for production systems.
- **Multi-Region** is the most resilient but often the most expensive and complex.
- **Backups only** are cheapest for recovery, but they produce the longest downtime.

A practical rule:
- If the business can tolerate hours of downtime, backup-and-restore may be enough.
- If the business needs minutes of downtime, warm standby or pilot light may be appropriate.
- If the business needs near-continuous availability, active-active or highly automated failover is more suitable.

**Performance versus cost trade-offs**

Performance tuning can quickly become expensive if you scale the wrong layer.

- Scaling up instances may be simpler, but it can be less cost-efficient than scaling out.
- Adding replicas improves read performance, but each replica adds cost and replication lag.
- Using provisioned IOPS improves storage performance, but only when the workload truly needs it.
- Keeping everything in the same Region can reduce latency and transfer cost, but it may limit geographic resilience.

Useful guidance:
- Start with the smallest design that meets the target.
- Measure bottlenecks before adding capacity.
- Prefer managed elasticity over permanent overprovisioning.
- Use caching and async patterns before brute-force scaling when they fit the workload.

**AWS service selection patterns**

Different services solve different parts of the trade-off space.

- **Amazon EC2 Auto Scaling**
  - Good for variable compute demand.
  - Helps balance performance and cost.
  - Requires more operational control than fully managed serverless options.

- **Amazon ECS or Amazon EKS**
  - Useful when containerized workloads need scaling and deployment control.
  - Better when you need portability or microservice orchestration.
  - Adds operational complexity compared with simpler managed services.

- **AWS Lambda**
  - Good for bursty, event-driven workloads.
  - Strong cost efficiency for intermittent traffic.
  - Not ideal for long-running or tightly latency-sensitive workloads with cold-start concerns.

- **Amazon RDS / Aurora**
  - Good for managed relational workloads.
  - Multi-AZ improves resilience.
  - Read replicas improve read performance.
  - Aurora often offers better scaling characteristics than standard relational deployments, depending on the use case.

- **Amazon DynamoDB**
  - Good for predictable low-latency key-value access at scale.
  - On-demand capacity can simplify burst handling.
  - Global tables can support multi-Region designs, but cost and replication behavior must be considered.

- **Amazon S3**
  - Durable and cost-effective for object storage.
  - Often the best choice for static assets, backups, and data lakes.
  - Pair with CloudFront for performance.

**Decision heuristics for SAP-C02**

Use these heuristics when reading a scenario:
- If the question emphasizes **lowest latency**, look for caching, edge delivery, or local reads.
- If it emphasizes **high availability**, look for Multi-AZ, load balancing, and automated failover.
- If it emphasizes **disaster recovery**, look for backups, replication, and multi-Region patterns.
- If it emphasizes **lowest cost**, look for elasticity, right-sizing, and storage tiering.
- If it emphasizes **bursty traffic**, look for serverless, queueing, or Auto Scaling.
- If it emphasizes **strict RTO/RPO**, choose the recovery pattern that matches those numbers, not the cheapest one.

**Practical architecture examples**

Example 1: Global static website with variable traffic
- Store assets in **S3**.
- Deliver through **CloudFront**.
- Use **Route 53** for DNS routing.
- Add **WAF** if security filtering is required.

Why this works:
- High performance through edge caching.
- Strong durability from S3.
- Low operational cost compared with running web servers.

Example 2: Transactional web app with moderate availability needs
- Use **ALB** behind an **Auto Scaling** group.
- Place instances across **multiple AZs**.
- Use **RDS Multi-AZ** for the database.
- Add **ElastiCache** for session and read caching.

Why this works:
- Good balance of resilience and cost.
- Scales with demand.
- Avoids the expense of multi-Region active-active.

Example 3: Critical system with regional DR requirement
- Run primary workloads in one Region.
- Replicate data to a second Region.
- Maintain a **pilot light** or **warm standby** environment.
- Automate DNS failover and infrastructure provisioning.

Why this works:
- Meets regional recovery objectives.
- Costs less than full active-active.
- Provides a clear recovery path.

**CLI and configuration examples**

Check Auto Scaling group health:
```bash
aws autoscaling describe-auto-scaling-groups \
  --auto-scaling-group-names web-asg
```

Inspect RDS Multi-AZ status:
```bash
aws rds describe-db-instances \
  --db-instance-identifier prod-db \
  --query 'DBInstances[0].MultiAZ'
```

Create a CloudFront distribution conceptually through infrastructure as code when you need repeatable performance optimization:
```yaml
Resources:
  Distribution:
    Type: AWS::CloudFront::Distribution
    Properties:
      DistributionConfig:
        Enabled: true
        Origins:
          - Id: s3-origin
            DomainName: my-bucket.s3.amazonaws.com
            S3OriginConfig: {}
        DefaultCacheBehavior:
          TargetOriginId: s3-origin
          ViewerProtocolPolicy: redirect-to-https
          AllowedMethods: [GET, HEAD]
          CachedMethods: [GET, HEAD]
```

**Exam approach**

When you face a trade-off question:
- Identify the primary business driver.
- Eliminate answers that exceed the requirement.
- Prefer managed services when they reduce operational burden without violating constraints.
- Validate whether the design addresses the failure domain mentioned in the prompt.
- Check whether the cost increase is justified by the stated performance or resilience gain.

If two answers both work, the better answer usually:
- Uses fewer moving parts.
- Matches the exact recovery or latency requirement.
- Avoids unnecessary multi-Region complexity.
- Uses elasticity instead of fixed capacity.

**Summary mindset**

The best AWS architecture is not the one with the most features. It is the one that meets the workload’s performance, resilience, and cost goals with the least unnecessary complexity. For SAP-C02, think in terms of business outcomes, failure domains, and measurable objectives such as latency, RTO, and RPO. Then choose the simplest design that satisfies them.
