## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Professional (Exam SAP-C02)

**Why this topic matters**

Chaos testing and failover validation are about proving that your architecture behaves as designed under stress, not just that it works in the happy path. For SAP-C02, you need to distinguish between resilience design, recovery execution, and operational readiness. The exam often tests whether you can choose the right validation method for the failure mode, the recovery objective, and the service being protected.

A strong answer usually combines:
- **Fault injection** to expose weak assumptions.
- **Failover drills** to verify routing, data consistency, and application behavior.
- **Operational readiness checks** to ensure monitoring, automation, and runbooks are actually usable during an incident.

**Core concepts and terminology**

<dl>
<dt><strong>Chaos testing</strong></dt>
<dd>Intentional introduction of faults to observe how systems behave under stress and whether recovery mechanisms work as expected.</dd>
<dt><strong>Failover validation</strong></dt>
<dd>Verification that traffic, data access, and application dependencies can move to a healthy target when a primary component or Region becomes unavailable.</dd>
<dt><strong>Operational readiness</strong></dt>
<dd>The state where monitoring, alerting, automation, permissions, backups, and runbooks are prepared for real incidents and recovery drills.</dd>
<dt><strong>Recovery Time Objective (RTO)</strong></dt>
<dd>The maximum acceptable time to restore service after an outage.</dd>
<dt><strong>Recovery Point Objective (RPO)</strong></dt>
<dd>The maximum acceptable data loss measured in time.</dd>
</dl>

**How AWS frames resilience**

AWS resilience work is usually layered:
- **Prevent** failures where possible with Multi-AZ, redundancy, and managed services.
- **Detect** failures with metrics, logs, alarms, and synthetic checks.
- **Respond** with automation, routing changes, and runbooks.
- **Recover** with backups, replicas, restore workflows, and cross-Region patterns.
- **Validate** continuously through drills and game days.

For exam scenarios, the best answer is rarely “just add more instances.” You need to show that the architecture can survive a specific failure domain:
- Instance failure
- Availability Zone failure
- Regional impairment
- Dependency failure
- Data corruption
- Operator error

**Chaos testing strategy**

Chaos testing should be controlled, observable, and reversible. You do not inject faults randomly into production without guardrails. Instead, you define the blast radius, success criteria, and rollback plan first.

Typical goals:
- Confirm that alarms fire and are actionable.
- Confirm that failover occurs within the expected RTO.
- Confirm that the application handles retries, timeouts, and partial failures.
- Confirm that data remains consistent enough for the workload.
- Confirm that operators can execute the runbook under pressure.

Common test dimensions:
- **Compute**: terminate instances, stop services, exhaust CPU or memory.
- **Network**: add latency, drop packets, block dependencies, simulate DNS issues.
- **Storage**: test volume detach, read-only behavior, restore from backup.
- **Database**: promote replicas, fail over clusters, test connection retries.
- **Region**: simulate loss of a Region or a critical dependency in that Region.

> Use controlled experiments with explicit stop conditions. The goal is to learn safely, not to create an uncontrolled outage.

**AWS services and tools for resilience testing**

AWS provides several services that support operational readiness and recovery drills.

- **AWS Fault Injection Service (FIS)**:
  - Runs controlled fault injection experiments.
  - Lets you test how systems respond to disruptions.
  - Supports actions such as stopping EC2 instances, injecting network faults, or stressing resources depending on the experiment template and supported targets.
  - Works best when paired with CloudWatch alarms and clear abort conditions.

- **AWS Resilience Hub**:
  - Assesses application resilience against recovery objectives.
  - Helps you define and track RTO and RPO targets.
  - Identifies resilience gaps across resources and dependencies.
  - Useful for validating whether the architecture meets business requirements before and after changes.

- **AWS Systems Manager**:
  - **Automation** runbooks can standardize recovery steps.
  - **Incident Manager** helps coordinate incident response and escalation.
  - **OpsCenter** centralizes operational issues and remediation tracking.
  - **Run Command** can execute controlled actions across managed instances.
  - These features are often part of a mature recovery drill process.

- **Amazon CloudWatch**:
  - Provides alarms, dashboards, logs, and metric math.
  - Synthetic canaries can validate user-facing paths.
  - Alarms should be tested, not assumed.

- **AWS Config**:
  - Helps detect configuration drift that can undermine resilience.
  - Useful for validating that backup, encryption, and network controls remain in place.

**Planning a safe chaos experiment**

Before you run a fault injection or failover drill, define the experiment carefully.

1. Identify the failure mode you want to test.
2. Define the expected behavior and success criteria.
3. Set the blast radius and scope.
4. Ensure monitoring, alerting, and rollback are ready.
5. Run the test in a non-production environment first when possible.
6. Review results and update the runbook.

A practical experiment plan includes:
- Target resources
- Fault type
- Duration
- Abort conditions
- Expected alarms
- Expected recovery path
- Owner and approver

Example experiment checklist:
- Application health checks are green before the test.
- CloudWatch alarms are enabled and routed to the right responders.
- Database backups are current and restorable.
- IAM permissions allow the recovery automation to run.
- DNS TTLs are low enough for planned failover if needed.

**Failover validation patterns**

Failover validation is not just “did traffic move?” You also need to verify that the application still behaves correctly after the move.

Key validation areas:
- **Traffic routing**: DNS, load balancers, Global Accelerator, or client-side routing.
- **State handling**: sessions, caches, queues, and in-flight requests.
- **Data consistency**: replication lag, write ordering, and conflict handling.
- **Dependency readiness**: downstream services, secrets, KMS keys, and network access.
- **Observability**: logs, metrics, traces, and alarms continue to work.

A useful distinction:
- **Planned failover**: controlled move to another target for maintenance or testing.
- **Unplanned failover**: automatic or emergency move after a failure.
- **Disaster recovery test**: a broader exercise that validates the full recovery process, including people, process, and technology.

**Cross-Region disaster recovery models**

SAP-C02 often expects you to choose the right DR pattern for the business requirement.

| Pattern | Characteristics | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| Backup and restore | Lowest cost, highest RTO/RPO | Non-critical workloads, long recovery windows |
| Pilot light | Minimal core services running in secondary Region | Faster recovery than backup/restore |
| Warm standby | Scaled-down but functional environment in secondary Region | Moderate RTO and RPO |
| Active-active | Workloads run in multiple Regions simultaneously | Lowest RTO, complex data consistency |

What to validate in each model:
- **Backup and restore**:
  - Backup integrity
  - Restore permissions
  - Encryption key availability
  - Network and security group readiness
- **Pilot light**:
  - Ability to scale core services quickly
  - Dependency startup order
  - DNS or traffic shift procedure
- **Warm standby**:
  - Capacity scaling under load
  - Data replication lag
  - Application configuration parity
- **Active-active**:
  - Conflict resolution
  - Session handling
  - Global routing behavior
  - Regional independence of dependencies

**Application state during failover**

A common exam trap is assuming infrastructure failover automatically means application success. Stateful applications need explicit validation.

Watch for:
- **Session persistence**:
  - If sessions are stored in memory, failover may log users out.
  - Prefer external session stores or stateless auth where possible.
- **Cache invalidation**:
  - Stale cache entries can survive failover and serve incorrect data.
  - Validate cache warm-up and eviction behavior.
- **Idempotency**:
  - Retries after timeouts can duplicate writes if operations are not idempotent.
  - Use idempotency tokens for critical workflows.
- **Queue processing**:
  - Messages may be reprocessed after failover.
  - Ensure consumers can handle duplicates safely.
- **Transaction boundaries**:
  - Partial commits can occur during interruption.
  - Design for compensating actions where needed.

Practical validation questions:
- Can a user resume a workflow after a Region switch?
- Do background jobs continue without duplication?
- Does the app tolerate stale DNS caches?
- Are write operations safe to retry?

**Service-specific failover validation**

Different AWS services fail over differently, so validation must match the service behavior.

- **Amazon RDS and Aurora**:
  - Validate Multi-AZ failover behavior and application reconnect logic.
  - Confirm connection pooling does not hide failover issues.
  - For Aurora, validate reader and writer endpoint behavior.
  - Test whether the application retries on transient connection loss.
  - For point-in-time recovery, ensure the restore window and target configuration are valid.

Example restore command pattern:
```bash
aws rds restore-db-instance-to-point-in-time \
  --source-db-instance-identifier prod-db \
  --target-db-instance-identifier prod-db-restore \
  --restore-time 2026-04-26T12:00:00Z \
  --db-subnet-group-name app-private-subnets \
  --vpc-security-group-ids sg-0123456789abcdef0 \
  --multi-az
```

- **Amazon ElastiCache**:
  - Validate node replacement and primary promotion behavior.
  - Confirm the application reconnects and repopulates cache safely.
  - Expect cache loss in some failure scenarios; design for cache-as-ephemeral.
  - Test whether the app degrades gracefully when cache is unavailable.

- **Amazon EFS**:
  - Validate mount target availability across AZs.
  - Confirm clients reconnect after network interruption.
  - Check file locking and application behavior if a mount becomes unavailable.
  - Ensure the application can tolerate temporary I/O errors.

- **Amazon S3**:
  - S3 is designed for high durability, but application access can still fail due to IAM, KMS, DNS, or network issues.
  - Validate bucket policy, replication, and cross-Region access patterns.
  - Test restore workflows for versioned objects and deleted objects.
  - Confirm that dependent services can read from the expected bucket or replica.

- **Amazon DynamoDB global tables**:
  - Validate multi-Region write behavior and conflict handling.
  - Confirm the application can tolerate eventual consistency across Regions.
  - Test regional endpoint switching and retry logic.
  - Ensure the data model supports last-writer-wins semantics where applicable.

- **Amazon Route 53**:
  - `aws route53 test-dns-answer` can show how Route 53 would answer a query for a record set.
  - It does **not** validate end-to-end health-check-driven failover by itself.
  - Use it to inspect DNS resolution behavior, not to prove the full failover path.

Example:
```bash
aws route53 test-dns-answer \
  --hosted-zone-id Z1234567890ABC \
  --record-name app.example.com \
  --record-type A
```

- **Elastic Load Balancing**:
  - Validate target health checks and deregistration delay.
  - Confirm that unhealthy targets stop receiving traffic.
  - Test whether the application handles connection draining correctly.

**Route 53 failover validation**

Route 53 failover records are typically created with `ChangeResourceRecordSets`, not as a standalone JSON object. In practice, you validate the record behavior and the health check, then confirm clients resolve the expected answer.

Example change batch:
```json
{
  "Comment": "Create failover records for app.example.com",
  "Changes": [
    {
      "Action": "UPSERT",
      "ResourceRecordSet": {
        "Name": "app.example.com",
        "Type": "A",
        "SetIdentifier": "primary",
        "Failover": "PRIMARY",
        "TTL": 60,
        "ResourceRecords": [
          { "Value": "203.0.113.10" }
        ],
        "HealthCheckId": "abcd1234-ef56-7890-ab12-cd34ef567890"
      }
    },
    {
      "Action": "UPSERT",
      "ResourceRecordSet": {
        "Name": "app.example.com",
        "Type": "A",
        "SetIdentifier": "secondary",
        "Failover": "SECONDARY",
        "TTL": 60,
        "ResourceRecords": [
          { "Value": "198.51.100.20" }
        ]
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

Validation steps:
1. Confirm the health check is healthy.
2. Query DNS from multiple resolvers.
3. Simulate primary failure in a controlled environment.
4. Wait for TTL expiration.
5. Confirm clients resolve the secondary record.
6. Verify the application works after the DNS switch.

Important caveat:
- DNS failover validation proves resolution behavior.
- It does not prove that the application, database, or downstream dependencies are healthy after the switch.

**Backup, encryption, and restore readiness**

Recovery drills should validate more than the existence of backups.

Check:
- **Backup integrity**: backups are complete and restorable.
- **Encryption**: backups are encrypted as expected.
- **KMS key availability**: the key exists and is usable in the restore Region.
- **Cross-account permissions**: the restore role can access the backup.
- **Cross-Region permissions**: replication and restore policies allow the target Region.
- **Network placement**: subnet groups, security groups, and routing are ready for the restored resource.

For encrypted backups, validate:
- The KMS key policy allows the restore principal.
- The key is enabled.
- Cross-Region key strategy is defined if the restore target is in another Region.
- The backup copy process preserves encryption requirements.

**Operational readiness with Systems Manager**

Systems Manager is central to repeatable recovery.

Common uses:
- **Automation runbooks** for database failover, instance replacement, or configuration repair.
- **Incident Manager** for escalation, incident timelines, and response coordination.
- **OpsCenter** for tracking operational issues and linking them to remediation.
- **Run Command** for controlled remediation on managed instances.

Example Automation document pattern:
```yaml
schemaVersion: '0.3'
description: "Restart application service and verify health"
assumeRole: '{{ AutomationAssumeRole }}'
parameters:
  InstanceId:
    type: String
mainSteps:
  - name: restartService
    action: aws:runCommand
    inputs:
      DocumentName: AWS-RunShellScript
      InstanceIds:
        - '{{ InstanceId }}'
      Parameters:
        commands:
          - sudo systemctl restart app
          - curl -f http://localhost:8080/health
```

Why this matters:
- Manual recovery is slower and more error-prone.
- Runbooks reduce operator variance.
- Automation makes drills repeatable and auditable.

**Observability readiness**

Your monitoring stack must also be resilient. If observability fails during an incident, you lose the ability to diagnose the incident.

Validate:
- **Metrics** are collected from all critical components.
- **Logs** are centralized and durable.
- **Traces** survive partial outages where possible.
- **Alerts** route to the right on-call path.
- **Dashboards** remain accessible during a Region or AZ issue.
- **Synthetic checks** run from independent locations.

Questions to ask:
- Is CloudWatch alarm delivery dependent on the same Region you are testing?
- Are log subscriptions or exporters single points of failure?
- Can responders access dashboards if the primary Region is impaired?
- Are alert thresholds tuned to avoid noise during failover?

**Testing observability itself**

A mature readiness program tests the monitoring system, not just the workload.

Examples:
- Trigger a known alarm and confirm paging.
- Verify that logs appear after a controlled restart.
- Confirm that dashboards show the expected metric change.
- Test whether synthetic canaries detect a broken user journey.
- Validate that incident tickets are created and assigned correctly.

**Practical validation examples**

Example: controlled EC2 disruption with FIS
```json
{
  "description": "Terminate one non-critical EC2 instance in an Auto Scaling group",
  "targets": {
    "asgInstances": {
      "resourceType": "aws:ec2:instance",
      "resourceTags": {
        "Role": "web"
      },
      "selectionMode": "COUNT(1)"
    }
  },
  "actions": {
    "terminateInstance": {
      "actionId": "aws:ec2:terminate-instances",
      "targets": {
        "Instances": "asgInstances"
      }
    }
  },
  "stopConditions": [
    {
      "source": "aws:cloudwatch:alarm",
      "value": "arn:aws:cloudwatch:us-east-1:123456789012:alarm:AbortExperiment"
    }
  ]
}
```

Use this pattern only as a conceptual example of experiment structure. In real experiments, confirm the exact FIS action IDs and target schema supported by the chosen resource type and action.

Example: application health validation after failover
```bash
curl -f https://app.example.com/health
curl -f https://app.example.com/api/orders/123
aws cloudwatch get-metric-statistics \
  --namespace AWS/ApplicationELB \
  --metric-name HTTPCode_Target_5XX_Count \
  --dimensions Name=LoadBalancer,Value=app/alb/1234567890abcdef \
  --start-time 2026-04-26T12:00:00Z \
  --end-time 2026-04-26T12:15:00Z \
  --period 60 \
  --statistics Sum
```

Example: verify DNS resolution after a planned switch
```bash
dig app.example.com
nslookup app.example.com
aws route53 test-dns-answer \
  --hosted-zone-id Z1234567890ABC \
  --record-name app.example.com \
  --record-type A
```

**Common exam scenarios**

- A workload must survive an AZ failure with minimal interruption:
  - Choose Multi-AZ, health checks, and automated failover.
  - Validate that the app reconnects and that state is externalized.

- A workload must recover from a Region outage:
  - Choose pilot light, warm standby, or active-active based on RTO/RPO.
  - Validate backups, replication, DNS or traffic shift, and runbooks.

- A database restore must be possible after corruption:
  - Validate point-in-time recovery, encryption, and restore permissions.
  - Confirm the target network and security settings are ready.

- An application uses caches and sessions:
  - Validate cache repopulation and session continuity.
  - Ensure retries are safe and idempotent.

- Monitoring must remain available during an incident:
  - Validate multi-AZ or multi-Region observability components.
  - Confirm alerts still reach responders.

**What good answers emphasize**

When the exam asks how to validate resilience, look for answers that include:
- Testing the actual failure mode, not a generic one.
- Verifying the application layer, not just infrastructure.
- Checking permissions, encryption, and restore prerequisites.
- Confirming observability and incident response readiness.
- Matching the DR pattern to the business objective.

**What to avoid**

- Assuming a backup is useful without testing restore.
- Assuming DNS failover proves the whole system works.
- Assuming Multi-AZ eliminates the need for application retries.
- Assuming cache or session state survives failover.
- Assuming observability is resilient without testing it.

**Quick review checklist**

- **RTO/RPO** are defined and mapped to the architecture.
- **Runbooks** exist and are tested.
- **Backups** are encrypted, accessible, and restorable.
- **KMS keys** are available in the restore path.
- **DNS and traffic routing** are validated.
- **Application state** is safe across failover.
- **Monitoring and alerting** survive the failure domain.
- **Cross-Region DR** is tested for the chosen pattern.
- **Operational teams** can execute the process under time pressure.
- **Resilience Hub** or similar governance tracks gaps and improvements.
