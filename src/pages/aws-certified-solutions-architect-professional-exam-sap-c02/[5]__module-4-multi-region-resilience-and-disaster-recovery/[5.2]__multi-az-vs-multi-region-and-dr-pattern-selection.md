## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Professional (Exam SAP-C02)

**Core decision framework**

Multi-AZ, Multi-Region, and disaster recovery (DR) patterns solve different failure domains. The right choice depends on your recovery time objective (<abbr title="Recovery Time Objective">RTO</abbr>), recovery point objective (<abbr title="Recovery Point Objective">RPO</abbr>), application state model, and budget.

- **Multi-AZ** protects against an Availability Zone failure inside one Region.
- **Multi-Region** protects against a Region-wide outage, control-plane issue, or regional dependency failure.
- **DR pattern selection** is about matching business tolerance to cost and operational complexity, not maximizing redundancy everywhere.

A useful exam mindset is to ask:
1. What failure are you designing for?
2. How fast must service return?
3. How much data loss is acceptable?
4. Can the application run active-active, or only active-passive?
5. What is the operational cost of keeping the standby warm?

**Multi-AZ architecture**

Multi-AZ means your workload spans at least two Availability Zones in the same Region. This is the default resilience pattern for many AWS managed services because it gives high availability without the complexity of cross-Region replication.

- **Availability Zone** failures are the primary target.
- **Latency** stays low because traffic remains regional.
- **Data transfer** costs are usually lower than cross-Region designs.
- **Operational complexity** is lower than Multi-Region.

Common Multi-AZ services and patterns:

- **Amazon RDS Multi-AZ**
  - Synchronous replication to a standby in another AZ.
  - Automatic failover when the primary becomes unavailable.
  - Best for high availability, not read scaling.
- **Amazon Aurora**
  - Storage is distributed across multiple AZs within a Region.
  - Aurora replicas can be placed in different AZs for failover and read scaling.
  - Aurora Global Database extends this to multiple Regions for DR and low-latency reads.
- **Amazon EFS**
  - Regional file system designed for access from multiple AZs.
  - Use replication or backup/restore for cross-Region recovery.
- **Elastic Load Balancing**
  - Application Load Balancer and Network Load Balancer can distribute traffic across targets in multiple AZs.
- **Amazon EC2**
  - You build Multi-AZ by placing instances in separate AZs behind a load balancer and using Auto Scaling.

> Multi-AZ is not the same as Multi-Region. It improves availability within a Region, but it does not protect you from a full Region outage.

**When Multi-AZ is enough**

Use Multi-AZ when the business can tolerate a Region-level outage for some period, or when the service itself is already regional and highly available.

- Internal business applications with moderate uptime requirements.
- Stateless web tiers behind load balancers.
- Databases that need automatic failover but not regional survivability.
- Systems where the cost of a second Region is not justified.

**Multi-Region architecture**

Multi-Region means you deploy resources in more than one AWS Region. This is the pattern for regional disaster recovery, global latency optimization, and some active-active designs.

- **Regional isolation** is the main benefit.
- **Latency** can improve for global users when you place workloads near them.
- **Cost** increases because you duplicate infrastructure and often data replication.
- **Complexity** increases because you must manage replication, routing, and failover.

Multi-Region is typically used for one of these goals:

- **Disaster recovery**
  - Keep a secondary Region ready to take over if the primary Region fails.
- **Global active-active**
  - Serve traffic from multiple Regions simultaneously.
- **Data locality**
  - Keep data and compute closer to users or regulatory boundaries.
- **Migration**
  - Move workloads gradually between Regions with minimal downtime.

**DR strategy patterns**

The classic DR patterns are often described as backup and restore, pilot light, warm standby, and multi-site active-active. The right choice depends on how quickly you must recover and how much you are willing to pay to keep the secondary environment ready.

| Pattern | RTO | RPO | Cost | Complexity | Typical use |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Backup and restore | High | High to moderate | Low | Low | Non-critical workloads, archival recovery |
| Pilot light | Moderate | Low to moderate | Low to moderate | Moderate | Core data replicated, minimal compute running |
| Warm standby | Low | Low | Moderate | Moderate to high | Business-critical apps with faster recovery |
| Multi-site active-active | Very low | Very low | High | High | Global, highly available, latency-sensitive systems |

**Backup and restore**

This is the simplest DR pattern. You store backups in a separate location and rebuild the environment after a failure.

- Use for workloads where downtime is acceptable.
- Store backups in a different AZ or Region depending on the failure domain you want to survive.
- Restore infrastructure with Infrastructure as Code (IaC) such as CloudFormation, CDK, or Terraform.

Practical example:

```bash
aws s3 cp s3://my-backup-bucket/app-backup-2026-04-26.tar.gz .
tar -xzf app-backup-2026-04-26.tar.gz
```

For AWS-native services, you often restore from snapshots or backup copies rather than raw files.

- **Amazon RDS**
  - Restore from a snapshot in the target Region after copying the snapshot.
- **Amazon EBS**
  - Copy snapshots to another Region, then create volumes from the copied snapshot.
- **AWS Backup**
  - Use backup plans and copy jobs to copy recovery points to another Region or account where supported.

Example EBS snapshot copy flow:

```bash
aws ec2 copy-snapshot \
  --source-region us-east-1 \
  --source-snapshot-id snap-0123456789abcdef0 \
  --description "Cross-Region copy for DR"
```

**Pilot light**

Pilot light keeps the critical data layer running in the secondary Region, while compute is minimized or stopped until failover.

- Keep databases replicated or continuously copied.
- Keep networking, IAM, and deployment artifacts ready.
- Scale compute up only during a disaster.

This pattern works well when data recovery is the hardest part, but application servers can be recreated quickly.

Typical pilot light components:

- **Database replication**
  - Aurora Global Database, RDS snapshot copy, DynamoDB global tables, or application-level replication.
- **Infrastructure templates**
  - Prebuilt VPC, subnets, security groups, and load balancers.
- **DNS or traffic management**
  - Route 53 failover routing or health checks.
- **Automation**
  - Scripts or pipelines to scale the standby environment.

**Warm standby**

Warm standby keeps a smaller but functional version of the application running in the secondary Region.

- The secondary Region serves traffic at reduced capacity.
- You can scale it up quickly during failover.
- This reduces RTO compared with pilot light.

Use warm standby when:
- You need faster recovery than backup and restore.
- You cannot tolerate the time needed to provision all compute from scratch.
- You want a tested, continuously running secondary stack.

A common warm standby design includes:
- A smaller Auto Scaling group in the secondary Region.
- Replicated data stores.
- Route 53 failover routing.
- Automated scaling policies to increase capacity after failover.

**Multi-site active-active**

Active-active means both Regions serve traffic at the same time.

- Best for very low RTO and low perceived downtime.
- Requires careful data consistency design.
- Often uses stateless application tiers and globally replicated data stores.

This pattern is powerful but difficult when the application has strong write consistency requirements.

Common building blocks:
- **Route 53 latency-based routing** or weighted routing.
- **AWS Global Accelerator** for static anycast IPs and fast traffic steering over the AWS global network.
- **DynamoDB global tables** for multi-Region active-active data access.
- **Aurora Global Database** for low-latency cross-Region replication with a primary Region and secondary Regions for disaster recovery and reads.

> Active-active is not automatically the best choice. If your data model cannot handle concurrent writes cleanly, the operational risk may outweigh the availability benefit.

**Service-specific DR considerations**

Different AWS services have different native resilience models. Exam questions often test whether you know which service already solves part of the problem.

**Amazon RDS and Aurora**

- **RDS Multi-AZ**
  - Synchronous standby in another AZ.
  - Automatic failover within the Region.
  - Good for HA, not for cross-Region DR by itself.
- **RDS snapshots**
  - Copy snapshots to another Region for DR.
  - Restore in the target Region when needed.
- **Aurora**
  - Storage is distributed across multiple AZs in a Region.
  - Aurora replicas can support failover and read scaling.
- **Aurora Global Database**
  - Primary Region plus secondary Regions with low-latency replication.
  - Suitable for global reads and disaster recovery.
  - Failover promotes a secondary Region to become the new primary Region.

**Amazon S3**

S3 is a regional service with very high durability and availability characteristics. For exam purposes, describe it as regional and highly durable rather than focusing on internal replication mechanics.

- Use **versioning** to protect against accidental deletes and overwrites.
- Use **cross-Region replication** for DR or data locality.
- Use **S3 Object Lock** when immutability is required.
- Use **lifecycle policies** to manage backup retention.

Example replication configuration concept:

```json
{
  "Role": "arn:aws:iam::123456789012:role/s3-replication-role",
  "Rules": [
    {
      "Status": "Enabled",
      "Priority": 1,
      "DeleteMarkerReplication": { "Status": "Disabled" },
      "Filter": { "Prefix": "" },
      "Destination": {
        "Bucket": "arn:aws:s3:::my-dr-bucket"
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

**Amazon DynamoDB**

DynamoDB global tables support multi-Region, multi-active replication for supported table versions.

- Good for globally distributed applications.
- Designed for low-latency access in multiple Regions.
- Requires careful conflict handling in application design.

A common CLI validation approach is to inspect the table and global table settings rather than relying on older commands that may vary by CLI version.

```bash
aws dynamodb describe-table --table-name orders
```

**Amazon EFS**

EFS is regional and can be mounted from multiple AZs in the same Region. For cross-Region recovery, use EFS replication or backup/restore.

- **EFS replication** is asynchronous.
- It is useful for DR, not for synchronous active-active file sharing across Regions.
- Restore testing matters because file metadata and permissions must be validated.

**Amazon Route 53**

Route 53 is a DNS service used heavily in DR designs.

- **Failover routing**
  - Sends traffic to a primary endpoint when healthy and a secondary endpoint when the primary fails.
- **Weighted routing**
  - Splits traffic by percentage.
- **Latency-based routing**
  - Sends users to the Region with the lowest network latency.
- **Geolocation routing**
  - Routes based on user location.
- **Health checks**
  - Detect endpoint health and drive failover decisions.

For non-alias failover records, remember that the record syntax differs from alias records.

```text
Primary record:
- Type: A
- Value: 203.0.113.10
- TTL: 60
- HealthCheckId: hchk-1234567890abcdef0

Secondary record:
- Type: A
- Value: 198.51.100.20
- TTL: 60
```

For alias records, you use `AliasTarget` instead of `TTL` and `ResourceRecords`.

**AWS Global Accelerator**

Global Accelerator improves global traffic routing by using static anycast IP addresses and the AWS global network.

- Useful for TCP and UDP workloads.
- Helps reduce the impact of internet path variability.
- Supports endpoint health checks and traffic dials.
- Often improves failover experience, but not every client sees instantaneous recovery because existing connections, session state, and health detection timing still matter.

Use it when:
- You need static IPs for global entry points.
- You want faster traffic steering than DNS-based approaches.
- You run latency-sensitive or multi-Region applications.

**CloudFront**

CloudFront is a global content delivery network (CDN) that can also help with DR for static and cacheable content.

- Origin failover can switch between primary and secondary origins.
- ACM certificates for CloudFront must be in `us-east-1`.
- It is not a full application DR solution by itself, but it can reduce origin load and improve resilience for edge-delivered content.

**IAM and control plane considerations**

IAM is a global service within a partition, which matters when designing cross-Region DR because identity policies and roles are not Region-scoped in the same way as many data plane resources.

- Use IAM roles for automation and service access.
- Replicate or template IAM resources carefully in multi-account and multi-Region designs.
- Remember that some services have Region-specific resources even when the identity layer is global.

**Pattern selection by requirement**

Choose the simplest pattern that meets the business objective.

- If you need only AZ resilience, choose **Multi-AZ**.
- If you need Region survivability, choose **Multi-Region DR**.
- If you need very low RTO and RPO, consider **active-active**.
- If you need low cost and can accept longer recovery, choose **backup and restore** or **pilot light**.
- If you need faster recovery without full active-active complexity, choose **warm standby**.

A practical decision guide:

1. Start with the required RTO and RPO.
2. Identify whether the failure domain is AZ, Region, or global.
3. Determine whether the application is stateless, stateful, or strongly consistent.
4. Select the least complex pattern that satisfies the objectives.
5. Test failover and restore procedures regularly.

**Common exam traps**

- **Confusing Multi-AZ with Multi-Region**
  - Multi-AZ is high availability within one Region.
  - Multi-Region is disaster recovery or global distribution.
- **Assuming DNS failover is instant**
  - DNS caching and TTL values affect how quickly clients switch.
- **Treating all data stores the same**
  - Some services support native multi-Region replication; others require snapshots or custom replication.
- **Overstating active-active simplicity**
  - Data conflicts, write ordering, and session consistency can make active-active difficult.
- **Using the wrong restore terminology**
  - Snapshots and copied recovery points are commonly restored cross-Region; automated backups are not always interchangeable with snapshots in every service.
- **Assuming a service is “global” in every sense**
  - Some services are global for control plane purposes, while data plane resources remain regional.

**Practical CLI and configuration examples**

Route 53 failover record example:

```json
{
  "Comment": "Primary failover record",
  "Changes": [
    {
      "Action": "UPSERT",
      "ResourceRecordSet": {
        "Name": "app.example.com",
        "Type": "A",
        "TTL": 60,
        "ResourceRecords": [
          { "Value": "203.0.113.10" }
        ],
        "HealthCheckId": "hchk-1234567890abcdef0"
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

S3 cross-Region replication concept:

```json
{
  "Role": "arn:aws:iam::123456789012:role/s3-replication-role",
  "Rules": [
    {
      "Status": "Enabled",
      "Priority": 1,
      "Filter": { "Prefix": "backups/" },
      "Destination": {
        "Bucket": "arn:aws:s3:::my-dr-bucket",
        "StorageClass": "STANDARD"
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

AWS Backup copy job concept:

```bash
aws backup start-copy-job \
  --recovery-point-arn arn:aws:backup:us-east-1:123456789012:recovery-point:abcd1234 \
  --source-backup-vault-name primary-vault \
  --destination-backup-vault-arn arn:aws:backup:us-west-2:123456789012:backup-vault:dr-vault \
  --iam-role-arn arn:aws:iam::123456789012:role/AWSBackupDefaultServiceRole
```

**Testing and operational readiness**

A DR design is only real if you can execute it.

- Run failover game days.
- Validate DNS cutover and client behavior.
- Test restore from backups, not just backup creation.
- Verify IAM permissions in the target Region.
- Confirm that dependencies such as KMS keys, secrets, and certificates exist where needed.
- Measure actual RTO and RPO during drills.

Useful operational checks:
- Can you rebuild the stack from IaC?
- Can you restore the database into a clean environment?
- Do application configuration values point to the correct Region?
- Are certificates, secrets, and parameter values replicated?
- Does the app tolerate stale caches and session loss?

**Quick comparison summary**

- **Multi-AZ**
  - Best for high availability within one Region.
  - Lower cost and complexity.
  - Does not survive a full Region outage.
- **Multi-Region**
  - Best for regional disaster recovery and global user distribution.
  - Higher cost and complexity.
  - Can support active-active or active-passive designs.
- **Backup and restore**
  - Cheapest and simplest.
  - Slowest recovery.
- **Pilot light**
  - Good balance for many DR scenarios.
- **Warm standby**
  - Faster recovery with moderate cost.
- **Active-active**
  - Highest resilience and availability potential.
  - Hardest to design and operate.

**Exam-ready takeaway**

If the question asks about AZ failure, choose Multi-AZ. If it asks about Region failure, choose Multi-Region DR. If it asks for the fastest recovery with the least downtime, consider warm standby or active-active depending on data consistency and budget. If it asks for the cheapest acceptable recovery, choose backup and restore.
