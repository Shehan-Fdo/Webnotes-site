## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Professional (Exam SAP-C02)

**Capstone design purpose**

A capstone design is the final architecture synthesis step where you turn business goals, technical constraints, and operational needs into a defensible AWS solution. For SAP-C02, you are not just naming services; you are showing that the design is traceable, reviewable, and aligned to measurable outcomes.

The strongest capstone answers explain:
- what the business needs,
- why the chosen architecture fits,
- what tradeoffs you accepted,
- how you will validate success,
- and how stakeholders will review and approve the design.

**Core requirement categories**

A complete capstone design usually starts with a structured requirements set. You should separate requirements into categories so you can map them to architecture decisions later.

- **Business requirements** define the outcome the organization wants.
  - Examples: reduce time to market, support global expansion, improve resilience, lower cost.
- **Functional requirements** define what the system must do.
  - Examples: ingest events, process orders, store documents, expose APIs.
- **Nonfunctional requirements** define quality attributes.
  - Examples: availability, latency, durability, scalability, security, compliance, maintainability.
- **Operational requirements** define how the system is run.
  - Examples: patching windows, backup retention, incident response, deployment frequency.
- **Constraints** limit the design space.
  - Examples: existing identity provider, data residency, legacy database, budget cap, skill gaps.

A good exam answer often begins by identifying which requirement is the real driver. For example, if the business says “global users need fast access,” the architectural driver may be latency and regional distribution, not just “use CloudFront.”

**Requirements discovery and clarification**

In real design work, requirements are rarely complete. You often need to clarify ambiguous statements before choosing services. In SAP-C02 scenarios, this means you should infer the missing details carefully and state assumptions explicitly.

Common clarification areas:
- traffic patterns and peak load
- data classification and compliance scope
- recovery objectives such as <abbr title="Recovery Time Objective">RTO</abbr> and <abbr title="Recovery Point Objective">RPO</abbr>
- integration dependencies with on-premises or third-party systems
- migration constraints and cutover windows
- budget and licensing limitations
- team operating model and support maturity

Use assumptions only when the scenario does not provide enough detail. Keep them visible in the deliverable so reviewers can challenge them.

**Requirements-to-design traceability**

Traceability is a major capstone deliverable and a common gap in weak designs. A requirements-to-design matrix shows how each requirement maps to a specific architectural choice, control, or operational practice.

A simple traceability matrix can look like this:

| Requirement ID | Requirement | Design Decision | AWS Service/Pattern | Validation Method |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| BR-1 | Support 99.99% availability | Multi-AZ deployment with automatic failover | Amazon RDS Multi-AZ, Auto Scaling | Failover test, uptime metrics |
| NFR-2 | Keep p95 API latency under 100 ms | Edge caching and regional compute | Amazon CloudFront, Amazon ECS | Load test, CloudWatch metrics |
| SEC-3 | Encrypt sensitive data at rest | Managed encryption with customer keys | AWS KMS, encrypted S3/RDS | Config audit, key policy review |
| OPS-4 | Restore service within 1 hour | Cross-Region backup and runbook | AWS Backup, pilot light DR | DR exercise, restore test |

This matrix matters because it proves the design is not arbitrary. It also helps you defend tradeoffs during review by showing which requirement each decision satisfies.

**Architecture decision documentation**

A capstone deliverable should include a decision log or architecture decision record set. This is where you capture the “why” behind the design, not just the “what.”

Each decision entry should include:
- decision title
- context
- options considered
- selected option
- rationale
- tradeoffs
- consequences
- owner and date

Example decision log entry:

```text
Decision: Use Amazon Aurora PostgreSQL instead of self-managed PostgreSQL on EC2
Context: The workload needs high availability, automated backups, and reduced operational overhead.
Options considered:
- Self-managed PostgreSQL on EC2
- Amazon RDS for PostgreSQL
- Amazon Aurora PostgreSQL
Selected: Amazon Aurora PostgreSQL
Rationale: Better availability, managed failover, read scaling, and lower admin burden.
Tradeoffs: Higher service cost than self-managed EC2; some engine-level behavior differs from vanilla PostgreSQL.
Consequences: Application team must validate SQL compatibility and connection handling.
```

This format is especially useful when multiple solutions are viable. SAP-C02 often rewards the option that best balances requirements rather than the one with the most features.

**Acceptance criteria and success metrics**

A design is incomplete unless you define how success will be measured. Acceptance criteria tell stakeholders when the solution is good enough to approve, and success metrics show whether the architecture meets business goals after implementation.

Good acceptance criteria are specific and testable:
- application availability meets the target during a failover test
- backup restore completes within the required RTO
- security controls pass review and evidence collection
- throughput supports peak load with acceptable latency
- data replication meets the required RPO

Useful success metrics include:
- availability percentage
- p95 or p99 latency
- error rate
- recovery time
- recovery point
- cost per transaction
- deployment frequency
- mean time to recover <abbr title="Mean Time to Recover">MTTR</abbr>

Example acceptance criteria set:
- The system must sustain 5,000 requests per second with p95 latency under 150 ms.
- A single-AZ failure must not cause more than 5 minutes of service interruption.
- Database restore from backup must complete within 45 minutes.
- All sensitive data must be encrypted in transit and at rest.
- Monthly infrastructure cost must remain within the approved budget envelope.

These criteria help you choose between architectures. If one design is cheaper but cannot meet the recovery target, it is not the right answer.

**Stakeholder communication deliverables**

Capstone work is not only technical. You also need artifacts that help executives, architects, security reviewers, and operations teams understand the design quickly.

Common communication deliverables:
- **executive summary**
  - one-page overview of the problem, recommendation, benefits, and risks
- **architecture review walkthrough**
  - presentation or narrated review of the design, assumptions, and tradeoffs
- **decision log**
  - record of major architecture choices and rationale
- **risk register**
  - list of known risks, mitigations, and owners
- **implementation roadmap**
  - phased plan for delivery, migration, or rollout
- **operational handoff notes**
  - runbooks, monitoring expectations, escalation paths

An executive summary should avoid implementation detail overload. It should answer:
- what problem are you solving?
- what is the recommended architecture?
- why is it the best fit?
- what are the major risks?
- what is the expected business impact?

**Sample architecture review package**

A practical review package often includes a small set of artifacts that can be shared before a design review meeting.

```text
Architecture Review Package
├── 01-executive-summary.pdf
├── 02-requirements-matrix.xlsx
├── 03-architecture-diagram.drawio
├── 04-decision-log.md
├── 05-risk-register.md
├── 06-validation-plan.md
├── 07-operational-readiness-checklist.md
└── 08-open-questions.md
```

This package is useful because it gives reviewers a complete view of the design without forcing them to infer missing context from a single diagram.

**Assumptions, dependencies, and open questions**

Structured capstone deliverables should explicitly separate assumptions, dependencies, and open questions. This prevents hidden gaps from becoming production issues.

- **Assumptions** are statements you accept as true for the design.
  - Example: “The application team can refactor the service to be stateless.”
- **Dependencies** are external items the design relies on.
  - Example: “Corporate identity federation must be available before launch.”
- **Open questions** are unresolved items that need follow-up.
  - Example: “Will the compliance team require customer-managed keys for all workloads?”

A simple format helps:

```markdown
### Assumptions
- Traffic growth will remain under 30% quarter over quarter.
- The source database can support logical replication during migration.

### Dependencies
- Network team will provision Direct Connect.
- Security team will approve KMS key policy structure.

### Open Questions
- Is cross-region active-active required or is warm standby acceptable?
- What is the retention requirement for audit logs?
```

This structure is valuable in exam scenarios because it shows disciplined design thinking even when the prompt is incomplete.

**Tradeoff analysis**

SAP-C02 expects you to compare alternatives, not just name a preferred service. Tradeoff analysis should connect the requirement to the consequence.

Common tradeoff dimensions:
- cost versus resilience
- simplicity versus flexibility
- managed service versus operational control
- latency versus consistency
- speed of delivery versus long-term maintainability
- regional isolation versus global availability

Example scenario:
- If the workload needs rapid deployment and low operational overhead, managed services such as Amazon RDS, Amazon ECS, or AWS Lambda may be preferable.
- If the workload needs deep OS-level control or custom networking, EC2-based designs may be justified.
- If the workload requires strong regional isolation, a multi-region active-active design may be too complex unless the business impact of downtime is severe.

A strong answer states the tradeoff directly:
- “I choose managed failover because the team lacks 24/7 database administration capacity.”
- “I avoid active-active because the added complexity does not materially improve the business outcome for this RTO.”

**Scenario-based decision examples**

These examples reflect the kind of reasoning SAP-C02 rewards.

- **Scenario: global read-heavy application**
  - Requirement: low latency for users in multiple continents.
  - Better fit: CloudFront for edge caching, regional application tiers, and read replicas or global database patterns where appropriate.
  - Why: latency is the primary driver, so edge distribution and regional proximity matter more than a single centralized deployment.

- **Scenario: regulated financial workload**
  - Requirement: strict auditability, encryption, and controlled access.
  - Better fit: centralized logging, AWS KMS, least-privilege IAM, VPC endpoints, and strong separation of duties.
  - Why: compliance and evidence collection outweigh convenience.

- **Scenario: legacy application migration with minimal change**
  - Requirement: move quickly with limited refactoring.
  - Better fit: rehost or replatform with EC2, Application Load Balancer, and managed storage where possible.
  - Why: the business values migration speed and reduced risk over modernization.

- **Scenario: disaster recovery for a critical service**
  - Requirement: restore service within 30 minutes and lose no more than 5 minutes of data.
  - Better fit: warm standby or pilot light with automated failover, depending on budget and complexity.
  - Why: backup-only approaches usually cannot meet aggressive recovery objectives.

**Deliverables by audience**

Different stakeholders need different levels of detail. A capstone package should be tailored accordingly.

| Audience | Primary Deliverable | What They Need |
|---|---|---|
| Executive sponsor | Executive summary | Business value, cost, risk, timeline |
| Architecture board | Review deck, decision log, traceability matrix | Rationale, alternatives, tradeoffs |
| Security team | Security controls mapping, risk register | Encryption, identity, logging, compliance |
| Operations team | Runbooks, monitoring plan, rollback plan | Supportability, alerts, recovery steps |
| Delivery team | Implementation roadmap, diagrams, dependencies | Build sequence, integration points, assumptions |

This separation helps you avoid overloading one artifact with every detail. It also mirrors real-world review processes.

**Validation and testing plan**

A design should include a plan for proving that it works. Validation is not only functional testing; it also includes resilience, security, and operational testing.

Validation activities may include:
- load testing to confirm throughput and latency
- failover testing to verify high availability
- restore testing to validate backup strategy
- security testing to confirm access controls and encryption
- game days or chaos testing for operational readiness
- cost review after pilot deployment

Example validation checklist:
1. Verify infrastructure deployment matches the approved diagram.
2. Confirm IAM roles and policies follow least privilege.
3. Test application failover across Availability Zones.
4. Restore a database backup into a test environment.
5. Measure latency and error rates under peak load.
6. Review logs, alarms, and dashboards with operations.
7. Document results and update the decision log if the design changes.

**Practical AWS examples**

A capstone design often combines multiple AWS services into a coherent pattern.

```yaml
# Example high-level deployment pattern
Application:
  Compute: Amazon ECS on Fargate
  LoadBalancing: Application Load Balancer
  Edge: Amazon CloudFront
Data:
  PrimaryDB: Amazon Aurora PostgreSQL
  Cache: Amazon ElastiCache for Redis
Security:
  Encryption: AWS KMS
  Secrets: AWS Secrets Manager
  Logging: AWS CloudTrail, Amazon CloudWatch Logs
Resilience:
  MultiAZ: true
  Backup: AWS Backup
  DR: Cross-region snapshot copy
```

These examples show how design artifacts connect to implementation and verification.

**Common failure modes**

Weak capstone designs usually fail for predictable reasons.

- They list services without explaining the requirement each service satisfies.
- They ignore operational realities such as patching, monitoring, and rollback.
- They omit recovery objectives and therefore cannot justify resilience choices.
- They do not document assumptions, so hidden dependencies remain invisible.
- They present a diagram but no decision log, traceability matrix, or validation plan.
- They choose the most advanced architecture instead of the most appropriate one.

A useful exam habit is to ask: “What requirement forced this decision?” If you cannot answer that clearly, the design is probably too generic.

**How to structure a strong capstone deliverable**

A complete deliverable set usually follows a repeatable flow.

1. Define the business problem and success criteria.
2. Capture functional, nonfunctional, operational, and compliance requirements.
3. Record assumptions, dependencies, and open questions.
4. Compare candidate architectures and document tradeoffs.
5. Select the preferred design and explain why it wins.
6. Map requirements to design decisions in a traceability matrix.
7. Produce diagrams, decision logs, and review materials.
8. Define validation tests and acceptance criteria.
9. Prepare operational handoff and rollout artifacts.

This sequence keeps the design defensible from first principles through implementation readiness.

**Exam-focused guidance**

For SAP-C02, the best answer is usually the one that is:
- aligned to the stated business outcome,
- realistic for the operational model,
- secure by default,
- resilient to the required failure domain,
- and supported by clear documentation.

When two options both work, prefer the one that:
- reduces operational burden,
- meets the recovery target with the least complexity,
- and provides the clearest path to validation and support.

If the prompt asks for a design recommendation, include the deliverables mindset in your reasoning:
- what you would present to stakeholders,
- how you would prove the design,
- and how you would track unresolved items.

**Quick review checklist**

Use this checklist before finalizing a capstone answer:
- requirements are categorized and prioritized
- assumptions are explicit
- dependencies are identified
- open questions are listed
- architecture decisions are justified
- tradeoffs are acknowledged
- requirements map to design choices
- acceptance criteria are measurable
- validation steps are defined
- stakeholder-facing artifacts are included
