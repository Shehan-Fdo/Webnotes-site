## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Professional (Exam SAP-C02)

**Cross-account access fundamentals**

Cross-account access in AWS usually means one account assumes an IAM role in another account using AWS Security Token Service (<abbr title="Security Token Service">STS</abbr>). The trusted account owns the role, and the trusting account or service assumes it. For exam scenarios, the key question is not just “can it work?” but “how do you scope trust so only the intended principal can assume the role?”

A role trust policy controls who can call `sts:AssumeRole`. The permissions policy attached to the role controls what the assumed role can do after it is assumed. These are separate controls, and both matter.

- **Trust policy** answers: who may assume the role?
- **Permissions policy** answers: what can the role do once assumed?
- **Session policy** can further restrict the permissions granted during a specific assume-role session.
- **External ID** helps prevent confused deputy problems in third-party access patterns.

A basic cross-account role trust policy often looks like this:

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "AllowCrossAccountAssumeRole",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Principal": {
        "AWS": "arn:aws:iam::111122223333:root"
      },
      "Action": "sts:AssumeRole"
    }
  ]
}
```

This allows principals from account `111122223333` to assume the role, but it is broader than many exam scenarios require. In practice, you often scope trust to a specific role, a specific user, or an AWS service principal.

**Trust policy principal variants**

The `Principal` element in a role trust policy can target different identity types. The right choice depends on whether the caller is another AWS account, an AWS service, or a federated identity path.

- **Account root principal**: `arn:aws:iam::<account-id>:root`
  - Trusts the entire account, not just the root user.
  - Use it when you want any principal in that account to be able to assume the role, subject to additional conditions.
  - This is common in simple account-to-account delegation, but it is often too broad for least privilege.

- **Specific IAM role principal**: `arn:aws:iam::<account-id>:role/<role-name>`
  - Trusts only that role.
  - This is the preferred pattern when one workload role in the source account should assume a target role.
  - It reduces blast radius compared with trusting the whole account.

- **Specific IAM user principal**: `arn:aws:iam::<account-id>:user/<user-name>`
  - Trusts a named IAM user.
  - Less common in modern designs because roles are preferred over long-term credentials.
  - Can appear in legacy or administrative workflows.

- **AWS service principal**: for example, `ec2.amazonaws.com`, `lambda.amazonaws.com`, `ecs-tasks.amazonaws.com`
  - Used when an AWS service assumes the role on your behalf.
  - Common for service-linked or service execution roles.
  - This is not cross-account by itself, but it is a frequent trust-policy pattern you should recognize.

- **Federated principal**
  - Used for identity providers such as SAML or OIDC.
  - More relevant to workforce federation than cross-account role assumption, but it can appear in the same trust-policy discussions.

A more scoped trust policy for a specific role in another account looks like this:

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "AllowSpecificRoleOnly",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Principal": {
        "AWS": "arn:aws:iam::111122223333:role/ThirdPartyIntegrationRole"
      },
      "Action": "sts:AssumeRole"
    }
  ]
}
```

This is usually the best starting point for exam answers when the scenario says “only this workload should access the role.”

**Confused deputy problem**

A confused deputy occurs when a trusted third party is tricked into using its own permissions on behalf of the wrong customer. In AWS exam terms, the classic case is a SaaS vendor that has a role in each customer account or a role in the vendor account that accesses customer resources.

A concrete example:

- Vendor `AcmeSaaS` integrates with AWS accounts for log collection.
- Customer A and Customer B both create roles that trust the vendor.
- The vendor’s application receives a request from Customer A.
- Without a unique external identifier, the vendor might accidentally use the role or credentials associated with Customer B.
- Customer B’s resources could be accessed in response to Customer A’s request.

The problem is not that the vendor is malicious. The problem is that the vendor is trusted by multiple customers and may not be able to distinguish which customer initiated the request unless the trust relationship includes a customer-specific signal.

<mark>External IDs</mark> are the standard mitigation for this pattern.

**External IDs and `sts:ExternalId`**

An external ID is a value supplied by the customer and checked by AWS during role assumption. It is passed as the `ExternalId` parameter to `sts:AssumeRole` and compared against a condition in the role trust policy.

Important exam-level points:

- The external ID is typically **generated or assigned by the customer or by the vendor on behalf of that customer**, depending on the integration design.
- It can be **any string** that both sides agree on.
- It does **not** need to be globally unique.
- It **should be unique per vendor-customer relationship** to prevent cross-customer confusion.
- It is not a secret in the same way as a password, but it should still be treated as a control value and not casually exposed.
- It is especially common in **third-party SaaS** and **AWS Marketplace** style integrations.

A trust policy using `sts:ExternalId` looks like this:

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "AllowVendorWithExternalId",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Principal": {
        "AWS": "arn:aws:iam::444455556666:role/AcmeSaaSAccessRole"
      },
      "Action": "sts:AssumeRole",
      "Condition": {
        "StringEquals": {
          "sts:ExternalId": "customer-a-7f3c2b91"
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

The vendor must pass the same value when assuming the role:

```bash
aws sts assume-role \
  --role-arn arn:aws:iam::123456789012:role/CustomerAccessRole \
  --role-session-name acme-customer-a \
  --external-id customer-a-7f3c2b91
```

If the vendor omits the external ID or passes the wrong one, the assume-role call fails.

**When to use external IDs**

Use an external ID when a third party assumes a role in your account and that third party serves multiple customers. This is the classic confused deputy defense.

Common contexts include:

- SaaS products that ingest logs, metrics, or security findings
- Managed service providers
- AWS Marketplace integrations that require cross-account access
- Automation vendors that deploy or read resources across customer accounts

You usually do not need an external ID for simple account-to-account access between two accounts you control, because the trust boundary is different. In those cases, scoping the principal to a specific role and using conditions such as `aws:PrincipalArn` or organization-based controls may be enough.

**How external IDs fit into trust policy conditions**

The trust policy can combine `sts:ExternalId` with other conditions to narrow access further.

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "VendorAccessWithConditions",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Principal": {
        "AWS": "arn:aws:iam::444455556666:role/AcmeSaaSAccessRole"
      },
      "Action": "sts:AssumeRole",
      "Condition": {
        "StringEquals": {
          "sts:ExternalId": "customer-a-7f3c2b91",
          "aws:PrincipalArn": "arn:aws:iam::444455556666:role/AcmeSaaSAccessRole"
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

In practice, `Principal` already identifies the role, so `aws:PrincipalArn` is often redundant in a trust policy. It is more useful in resource policies or when you need additional conditional logic. For exam purposes, the important idea is that conditions can narrow trust beyond the principal alone.

**AWS Organizations and `aws:PrincipalOrgID`**

`aws:PrincipalOrgID` is a condition key that restricts access to principals from a specific AWS Organization. It is often used in resource policies and sometimes in trust policies to ensure that only identities from accounts in your organization can access a resource.

This is useful when:

- You want to allow access from any account in your organization.
- You want to prevent access from external accounts even if they know the resource ARN.
- You are building centralized controls across many accounts.

Example resource policy pattern:

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "AllowOnlyMyOrg",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Principal": "*",
      "Action": "s3:GetObject",
      "Resource": "arn:aws:s3:::my-shared-bucket/*",
      "Condition": {
        "StringEquals": {
          "aws:PrincipalOrgID": "o-a1b2c3d4e5"
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

Key exam distinction:

- `sts:ExternalId` protects against confused deputy in third-party assume-role flows.
- `aws:PrincipalOrgID` restricts access to identities from your organization.
- They solve different problems and can be used together in broader designs.

**MFA in trust policies**

Multi-factor authentication (<abbr title="Multi-Factor Authentication">MFA</abbr>) can be required in a trust policy, but it is usually relevant for human access rather than third-party automation. If a role is assumed by a person or by a process that uses MFA-backed credentials, you can require MFA with `aws:MultiFactorAuthPresent`.

Example:

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "RequireMFAForAssumeRole",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Principal": {
        "AWS": "arn:aws:iam::111122223333:root"
      },
      "Action": "sts:AssumeRole",
      "Condition": {
        "Bool": {
          "aws:MultiFactorAuthPresent": "true"
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

Use MFA when the caller is a human operator and the risk profile justifies it. It is generally not the right control for SaaS integrations, because automated vendors cannot present MFA in the same way a person can.

**Session tags and attribute-based access control**

Session tags let the caller pass tags into the assumed role session. These tags can then be used in permissions policies or resource policies for attribute-based access control (<abbr title="Attribute-Based Access Control">ABAC</abbr>).

This is useful when you want to carry context such as:

- customer ID
- environment
- application name
- cost center

Example assume-role call with session tags:

```bash
aws sts assume-role \
  --role-arn arn:aws:iam::123456789012:role/SharedAccessRole \
  --role-session-name app-prod \
  --tags Project=Payments Environment=Prod \
  --transitive-tag-keys Project
```

A trust policy can allow tag passing with `sts:TagSession` and can restrict which tags are allowed:

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "AllowAssumeRoleAndTagSession",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Principal": {
        "AWS": "arn:aws:iam::111122223333:role/AppRole"
      },
      "Action": [
        "sts:AssumeRole",
        "sts:TagSession"
      ],
      "Condition": {
        "StringEquals": {
          "sts:ExternalId": "customer-a-7f3c2b91"
        },
        "ForAllValues:StringEquals": {
          "aws:TagKeys": [
            "Project",
            "Environment"
          ]
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

Session tags are relevant when the authorization decision depends on session context, not just on who assumed the role. For example, you might allow access to resources tagged `Project=Payments` only when the session tag matches.

**Practical trust policy patterns**

A good exam answer often combines a narrow principal with one or more conditions.

1. **Specific role plus external ID**
   - Best for third-party SaaS access.
   - Prevents cross-customer confusion.

2. **Account root plus organization condition**
   - Useful when any account in an organization may assume the role.
   - Less precise than a specific role, but manageable in centralized environments.

3. **Specific role plus MFA**
   - Useful for human-operated cross-account admin access.
   - Not ideal for automation.

4. **Specific role plus session tags**
   - Useful for ABAC and context propagation.
   - Often paired with `sts:TagSession`.

Example of a tightly scoped trust policy for a vendor integration:

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "VendorAssumeRoleOnlyWithExternalId",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Principal": {
        "AWS": "arn:aws:iam::444455556666:role/AcmeSaaSAccessRole"
      },
      "Action": [
        "sts:AssumeRole",
        "sts:TagSession"
      ],
      "Condition": {
        "StringEquals": {
          "sts:ExternalId": "cust-12345"
        },
        "ForAllValues:StringEquals": {
          "aws:TagKeys": [
            "CustomerId"
          ]
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

**How to reason about exam scenarios**

When you see a question about cross-account access, ask these questions:

- Is the caller another AWS account you control?
- Is the caller a third-party vendor or SaaS provider?
- Is the access human-driven or automated?
- Do you need to prevent confused deputy?
- Should the trust be limited to one role, one user, or an entire account?
- Is the access limited to accounts in your AWS Organization?

A useful decision guide:

- If the scenario is **third-party SaaS** and multiple customers exist, choose **external ID**.
- If the scenario is **internal multi-account access**, choose a **specific role principal** or an **organization-based condition**.
- If the scenario is **human cross-account admin**, consider **MFA**.
- If the scenario needs **context-aware authorization**, consider **session tags**.
- If the scenario spans **many accounts in one organization**, consider **`aws:PrincipalOrgID`** in resource policies.

**CLI examples for validation and troubleshooting**

You can test role assumption directly with the AWS CLI. This is useful for verifying trust policy changes.

```bash
aws sts assume-role \
  --role-arn arn:aws:iam::123456789012:role/CrossAccountReadOnly \
  --role-session-name test-session
```

With an external ID:

```bash
aws sts assume-role \
  --role-arn arn:aws:iam::123456789012:role/VendorAccessRole \
  --role-session-name vendor-test \
  --external-id customer-a-7f3c2b91
```

If the trust policy requires MFA, the caller must use MFA-backed credentials. If the trust policy requires session tags, include `--tags` and ensure the role allows `sts:TagSession`.

You can also inspect the caller identity after assuming the role:

```bash
aws sts get-caller-identity
```

This helps confirm which account and role are active in the session.

**Common pitfalls**

- **Trusting the whole account when only one role is needed**
  - This expands access unnecessarily.
  - Prefer a specific role principal.

- **Using external ID as a secret**
  - It is a control value, not a password.
  - Its purpose is to distinguish customers, not to hide credentials.

- **Assuming external ID must be globally unique**
  - It does not.
  - It should be unique within the vendor-customer relationship.

- **Using MFA for vendor automation**
  - Usually impractical.
  - Use external ID instead.

- **Confusing trust policy with permissions policy**
  - Trust policy controls who can assume.
  - Permissions policy controls what the role can do.

- **Forgetting `sts:TagSession` when session tags are required**
  - The assume-role call can fail or tags may not be usable as intended.

- **Using `aws:PrincipalOrgID` for third-party SaaS**
  - This only helps when the caller is inside your organization.
  - It does not replace external ID for vendor access.

**Exam-ready mental model**

Think of cross-account trust as a layered defense:

- The **principal** says who may ask.
- The **condition** says under what circumstances they may ask.
- The **permissions policy** says what they may do after they are trusted.
- The **session context** can carry extra attributes for fine-grained authorization.

For confused deputy mitigation, the most important pattern is:

- third-party vendor
- multiple customers
- role assumption into customer accounts
- unique external ID per customer-vendor relationship

That combination is the classic signal that `sts:ExternalId` belongs in the trust policy.

**Quick reference**

- `sts:AssumeRole` grants temporary credentials for a role.
- `sts:ExternalId` mitigates confused deputy in third-party access.
- `aws:PrincipalOrgID` restricts access to principals in an AWS Organization.
- `aws:MultiFactorAuthPresent` enforces MFA for human-driven assume-role flows.
- `sts:TagSession` enables session tags for ABAC.
