## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Professional (Exam SAP-C02)

**Hybrid connectivity overview**

AWS Direct Connect provides a dedicated network connection from your on-premises environment to AWS. You use it when you need more consistent latency, higher throughput, or private connectivity that does not traverse the public internet. For SAP-C02, the key is not just knowing the service names, but understanding where traffic terminates, how routes are exchanged, and how to design for redundancy.

Direct Connect is commonly combined with VPN for layered resilience. Direct Connect gives predictable primary connectivity, while AWS Site-to-Site VPN provides encrypted backup or an alternate path. In multi-Region or multi-VPC designs, Direct Connect Gateway helps you avoid building separate physical connections for every Region or VPC attachment model.

**Core Direct Connect concepts**

Direct Connect uses <abbr title="Border Gateway Protocol">BGP</abbr> for route exchange over a private circuit. You typically provision a connection at a Direct Connect location, then create a virtual interface (<abbr title="VIF">virtual interface</abbr>) to carry traffic for a specific use case.

- **Dedicated connection**: A physical link between your router and AWS at a Direct Connect location.
- **Hosted connection**: A connection delivered by an AWS Direct Connect Partner.
- **Virtual interface**: A logical interface on the physical connection.
- **BGP session**: The routing adjacency used to exchange prefixes.
- **802.1Q VLAN**: Used to separate traffic on a connection when multiple VIFs are present.

Direct Connect does not encrypt traffic by default. If you need encryption, you usually add VPN over the internet or use application-layer encryption. For many exam scenarios, the right answer is to combine Direct Connect with VPN rather than assume Direct Connect alone satisfies confidentiality requirements.

**Virtual interface types**

AWS supports different VIF types depending on the destination and routing model.

- **Private VIF**: Used for private connectivity to VPC resources through a virtual private gateway or through a Direct Connect Gateway association model.
- **Public VIF**: Used to reach public AWS service endpoints over Direct Connect.
- **Transit VIF**: Used to connect Direct Connect to a Transit Gateway for centralized routing across multiple VPCs and, in supported designs, multiple Regions.

A private VIF does not directly “attach” to both a virtual private gateway and a Direct Connect Gateway at the same time. The association model depends on the architecture. In practice, the VIF terminates into the Direct Connect side of the design, and routing is then associated to the relevant gateway construct. This distinction matters on the exam because the question may ask where the VIF terminates versus where route propagation occurs.

**Direct Connect Gateway**

A Direct Connect Gateway (<abbr title="Direct Connect Gateway">DXGW</abbr>) is a global construct that helps you connect a Direct Connect private VIF to one or more virtual private gateways, and in supported designs to Transit Gateways through a transit VIF pattern. It is especially useful when you need to reach multiple VPCs across Regions without creating separate Direct Connect connections for each Region.

Use DXGW when you need:

- **Multi-Region reachability** from a single Direct Connect connection.
- **Centralized on-premises connectivity** to multiple VPCs.
- **Simplified routing** for shared services or hub-and-spoke topologies.
- **Separation of concerns** between the physical connection and the VPC attachment layer.

DXGW is not the same thing as Transit Gateway. Transit Gateway centralizes routing between VPCs, VPNs, and Direct Connect attachments. DXGW is specifically part of the Direct Connect integration story and is used to extend a Direct Connect-based design to supported gateway attachments. On exam questions, choose Transit Gateway when the problem is about VPC-to-VPC or hub routing, and choose DXGW when the problem is about scaling Direct Connect connectivity across multiple gateways or Regions.

**Common architecture patterns**

A few patterns appear frequently in professional-level scenarios.

- **Single VPC, private connectivity**
  - Direct Connect private VIF to a virtual private gateway.
  - Best when you only need one VPC or a simple private path.
- **Multi-VPC, multi-Region via DXGW**
  - Direct Connect private VIF to DXGW.
  - DXGW associated with multiple virtual private gateways in different Regions.
  - Useful when on-premises networks must reach several VPCs without separate circuits.
- **Centralized transit architecture**
  - Direct Connect transit VIF to Transit Gateway.
  - Transit Gateway propagates routes to attached VPCs and VPNs.
  - Best for large-scale hub-and-spoke designs.
- **Hybrid resilience**
  - Direct Connect as primary path.
  - Site-to-Site VPN as backup or secondary path.
  - BGP route preference controls failover behavior.

**Direct Connect and VPN together**

VPN is often paired with Direct Connect for redundancy or encryption. AWS Site-to-Site VPN creates IPsec tunnels over the internet, and AWS provides two tunnels for high availability. However, tunnel redundancy does not guarantee application failover by itself. Your routing, BGP preferences, and device configuration must be correct.

Typical reasons to combine them:

- **Encryption requirement**: Direct Connect is private, but not inherently encrypted.
- **Backup path**: VPN can take over if the Direct Connect circuit fails.
- **Migration support**: You can move traffic gradually from VPN to Direct Connect.
- **Operational flexibility**: You can keep VPN as a control-plane or emergency path.

A common design is to advertise the same prefixes over both Direct Connect and VPN, then prefer Direct Connect using BGP attributes such as local preference on your edge router. If Direct Connect fails, the VPN route becomes active.

**VPN tunnel behavior and failover**

AWS Site-to-Site VPN uses two tunnels per VPN connection. This gives you redundancy, but you still need to configure your customer gateway device correctly.

- **BGP over VPN** is preferred when you want dynamic route exchange and automatic failover.
- **Static routes** can work, but they are less flexible and usually less desirable for large environments.
- **IKE** (<abbr title="Internet Key Exchange">IKE</abbr>) and IPsec parameters must match AWS requirements.
- **Dead Peer Detection** helps detect tunnel failure faster.

Example strong design choices:

- Use BGP on both tunnels.
- Advertise identical prefixes on both tunnels.
- Set route preference so the primary tunnel or Direct Connect path is preferred.
- Test failover by disabling the primary path and verifying route convergence.

**Practical CLI examples**

Create a virtual private gateway and attach it to a VPC:

```bash
aws ec2 create-virtual-private-gateway \
  --amazon-side-asn 64512
```

Attach the gateway to a VPC:

```bash
aws ec2 attach-virtual-private-gateway \
  --virtual-private-gateway-id vgw-0123456789abcdef0 \
  --vpc-id vpc-0123456789abcdef0
```

Create a customer gateway for a VPN device:

```bash
aws ec2 create-customer-gateway \
  --type ipsec.1 \
  --public-ip 203.0.113.10 \
  --bgp-asn 65000
```

Create a VPN connection to a virtual private gateway:

```bash
aws ec2 create-vpn-connection \
  --type ipsec.1 \
  --customer-gateway-id cgw-0123456789abcdef0 \
  --vpn-gateway-id vgw-0123456789abcdef0 \
  --options '{"StaticRoutesOnly":false}'
```

> Use the exact Direct Connect or VPN association command for the attachment model you are implementing. The API shape differs between virtual private gateway, Direct Connect Gateway, and Transit Gateway workflows.

**Routing and BGP design**

BGP is central to hybrid routing. You should understand both AWS-side and on-premises-side route control.

- **Prefix advertisement**: Advertise only the networks you intend to expose.
- **Route filtering**: Prevent accidental route leaks between environments.
- **Route preference**: Use BGP attributes to prefer Direct Connect over VPN when appropriate.
- **Asymmetric routing**: Avoid designs where return traffic takes a different path unless the devices and security controls support it.

Important routing behaviors:

- Direct Connect private connectivity is usually preferred for stable, high-throughput traffic.
- VPN is often used as a backup because it is easier to fail over automatically when BGP is configured correctly.
- Public VIFs advertise AWS public prefixes and are not used for private VPC routing.
- Transit Gateway route tables control propagation and association for attached networks.

**Security and operational considerations**

Direct Connect improves network consistency, but it does not replace security controls.

- **Encryption**: Add VPN or application-layer encryption if required.
- **Segmentation**: Use separate VIFs, VLANs, route tables, and security groups to isolate traffic.
- **Least privilege routing**: Advertise only required prefixes.
- **Monitoring**: Track BGP session state, tunnel status, and circuit health.
- **Diversity**: Use separate devices, diverse paths, and different Direct Connect locations when possible.

Operationally, you should validate:

- Physical link status.
- BGP session establishment.
- Route propagation into the correct gateway or transit table.
- Failover timing between Direct Connect and VPN.
- MTU and fragmentation behavior, especially for IPsec overlays.

**Exam decision guide**

When a question describes a need for private, predictable connectivity to AWS, Direct Connect is usually the first candidate. Then refine the answer based on scale and routing scope.

- Choose **private VIF to VGW** when the design is simple and centered on one VPC.
- Choose **DXGW** when one Direct Connect connection must reach multiple VPCs or Regions through supported gateway associations.
- Choose **Transit Gateway** when the main problem is centralized routing across many VPCs, VPNs, and Direct Connect attachments.
- Choose **VPN** when you need encryption, rapid deployment, or backup connectivity.
- Choose **Direct Connect plus VPN** when you need both private transport and encrypted failover.

A useful mental model is:

- **Direct Connect** = transport path
- **VIF** = logical interface on that path
- **DXGW or VGW/TGW** = routing association layer
- **VPN** = encrypted overlay and failover option

**Troubleshooting checklist**

If connectivity fails, work from the edge inward.

1. Verify the physical Direct Connect circuit and port status.
2. Confirm the VIF is up and the BGP session is established.
3. Check that the correct prefixes are advertised and accepted.
4. Validate gateway associations and route propagation.
5. Confirm security groups, network ACLs, and on-premises firewall rules.
6. Test VPN tunnel status if it is part of the design.
7. Review MTU, MSS clamping, and fragmentation issues if traffic is intermittent.

Common symptoms and likely causes:

- **No routes learned**: BGP session down, wrong ASN, or route filters.
- **One-way traffic**: Asymmetric routing or firewall state issues.
- **VPN up but no application traffic**: Missing route propagation or incorrect prefix advertisement.
- **Direct Connect up, VPN not failing over**: Route preference or BGP configuration problem.

**Key takeaways**

- Direct Connect gives private, predictable network connectivity, but not encryption by default.
- A private VIF is part of the Direct Connect routing model and must be associated correctly with the intended gateway architecture.
- DXGW helps scale Direct Connect connectivity across multiple gateway associations and Regions.
- Transit Gateway is the hub-routing service; DXGW is the Direct Connect integration construct.
- VPN is the usual companion for encryption and failover, but redundancy only works when routing is configured correctly.
