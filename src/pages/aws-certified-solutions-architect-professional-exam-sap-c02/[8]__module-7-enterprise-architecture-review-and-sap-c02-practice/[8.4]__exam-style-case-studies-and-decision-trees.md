## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Professional (Exam SAP-C02)

**How SAP-C02 case studies work**

SAP-C02 case studies usually present a business goal, constraints, and a few tempting architectures. Your job is not to find a technically possible answer; it is to find the best answer under the stated requirements. That means you must weigh availability, resilience, performance, operational overhead, cost, security, and migration effort together.

A strong approach is to identify the dominant constraint first:

- **Availability target**: single-AZ, Multi-AZ, multi-region, or global active-active.
- **Recovery objective**: RTO (Recovery Time Objective) and RPO (Recovery Point Objective).
- **Traffic pattern**: steady, bursty, spiky, latency-sensitive, or batch-oriented.
- **Operational model**: managed service preferred, minimal ops, or full control required.
- **Data access pattern**: transactional, analytical, streaming, search, or archival.
- **Integration style**: synchronous request/response or asynchronous event-driven.

When you read a scenario, translate the business language into architecture signals:

- “Must survive an AZ failure” usually means Multi-AZ design.
- “Must survive a region failure” usually means multi-region failover or active-active.
- “Need to reduce ops overhead” usually pushes you toward managed services, serverless, or fully managed containers.
- “Need low-latency global access” often points to CloudFront, Global Accelerator, or Route 53 routing policies.
- “Need decoupling and buffering” often points to SQS, SNS, EventBridge, or Kinesis.

**A practical elimination mindset**

You can often eliminate distractors by checking whether a service matches the workload shape:

- If the workload is event-driven and short-lived, Lambda is often a fit.
- If the workload needs long-running processes, custom runtimes, or local state, containers or EC2 are usually better.
- If the workload needs durable queueing and backpressure, SQS is a strong candidate.
- If the workload needs fanout notifications, SNS or EventBridge is often better than direct point-to-point calls.
- If the workload is analytical and SQL-friendly over S3 data, Athena is often simpler than standing up a cluster.

> If two answers both “work,” prefer the one that best satisfies the stated constraints with the least operational burden.

**Compute decision tree: EC2 vs Auto Scaling vs Lambda vs ECS/EKS vs Fargate**

Compute questions are common because many architectures can run the same application, but the best answer depends on control, scaling, and operational model.

**1. Start with the execution model**

- Choose **Lambda** when:
  - The code is event-driven.
  - Execution is short-lived.
  - You want no server management.
  - The workload scales to zero.
  - You can tolerate Lambda runtime limits and stateless design.

- Choose **EC2** when:
  - You need full OS control.
  - You need custom agents, drivers, or kernel-level tuning.
  - You need specialized networking or legacy software.
  - You need persistent local state or long-running processes.
  - You are migrating a traditional server-based application with minimal refactoring.

- Choose **Auto Scaling groups** when:
  - You need EC2 but also need elasticity and self-healing.
  - You want to scale on metrics like CPU, request count, or queue depth.
  - You need Multi-AZ instance distribution and replacement of unhealthy nodes.

- Choose **ECS** when:
  - You want container orchestration with lower operational overhead than Kubernetes.
  - You want tight AWS integration and simpler operations.
  - You need service scheduling, task placement, and rolling deployments.

- Choose **EKS** when:
  - You need Kubernetes APIs, ecosystem compatibility, or portability.
  - You have an existing Kubernetes platform or team expertise.
  - You need advanced Kubernetes-native tooling and controllers.

- Choose **Fargate** when:
  - You want containers without managing servers.
  - You want per-task isolation and simpler operations.
  - You do not need host-level control.
  - You want to avoid cluster capacity management.

**2. Then decide whether you need containers at all**

- Use **Lambda** instead of containers when:
  - The workload is triggered by events, API calls, or schedules.
  - The code is stateless and fits runtime limits.
  - You want the fastest path to low-ops scaling.

- Use **containers** instead of Lambda when:
  - You need custom binaries or long-running workers.
  - You need predictable runtime behavior.
  - You need to process streaming jobs, background workers, or web services with more control.

- Use **EC2** instead of containers when:
  - You need host-level access.
  - You need legacy licensing or software that is not container-friendly.
  - You need specialized storage or networking behavior.

**3. Then decide the orchestration layer**

| Requirement | Best fit | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Minimal ops, containerized app | Fargate | No server management |
| AWS-native container orchestration | ECS | Simpler than Kubernetes |
| Kubernetes compatibility | EKS | Standard Kubernetes control plane |
| Full OS and app stack control | EC2 | Maximum flexibility |
| Event-driven, short tasks | Lambda | Scales automatically, no servers |

**Common compute distractors**

- **EC2 without Auto Scaling** is weak when the scenario mentions variable traffic or high availability.
- **Lambda** is weak when the scenario requires long-lived connections, heavy local processing, or large dependency footprints.
- **EKS** is often overkill if the question only asks for simple container hosting.
- **Fargate** is weak if the scenario explicitly requires host-level access or daemon-style agents.
- **ECS on EC2** can be valid when you need container orchestration plus control over the underlying instances.

**Load balancing and traffic management decision tree**

Load balancing questions often hinge on protocol, target type, source IP preservation, and whether you need Layer 7 or Layer 4 behavior.

**Application Load Balancer vs Network Load Balancer vs Gateway Load Balancer**

- Choose **ALB** (Application Load Balancer) when:
  - You need HTTP/HTTPS routing.
  - You need path-based or host-based routing.
  - You need WebSocket support or modern web app routing.
  - You want content-based routing to multiple target groups.
  - You need integration with authentication or application-layer features.

- Choose **NLB** (Network Load Balancer) when:
  - You need ultra-low latency at Layer 4.
  - You need TCP, TLS, or UDP support.
  - You need static IPs or Elastic IPs.
  - You need to preserve source IP.
  - You need very high throughput and connection performance.

- Choose **GWLB** (Gateway Load Balancer) when:
  - You need to insert third-party network appliances.
  - You need transparent traffic inspection or service chaining.
  - You are building centralized security or firewall architectures.

**Target groups and health checks**

Target groups define where traffic goes and how health is evaluated.

- Use **instance** targets when:
  - You register EC2 instances directly.
  - You want simple instance-based routing.

- Use **IP** targets when:
  - You need to route to on-premises systems, containers, or non-EC2 endpoints.
  - You need hybrid or custom network targets.

- Use **Lambda** targets when:
  - You want ALB to invoke Lambda functions directly for HTTP-style workloads.

Health checks matter because they determine failover behavior and target replacement.

- Use application-aware health checks when:
  - You want the load balancer to verify the app is actually serving requests.
  - You need to detect partial failures, not just instance reachability.

- Tune health check thresholds when:
  - You need faster failover.
  - You need to avoid flapping during transient issues.

**Sticky sessions**

Sticky sessions are useful when:

- The application stores session state in memory.
- You cannot externalize session state quickly.
- You need short-term compatibility during migration.

They are usually a workaround, not the ideal design. Prefer external session stores such as ElastiCache or DynamoDB when possible.

**Traffic management patterns**

- Use **Route 53 weighted routing** when:
  - You want gradual traffic shifting.
  - You are doing blue/green or canary releases.
  - You want to split traffic across regions or endpoints.

- Use **Route 53 latency-based routing** when:
  - You want users sent to the lowest-latency healthy endpoint.
  - You have multi-region active-active or active-passive designs.

- Use **Route 53 failover routing** when:
  - You want primary/secondary behavior.
  - You need health-check-driven regional failover.

- Use **Route 53 geolocation routing** when:
  - You need region-specific content or compliance-based routing.

- Use **Route 53 geoproximity routing** when:
  - You want to bias traffic based on geography and traffic distribution.

**Messaging and integration decision tree**

SAP-C02 often tests whether you can decouple systems correctly. The key is to match the service to the communication pattern.

**SQS vs SNS vs EventBridge vs Kinesis vs MSK vs Step Functions**

- Choose **SQS** when:
  - You need a durable queue.
  - You want buffering and backpressure.
  - You need one consumer or competing consumers.
  - You want to decouple producers from consumers.

- Choose **SNS** when:
  - You need pub/sub fanout.
  - You want to push notifications to multiple subscribers.
  - You need simple event distribution to SQS, Lambda, HTTP endpoints, or email/SMS.

- Choose **EventBridge** when:
  - You need event routing across AWS services or SaaS integrations.
  - You want content-based filtering and event buses.
  - You need a central event-driven integration layer.

- Choose **Kinesis Data Streams** when:
  - You need high-throughput streaming ingestion.
  - You need ordered processing per shard.
  - You need near-real-time analytics or stream processing.

- Choose **MSK** (Managed Streaming for Apache Kafka) when:
  - You need Kafka compatibility.
  - You already use Kafka tooling or protocols.
  - You need a managed Kafka cluster in AWS.

- Choose **Step Functions** when:
  - You need workflow orchestration.
  - You need retries, branching, parallelism, and human-readable state transitions.
  - You need to coordinate multiple Lambda functions, ECS tasks, or service calls.

**How to choose quickly**

- If the question says “buffer requests” or “absorb spikes,” think **SQS**.
- If it says “notify multiple systems,” think **SNS** or **EventBridge**.
- If it says “event routing with filtering,” think **EventBridge**.
- If it says “stream processing with ordering,” think **Kinesis**.
- If it says “Kafka ecosystem,” think **MSK**.
- If it says “multi-step business process,” think **Step Functions**.

**Analytics and data processing decision tree**

Analytics questions often ask you to choose the simplest service that satisfies query, transformation, and scale requirements.

- Choose **Athena** when:
  - You want serverless SQL over data in S3.
  - You need ad hoc queries.
  - You want minimal operational overhead.

- Choose **Glue** when:
  - You need ETL (Extract, Transform, Load) or ELT (Extract, Load, Transform).
  - You need a managed data catalog.
  - You need schema discovery and transformation jobs.

- Choose **EMR** when:
  - You need Hadoop, Spark, Hive, or other big-data frameworks.
  - You need cluster-level tuning or custom processing.
  - You need control over distributed compute for large-scale jobs.

- Choose **Redshift** when:
  - You need a data warehouse.
  - You need structured analytics with SQL and BI tools.
  - You need performance for large analytical queries over curated data.

- Choose **OpenSearch** when:
  - You need search, log analytics, or near-real-time indexing.
  - You need text search, filtering, and dashboards.
  - You need operational search rather than warehouse-style analytics.

**Decision shortcuts**

- Raw files in S3 + SQL queries = **Athena**
- Data cleansing and cataloging = **Glue**
- Large-scale distributed processing = **EMR**
- Curated warehouse and BI = **Redshift**
- Search and log exploration = **OpenSearch**

**Example data pipeline**

```text
S3 raw landing zone
  -> Glue crawler/catalog
  -> Glue ETL job
  -> S3 curated zone
  -> Athena for ad hoc SQL
  -> Redshift for warehouse reporting
```

**Backup, disaster recovery, and multi-region strategy**

DR questions are often about matching cost to recovery speed. The main patterns are backup/restore, pilot light, warm standby, and active-active.

**Backup/restore**

Use this when:

- Cost is the primary concern.
- Longer recovery time is acceptable.
- You can rebuild infrastructure from templates.
- Data is backed up to durable storage such as S3, AWS Backup, or snapshots.

This pattern usually has the lowest cost and the highest RTO.

**Pilot light**

Use this when:

- You need a minimal core running in the secondary region.
- You want faster recovery than backup/restore.
- You can scale up the rest of the stack during failover.

Typical pilot light components:

- Replicated data stores.
- Minimal application footprint.
- Infrastructure templates ready for rapid expansion.

**Warm standby**

Use this when:

- You need a scaled-down but functional secondary environment.
- You want lower RTO than pilot light.
- You can afford some always-on capacity in the secondary region.

**Active-active**

Use this when:

- You need the fastest failover and highest availability.
- You can design the application and data layer for multi-region writes or conflict handling.
- You can tolerate the complexity and cost.

**How to choose**

| Pattern | Cost | RTO | RPO | Complexity |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Backup/restore | Low | High | Variable | Low |
| Pilot light | Low-medium | Medium | Low-medium | Medium |
| Warm standby | Medium | Low-medium | Low | Medium-high |
| Active-active | High | Lowest | Lowest | High |

**Multi-region design signals**

- Use **Route 53 failover routing** for primary/secondary regional failover.
- Use **latency-based routing** for active-active user distribution.
- Use **Global Accelerator** when you need fast failover and static anycast entry points.
- Use **CloudFront** when you need edge caching and global content delivery.

**Backup and replication tools**

- Use **AWS Backup** for centralized backup policy management.
- Use **EBS snapshots** for EC2 volume backups.
- Use **RDS automated backups** and snapshots for relational databases.
- Use **S3 versioning** and replication for object durability and cross-region copies.
- Use **DynamoDB global tables** when you need multi-region active-active NoSQL replication.

> A common exam trap is choosing a multi-region front door without solving data replication. Traffic failover is only half the design.

**Application delivery and edge patterns**

Edge and delivery questions often test whether you understand where to terminate traffic and how to fail over origins.

**CloudFront vs Global Accelerator**

- Choose **CloudFront** when:
  - You need CDN (Content Delivery Network) caching.
  - You serve static or cacheable dynamic content globally.
  - You want edge locations to reduce latency and offload origins.

- Choose **Global Accelerator** when:
  - You need static anycast IPs.
  - You need fast regional failover for TCP/UDP applications.
  - You want to improve global network performance without caching.

**Route 53 routing policies in detail**

- **Simple routing**: one endpoint, no advanced failover logic.
- **Weighted routing**: split traffic by percentage.
- **Latency-based routing**: send users to the lowest-latency region.
- **Failover routing**: primary/secondary with health checks.
- **Geolocation routing**: route by user location.
- **Geoproximity routing**: bias traffic by geography and optional traffic flow controls.
- **Multi-value answer routing**: return multiple healthy records for simple client-side resilience.

**Origin failover**

Use origin failover when:

- You want CloudFront to fail over between primary and secondary origins.
- You need regional resilience for static or cacheable content.
- You want to keep the same distribution while switching origins automatically.

A common pattern is:

- CloudFront distribution
- Primary origin in Region A
- Secondary origin in Region B
- Route 53 or origin failover health checks
- S3, ALB, or custom origin endpoints

**Practical edge example**

```text
Users
  -> Route 53 latency-based routing
  -> CloudFront
  -> Primary ALB in us-east-1
  -> Secondary ALB in us-west-2
```

**Security and network decision cues**

Security and network architecture questions often hide the real requirement in the wording.

- Use **VPC endpoints** when:
  - You want private access to AWS services.
  - You want to avoid public internet exposure.
  - You need to keep traffic on the AWS network.

- Use **Transit Gateway** when:
  - You need hub-and-spoke connectivity across many VPCs.
  - You want centralized routing and simplified network management.

- Use **Direct Connect** when:
  - You need private, predictable connectivity from on-premises to AWS.
  - You need lower jitter or more consistent bandwidth than the public internet.

- Use **Site-to-Site VPN** when:
  - You need quick, lower-cost hybrid connectivity.
  - You can tolerate internet-based transport.

- Use **AWS Network Firewall** or third-party appliances with **GWLB** when:
  - You need centralized inspection or policy enforcement.

**Case study walkthroughs**

**Case 1: Bursty API with session state**

A company runs a web API that receives unpredictable bursts during promotions. The app stores session state in memory on each server, and users complain when instances scale in or fail over.

Decision tree:

1. The traffic is HTTP-based, so you need a web-facing load balancer.
2. The bursty pattern suggests Auto Scaling or serverless.
3. In-memory session state makes instance replacement risky.
4. The best design is usually:
   - ALB in front
   - Auto Scaling group for EC2 if the app must stay on servers
   - External session store such as ElastiCache or DynamoDB
   - Health checks on the ALB target group

Why not the distractors:

- **Lambda** is weak if the app is not easily refactored to stateless execution.
- **NLB** is weak because this is an HTTP application that benefits from Layer 7 routing.
- **Sticky sessions** may help temporarily, but they do not solve resilience well.

**Case 2: Global read-heavy content site**

A media company serves mostly static content to a global audience and wants lower latency and origin offload. They also need regional failover if the primary region fails.

Decision tree:

1. The content is cacheable, so edge caching matters.
2. Global users need low latency.
3. Regional failover is required.
4. The best design is usually:
   - CloudFront in front
   - S3 or ALB origins
   - Route 53 failover or latency-based routing
   - Secondary origin in another region

Why not the distractors:

- **Global Accelerator** helps with network performance, but it does not cache content.
- **ALB alone** does not provide edge caching.
- **NLB** is not the right fit for content delivery.

**Case 3: Event-driven order processing**

An e-commerce platform needs to accept orders quickly, absorb spikes, and process downstream tasks like fraud checks, inventory updates, and notifications.

Decision tree:

1. The front end should return quickly.
2. Downstream systems should be decoupled.
3. Multiple consumers need the same event.
4. The best design is usually:
   - API or ALB front end
   - SQS for buffering order jobs
   - SNS or EventBridge for fanout events
   - Step Functions for orchestration if the workflow has multiple dependent steps
   - Lambda or ECS tasks for individual processing steps

Why not the distractors:

- **Direct synchronous calls** create coupling and poor spike handling.
- **Kinesis** is better for streaming telemetry than simple order buffering.
- **MSK** is only justified if Kafka compatibility is a requirement.

**Case 4: Analytics platform over S3 data**

A team stores raw logs in S3 and wants SQL access, scheduled transformations, and a warehouse for executive reporting.

Decision tree:

1. Raw data lives in S3.
2. They need ad hoc SQL and transformation.
3. They also need curated reporting.
4. The best design is usually:
   - Glue crawler and catalog
   - Glue ETL jobs for transformation
   - Athena for ad hoc queries
   - Redshift for warehouse reporting

Why not the distractors:

- **EMR** is more complex unless they need heavy distributed processing.
- **OpenSearch** is better for search and log exploration, not warehouse reporting.
- **RDS** is not a data warehouse.

**Case 5: Multi-region recovery with tight RTO**

A financial application must survive a regional outage with minimal downtime. The company can afford some always-on secondary capacity, but not full active-active complexity.

Decision tree:

1. The RTO is tight, so backup/restore is too slow.
2. Full active-active is too complex and expensive.
3. Warm standby is a strong fit.
4. The best design is usually:
   - Primary region serving traffic
   - Secondary region running scaled-down capacity
   - Replicated data stores
   - Route 53 failover routing
   - Automated infrastructure deployment

Why not the distractors:

- **Pilot light** may be too slow if the app needs more than minimal startup time.
- **Backup/restore** likely misses the RTO.
- **Active-active** may exceed budget or operational tolerance.

**How to eliminate distractors fast**

Use these checks during the exam:

- If a choice adds complexity without solving the stated problem, reject it.
- If a choice solves the problem but violates a constraint like cost, ops overhead, or latency, reject it.
- If a choice is a lower-level primitive when a managed service is available and appropriate, prefer the managed service.
- If the scenario mentions “real-time,” “streaming,” or “ordered events,” verify whether the service is a queue, stream, or bus.
- If the scenario mentions “global,” verify whether the need is caching, routing, or failover.

**Exam heuristics to remember**

- **Stateless and event-driven** often means Lambda.
- **Containerized but serverless** often means Fargate.
- **Kubernetes requirement** often means EKS.
- **Simple AWS-native containers** often means ECS.
- **Legacy server control** often means EC2 with Auto Scaling.
- **HTTP routing and content rules** often means ALB.
- **TCP/UDP and static IPs** often means NLB.
- **Network appliance insertion** often means GWLB.
- **Durable buffering** often means SQS.
- **Fanout and notifications** often means SNS.
- **Event routing and filtering** often means EventBridge.
- **Streaming analytics** often means Kinesis.
- **Kafka compatibility** often means MSK.
- **Workflow orchestration** often means Step Functions.
- **SQL over S3** often means Athena.
- **ETL and catalog** often means Glue.
- **Warehouse analytics** often means Redshift.
- **Search and logs** often means OpenSearch.
- **Fast regional failover** often means Route 53 plus Global Accelerator or CloudFront, depending on whether caching is needed.

**Final exam strategy**

Read the scenario twice: once for the business goal and once for the constraints. Then map each requirement to a service family before comparing answer choices. The best SAP-C02 answer is usually the one that satisfies the most important constraint with the least unnecessary complexity.
