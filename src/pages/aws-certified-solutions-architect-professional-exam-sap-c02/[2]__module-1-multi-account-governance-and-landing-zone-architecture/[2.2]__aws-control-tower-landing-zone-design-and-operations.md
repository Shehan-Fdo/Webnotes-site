## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Professional (Exam SAP-C02)

**AWS Control Tower landing zone overview**

AWS Control Tower provides a governed multi-account foundation built on <abbr title="AWS Organizations">Organizations</abbr>, AWS IAM Identity Center, AWS CloudTrail, and AWS Config. You use it when you want a standardized way to create accounts, apply baseline controls, and monitor drift across a multi-account environment.

A Control Tower landing zone is not just a set of accounts. It is an opinionated governance framework that establishes:
- A **management account** for the organization and Control Tower administration.
- A **log archive account** for centralized log retention.
- An **audit account** for security visibility and read-only oversight.
- A set of **organizational units (OUs)** and baseline controls that you extend for workloads.

Control Tower is most useful when you need repeatable governance at scale, especially for enterprises that want guardrails without building the entire landing zone manually.

**Prerequisites and setup requirements**

Before you deploy a landing zone, the environment must satisfy Control Tower prerequisites. These are common exam points because setup failures often come from missing organizational or identity prerequisites.

- **AWS Organizations** must be enabled, and Control Tower expects a supported organization structure.
- The **management account** must be able to administer the organization and launch Control Tower.
- **IAM Identity Center** is required for federated access and account access management in the landing zone.
- The target Region must be supported for Control Tower landing zone operations.
- Existing organization settings, SCPs, and identity configuration must not conflict with Control Tower baseline requirements.

Control Tower also depends on baseline services:
- **AWS CloudTrail** for organization-level audit logging.
- **AWS Config** for configuration recording and compliance evaluation.
- Service-linked roles and baseline resources that Control Tower creates and manages.

> If your organization already has custom CloudTrail or Config deployments, verify that they do not conflict with Control Tower’s baseline setup. Conflicts during landing zone creation or update are a common failure mode.

**Landing zone architecture and account roles**

Control Tower uses a small set of foundational accounts and OUs to separate duties.

- **Management account**
  - Hosts the organization administration boundary.
  - Launches and updates the landing zone.
  - Should be reserved for governance, not application workloads.

- **Log archive account**
  - Centralizes immutable or long-retention logs.
  - Commonly receives CloudTrail and Config-related log data.
  - Helps isolate audit data from workload administrators.

- **Audit account**
  - Provides security and compliance teams with read-only visibility.
  - Often used for security tooling, detective workflows, and cross-account inspection.

- **Organizational units**
  - Group accounts by environment or business function.
  - Control Tower applies many controls at the OU level, so OU design is a primary governance decision.

A common exam-friendly OU pattern is:
- `Security` OU for log archive and audit-related governance accounts.
- `Sandbox` OU for experimentation and developer onboarding.
- `NonProd` OU for shared development and test workloads.
- `Prod` OU for production workloads with stricter controls.

**Designing OUs and guardrail strategy**

Control Tower guardrails are the core governance mechanism. They are not a replacement for all security controls, but they provide standardized preventive and detective controls across the organization.

Guardrails are typically managed at the **root** and **OU** levels:
- **Root-level controls** establish broad baseline governance for the organization.
- **OU-level controls** refine governance for groups of accounts.
- **Account placement** determines which inherited controls apply.

Control Tower guardrails fall into two broad categories:
- **Preventive controls**
  - Enforced primarily with <abbr title="Service Control Policy">SCP</abbr>s.
  - Block disallowed actions before they occur.
  - Best for hard boundaries such as region restrictions or disabling risky services.
- **Detective controls**
  - Evaluate compliance after the fact.
  - Commonly rely on AWS Config rules and related evaluation logic.
  - Best for visibility, drift detection, and compliance reporting.

Practical implications:
- Preventive controls are the right choice when you must stop an action.
- Detective controls are the right choice when you want to detect and report noncompliance.
- Moving an account to a different OU changes the inherited preventive posture immediately once the account is in the new OU, but detective evaluation and remediation follow their own evaluation cycles.

A simple strategy:
- Use stricter controls in `Prod`.
- Use more permissive controls in `NonProd`, but keep baseline detective controls enabled.
- Use `Sandbox` for experimentation with a limited set of guardrails that still protect the organization.

**Example OU design**

```text
Root
├── Security
│   ├── LogArchive
│   └── Audit
├── Infrastructure
├── NonProd
│   ├── Dev
│   └── Test
└── Prod
```

This pattern separates security accounts from workload accounts and makes it easier to apply different guardrail sets by environment.

**Control Tower controls and inheritance**

Control Tower controls are enabled against organizational scopes and inherited downward.

- Controls enabled at the **root** apply broadly to all child OUs and accounts unless excluded by design.
- Controls enabled at an **OU** apply to all accounts in that OU and its child OUs.
- Controls are not usually designed as ad hoc per-account exceptions; instead, you place the account into the correct OU for the desired governance posture.

This inheritance model matters for exam scenarios:
- If a workload needs stricter restrictions, move it to a more restrictive OU.
- If a workload needs a different compliance posture, create a dedicated OU rather than trying to micromanage controls per account.
- If a detective control flags drift, you investigate and remediate the resource or configuration, not the OU structure itself.

**Landing zone versioning and upgrades**

Control Tower landing zones are versioned. Updates can introduce new baseline capabilities, improved controls, or changes to the underlying governance framework.

When you plan an upgrade:
- Review the landing zone version release notes and compatibility notes.
- Check whether existing customizations conflict with the new baseline.
- Validate that enrolled accounts, OUs, and custom guardrails remain supported.
- Expect some changes to baseline resources, not a full redesign of your organization.

Important operational points:
- Upgrades are not a generic “reset” of the landing zone.
- Existing accounts usually remain enrolled, but you should verify control inheritance and baseline health after the update.
- New versions may add features or change operational behavior, so test in a nonproduction environment when possible.

A practical approach is to:
1. Review the current landing zone version.
2. Compare it with the target version and note feature changes.
3. Validate custom SCPs, Config rules, and identity integrations.
4. Upgrade during a maintenance window.
5. Verify baseline health, account enrollment status, and control compliance afterward.

**Account Factory and account provisioning**

Control Tower Account Factory provides a standardized way to create new accounts with baseline governance already applied.

Typical use cases:
- Onboarding a new application team.
- Creating a sandbox account for experimentation.
- Provisioning a new environment account with standard logging and guardrails.

Account Factory workflows usually include:
- Selecting an account blueprint or account request template.
- Providing account metadata such as email, account name, and OU placement.
- Applying baseline controls and identity access.
- Waiting for asynchronous provisioning to complete.

A practical provisioning flow:
1. Choose the target OU.
2. Submit the account request through Account Factory or the supported provisioning workflow.
3. Wait for the account to be created and enrolled.
4. Verify that baseline logging, Config recording, and guardrails are active.
5. Assign access through IAM Identity Center.

Example conceptual workflow:
```bash
# Illustrative AWS Organizations lookup after account creation
aws organizations list-accounts
aws organizations list-parents --child-id 123456789012
```

Account Factory limitations and edge cases:
- Existing SCP conflicts can block provisioning or enrollment.
- Unsupported Regions can prevent the account from being fully governed in the desired way.
- Pre-existing resources in an account may not automatically conform to baseline controls.
- Some workloads require manual validation after enrollment because Control Tower governs the account, but it does not rewrite every existing resource.

> Enrollment does not magically fix all pre-existing drift. You still need to assess existing IAM, networking, logging, and resource configurations after onboarding an account.

**Account enrollment and existing accounts**

You can enroll existing accounts into Control Tower, but enrollment is more sensitive than creating a fresh account.

Common considerations:
- The account must be compatible with the landing zone’s governance model.
- Existing SCPs may conflict with Control Tower’s required policies.
- Existing CloudTrail or Config setups may need adjustment.
- The account’s current OU placement must align with the intended control set.

What happens to pre-existing resources:
- Control Tower does not delete or replace arbitrary application resources during enrollment.
- It establishes governance baselines and then evaluates compliance.
- Resources that violate detective controls are reported.
- Resources blocked by preventive controls may fail future actions, even if they existed before enrollment.

Typical enrollment failure causes:
- The account already has a conflicting organization policy.
- Required baseline services are disabled or misconfigured.
- The account is in a Region or configuration state not supported by the landing zone.
- Identity or delegated administration prerequisites are incomplete.

**CloudTrail and AWS Config baseline behavior**

Control Tower relies on organization-wide logging and configuration recording to support governance and drift detection.

CloudTrail baseline:
- Control Tower expects centralized audit logging.
- Organization trails help capture activity across member accounts.
- Logs are typically routed to the log archive account for retention and separation of duties.

AWS Config baseline:
- Config records resource configuration changes.
- Detective controls often depend on Config evaluations.
- Baseline recording helps identify drift and policy violations.

Operationally, this means:
- If CloudTrail is missing or broken, audit visibility suffers.
- If Config is not recording correctly, detective controls may not evaluate as expected.
- Baseline health checks are important after landing zone creation, upgrade, or account enrollment.

A useful mental model:
- **CloudTrail** answers “who did what?”
- **AWS Config** answers “what changed, and is it compliant?”

**Region support and service limitations**

Control Tower features are not always available everywhere. Region support and landing zone version matter.

Be careful with:
- Landing zone availability in the target Region.
- Feature availability that may depend on the landing zone version.
- Service integrations that are Region-specific or have rollout differences.
- Account enrollment behavior in Regions that are not governed by the landing zone’s supported scope.

Exam-relevant takeaway:
- Do not assume every Control Tower feature is globally available.
- Always validate the supported Region and landing zone version before designing a multi-Region governance model.

**Operational lifecycle: updates, drift, and remediation**

Control Tower is operationally about maintaining a governed baseline over time.

Common lifecycle activities:
- Creating the landing zone.
- Enrolling accounts.
- Adding or moving accounts between OUs.
- Enabling or disabling controls.
- Upgrading the landing zone version.
- Detecting and remediating drift.

Drift can occur when:
- Someone changes a baseline resource outside the expected workflow.
- A required service is disabled.
- An SCP or identity configuration conflicts with the landing zone.
- An account is moved without considering inherited controls.

Remediation approach:
- Identify whether the issue is preventive, detective, or baseline-related.
- Check the account’s OU placement and inherited controls.
- Validate CloudTrail, Config, and identity prerequisites.
- Repair the specific drifted resource or configuration.

Control Tower is not a generic “reset” button for arbitrary account problems. Use the platform’s repair and lifecycle workflows for supported baseline issues, and use normal AWS service operations for workload-level remediation.

**Decommissioning and unenrolling accounts**

When you remove an account from Control Tower governance, treat it as a controlled change, not a simple delete.

Operational considerations:
- Remove or move the account from the governed OU as appropriate.
- Understand which controls and baseline resources will no longer apply.
- Preserve logs and compliance evidence if the account is being retired.
- Verify that workload owners understand the governance implications.

If you unenroll an account:
- Control Tower governance for that account is reduced or removed according to the supported workflow.
- Existing resources remain unless you explicitly delete or migrate them.
- You may need to clean up baseline artifacts, access assignments, and organization relationships.

A safe decommissioning sequence:
1. Confirm the account’s business owner and retirement plan.
2. Archive required logs and evidence.
3. Remove workloads or migrate them elsewhere.
4. Unenroll or move the account according to the governance model.
5. Validate that no residual access or compliance dependencies remain.

**Practical configuration examples**

A production-oriented OU strategy often uses stricter preventive controls and stronger detective coverage.

Example policy intent:
- Restrict Regions to approved Regions only.
- Prevent disabling CloudTrail or Config.
- Detect public S3 buckets and overly permissive security groups.
- Require centralized logging and security visibility.

Example SCP concept:
```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "DenyDisablingCloudTrail",
      "Effect": "Deny",
      "Action": [
        "cloudtrail:StopLogging",
        "cloudtrail:DeleteTrail"
      ],
      "Resource": "*"
    }
  ]
}
```

Example account access workflow with IAM Identity Center:
```bash
# Illustrative identity and account administration checks
aws sso-admin list-instances
aws organizations list-accounts
```

Example account factory customization idea:
- Standardize tags such as `Environment`, `Owner`, and `CostCenter`.
- Pre-attach baseline roles through Identity Center assignments.
- Use a provisioning template or pipeline to ensure consistent OU placement.

**Common exam scenarios and how to reason about them**

- A team needs a new account with standard logging and guardrails.
  - Use Account Factory or the supported account provisioning workflow.
  - Place the account in the correct OU from the start.

- A production account needs stricter restrictions than development.
  - Create separate OUs and apply different control sets.
  - Do not rely on one-off account exceptions unless the workflow explicitly supports them.

- An existing account already has custom SCPs.
  - Check for conflicts before enrollment.
  - Expect enrollment or control application to fail if the policies block required Control Tower operations.

- A detective control reports noncompliance after a resource change.
  - Investigate the resource drift.
  - Remediate the resource or configuration.
  - Verify that AWS Config is recording correctly.

- A landing zone upgrade introduces new baseline behavior.
  - Review version notes.
  - Test compatibility with existing accounts and controls.
  - Validate post-upgrade health and compliance.

**Key exam takeaways**

- Control Tower is a governed multi-account framework built on Organizations, IAM Identity Center, CloudTrail, and Config.
- The landing zone establishes baseline accounts, OUs, and controls.
- Guardrails are primarily inherited through root and OU placement.
- Preventive controls block actions; detective controls report noncompliance.
- Account Factory standardizes new account creation, but existing accounts may need careful enrollment planning.
- CloudTrail and AWS Config are foundational to auditability and drift detection.
- Landing zone versioning, Region support, and SCP conflicts are frequent operational edge cases.
- Decommissioning and unenrollment require deliberate cleanup and governance review.
