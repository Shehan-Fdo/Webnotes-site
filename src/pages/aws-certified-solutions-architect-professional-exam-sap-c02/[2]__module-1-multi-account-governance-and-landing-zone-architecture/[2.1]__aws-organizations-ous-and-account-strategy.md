## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Professional (Exam SAP-C02)

**AWS Organizations overview**

AWS Organizations is the control plane for multi-account governance. You use it to centrally manage accounts, apply guardrails, and standardize billing and security across a portfolio of workloads.

A strong account strategy starts with the idea that accounts are the primary isolation boundary. You separate workloads by environment, business unit, compliance scope, or blast radius rather than trying to solve everything inside one account.

- **Central governance** lets you apply organization-wide controls without manually configuring every account.
- **Billing consolidation** gives you one payer view while preserving account-level chargeback or showback.
- **Delegated administration** lets selected member accounts manage specific services without giving them full organization control.
- **Service control policies** act as preventive guardrails for member accounts and OUs.

**Core organization structure**

An organization contains a management account, member accounts, and a hierarchy of organizational units (OUs). OUs are containers used to group accounts so you can apply policies at scale.

The management account is the account that creates and administers the organization. It is not the place to run workloads if you can avoid it. In exam scenarios, treat it as a privileged administrative account with special handling.

- **Management account** owns the organization and pays the consolidated bill.
- **Member accounts** are governed accounts that can host workloads and inherit policies.
- **OUs** provide hierarchical policy inheritance and simplify account placement.
- **Root of the organization** is the top-level container above all OUs and accounts.

> The management account does not receive SCP enforcement in the same way member accounts do. Use it for organization administration, not for normal workload governance.

**Why use multiple accounts**

Multiple accounts reduce blast radius and improve governance. They also help you align security, compliance, and operational ownership with AWS-native boundaries.

Use separate accounts when you need:
- **Environment isolation** such as dev, test, staging, and prod.
- **Security separation** for logging, security tooling, and audit functions.
- **Compliance boundaries** for regulated workloads or data domains.
- **Cost allocation** by team, product, or business unit.
- **Operational independence** so teams can deploy without interfering with each other.

A common anti-pattern is overloading one account with too many unrelated workloads. That makes IAM, networking, billing, and incident response harder to reason about.

**OU design strategy**

Design OUs around policy needs, not just org charts. The best OU structure reflects how you want to apply guardrails and delegate administration.

Typical patterns include:
- **Environment-based OUs** such as `Sandbox`, `NonProd`, and `Prod`.
- **Security-based OUs** such as `Security`, `LogArchive`, and `SharedServices`.
- **Compliance-based OUs** for workloads with distinct regulatory requirements.
- **Business-unit OUs** when teams need different guardrails or delegated control.

A practical design often combines these ideas. For example, you might keep foundational accounts in dedicated OUs and place application accounts under environment OUs.

Example structure:

```text
Root
├── Security
│   ├── LogArchive
│   └── Audit
├── Infrastructure
│   ├── Network
│   └── SharedServices
├── Sandbox
├── NonProd
└── Prod
```

- **Keep foundational accounts separate** from application accounts.
- **Minimize OU depth** unless you need inheritance layers for policy control.
- **Move accounts deliberately** because OU changes can alter effective guardrails.
- **Use consistent naming** so operators can infer purpose quickly.

**Policy types in AWS Organizations**

Organizations supports several policy types. For SAP-C02, the most important are service control policies and the governance policies used for centralized control.

Key policy types include:
- **Service control policies (SCPs)** to define the maximum permissions available to member accounts.
- **Tag policies** to standardize tag keys and values.
- **Backup policies** to centrally define backup plans across accounts.
- **AI services opt-out policies** to restrict use of certain AI services where supported.

SCPs are the most important exam topic. They do not grant permissions. Instead, they set the upper bound of what IAM can allow in member accounts.

**How SCPs work**

SCPs are organization-level permission filters. They are evaluated before IAM authorization in member accounts and determine whether an action is even eligible to be allowed.

Important SCP facts:
- **Default behavior** is deny unless allowed by the effective policy set.
- **Allow list mode** can be used to explicitly permit only selected actions.
- **Deny statements** are powerful because explicit deny always wins.
- **Inheritance** flows from the root to OUs to accounts.
- **Intersection** with IAM means the final permission must be allowed by both SCPs and IAM, but SCPs are not just another IAM policy type.

A useful mental model is:
1. The request is checked against the organization’s SCP guardrails.
2. If the action is not permitted by SCPs, it is denied.
3. If SCPs allow it, IAM and other authorization layers determine whether the principal can actually perform it.

> SCPs do not grant access by themselves. If IAM does not allow an action, SCP allowance alone is not enough.

Example SCP that denies disabling CloudTrail in member accounts:

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "DenyCloudTrailTampering",
      "Effect": "Deny",
      "Action": [
        "cloudtrail:StopLogging",
        "cloudtrail:DeleteTrail",
        "cloudtrail:UpdateTrail"
      ],
      "Resource": "*"
    }
  ]
}
```

This pattern is useful for protecting audit controls. You usually attach it to an OU that contains workload accounts, not to the management account.

**SCP design patterns**

Use SCPs to prevent dangerous actions, not to implement fine-grained application authorization. They are best for guardrails that should apply broadly.

Common SCP patterns:
- **Deny root-level risky actions** in member accounts where appropriate.
- **Deny region usage** outside approved AWS Regions.
- **Deny disabling security services** such as CloudTrail, Config, GuardDuty, or Security Hub where those services are part of your baseline.
- **Deny leaving the organization** from member accounts.
- **Deny creation of unsupported resource types** in restricted environments.

Example region restriction SCP:

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "DenyOutsideApprovedRegions",
      "Effect": "Deny",
      "NotAction": [
        "iam:*",
        "organizations:*",
        "route53:*",
        "cloudfront:*",
        "support:*"
      ],
      "Resource": "*",
      "Condition": {
        "StringNotEquals": {
          "aws:RequestedRegion": [
            "us-east-1",
            "us-west-2"
          ]
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

This kind of policy is common in exam scenarios. Be careful with global services and service exceptions, because a naive region deny can break essential operations.

**Root user considerations**

The root user exists in every AWS account and has full power inside that account unless constrained by organization-level controls in member accounts. In practice, you should minimize root usage and protect it with MFA and strong operational controls.

For SCPs, do not rely on misleading patterns that try to identify root with a generic ARN wildcard. Root is account-specific, and SCP conditions should be designed carefully. For exam purposes, the safer takeaway is:
- **Do not depend on root for normal operations.**
- **Use SCPs to reduce what member accounts can do, including limiting risky root actions where applicable.**
- **Protect the management account especially carefully because it is the organization control plane.**

**Management account strategy**

The management account should be treated as a highly privileged administrative account. Keep it minimal and avoid running workloads there.

Best practices:
- **Use it only for organization administration and billing.**
- **Do not deploy application workloads there.**
- **Restrict access tightly with MFA and least privilege.**
- **Use separate admin roles for day-to-day organization management.**

The management account cannot simply leave the organization like a member account. If you need a different account to become the management account, the practical path is to create a new organization and move or recreate accounts as needed. This is an operationally significant design decision, not a routine account action.

**Account strategy and landing zones**

A landing zone is the initial multi-account foundation for governance, security, and networking. In AWS architecture discussions, it usually includes a management account, security accounts, logging accounts, shared services, and workload accounts.

A common landing zone pattern includes:
- **Management account** for organization administration.
- **Log archive account** for immutable or centralized logs.
- **Security account** for security tooling and delegated admin.
- **Shared services account** for common internal services.
- **Workload accounts** for application teams and environments.

This pattern improves separation of duties and makes it easier to apply different controls to different account classes.

**Practical account placement**

Place accounts based on operational purpose and policy needs.

Examples:
- **Prod application accounts** in a `Prod` OU with stricter SCPs.
- **Developer sandbox accounts** in a `Sandbox` OU with looser guardrails but still with safety limits.
- **Security tooling accounts** in a `Security` OU with elevated delegated administration.
- **Network hub accounts** in an `Infrastructure` OU for Transit Gateway, centralized egress, or shared DNS.

A good rule is to keep accounts homogeneous within an OU. If accounts in the same OU need very different guardrails, split the OU.

**Delegated administration**

Delegated administration lets you register a member account to administer a specific AWS service for the organization. This reduces the need to use the management account for service operations.

Use delegated admin when:
- **A security team** manages GuardDuty, Security Hub, or similar services.
- **A networking team** manages shared network services.
- **A platform team** manages centralized governance services.

This pattern supports separation of duties and reduces risk in the management account.

**CLI examples for common operations**

Create an organization:

```bash
aws organizations create-organization --feature-set ALL
```

Create an OU:

```bash
aws organizations create-organizational-unit \
  --parent-id r-xxxx \
  --name Prod
```

List OUs under a parent:

```bash
aws organizations list-organizational-units-for-parent \
  --parent-id r-xxxx
```

Move an account to an OU:

```bash
aws organizations move-account \
  --account-id 123456789012 \
  --source-parent-id ou-abcd-11111111 \
  --destination-parent-id ou-abcd-22222222
```

Attach an SCP to an OU:

```bash
aws organizations attach-policy \
  --policy-id p-abc123 \
  --target-id ou-abcd-22222222
```

Create an account asynchronously:

```bash
aws organizations create-account \
  --email new-account@example.com \
  --account-name Prod-App-01
```

The `create-account` call returns a status ID. You must poll for completion before assuming the account exists.

```bash
aws organizations describe-create-account-status \
  --create-account-request-id 11111111-2222-3333-4444-555555555555
```

A practical automation flow is:
1. Call `create-account`.
2. Capture the `CreateAccountStatusId`.
3. Poll `describe-create-account-status`.
4. Continue only after the status becomes `SUCCEEDED`.

**Propagation and eventual consistency**

Organizations changes are not always instantaneous from an operational perspective. SCP attachment, OU moves, and account creation can take time to propagate.

Important implications:
- **Policy changes may take time** before they are fully enforced everywhere.
- **OU moves can temporarily create confusion** if automation assumes immediate consistency.
- **Operational runbooks should include retries and verification.**

Do not assume that a successful API call means every dependent service has already updated its view.

**Common exam scenarios**

SAP-C02 often tests your ability to choose the right governance model for a business requirement.

Typical scenarios:
- **Prevent developers from disabling logging** while still allowing them to manage application resources.
- **Restrict production accounts to approved Regions** without affecting the management account.
- **Create separate accounts for each product team** to reduce blast radius and simplify chargeback.
- **Centralize security tooling** while allowing workload teams to operate independently.
- **Use OU-based guardrails** instead of trying to manage every account individually.

When the question mentions broad guardrails, think SCPs and OU design. When it mentions workload isolation, think separate accounts. When it mentions centralized administration, think delegated admin and landing zone structure.

**Troubleshooting and design pitfalls**

A few mistakes show up repeatedly in real deployments and exam questions.

- **Using one account for everything** creates excessive coupling and weakens blast-radius control.
- **Putting workloads in the management account** increases risk and complicates governance.
- **Using SCPs as a substitute for IAM** leads to confusing authorization failures.
- **Forgetting service exceptions** can break global services when applying region restrictions.
- **Over-nesting OUs** can make policy inheritance hard to reason about.
- **Assuming immediate consistency** can cause automation failures after account or policy changes.

If a permission issue appears in a member account, check both IAM and SCPs. If an action works in one account but not another, compare OU placement and inherited policies.

**Quick comparison**

| Concept | Purpose | Key point |
|---|---|---|
| Organization | Top-level governance boundary | Centralizes billing and policy control |
| Management account | Org administration account | Avoid workload use; special handling |
| Member account | Governed workload or service account | Receives inherited guardrails |
| OU | Policy grouping container | Simplifies inheritance and delegation |
| SCP | Preventive guardrail | Limits maximum permissions in member accounts |

**Practical design checklist**

Use this checklist when designing an AWS Organizations strategy:
1. Define the account boundaries by workload, environment, and compliance needs.
2. Create foundational accounts for logging, security, and shared services.
3. Group accounts into OUs based on common guardrail requirements.
4. Apply SCPs to enforce baseline restrictions.
5. Use delegated administration for service ownership.
6. Keep the management account minimal and tightly controlled.
7. Validate propagation and automation after account moves or policy changes.

**Key takeaways**

AWS Organizations is the foundation for multi-account governance. OUs let you scale policy application, SCPs let you enforce guardrails, and separate accounts reduce blast radius and improve operational clarity.

For SAP-C02, focus on choosing the right boundary:
- **Account boundary** for isolation.
- **OU boundary** for policy inheritance.
- **SCP boundary** for preventive governance.
- **Delegated admin** for scalable operations.
