## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Professional (Exam SAP-C02)

**Enterprise OU design goals**

AWS Organizations gives you a hierarchy for grouping accounts and applying governance consistently. In a sample enterprise, you usually design around business function, environment, and risk boundary rather than around individual applications alone. The goal is to make policy inheritance predictable while keeping account ownership clear.

A practical design balances three forces:

- **Isolation** for workloads with different compliance or blast-radius needs.
- **Centralized governance** for security, logging, and billing controls.
- **Operational flexibility** so teams can move fast without bypassing guardrails.

For SAP-C02, you should be able to explain why you would place an account in a separate account versus a separate OU, and when an SCP is the right control versus a tag policy or a backup policy.

**Core AWS Organizations hierarchy**

AWS Organizations uses a tree structure:

- The **management account** owns the organization and pays the consolidated bill.
- The **root** is the top-level container under the organization.
- **Organizational Units (OUs)** group accounts and other OUs.
- **Accounts** are the leaf nodes where workloads run.

Important design constraints:

- An account can belong to only one parent at a time.
- Policies attached to a parent OU inherit to child OUs and accounts.
- The management account is not governed by SCPs in the same way as member accounts; SCPs apply to member accounts only.
- Root-level policies affect all member accounts unless explicitly exempted by inheritance design.

A common enterprise pattern is:

- Root
  - Security OU
  - Infrastructure OU
  - Sandbox OU
  - Workloads OU
    - Prod OU
    - NonProd OU
    - SharedServices OU

This structure keeps broad guardrails at the top and tighter controls closer to the workloads.

**When to use separate accounts versus separate OUs**

Use separate accounts when you need hard isolation or independent administration. Use OUs when you need shared governance across a set of accounts.

Choose a separate account for:

- Production versus nonproduction isolation.
- Different compliance boundaries.
- Distinct billing or chargeback needs.
- Workloads owned by different teams with separate blast-radius requirements.
- Shared services that must be isolated from application accounts.

Choose a separate OU for:

- Applying the same SCPs to a group of accounts.
- Organizing accounts by lifecycle stage or business unit.
- Creating a governance boundary for delegated administration.
- Staging different policy sets for sandbox, dev, test, and prod.

A useful exam heuristic:

- If the requirement is about **access, blast radius, or compliance**, prefer a separate account.
- If the requirement is about **policy inheritance or grouping**, prefer an OU.

**Sample enterprise OU model**

A realistic enterprise often includes a landing zone with foundational accounts and workload accounts.

- **Root**
  - **Security OU**
    - Log archive account
    - Security tooling account
  - **Infrastructure OU**
    - Network account
    - Shared services account
  - **Workloads OU**
    - **Prod OU**
      - Finance-prod account
      - Orders-prod account
    - **NonProd OU**
      - Finance-dev account
      - Orders-test account
    - **Sandbox OU**
      - Developer sandbox accounts
  - **Suspended OU**
    - Quarantined accounts
    - Accounts pending closure

The name `Suspended` is just a user-defined OU name. AWS does not provide a special suspended OU type.

This model supports a common enterprise lifecycle:

- New accounts are provisioned into a controlled OU.
- Guardrails are attached at the OU level.
- Accounts move between OUs as they mature or change ownership.
- Quarantined accounts are isolated by moving them to a restricted OU.

**AWS Control Tower relationship**

AWS Control Tower is often used to create and govern a landing zone on top of AWS Organizations. It standardizes account vending, guardrails, and baseline structure.

Key Control Tower concepts:

- The **landing zone** is the governed multi-account environment.
- **Account Factory** provisions new accounts into approved OUs.
- **Guardrails** are preventive or detective controls applied to OUs.
- **Lifecycle management** moves accounts through onboarding, steady state, and retirement.

In practice:

- You create foundational OUs for security, logging, and shared services.
- Account Factory places new accounts into the correct OU based on workload type.
- Guardrails enforce baseline security and compliance.
- Teams request new accounts instead of creating them manually.

This matters for SAP-C02 because enterprise architecture questions often combine account vending, OU placement, and governance controls in one scenario.

**Policy types in AWS Organizations**

Do not treat all Organizations policies as SCPs. They serve different purposes.

| Policy type | Purpose | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| **Service control policy (SCP)** | Sets the maximum permissions for member accounts | Prevents risky actions, enforces region restrictions |
| **Tag policy** | Standardizes tag keys and values | Cost allocation and governance consistency |
| **Backup policy** | Centralizes backup configuration | Enforces backup plans across accounts |
| **AI services opt-out policy** | Controls use of certain AI services | Governance and data handling restrictions |

SCPs are the main tool for permission boundaries at the organization level, but they do not replace IAM. IAM still grants permissions inside the boundary set by SCPs.

**SCP design principles**

SCPs define what an account can ever do, not what a user is explicitly allowed to do. The effective permission is the intersection of IAM permissions and SCP allowances.

Use SCPs to:

- Deny dangerous actions globally.
- Restrict regions.
- Prevent disabling security services.
- Limit changes to logging, networking, or identity controls.
- Enforce guardrails for sandbox or developer accounts.

Use SCPs carefully:

- Start with deny-only guardrails where possible.
- Test in a sandbox OU before broad rollout.
- Avoid overly broad `Deny` statements that block service-linked operations.
- Remember that some AWS services use global or regionless APIs and may need exceptions.

A common pattern is to attach a baseline SCP at the root and stricter SCPs to specific OUs.

**Example: deny disabling CloudTrail and Config**

This is a common guardrail for security and auditability.

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "DenyDisablingAuditServices",
      "Effect": "Deny",
      "Action": [
        "cloudtrail:StopLogging",
        "cloudtrail:DeleteTrail",
        "cloudtrail:UpdateTrail",
        "config:DeleteConfigurationRecorder",
        "config:StopConfigurationRecorder"
      ],
      "Resource": "*"
    }
  ]
}
```

This kind of policy is useful at the root or security-sensitive OU because it protects the organization’s evidence trail.

**Example: region restriction SCP**

A region restriction SCP is often used to keep workloads in approved regions. The safest pattern is to deny actions outside approved regions while allowing necessary global services and exceptions.

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "DenyOutsideApprovedRegions",
      "Effect": "Deny",
      "NotAction": [
        "iam:*",
        "organizations:*",
        "route53:*",
        "cloudfront:*",
        "support:*",
        "sts:*"
      ],
      "Resource": "*",
      "Condition": {
        "StringNotEquals": {
          "aws:RequestedRegion": [
            "us-east-1",
            "us-west-2"
          ]
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

Notes on this pattern:

- `aws:RequestedRegion` is evaluated for many regional services.
- Global services and some regionless operations need explicit exceptions.
- The `NotAction` list often needs tuning for real environments.
- You should validate the policy against the services your workloads actually use.

> A region guardrail is rarely “one size fits all.” Global services, service-linked roles, and control-plane operations often require exceptions.

**Example: deny public S3 bucket ACLs**

If the goal is to prevent public S3 access, use the correct control points. A common SCP approach is to deny public ACLs on bucket creation or ACL updates, and pair that with S3 Block Public Access.

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "DenyPublicS3ACLs",
      "Effect": "Deny",
      "Action": [
        "s3:PutBucketAcl",
        "s3:PutObjectAcl"
      ],
      "Resource": "*",
      "Condition": {
        "StringEquals": {
          "s3:x-amz-acl": [
            "public-read",
            "public-read-write",
            "website"
          ]
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

Practical guidance:

- Use S3 Block Public Access as the primary control.
- Use SCPs to prevent bypass through ACL changes where appropriate.
- Do not rely on `PutBucketPolicy` with `s3:x-amz-acl`; that condition key does not apply there.
- The value `website` is not a valid canned ACL.

A better enterprise posture is:

- Block public access at the account and bucket level.
- Restrict ACL changes with SCPs if needed.
- Use bucket policies for intended private access patterns only.

**Example: deny leaving the sandbox OU**

A sandbox OU often allows experimentation but still needs guardrails.

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "DenyHighRiskServicesInSandbox",
      "Effect": "Deny",
      "Action": [
        "ec2:TerminateInstances",
        "iam:CreateUser",
        "iam:CreateAccessKey"
      ],
      "Resource": "*"
    }
  ]
}
```

This example is intentionally simple. In a real environment, you would usually tailor sandbox restrictions to allow experimentation while preventing identity sprawl and uncontrolled cost.

**Account placement workflow**

A practical enterprise workflow ties account vending to OU placement and lifecycle controls.

1. Define the target workload category.
2. Provision the account through Account Factory or an equivalent controlled process.
3. Place the account into the correct OU.
4. Attach baseline SCPs and any OU-specific policies.
5. Enable logging, security tooling, and backup controls.
6. Review the account periodically and move it if its purpose changes.

Example lifecycle:

- A new developer account is created in `NonProd OU`.
- The account inherits baseline security and region restrictions.
- The team deploys test workloads.
- When the workload becomes production-ready, the account is moved to `Prod OU`.
- Additional production guardrails apply automatically after the move.

This is one of the strongest reasons to use OUs: moving an account changes its inherited governance without recreating the account.

**Moving accounts between OUs**

You can move an account with the AWS CLI. Use valid parent IDs from your organization.

```bash
aws organizations move-account \
  --account-id 123456789012 \
  --source-parent-id ou-abcd-11111111 \
  --destination-parent-id ou-abcd-22222222
```

Important details:

- `--source-parent-id` must be the current parent OU ID or root ID.
- `--destination-parent-id` must be a valid OU or root ID.
- The account must be a member account, not the management account.
- Policy inheritance changes immediately after the move.

You can inspect the hierarchy first:

```bash
aws organizations list-roots
aws organizations list-organizational-units-for-parent --parent-id r-abcd
aws organizations list-accounts-for-parent --parent-id ou-abcd-11111111
```

**Design constraints and limits**

AWS Organizations has practical limits and design considerations that affect OU planning.

- The hierarchy is not infinitely deep; keep it simple.
- Too many OUs can make governance hard to understand.
- Too few OUs can force unrelated workloads into the same policy boundary.
- SCPs have size and attachment limits, so policy sprawl matters.
- Some services and operations are global, which complicates region-based guardrails.
- Delegated administration and service access may require explicit enablement.

For exam purposes, the best design is usually the simplest one that satisfies isolation, governance, and operational needs.

**How to think about borderline scenarios**

When a question asks whether to create a new OU, a new account, or a new policy, use this decision logic:

- Create a **new account** when you need strong isolation, separate credentials, or separate billing ownership.
- Create a **new OU** when you need a different set of inherited controls for a group of accounts.
- Use an **SCP** when you need to prevent actions regardless of IAM permissions.
- Use a **tag policy** when you need consistent metadata, not access control.
- Use a **backup policy** when the requirement is backup standardization.
- Use an **AI services opt-out policy** when the requirement is to restrict certain AI service usage.

If the requirement mentions “all accounts in this business unit must not use a region,” that is an OU plus SCP problem. If it says “this workload must be isolated from all other workloads,” that is usually a separate account.

**Worked enterprise example**

Consider a company with these workloads:

- Corporate security and logging
- Shared networking
- Finance production
- Finance development
- E-commerce production
- E-commerce testing
- Developer sandbox

A clean OU and account layout could be:

- **Root**
  - **Security OU**
    - Log archive account
    - Security audit account
  - **Infrastructure OU**
    - Network account
    - Shared services account
  - **Finance OU**
    - Finance-Prod account
    - Finance-Dev account
  - **Commerce OU**
    - Commerce-Prod account
    - Commerce-Test account
  - **Sandbox OU**
    - Sandbox accounts

Policy attachment strategy:

- Root:
  - Baseline deny for disabling audit services
  - Broad region restriction with exceptions
- Security OU:
  - Stronger logging and access restrictions
- Infrastructure OU:
  - Network and shared service exceptions
- Finance OU:
  - Production-grade restrictions and tighter region controls
- Commerce OU:
  - Similar production guardrails, but separate from finance for ownership and compliance
- Sandbox OU:
  - Cost and identity restrictions, looser service access for experimentation

Account assignment example:

- `log-archive` stays in `Security OU`.
- `network-shared` stays in `Infrastructure OU`.
- `finance-dev` starts in `Finance OU` under a nonproduction branch.
- `commerce-prod` is placed in `Commerce OU` under production controls.
- `sandbox-001` is placed in `Sandbox OU` with restrictive but flexible experimentation policies.

This layout makes it easy to answer exam questions about inherited controls and blast radius.

**Common mistakes to avoid**

- Treating SCPs as if they grant permissions.
- Assuming the management account is constrained by SCPs like member accounts.
- Using an OU when the requirement really needs a separate account.
- Overcomplicating the hierarchy with too many nested OUs.
- Forgetting that global services may need region guardrail exceptions.
- Confusing tag policies with access control.
- Assuming a user-defined OU name like `Suspended` has special AWS behavior.
- Writing S3 public-access controls against the wrong API or condition key.

**Exam-focused takeaways**

For SAP-C02, remember these patterns:

- Use **AWS Organizations** for multi-account governance.
- Use **OUs** to inherit policies across related accounts.
- Use **SCPs** to set permission ceilings, not to grant access.
- Use **Control Tower** when the scenario calls for a governed landing zone and account vending.
- Use **separate accounts** for strong isolation and compliance boundaries.
- Use **policy layering** to combine root guardrails with OU-specific controls.

If a scenario describes enterprise onboarding, account factory, guardrails, and standardized account placement, the answer usually involves a landing zone design with OUs and SCPs rather than ad hoc account creation.
