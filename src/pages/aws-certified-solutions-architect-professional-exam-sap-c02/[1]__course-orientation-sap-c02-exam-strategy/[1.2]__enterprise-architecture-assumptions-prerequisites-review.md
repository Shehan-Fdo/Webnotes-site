## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Professional (Exam SAP-C02)

**Enterprise architecture review mindset**

For SAP-C02, you are not just choosing services; you are validating whether the target architecture can actually be built, governed, operated, and recovered at enterprise scale. Prerequisites are the conditions that must exist before a design is viable. Assumptions are the conditions you accept as true for the design exercise. Constraints are hard limits that shape the solution. Dependencies are external capabilities or services the design relies on.

A strong review starts by separating those four ideas:

- **Prerequisite**: must be in place before implementation can succeed.
- **Assumption**: accepted as true for planning, but not yet proven.
- **Constraint**: a restriction you must design around.
- **Dependency**: a required upstream or downstream capability.

Examples:

- A landing zone with centralized identity and logging is a **prerequisite** for many enterprise patterns.
- “All workloads can use IPv6” is often an **assumption** unless validated.
- “Data must remain in one Region” is a **constraint**.
- A third-party IdP or on-premises DNS forwarder is a **dependency**.

> If you confuse assumptions with prerequisites, you can select a design that looks correct on paper but fails during implementation, migration, or audit.

**How SAP-C02 frames enterprise readiness**

The exam often expects you to validate whether the organization has the right foundation for the target architecture. That includes governance, account structure, identity, network, security, observability, resilience, and operational controls. You should think in terms of enterprise guardrails rather than isolated workloads.

Key review questions:

- Is there a multi-account strategy with clear separation of duties?
- Are centralized security and logging services already deployed?
- Can the network support hybrid connectivity, DNS resolution, and segmentation?
- Are quotas, limits, and capacity sufficient for the target scale?
- Does the disaster recovery design have backup, replication, and failover testing in place?
- Does the solution align with the AWS Well-Architected Framework?

**AWS Well-Architected Framework considerations**

The AWS Well-Architected Framework is a core lens for SAP-C02. When reviewing assumptions and prerequisites, map them to the six pillars:

- **Operational Excellence**: automation, runbooks, change control, and observability.
- **Security**: identity, least privilege, encryption, logging, and detection.
- **Reliability**: recovery objectives, redundancy, backup, and failover.
- **Performance Efficiency**: scaling model, quotas, latency, and right-sizing.
- **Cost Optimization**: chargeback, tagging, budgets, and resource lifecycle.
- **Sustainability**: efficient use of resources and avoiding unnecessary overprovisioning.

Practical exam use:

- If a design assumes manual operations during failover, question the **Operational Excellence** and **Reliability** posture.
- If centralized logging is missing, the **Security** and **Operational Excellence** pillars are incomplete.
- If the architecture depends on burst scaling without quota validation, **Performance Efficiency** is not fully validated.
- If the design requires always-on multi-Region active-active without business justification, challenge the **Cost Optimization** assumption.

**Landing zone and governance prerequisites**

Enterprise designs usually begin with a landing zone. In AWS, that typically means a standardized multi-account foundation with guardrails, identity integration, logging, and network segmentation. AWS Control Tower is a common starting point for this model.

Typical governance artifacts and prerequisites:

- **AWS Control Tower** for landing zone setup and guardrails.
- **Organizational Units (OUs)** to group accounts by environment, business unit, or risk profile.
- **Account vending** to provision accounts consistently and with baseline controls.
- **Delegated administrator** setup for services such as Security Hub, GuardDuty, AWS Config, or IAM Access Analyzer.
- **Service Control Policies (SCPs)** to enforce organization-wide restrictions.
- **Tagging standards** for cost allocation, ownership, and automation.

Common OU design patterns:

- **Security OU** for log archive, security tooling, and audit functions.
- **Infrastructure OU** for shared services such as networking and identity integration.
- **Sandbox OU** for experimentation with strict guardrails.
- **Workload OUs** split by environment, such as `Prod`, `NonProd`, and `Shared`.

A practical account structure often includes:

- **Management account** for organization administration only.
- **Log archive account** for immutable or centralized logs.
- **Security account** for detection and response tooling.
- **Shared services account** for DNS, directory integration, CI/CD, or network hubs.
- **Workload accounts** for application tiers and business units.

Example Control Tower-aligned account vending flow:

1. Create the organization and enable Control Tower.
2. Define OUs and guardrails.
3. Configure account factory or equivalent provisioning workflow.
4. Assign baseline roles, logging, and security integrations.
5. Delegate administration for supported security services.
6. Enforce SCPs and tagging policies.

**Identity and access prerequisites**

Enterprise architecture depends on centralized identity. You should validate whether the organization has a reliable identity source and a clear access model before selecting the target design.

Common prerequisites:

- Federation with an external identity provider such as Microsoft Entra ID, Okta, or another SAML 2.0 provider.
- AWS IAM Identity Center for workforce access.
- Role-based access control with least privilege.
- Break-glass access for emergency administration.
- MFA (multi-factor authentication) for privileged users.
- Permission boundaries or SCPs for delegated administration.

Practical considerations:

- Use IAM Identity Center for human access across multiple accounts.
- Use IAM roles for workload-to-workload and service-to-service access.
- Avoid long-lived access keys for administrators.
- Validate that emergency access is documented and tested.

Example trust policy for cross-account role assumption:

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Principal": { "AWS": "arn:aws:iam::111122223333:root" },
      "Action": "sts:AssumeRole",
      "Condition": {
        "StringEquals": {
          "aws:PrincipalTag/Department": "Finance"
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

**Centralized logging and security prerequisites**

A common enterprise prerequisite is centralized logging across all accounts and Regions. This is not optional in many regulated environments because it supports auditability, incident response, and forensic analysis.

Typical logging and security baseline:

- **AWS CloudTrail** organization trails for API activity.
- **AWS Config** for configuration history and compliance evaluation.
- **Amazon CloudWatch Logs** for application and system logs.
- **Amazon S3** log archive with lifecycle and retention controls.
- **AWS Security Hub** for aggregated findings.
- **Amazon GuardDuty** for threat detection.
- **AWS IAM Access Analyzer** for external access review.

Cross-account logging pattern:

- Workload accounts publish logs to a centralized S3 bucket in the log archive account.
- Bucket policies allow only approved principals and services.
- KMS keys are managed centrally with strict key policies.
- CloudTrail is enabled organization-wide and delivered to the archive account.

Example S3 bucket policy for centralized log delivery:

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "AllowCloudTrailWrite",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Principal": { "Service": "cloudtrail.amazonaws.com" },
      "Action": "s3:PutObject",
      "Resource": "arn:aws:s3:::central-log-archive/AWSLogs/*",
      "Condition": {
        "StringEquals": {
          "s3:x-amz-acl": "bucket-owner-full-control"
        }
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

Important validation points:

- Logs must be protected from accidental deletion.
- Retention must satisfy compliance and investigation needs.
- Time synchronization should be consistent across systems.
- Access to logs should be tightly controlled and audited.

**Network and hybrid connectivity prerequisites**

Enterprise solutions often depend on a validated network foundation. Before choosing a design, confirm whether the organization already has the required connectivity, segmentation, and name resolution.

Common prerequisites:

- Transit Gateway or equivalent hub-and-spoke routing.
- Direct Connect, Site-to-Site VPN, or both for hybrid connectivity.
- Segmented VPC design with shared services and workload isolation.
- Centralized egress and ingress controls.
- DNS resolution across on-premises and AWS environments.
- IP address planning that avoids overlap.

Hybrid DNS resolution example:

- On-premises clients query an internal DNS server.
- The internal DNS server forwards AWS private zone queries to Route 53 Resolver inbound endpoints.
- AWS workloads resolve on-premises names through Route 53 Resolver outbound endpoints and rules.

Example Route 53 Resolver rule association concept:

```bash
aws route53resolver create-resolver-rule \
  --creator-request-id dns-forwarding-001 \
  --name onprem-corp \
  --rule-type FORWARD \
  --domain-name corp.example.com \
  --target-ips Ip=10.0.10.10,Port=53 Ip=10.0.10.11,Port=53
```

Validation questions:

- Are CIDR blocks unique across all connected networks?
- Is there enough route table capacity and segmentation for the target scale?
- Are security groups and network ACLs aligned with the traffic model?
- Is DNS failover behavior understood for hybrid applications?
- Are firewall rules and inspection points documented?

**Service limits and quota planning**

Enterprise-scale designs frequently fail because of quotas, not because of service features. SAP-C02 expects you to recognize when a solution is blocked by account limits, regional limits, or soft quotas that require planning and increase requests.

Quota planning should cover:

- EC2 instance limits by family and Region.
- VPC limits such as subnets, route tables, and security groups.
- Elastic Load Balancing target group and listener scaling.
- Lambda concurrency and burst behavior.
- NAT Gateway, Elastic IP, and ENI limits.
- KMS, IAM, and API rate limits where relevant.
- AWS Organizations account and SCP scaling considerations.

Practical validation steps:

1. Estimate peak demand, not average demand.
2. Map demand to service quotas in each Region.
3. Identify which quotas are adjustable and which are hard limits.
4. Request increases before migration or launch.
5. Re-test after failover because secondary Regions need the same capacity.

Example quota review command:

```bash
aws service-quotas list-service-quotas \
  --service-code ec2 \
  --query 'Quotas[?Adjustable==`true`].[QuotaName,Value]' \
  --output table
```

Why this matters:

- A DR Region with insufficient capacity is not a real recovery target.
- A multi-account rollout can stall if account-level limits are not pre-approved.
- Auto scaling does not help if the quota ceiling is too low.

**Disaster recovery prerequisites**

Disaster recovery (DR) architecture requires more than an RTO (Recovery Time Objective) and RPO (Recovery Point Objective) statement. You need backup design, replication strategy, restore validation, and failover testing workflows.

Core DR prerequisites:

- Defined RTO and RPO per workload.
- Backup vault design with retention and access controls.
- Cross-Region replication for critical data where required.
- Restore testing and failover testing runbooks.
- DNS or traffic management strategy for failover.
- Dependency mapping for databases, queues, secrets, and identity.

Backup vault design considerations:

- Use separate backup vaults for production and non-production.
- Restrict vault access with IAM and KMS key policies.
- Consider Vault Lock for compliance-driven immutability.
- Replicate backups to a secondary Region if recovery requires it.

Example AWS Backup plan concept:

```json
{
  "BackupPlanName": "prod-daily",
  "Rules": [
    {
      "RuleName": "daily",
      "TargetBackupVaultName": "prod-vault",
      "ScheduleExpression": "cron(0 5 * * ? *)",
      "Lifecycle": {
        "DeleteAfterDays": 35
      },
      "CopyActions": [
        {
          "DestinationBackupVaultArn": "arn:aws:backup:us-west-2:111122223333:backup-vault:dr-vault"
        }
      ]
    }
  ]
}
```

Failover workflow example:

1. Detect the incident and declare the event.
2. Freeze writes if the application requires consistency.
3. Restore or promote data in the recovery Region.
4. Update DNS, load balancers, or traffic policies.
5. Validate application health and downstream integrations.
6. Record the event and test the failback plan.

Important distinction:

- **Backup** protects against deletion, corruption, and some outages.
- **Replication** reduces recovery time but can propagate corruption.
- **Failover testing** proves the plan works under realistic conditions.

**Operational prerequisites and automation**

Enterprise architecture should assume repeatable operations. Manual steps are acceptable only when they are documented, rare, and low risk. For SAP-C02, automation is often the better answer when it reduces human error and improves recovery.

Operational prerequisites include:

- Infrastructure as Code (IaC) such as AWS CloudFormation, AWS CDK, or Terraform.
- Standardized runbooks and change management.
- Monitoring and alerting with actionable thresholds.
- Patch and image management for compute fleets.
- Secrets management and rotation.
- Deployment pipelines with approval gates where needed.

Example CloudFormation snippet for a centralized log bucket:

```yaml
Resources:
  LogArchiveBucket:
    Type: AWS::S3::Bucket
    Properties:
      BucketName: central-log-archive
      VersioningConfiguration:
        Status: Enabled
      PublicAccessBlockConfiguration:
        BlockPublicAcls: true
        BlockPublicPolicy: true
        IgnorePublicAcls: true
        RestrictPublicBuckets: true
```

Operational review questions:

- Can the environment be rebuilt from code?
- Are changes traceable and approved?
- Are alarms tied to business-impacting symptoms?
- Are runbooks current and tested?
- Can the team operate the system during a Region impairment?

**Common enterprise prerequisite scenarios**

Centralized security services:

- Security tooling often lives in a dedicated security account.
- Findings from GuardDuty, Security Hub, and Config aggregate centrally.
- Delegated admin reduces the need to grant broad organization-wide access.

Cross-account logging:

- Workload accounts send logs to a log archive account.
- The archive account owns retention, encryption, and access policy.
- This pattern supports audit and incident response.

Hybrid DNS resolution:

- On-premises and AWS names must resolve consistently.
- Route 53 Resolver endpoints and forwarding rules are often required.
- Split-horizon DNS may be needed for internal and external views.

Shared network services:

- A shared services VPC can host DNS, directory integration, inspection, and egress controls.
- Transit Gateway can connect workload VPCs to shared services and on-premises networks.

Multi-Region DR:

- Primary and secondary Regions should both be validated for quotas, IAM, KMS, and network dependencies.
- Replication alone is not enough; restore and failover workflows must be tested.

**Exam-oriented distinctions**

Use these distinctions to eliminate wrong answers quickly:

- **Prerequisite**: “Before we can deploy this, we need centralized identity and logging.”
- **Constraint**: “The application must remain in one Region due to data residency.”
- **Assumption**: “We assume the secondary Region has enough capacity.”
- **Dependency**: “The design depends on an on-premises DNS forwarder.”

How these affect solution selection:

- If a prerequisite is missing, the design may be premature.
- If a constraint is violated, the design is invalid.
- If an assumption is unverified, the design needs validation or a fallback.
- If a dependency is fragile, the design needs redundancy or decoupling.

Example exam reasoning:

- A multi-account security design is attractive, but if AWS Organizations is not established, account vending and SCP enforcement are not yet possible.
- A multi-Region active-active design may be technically sound, but if quotas are unverified, it is not operationally safe.
- A hybrid application may require Route 53 Resolver, but if on-premises DNS cannot forward queries, name resolution will fail.

**Practical validation checklist**

Before approving an enterprise architecture, verify:

- Identity federation and privileged access model.
- AWS Organizations structure, OUs, and account vending process.
- Delegated admin assignments for security services.
- Centralized logging, retention, and encryption.
- Network segmentation, hybrid connectivity, and DNS resolution.
- Service quotas in primary and recovery Regions.
- Backup, replication, and restore testing.
- Well-Architected alignment across all relevant pillars.
- Operational readiness, automation, and runbooks.
- Compliance, audit, and tagging standards.

**Key takeaways**

Enterprise architecture review is about proving readiness, not just selecting services. For SAP-C02, you should validate governance, identity, logging, network, quotas, DR, and operational automation before you accept a design as viable. The best answer is often the one that identifies the missing prerequisite or the hidden dependency.
