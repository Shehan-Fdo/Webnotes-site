## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Professional (Exam SAP-C02)

**Architecture decision framework**

An architecture decision framework helps you choose between valid AWS designs by making tradeoffs explicit. In the SAP-C02 context, the goal is rarely to find a single “best” service. Instead, you compare options against business drivers such as availability, latency, durability, security, compliance, cost, and operational complexity.

A strong decision process starts with requirements, then narrows the design space using constraints and measurable outcomes. You should be able to explain not only *what* you chose, but *why* the alternative was rejected.

- **Business outcome**: define the user or system goal first.
- **Constraints**: identify hard limits such as region, data residency, RTO, or budget.
- **Tradeoffs**: compare options using the same criteria.
- **Risk**: note what can fail and how you reduce blast radius.
- **Operations**: account for deployment, monitoring, patching, and recovery effort.

**Core evaluation dimensions**

AWS architecture questions often hide a tradeoff between competing priorities. You should evaluate each option across a consistent set of dimensions rather than optimizing one metric in isolation.

| Dimension | What to ask | Common AWS implications |
|---|---|---|
| Cost | What is the steady-state and peak cost? | Reserved capacity, serverless, data transfer, storage tiering |
| Reliability | What happens when a component fails? | Multi-AZ, multi-Region, retries, decoupling |
| Performance | What is the latency and throughput target? | Caching, autoscaling, placement, acceleration |
| Security | How is access controlled and data protected? | IAM, KMS, private connectivity, segmentation |
| Operational excellence | How much effort is required to run it? | Managed services, automation, observability |
| Scalability | Can it grow without redesign? | Horizontal scaling, queues, partitioning |
| Durability | How likely is data loss? | S3, backups, replication, versioning |
| Compliance | Can it satisfy audit and residency needs? | Encryption, logging, account boundaries, region choice |

A useful habit is to rank the dimensions by importance for the workload. For example, a payment system may prioritize security and durability over cost, while a batch analytics pipeline may prioritize cost efficiency and throughput over low latency.

**How to reason about tradeoffs**

Tradeoffs are not just “cheaper versus better.” They usually involve a combination of service behavior, failure modes, and operational burden. For example, moving from self-managed EC2 instances to a managed service can reduce patching and scaling effort, but may reduce low-level control or increase per-unit cost.

When comparing designs, ask these questions:

- What is the failure domain?
- How quickly does the system recover?
- What is the impact of partial failure?
- What is the operational overhead?
- What is the cost of idle capacity?
- What changes when traffic doubles or triples?
- What is the blast radius of a bad deployment?

> If two designs meet the requirement, prefer the one that reduces operational burden and failure handling, unless cost or control is the explicit priority.

**Decision framework steps**

Use a repeatable process so your answer is defensible and easy to communicate.

1. Identify the requirement.
   - Capture the business goal, technical target, and nonfunctional requirements.
   - Separate hard constraints from preferences.
2. Identify candidate architectures.
   - Include at least two realistic options.
   - Avoid comparing a viable design to an obviously incorrect one.
3. Evaluate each option against the same criteria.
   - Use latency, availability, cost, security, and operations as a baseline.
   - Add workload-specific criteria such as data locality or write amplification.
4. Quantify where possible.
   - Use RTO, RPO, request rate, storage growth, or monthly spend.
   - If exact numbers are unavailable, state assumptions.
5. Select the best fit and justify it.
   - Explain why the chosen design satisfies the highest-priority requirements.
   - Mention the accepted tradeoff explicitly.
6. Validate with failure scenarios.
   - Consider AZ loss, instance failure, network partition, and deployment rollback.
   - Confirm the design still meets the minimum acceptable outcome.

**Common AWS tradeoff patterns**

Many exam scenarios reuse the same architectural tensions. Recognizing them helps you eliminate wrong answers quickly.

- **Managed service vs self-managed control**
  - Managed services reduce patching, scaling, and recovery work.
  - Self-managed solutions can provide deeper customization and sometimes lower direct service cost.
- **Multi-AZ vs single-AZ**
  - Multi-AZ improves availability and fault tolerance.
  - Single-AZ can be acceptable for noncritical, easily recreated workloads.
- **Multi-Region vs single-Region**
  - Multi-Region improves disaster recovery and geographic resilience.
  - It increases complexity, replication cost, and operational overhead.
- **Synchronous vs asynchronous processing**
  - Synchronous flows are simpler for immediate responses.
  - Asynchronous flows improve decoupling and absorb bursts better.
- **Scale up vs scale out**
  - Scale up can be simpler but has instance-size limits.
  - Scale out improves elasticity and resilience.
- **Strong consistency vs eventual consistency**
  - Strong consistency simplifies correctness.
  - Eventual consistency can improve availability and distribution at the cost of temporary divergence.

**Reliability and availability tradeoffs**

Reliability is about keeping the system working correctly under failure. Availability is about how often the system is usable. In practice, you improve both by reducing single points of failure and by designing for graceful degradation.

Typical AWS patterns include:

- **Multi-AZ deployment**
  - Use for production workloads that need high availability.
  - Place stateless compute behind load balancers and keep data services replicated.
- **Auto Scaling**
  - Use to replace failed instances and handle demand changes.
  - Combine with health checks and immutable deployments.
- **Decoupling with queues or streams**
  - Use Amazon SQS or Amazon Kinesis to buffer spikes and isolate failures.
  - This prevents downstream outages from immediately breaking upstream systems.
- **Backups and replication**
  - Use snapshots, versioning, and cross-Region replication where recovery matters.
  - Backups protect against accidental deletion and corruption, not just infrastructure failure.

A common design mistake is assuming that more replicas automatically solve every reliability problem. Replication helps with availability, but it does not fix bad deployments, corrupted data, or application-level bugs.

**Performance and scalability tradeoffs**

Performance tuning should match the bottleneck. Low latency, high throughput, and burst tolerance often require different tactics.

- **Caching**
  - Use Amazon CloudFront for edge caching and reduced origin load.
  - Use Amazon ElastiCache for application-level caching of hot data.
- **Partitioning**
  - Use sharding or partition keys when a single database node becomes a bottleneck.
  - Choose keys carefully to avoid hot partitions.
- **Asynchronous buffering**
  - Use queues to smooth traffic spikes and protect backends.
  - This improves throughput stability but adds processing delay.
- **Placement and locality**
  - Keep compute close to data when latency matters.
  - Cross-AZ or cross-Region calls add latency and data transfer cost.
- **Right-sizing**
  - Choose instance families and storage types based on workload profile.
  - Overprovisioning wastes cost; underprovisioning causes throttling and retries.

Example: if a web application has read-heavy traffic with repeated requests for the same objects, CloudFront plus origin caching may outperform scaling the application tier alone.

```bash
aws cloudfront create-distribution \
  --origin-domain-name myapp-alb-123456.us-east-1.elb.amazonaws.com \
  --default-root-object index.html
```

**Cost tradeoffs**

Cost is not only the monthly bill. It also includes engineering time, operational overhead, and the cost of failure. A cheaper design that causes frequent incidents may be more expensive overall.

Consider these cost levers:

- **Reserved capacity**
  - Use Reserved Instances or Savings Plans for steady workloads.
  - This lowers compute cost when usage is predictable.
- **Serverless**
  - Use AWS Lambda or managed services when traffic is spiky or unpredictable.
  - You pay for usage, but may trade away fine-grained control.
- **Storage tiering**
  - Move infrequently accessed data to lower-cost storage classes.
  - Balance retrieval cost against access patterns.
- **Data transfer**
  - Cross-AZ, cross-Region, and internet egress charges can dominate at scale.
  - Minimize unnecessary movement of large datasets.
- **Operational cost**
  - Managed services often reduce staff time even if unit pricing is higher.

A useful rule is to compare cost per business outcome, not just cost per resource. For example, a higher-priced managed database may be justified if it eliminates manual failover design and reduces recovery time.

**Security and compliance tradeoffs**

Security decisions often constrain architecture more than performance does. The key is to reduce exposure while preserving the required functionality.

- **Least privilege**
  - Grant only the permissions needed for the task.
  - Use IAM roles instead of long-lived credentials where possible.
- **Network isolation**
  - Prefer private subnets, security groups, and VPC endpoints for internal traffic.
  - Reduce public exposure unless internet access is required.
- **Encryption**
  - Encrypt data at rest with AWS KMS and in transit with TLS.
  - Consider key management requirements and auditability.
- **Segmentation**
  - Separate environments and workloads using accounts, VPCs, and resource boundaries.
  - This limits blast radius and supports governance.
- **Logging and traceability**
  - Enable CloudTrail, application logs, and service logs where auditability matters.
  - Retain logs according to compliance needs.

> Compliance requirements can override otherwise attractive designs. If a workload requires data residency in a specific Region, a multi-Region design may be unacceptable even if it improves resilience.

**Operational excellence tradeoffs**

Operational excellence means you can deploy, observe, and recover the system efficiently. In many exam scenarios, the best answer is the one that reduces manual intervention.

- **Managed services**
  - Reduce patching, backups, and failover work.
  - Often the best choice when the workload does not require deep OS-level control.
- **Infrastructure as Code (IaC)**
  - Use AWS CloudFormation, AWS CDK, or Terraform to make environments repeatable.
  - This lowers drift and improves rollback confidence.
- **Observability**
  - Use metrics, logs, and traces to detect issues early.
  - Alert on symptoms that matter to users, not just infrastructure health.
- **Automation**
  - Automate deployment, scaling, and recovery steps.
  - Manual runbooks are slower and more error-prone during incidents.

Example CloudFormation snippet for a simple Auto Scaling Group launch template pattern:

```yaml
Resources:
  AppLaunchTemplate:
    Type: AWS::EC2::LaunchTemplate
    Properties:
      LaunchTemplateData:
        ImageId: ami-0123456789abcdef0
        InstanceType: t3.micro
        SecurityGroupIds:
          - sg-0123456789abcdef0

  AppASG:
    Type: AWS::AutoScaling::AutoScalingGroup
    Properties:
      MinSize: "2"
      MaxSize: "6"
      DesiredCapacity: "2"
      VPCZoneIdentifier:
        - subnet-aaa
        - subnet-bbb
      LaunchTemplate:
        LaunchTemplateId: !Ref AppLaunchTemplate
        Version: !GetAtt AppLaunchTemplate.LatestVersionNumber
```

**Availability design patterns**

Availability improves when you remove single points of failure and design for fast recovery. The right pattern depends on the recovery objective and the acceptable downtime.

- **Active-active**
  - Multiple sites serve traffic simultaneously.
  - Best for very high availability, but more complex and expensive.
- **Active-passive**
  - One site serves traffic while another waits for failover.
  - Simpler than active-active and often sufficient for disaster recovery.
- **Pilot light**
  - Core components stay running at minimal scale.
  - You scale up during a disaster.
- **Warm standby**
  - A smaller but functional copy of the environment is always on.
  - Faster recovery than pilot light, with higher cost.
- **Backup and restore**
  - Lowest cost, slowest recovery.
  - Suitable when downtime is acceptable.

Use the recovery objective to choose the pattern:

- **RTO (Recovery Time Objective)**: how quickly you must restore service.
- **RPO (Recovery Point Objective)**: how much data loss is acceptable.

**Data architecture tradeoffs**

Data decisions often drive the whole solution. Storage, consistency, and replication choices affect both correctness and cost.

- **Relational vs NoSQL**
  - Relational databases fit structured data, joins, and transactional integrity.
  - NoSQL databases fit flexible schemas, high scale, and specific access patterns.
- **Strongly consistent writes vs distributed writes**
  - Strong consistency simplifies correctness and reporting.
  - Distributed writes can improve availability and scale but require conflict handling.
- **Single writer vs multi-writer**
  - Single-writer models are simpler.
  - Multi-writer models improve locality and resilience but complicate conflict resolution.
- **Snapshot vs continuous replication**
  - Snapshots are simpler and cheaper.
  - Continuous replication reduces data loss and speeds recovery.

Example: for a globally distributed application, you may choose a design that keeps writes regional and replicates asynchronously to other Regions for read access and disaster recovery. That reduces write latency but introduces replication lag.

**How to present tradeoffs in an exam answer**

When you justify an architecture, make the tradeoff explicit. A strong answer usually follows this pattern:

1. State the primary requirement.
2. Name the chosen AWS services.
3. Explain why the design fits the requirement.
4. Acknowledge the tradeoff you accept.
5. Mention how you mitigate the downside.

Example response structure:

- The workload requires low operational overhead and high availability.
- You choose a managed, multi-AZ service instead of self-managed instances.
- This reduces patching and failover complexity.
- The tradeoff is less control and potentially higher unit cost.
- You mitigate cost with right-sizing and autoscaling.

**Practical comparison examples**

**Example 1: Web application with bursty traffic**

A stateless web app with unpredictable traffic usually benefits from load balancing, Auto Scaling, and caching.

- Use an Application Load Balancer for request distribution.
- Use Auto Scaling for compute elasticity.
- Use CloudFront for static content and edge caching.
- Use ElastiCache if the app repeatedly reads the same data.

This design trades more moving parts for better burst handling and lower origin load.

**Example 2: Transactional system with strict recovery needs**

A financial workflow may need strong durability, auditability, and low RPO.

- Use a managed relational database with Multi-AZ.
- Enable backups, point-in-time recovery, and encryption.
- Use queues to isolate downstream processing.
- Log all critical actions to an immutable audit trail where required.

This design may cost more than a simpler setup, but it reduces the risk of data loss and supports compliance.

**Example 3: Batch processing pipeline**

A nightly ETL (extract, transform, load) job often values cost efficiency and throughput over immediate response.

- Use S3 as the landing zone.
- Use AWS Glue, EMR, or Lambda depending on transformation complexity.
- Buffer work with SQS or Step Functions if tasks are independent.
- Use spot capacity where interruption is acceptable.

This design accepts delayed completion in exchange for lower cost and better elasticity.

**Decision checklist**

Use this checklist when comparing AWS architectures:

- Does the design meet the stated functional requirement?
- Does it satisfy the required RTO and RPO?
- What happens if one AZ fails?
- What happens if one instance fails?
- What happens if traffic doubles suddenly?
- What is the cost of idle capacity?
- Can you operate it with the available team size?
- Does it satisfy security and compliance constraints?
- Is the failure mode acceptable?
- Is the tradeoff explicitly justified?

**Common exam traps**

- Choosing the cheapest option when the question emphasizes resilience or compliance.
- Choosing the most available option when the workload is noncritical and cost-sensitive.
- Ignoring data transfer cost in cross-AZ or cross-Region designs.
- Using self-managed infrastructure when a managed service better matches the operational requirement.
- Overengineering with multi-Region active-active when the RTO/RPO does not require it.
- Assuming caching solves write bottlenecks.
- Treating backups as a substitute for high availability.

**Summary mindset**

The best architecture decision is the one that aligns the design with the workload’s priorities. For SAP-C02, you should think in terms of measurable requirements, explicit tradeoffs, and failure-aware design. When two solutions both work, choose the one that best balances business value, operational simplicity, and risk reduction.
