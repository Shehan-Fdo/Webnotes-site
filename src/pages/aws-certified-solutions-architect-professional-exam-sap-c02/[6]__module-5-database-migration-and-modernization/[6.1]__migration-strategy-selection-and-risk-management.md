## **Course:** AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Professional (Exam SAP-C02)

**Migration strategy selection: the decision framework**

A migration strategy is the path you choose to move an application, data set, or platform from a source environment into AWS with the right balance of speed, cost, operational effort, and risk. For SAP-C02, you need to choose the strategy that best fits business constraints, technical dependencies, and acceptable downtime.

The common migration patterns are often summarized as the “6 Rs”:

- **Rehost**: lift and shift with minimal changes.
- **Replatform**: make a few cloud-friendly changes without redesigning the app.
- **Refactor/Re-architect**: redesign to use cloud-native services.
- **Repurchase**: move to a different product, usually SaaS.
- **Retire**: decommission the workload.
- **Retain**: keep the workload where it is for now.

A good strategy is not just about technical feasibility. You also evaluate:

- Business criticality and outage tolerance
- Regulatory and data residency constraints
- Application coupling and dependency depth
- Licensing and vendor support
- Time available for migration
- Skills available in the delivery team
- Long-term modernization goals

**The 6 Rs in practice**

Use the 6 Rs as a decision aid, not a rigid rule. Many enterprise migrations use multiple strategies across the portfolio.

- **Rehost** is useful when you need speed, have limited change windows, or want to exit a data center quickly.
- **Replatform** works when small changes can reduce operational burden, such as moving a database to Amazon RDS or Amazon Aurora.
- **Refactor** is appropriate when the workload needs elasticity, resilience, or faster delivery and the business accepts higher transformation effort.
- **Repurchase** is often chosen for commodity functions like CRM, ITSM, or collaboration tools.
- **Retire** removes technical debt and reduces migration scope.
- **Retain** is common for legacy systems, tightly coupled dependencies, or workloads awaiting a later phase.

A practical portfolio assessment often groups applications by complexity and business value. High-value, low-complexity systems are good candidates for early wins. High-complexity systems may need dependency mapping, landing zone readiness, and phased cutovers.

**Risk management fundamentals**

Migration risk is the chance that the move causes unacceptable impact to availability, integrity, confidentiality, cost, schedule, or compliance. Risk management means identifying, measuring, reducing, and monitoring those risks before, during, and after cutover.

Common migration risks include:

- **Downtime risk** from cutover errors, DNS delays, replication lag, or rollback complexity
- **Data loss risk** from incomplete replication, bad synchronization logic, or failed validation
- **Performance risk** from undersized instances, storage bottlenecks, or network latency
- **Security risk** from misconfigured IAM, exposed endpoints, or weak encryption controls
- **Compliance risk** from data residency, logging gaps, or retention failures
- **Operational risk** from missing runbooks, untested automation, or insufficient monitoring
- **Dependency risk** from hidden integrations, hardcoded IPs, or external partner systems

A strong migration plan includes:

- A risk register with owners, severity, likelihood, and mitigation
- Entry and exit criteria for each migration wave
- Backout or rollback procedures
- Validation checkpoints for data, application behavior, and observability
- Communication plans for business stakeholders and support teams

**Assessing workload suitability**

Before selecting a strategy, you should understand the workload’s architecture and constraints. Dependency discovery is essential because many migration failures come from unknown upstream or downstream systems.

Useful assessment activities include:

- Inventory servers, databases, middleware, batch jobs, and integrations
- Map network flows, ports, and authentication paths
- Identify stateful components and session handling
- Determine data size, change rate, and replication requirements
- Review licensing, OS support, and end-of-life timelines
- Classify data sensitivity and compliance obligations

AWS migration assessments often use tools and services such as:

- **AWS Application Discovery Service** for collecting server and application dependency data
- **AWS Migration Hub** for tracking migration progress across waves
- **AWS Application Migration Service (MGN)** for block-level replication and lift-and-shift migrations
- **AWS Database Migration Service (DMS)** for database replication and heterogeneous or homogeneous database moves
- **AWS Schema Conversion Tool (SCT)** for schema and code conversion planning

A simple dependency map can reveal whether a workload can be moved independently or must be migrated with related systems.

```text
[Users] -> [Web Tier] -> [App Tier] -> [Database]
                    \-> [Auth Service]
                    \-> [Reporting Job] -> [Data Warehouse]
```

If the database is shared by multiple applications, a direct rehost of only one app may fail unless you also address shared state and integration timing.

**Choosing between rehost, replatform, and refactor**

These three strategies are the most common exam focus because they represent increasing levels of change.

- **Rehost**
  - Lowest transformation effort
  - Fastest path to AWS
  - Often uses Amazon EC2 and AWS Application Migration Service
  - Best when the goal is to reduce data center footprint quickly
  - Risk: you may carry forward inefficiencies and technical debt

- **Replatform**
  - Moderate change with targeted improvements
  - Often replaces self-managed components with managed services
  - Examples include moving from self-managed MySQL to Amazon RDS, or from local file storage to Amazon EFS or Amazon S3
  - Best when you want operational simplification without full redesign
  - Risk: compatibility issues can appear if the app assumes local control or custom extensions

- **Refactor**
  - Highest engineering effort
  - Enables cloud-native scaling, decoupling, and resilience
  - Often uses microservices, event-driven patterns, containers, serverless, or managed databases
  - Best when the business needs long-term agility and can fund transformation
  - Risk: schedule, scope, and testing complexity increase significantly

A useful rule is to choose the least disruptive strategy that still meets the business objective. If the workload only needs to move quickly, rehost may be enough. If the workload is expensive to operate and has a clear modernization path, replatform may be better. If the workload is strategically important and constrained by legacy design, refactor may be justified.

**Data migration strategy and consistency**

Data movement is often the hardest part of migration. You need to decide whether to use offline transfer, online replication, or a hybrid approach.

Common options include:

- **Offline transfer** for large initial loads or low-change datasets
- **Online replication** for minimal downtime cutovers
- **Bulk transfer appliances** such as AWS Snowball for very large data sets or limited network bandwidth
- **Continuous replication** for databases and transactional systems

For databases, the main goal is to preserve consistency while minimizing downtime. AWS DMS can support ongoing replication after an initial full load, which helps you cut over with a shorter outage window. SCT helps when source and target engines differ, such as Oracle to PostgreSQL.

Practical considerations:

- Validate character sets, collation, and time zone handling
- Check large objects, sequences, triggers, and stored procedures
- Confirm application retry behavior during cutover
- Plan for read-only windows if strong consistency is required
- Test rollback if the target system fails validation

Example DMS task settings often emphasize full load plus CDC (change data capture):

```json
{
  "TargetMetadata": {
    "TargetSchema": "",
    "SupportLobs": true,
    "FullLobMode": false,
    "LobChunkSize": 64
  },
  "FullLoadSettings": {
    "TargetTablePrepMode": "DROP_AND_CREATE"
  },
  "Logging": {
    "EnableLogging": true
  }
}
```

**Risk reduction controls**

You reduce migration risk by designing for reversibility, observability, and validation.

Key controls include:

- **Pilot migrations** to prove the pattern on a low-risk workload
- **Wave planning** to group applications by dependency and business priority
- **Parallel run** periods where source and target operate together for validation
- **Blue/green cutover** for controlled traffic switching
- **Canary releases** for gradual exposure of new environments
- **Automated testing** for functional, performance, and security checks
- **Infrastructure as Code (IaC)** to make environments repeatable and auditable

A strong rollback plan should answer:

- What triggers rollback?
- How long can you wait before rollback becomes unsafe?
- What data must be reversed or re-synchronized?
- Who approves the decision?
- How do you communicate the rollback to stakeholders?

> If you cannot restore service quickly, a “rollback plan” is not a real risk control. It is only a document.

**Cutover patterns and when to use them**

Cutover is the point where users and systems begin using the new environment. The cutover pattern should match the workload’s tolerance for downtime and data divergence.

Common patterns:

- **Big bang**
  - Move everything at once
  - Simple conceptually, but highest risk
  - Best for small, low-complexity workloads

- **Phased migration**
  - Move components in stages
  - Reduces blast radius
  - Best for large enterprise systems with clear boundaries

- **Parallel run**
  - Source and target run together for a period
  - Useful for validation and business confidence
  - Requires extra cost and coordination

- **Pilot migration**
  - Move a small subset first
  - Helps validate tooling, process, and assumptions
  - Often used before broader waves

A phased approach is usually safer for complex systems because it limits the impact of a failure to one wave or one component group.

**AWS services commonly used in migration programs**

AWS provides services that support different parts of the migration lifecycle.

- **AWS Migration Hub**
  - Central visibility into migration status
  - Useful for tracking multiple applications and waves

- **AWS Application Migration Service (MGN)**
  - Replicates source servers into AWS
  - Supports lift-and-shift with minimal downtime
  - Useful for rehost strategies

- **AWS Database Migration Service (DMS)**
  - Migrates databases with minimal downtime
  - Supports ongoing replication and heterogeneous migrations

- **AWS Schema Conversion Tool (SCT)**
  - Assesses and converts database schemas and code
  - Helps identify conversion effort early

- **AWS Application Discovery Service**
  - Discovers on-premises servers and dependencies
  - Helps build the migration portfolio and wave plan

- **AWS DataSync**
  - Automates data transfer between on-premises storage and AWS storage services
  - Useful for file and object data migration

- **AWS Snowball**
  - Physical transfer for large-scale data migration
  - Useful when network transfer is too slow or expensive

- **AWS Direct Connect**
  - Dedicated network connection to AWS
  - Useful for stable, high-throughput replication and hybrid connectivity

**Network and connectivity considerations**

Migration risk often increases when network design is weak. You should verify routing, DNS, firewall rules, and latency before cutover.

Important checks include:

- Confirm CIDR overlap does not block connectivity
- Validate security groups and network ACLs
- Test name resolution across source and target environments
- Review VPN or Direct Connect failover behavior
- Ensure load balancers and certificates are ready
- Check MTU, throughput, and packet loss for replication traffic

Example connectivity validation commands:

```bash
ping -c 4 10.0.2.15
traceroute 10.0.2.15
nslookup app.internal.example.com
curl -I https://app.internal.example.com/health
```

For AWS-side inspection, you may use:

```bash
aws ec2 describe-security-groups --group-ids sg-0123456789abcdef0
aws ec2 describe-route-tables --filters Name=vpc-id,Values=vpc-0123456789abcdef0
aws rds describe-db-instances --db-instance-identifier prod-db
```

**Security, compliance, and governance**

Migration is a security event. You are moving data, credentials, and trust boundaries, so governance must be explicit.

Controls to include:

- Encrypt data in transit with TLS
- Encrypt data at rest with AWS KMS-managed keys where appropriate
- Use least-privilege IAM roles and temporary credentials
- Rotate secrets and remove hardcoded credentials
- Centralize logs in immutable or tightly controlled storage
- Review retention and deletion requirements
- Validate that data residency and regulatory controls are preserved

Governance also includes change management:

- Document the migration scope and exceptions
- Approve risk acceptance where mitigation is not possible
- Track dependencies on third-party vendors
- Maintain audit evidence for testing and cutover decisions

**Testing and validation**

Testing is how you prove the migration is safe. Do not rely on infrastructure success alone; validate application behavior and business outcomes.

Typical validation layers:

- **Infrastructure validation**
  - Instances, storage, networking, and IAM are correct

- **Application validation**
  - Login, transactions, batch jobs, and integrations work

- **Data validation**
  - Row counts, checksums, and reconciliation reports match

- **Performance validation**
  - Response times and throughput meet expectations

- **Operational validation**
  - Monitoring, alerting, backups, and patching are in place

Example validation checklist:

1. Confirm the application starts successfully in the target environment.
2. Verify authentication and authorization flows.
3. Run smoke tests for critical user journeys.
4. Compare source and target data samples.
5. Validate logs, metrics, and alarms.
6. Execute rollback rehearsal if required by the risk profile.

A simple checksum comparison can help detect data drift:

```bash
sha256sum export.csv
sha256sum import.csv
```

For database validation, you may compare counts and key aggregates:

```sql
SELECT COUNT(*) FROM orders;
SELECT customer_id, COUNT(*) FROM orders GROUP BY customer_id ORDER BY customer_id;
```

**Operational readiness**

A migration is not complete when the workload is live; it is complete when operations can support it reliably.

Operational readiness includes:

- Runbooks for common incidents and rollback
- Monitoring dashboards and alert thresholds
- Backup and restore procedures
- Patch and maintenance windows
- Access control and break-glass procedures
- Ownership handoff to the operations team

You should also confirm that the target environment supports the same or better service levels than the source. If the source had manual processes, migration is a chance to replace them with automation.

**Exam-oriented decision cues**

For SAP-C02, look for clues in the scenario that point to the right strategy:

- Choose **rehost** when speed, minimal change, and quick exit from on-premises matter most.
- Choose **replatform** when small managed-service changes reduce operational burden without major redesign.
- Choose **refactor** when the business wants cloud-native scalability, resilience, or faster innovation.
- Choose **repurchase** when a SaaS product can replace a custom or commodity application.
- Choose **retire** when the workload no longer provides value.
- Choose **retain** when dependencies, risk, or timing make migration inappropriate right now.

Risk management questions often hinge on:

- Downtime tolerance
- Data consistency requirements
- Dependency complexity
- Compliance constraints
- Rollback feasibility
- Cost of failure versus cost of transformation

**Practical migration planning example**

A common enterprise pattern is to migrate a three-tier application with a database and reporting jobs.

```text
Phase 1: Discover dependencies and classify data
Phase 2: Build landing zone and connectivity
Phase 3: Pilot rehost of a noncritical app
Phase 4: Migrate database with DMS full load + CDC
Phase 5: Cut over web and app tiers
Phase 6: Validate reports and batch jobs
Phase 7: Decommission source after stabilization
```

This approach reduces risk because you validate the process on a small workload before moving critical systems. It also creates reusable patterns for later waves.

**Common pitfalls**

- Migrating without dependency discovery
- Underestimating data transfer time
- Ignoring DNS TTL and cutover propagation
- Forgetting shared services such as authentication or file shares
- Treating testing as optional
- Skipping rollback rehearsal
- Moving too many workloads in one wave
- Failing to align business stakeholders on outage windows

**Key takeaway**

The best migration strategy is the one that satisfies business goals with the least necessary change and the lowest acceptable risk. In practice, that means assessing dependencies, choosing the right R from the 6 Rs, planning data movement carefully, and proving the cutover with testing, rollback, and operational readiness.
