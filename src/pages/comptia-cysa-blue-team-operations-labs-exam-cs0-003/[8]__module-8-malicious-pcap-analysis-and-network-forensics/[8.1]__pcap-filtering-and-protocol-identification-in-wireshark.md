## **Course:** CompTIA CySA+ Blue Team Operations Labs (Exam CS0-003)

**Wireshark for packet review**

Wireshark is a network protocol analyzer that lets you inspect live traffic or pre-recorded packet capture files (PCAPs). In security work, you use it to review suspicious traffic, troubleshoot network issues, and study how protocols behave in real traffic.

Display filters are the main way to narrow what you see while viewing a capture. They are different from capture filters, which control what gets recorded in the first place. For analysis work, this distinction matters because you often need to keep the full capture and refine your view afterward.

- Use Wireshark to break traffic into individual packets for detailed inspection.
- Focus on display filters when you want to review specific traffic after capture.
- Treat PCAP review as a common workflow for suspicious or malware-related network activity.

**Display filters and syntax**

Wireshark display filters let you drill down to exact traffic fields and are also used by coloring rules. The filter reference contains a very large set of protocol fields, so the practical skill is knowing how to search for the right field and apply it correctly.

A display filter is not the same as a capture filter. For example, `tcp.port == 80` is a display filter, while `tcp port 80` is a capture filter. Use the display filter form when you want to hide unrelated packets without changing the capture itself.

- Display filters work on packets already in the capture.
- Capture filters limit what gets saved during capture.
- Wireshark’s field reference helps you find protocol-specific filter names.

> Use conservative filter logic when you are unsure. Start broad, then narrow the view as you confirm the protocol or host behavior.

**Common filter patterns**

Wireshark supports simple expressions that combine protocol names, ports, addresses, and field comparisons. These patterns help you isolate common traffic types during triage.

```text
tcp.port eq 25 or icmp
ip.src==192.168.0.0/16 and ip.dst==192.168.0.0/16
tcp.window_size == 0 && tcp.flags.reset != 1
smb || nbns || dcerpc || nbss || dns
```

- `tcp.port eq 25 or icmp` shows SMTP and ICMP traffic.
- `ip.src==192.168.0.0/16 and ip.dst==192.168.0.0/16` limits results to internal LAN traffic.
- `tcp.window_size == 0 && tcp.flags.reset != 1` helps identify a TCP buffer-full condition.
- `smb || nbns || dcerpc || nbss || dns` reduces noise when watching Windows client and domain controller exchanges.

**Protocol identification workflow**

When you inspect a PCAP, your goal is to identify which protocols are present and which packets matter. Sample captures and protocol references help you practice this process with known traffic types such as HTTP, SMB, TCP, UDP, DNS, and other protocol families.

A practical workflow is to start with broad protocol filters, confirm the traffic pattern, and then narrow to the relevant stream or host pair. This is especially useful when reviewing malicious traffic from infected hosts or challenge captures.

1. Open the PCAP in Wireshark.
2. Apply a broad display filter for the protocol or host range you want to inspect.
3. Confirm the packet fields and conversation pattern.
4. Narrow the filter to the specific stream, port, or endpoint of interest.
5. Compare the filtered packets against the behavior you expect from that protocol.

- Protocol identification becomes easier when you know the common field names for the traffic family.
- Sample captures are useful for learning protocol behavior before you analyze unknown traffic.
- Small captures are often easier to review directly in Wireshark.

**Malicious traffic review**

Security analysts often use Wireshark to review PCAPs that contain malware-generated traffic. In that context, filtering helps you separate the signal from the noise and focus on the packets that show command, control, or suspicious host activity.

When working with potentially malicious captures, keep your analysis environment controlled. A common practice is to use an isolated virtual machine and disable unnecessary network exposure while examining the traffic.

- Review packet captures to understand what a suspicious host is doing on the network.
- Use filters to isolate the traffic generated by the infected system.
- Keep the analysis environment isolated when you may need to inspect extracted content.

**Key takeaways**

Wireshark is most effective when you combine protocol knowledge with precise display filters. You use the filter language to reduce packet noise, identify relevant protocols, and focus on the traffic that supports your investigation.

- Display filters refine what you see during analysis.
- Capture filters control what gets recorded.
- Protocol references and sample captures speed up identification.
- Isolated analysis is a safer choice when reviewing suspicious traffic.
