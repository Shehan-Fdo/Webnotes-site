## **Course:** CompTIA CySA+ Blue Team Operations Labs (Exam CS0-003)

**Beaconing and command-and-control basics**

Beaconing is a recurring communication pattern where an infected host checks in with a command-and-control (C2 or C&C) server on a regular schedule. Analysts use this pattern to separate malicious traffic from normal traffic because the timing is often unusually regular or intentionally randomized to avoid detection.

C2 infrastructure can direct many malicious actions, including ransomware activity, denial of service, and data exfiltration. Attackers often hide this traffic on common web ports and protocols such as `HTTP:80` and `HTTPS:443`, which makes it blend into legitimate traffic and helps it evade firewalls.

- **Beaconing** often shows repeated connections from the same source to the same destination.
- **Regular intervals** are a strong clue when the traffic repeats with consistent timing.
- **Randomized delays** can also be suspicious when they appear as long, uneven gaps meant to reduce visibility.
- **Common ports** do not prove legitimacy; malicious traffic can hide on standard web ports.
- **C2** traffic may look like normal web activity unless you inspect behavior over time.

**What to look for in PCAPs**

Behavioral analysis is especially useful when payloads are encrypted. Tools such as Shrike focus on flow statistics and timing patterns rather than reading encrypted content, which helps identify C2 beaconing, data exfiltration, DNS tunneling, and related threats in packet captures.

A practical review starts with the communication pattern, then narrows to the endpoints, timing, and request shape. Repeated small requests, unusual URLs, and consistent source-to-destination pairs are common indicators of beaconing or low-and-slow malicious activity.

- **Flow statistics** help you spot repeated patterns without decrypting payloads.
- **Timing patterns** matter when the traffic volume is low but persistent.
- **Repeated small requests** can indicate a host phoning home quietly.
- **Unusual URLs** may reveal hidden C2 or download activity.
- **Same source and destination** over time is a useful first filter in a PCAP.

**Download and exfiltration indicators**

Download activity and exfiltration can both appear as repeated or patterned transfers, but the direction and behavior differ. Downloads usually bring data or tools into the environment, while exfiltration sends data out. In both cases, the traffic may be designed to look routine and may use low-and-slow behavior to avoid attention.

Look for patterns that suggest a host is receiving instructions or payloads, or sending data away in a way that does not match normal business use. When traffic is encrypted, you rely more on metadata and behavior than on content inspection.

- **Downloads** may appear as repeated retrievals from the same external host.
- **Exfiltration** often shows outbound transfers that are steady, quiet, or unusually persistent.
- **Low-and-slow** behavior is a common evasion tactic for both downloads and exfiltration.
- **Encrypted traffic** requires you to focus on metadata, timing, and repetition.
- **Behavioral clues** are often more reliable than a single packet or request.

**Analyst workflow**

1. Identify repeated connections between the same endpoints.
2. Check whether the timing is regular, randomized, or unusually persistent.
3. Review ports, protocols, and request size for signs of blending into normal traffic.
4. Compare the pattern against expected host behavior.
5. Escalate traffic that suggests C2 beaconing, suspicious downloads, or outbound data movement.

> Use caution when judging periodic traffic: legitimate health checks and update traffic can also be repetitive, so you need context before labeling it malicious.

**Quick comparison**

| Pattern | Typical direction | Common clue | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| Beaconing | Host to C2 server | Repeated check-ins | Suggests an implant is still alive |
| Download activity | External to host | Repeated retrievals | May deliver tools or payloads |
| Exfiltration | Host to external | Outbound data movement | May indicate stolen data leaving the network |

**Key takeaway**

The most useful indicators are behavioral: repetition, timing, endpoint consistency, and traffic shape. If you can recognize those patterns in PCAP data, you can spot beaconing, C2 communication, downloads, and exfiltration even when the payload is encrypted.
