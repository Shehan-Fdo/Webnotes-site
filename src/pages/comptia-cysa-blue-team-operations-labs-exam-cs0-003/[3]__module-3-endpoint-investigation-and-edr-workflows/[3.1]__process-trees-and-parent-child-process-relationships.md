## **Course:** CompTIA CySA+ Blue Team Operations Labs (Exam CS0-003)

**Process tree fundamentals**

A process tree shows how one process starts another and how execution flows across an endpoint. The parent process is the process that launches another process, and the child process is the one that is created. This relationship helps you understand normal system behavior and spot suspicious deviations.

Process lineage analysis focuses on the ancestry and siblings of a process, along with metadata such as execution order and duration. In practice, you use this context to decide whether a process looks expected, unusual, or clearly malicious.

- **Ancestry** helps you trace where a process came from.
- **Siblings** help you compare related processes started around the same time.
- **Metadata** such as order and duration adds context to the tree.
- **Deviation** from expected launch patterns is often the signal you investigate.

**Why parent-child relationships matter**

Attackers often try to hide inside normal activity by spawning malicious processes from trusted applications. That makes child processes a useful indicator of compromise because they can reveal execution paths that should not exist in routine use.

Examples from the evidence show why this matters:

- A file transfer service spawning `cmd.exe`, `powershell.exe`, or `wscript.exe` can indicate post-exploitation activity.
- Trusted binaries or administrative tools launching WMI can be normal, but unusual binaries spawning WMI or being spawned by WMI can be suspicious.
- A process that is not initiated from boot or not initiated by another process may stand out as suspicious.

These relationships help you connect a single alert to broader activity such as privilege escalation, persistence, discovery, or lateral movement.

**What to look for in a process tree**

When you review a tree, focus on whether the launch path matches the expected behavior of the host and application. A process tree is most useful when you compare the observed chain against what should normally happen.

- **Unexpected child processes** from trusted applications deserve attention.
- **Unusual launch sources** can indicate abuse of legitimate software.
- **Execution order** matters when a process appears before a normal boot-started process.
- **Creator context** matters when the process does not appear to come from a user or expected parent.

> Attackers may spoof the parent process ID, so process lineage alone is not always enough. Use it as one part of a broader investigation.

**Investigation workflow**

1. Identify the parent process and the child process.
2. Check whether the child process is expected for that parent.
3. Review the ancestry for unusual launch paths or missing context.
4. Compare siblings and timing to see whether the behavior fits normal activity.
5. Correlate the tree with other telemetry, such as files, network activity, or alert context.

**Common suspicious patterns**

- A trusted application launches a scripting or command interpreter unexpectedly.
- A legitimate Windows binary spawns WMI in a way that does not match normal administration.
- A process appears without a clear parent-child chain that fits the host’s normal startup behavior.
- A child process appears in a sequence that suggests post-exploitation rather than routine use.

**Operational value for analysts**

Process trees help you move from a single event to a narrative of what happened on the endpoint. That makes them useful for triage because they support quick classification, scoping, and prioritization.

They also help you separate normal administrative activity from suspicious behavior. In blue team work, that distinction is important because many legitimate tools can look risky until you inspect the parent-child relationship and the surrounding context.

**Key takeaway**

Process trees are a practical way to analyze process lineage, identify suspicious children, and spot deviations from expected execution. Use them to understand how a process started, what it launched, and whether the chain fits normal endpoint behavior.
