## **Course:** CompTIA CySA+ Blue Team Operations Labs (Exam CS0-003)

**Triage notes in SOC operations**

A security operations center (SOC) continuously monitors, triages, and responds to security events. Your triage notes should capture what happened, what you checked, and what you recommend next. Clear documentation matters because incident tickets are evidence, communication tools, and a record of your investigation quality.

When you write notes, keep them concise but complete. Include the alert trigger, affected asset, timeline, and any indicators of compromise (IOCs) you verified. If you do not document the work, the handoff becomes harder and the response loses continuity.

- Record the alert type and affected asset in the title.
- Note the detection timeline and the first observed activity.
- Include relevant IP addresses, user accounts, and systems.
- Summarize what logs or artifacts you reviewed.
- Capture any IOC checks, email header review, or endpoint behavior analysis.

**Priority assignment**

Priority should reflect both the severity of the event and the likely impact on the environment. In SOC ticketing, severity labels such as Critical, High, Medium, and Low help you communicate urgency quickly. Use the evidence you have, not assumptions, and keep the category aligned with the event type, such as phishing, malware, or policy violation.

A practical triage note separates what is known from what still needs validation. That makes it easier to decide whether the event needs immediate escalation or can be monitored while you gather more context.

| Ticket element | What to capture |
|---|---|
| Title | Alert type, affected asset, and threat nature |
| Severity | Critical, High, Medium, or Low |
| Category | Phishing, malware, policy violation, or similar |
| Summary | Trigger, timeline, and affected entities |
| Analyst actions | Logs reviewed and checks performed |

**Writing effective incident tickets**

A strong ticket gives the next analyst enough context to continue without redoing your work. Use a clear title, then add a short summary that explains the alert and the scope of the event. Follow that with the steps you took so the response trail stays auditable.

1. Write a concise title that names the alert and the affected asset.
2. Assign a severity level that matches the observed impact.
3. Add a summary of the trigger, timeline, and involved entities.
4. List the checks you performed and the evidence you reviewed.
5. Update the ticket as new facts confirm or change the priority.

**Initial containment recommendation**

Initial containment should be safe, targeted, and consistent with the evidence you have. The goal is to reduce risk quickly without creating unnecessary disruption. In practice, that means recommending the smallest action that limits spread or further impact while investigation continues.

Managed SOC and automated response models can help with faster response, but they still depend on safe automation and an escalation plan. Start by understanding your tools, people, and processes, then choose containment actions that fit your environment.

- Isolate the affected endpoint if the evidence suggests active compromise.
- Disable or restrict the impacted account if account abuse is likely.
- Block known malicious indicators when they are confirmed.
- Pause or suppress noisy alerts only when you can justify the change.
- Escalate quickly when the event may affect multiple systems or users.

> Do not recommend broad disruption unless the evidence supports it. Prefer targeted containment that limits risk while preserving investigation value.

**Practical note structure**

Use a repeatable format so your notes stay readable under pressure. A simple structure helps you move from observation to action without missing key details.

- **What happened:** the alert and the observed behavior.
- **What you verified:** logs, headers, endpoint activity, or IOC checks.
- **How you classified it:** severity and category.
- **What you recommend:** the first containment step and why it is appropriate.

**Quick example template**

```text
Title: Phishing Email Detected - HR Mailbox - Suspicious Link
Severity: High
Category: Phishing
Summary: Alert triggered by suspicious link in email delivered to HR mailbox. Reviewed message headers, sender details, and user activity.
Analyst actions: Checked email headers, validated URLs, and reviewed related endpoint activity.
Recommendation: Quarantine the message and restrict the affected account if follow-up activity confirms abuse.
```

**Key takeaways**

Triage notes should be specific enough to support handoff and escalation. Priority assignment should reflect observed impact, not guesswork. Initial containment should be the least disruptive action that meaningfully reduces risk and preserves the investigation.
