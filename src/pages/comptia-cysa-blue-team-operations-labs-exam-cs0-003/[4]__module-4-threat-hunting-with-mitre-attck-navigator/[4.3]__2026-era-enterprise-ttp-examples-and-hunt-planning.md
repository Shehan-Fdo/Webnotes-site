## **Course:** CompTIA CySA+ Blue Team Operations Labs (Exam CS0-003)

**Identity-first threats shape modern enterprise hunts**

Modern enterprise intrusion patterns increasingly center on identity abuse rather than noisy endpoint malware. The evidence points to credential theft, token abuse, and valid account use as dominant paths into environments, especially across hybrid and multi-cloud estates.

Credential theft is a high-value hunt area because it is both common and costly. The supplied evidence highlights that stolen credentials initiated 22% of breaches in 2025, while account compromise surged sharply year over year. It also notes that attackers industrialize credential harvesting through infostealer ecosystems and dark web credential compilations.

- **Credential theft** often starts outside the perimeter and ends inside trusted services.
- **Identity abuse** can bypass many endpoint-centric detections.
- **Continuous monitoring** matters because compromise can persist for long periods before discovery.

> Traditional push-based MFA is not enough against real-time phishing and session interception. Favor phishing-resistant MFA such as FIDO2/passkeys where possible.

**Map hunts to attacker behavior, not just alerts**

Identity Threat Detection and Response (ITDR) is a useful organizing concept for hunt planning. ITDR focuses on detecting, investigating, and mitigating identity-based threats across hybrid and multi-cloud environments. In practice, it complements SIEM (Security Information and Event Management), EDR (Endpoint Detection and Response), and XDR (Extended Detection and Response) by concentrating on account misuse, compromise, and abuse.

A practical hunt program should assume that adversaries may use legitimate access to blend in. MITRE ATT&CK technique T1078, Valid Accounts, captures this pattern and includes default, domain, local, and cloud account abuse. The technique can support initial access, persistence, privilege escalation, and defense evasion.

- Hunt for **valid accounts** used from unusual locations, devices, or times.
- Look for **inactive account** abuse when access appears legitimate but context is wrong.
- Treat **cloud accounts** as first-class identity targets, not a separate problem.

**Enterprise TTP examples to prioritize**

The evidence supports a few recurring enterprise tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) that are especially relevant for 2026-era hunts:

- **Credential theft and reuse**
  - Stolen credentials remain a primary breach vector.
  - Attackers may use them to access VPNs, webmail, remote desktop, and other externally exposed services.
- **Real-time MFA bypass**
  - Push-based MFA can be defeated in phishing flows that relay authentication in real time.
  - Phishing-resistant MFA reduces this risk.
- **Valid account abuse**
  - Adversaries may avoid malware and use legitimate access to reduce visibility.
  - Compromised credentials can enable persistence and privilege escalation.
- **Cloud resource enumeration**
  - Tools such as AzureHound are intended for penetration testing but can be misused to enumerate Azure resources and map attack paths.
  - This activity often shows up in cloud logs and can support later malicious operations.

**What to look for in hunts**

Use a layered view across identity, network, and cloud telemetry. The goal is to connect weak signals into a coherent story before the attacker expands access.

- **Identity signals**
  - Unusual sign-in patterns
  - New device or location for a known user
  - Repeated authentication failures followed by success
  - Privilege changes that do not match normal role behavior
- **Cloud signals**
  - Resource enumeration at unusual volume or cadence
  - Access to management-plane data that does not fit the user’s role
  - New service principal or account activity that lacks a clear business reason
- **Behavioral signals**
  - Access that looks valid but occurs at odd times
  - Rapid movement across services after first login
  - Use of legitimate tools in ways that resemble reconnaissance

**Hunt planning workflow**

1. Start with the identity attack surface.
   - Identify the accounts, services, and authentication paths most likely to be targeted.
2. Define the behaviors you expect to see.
   - Translate attacker goals into observable actions such as sign-in anomalies, privilege changes, or cloud enumeration.
3. Map each behavior to available telemetry.
   - Use identity logs, cloud logs, and network visibility together.
4. Prioritize high-impact scenarios.
   - Focus first on credential theft, valid account abuse, and cloud discovery.
5. Validate detections against real attacker paths.
   - Check whether your coverage can distinguish normal admin activity from malicious reconnaissance.
6. Refine hunts based on gaps.
   - If a behavior is visible only in one data source, add supporting telemetry or adjust the hunt scope.

**Detection and defense themes**

Effective defense against these threats depends on unified observability. The evidence emphasizes behavioral analytics, ITDR, zero trust architecture, and continuous credential monitoring across network, identity, and cloud.

- **Behavioral analytics** helps identify misuse that looks legitimate at first glance.
- **Zero trust** reduces implicit trust in accounts and sessions.
- **Continuous credential monitoring** helps surface exposure before attackers exploit it.
- **Phishing-resistant MFA** is a stronger control than push-based approval alone.

**Practical takeaway for CySA+ labs**

For exam prep, think in terms of attacker intent and observable identity behavior. If a user account suddenly behaves like a reconnaissance tool, or a cloud identity starts enumerating resources outside its normal pattern, that is a strong hunt candidate.

The most useful modern hunts are not single-alert checks. They are correlation exercises that connect identity compromise, valid account use, and cloud discovery into one timeline.
