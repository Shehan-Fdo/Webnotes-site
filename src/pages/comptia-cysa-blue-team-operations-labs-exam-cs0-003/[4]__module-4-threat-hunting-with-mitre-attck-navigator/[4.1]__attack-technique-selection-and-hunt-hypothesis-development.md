## **Course:** CompTIA CySA+ Blue Team Operations Labs (Exam CS0-003)

**ATT&CK as a hunting framework**

MITRE ATT&CK is a globally accessible knowledge base of adversary tactics and techniques based on real-world observations. In threat hunting, you use it to move from a news-style incident narrative to a defensive strategy that focuses on behavior rather than easy-to-change artifacts like hashes or IP addresses.

The practical value is in asking how an attacker operated:
- How did they gain access?
- How did they move through the environment?
- How did they achieve their objective?

ATT&CK helps you map those questions to a structured model of adversary behavior. That makes it easier to choose hunt targets that are meaningful, repeatable, and tied to observed attacker tradecraft.

**Tactics and techniques**

ATT&CK organizes adversary behavior into tactics and techniques.

- **Tactic**: the adversary’s tactical objective, or the “why”
- **Technique**: the method used to accomplish that objective, or the “how”
- **Sub-technique**: a more specific variation of a technique when you need finer detail

This structure helps you avoid vague hunts. Instead of searching broadly for “suspicious activity,” you can focus on a specific behavior category such as initial access, execution, persistence, privilege escalation, defense evasion, credential access, discovery, lateral movement, collection, or command and control.

**Selecting a technique for a hunt**

Technique selection starts with a question or signal that is worth investigating. You then translate that concern into a behavior-based hunt target.

Use this approach:
1. Start with a threat report, incident pattern, or environmental concern.
2. Identify the attacker behavior described in the source material.
3. Map that behavior to the most relevant ATT&CK tactic or technique.
4. Narrow the scope to a technique or sub-technique that you can actually observe in your environment.
5. Build the hunt around evidence you can collect and validate.

This keeps the hunt grounded in observable behavior instead of relying on indicators that attackers can easily replace.

**Building a hunt hypothesis**

A hunt hypothesis is a testable statement about suspicious or malicious activity that may not yet have triggered an alert. Strong hypotheses give you direction and help you decide what data to inspect.

A practical hypothesis usually includes:
- **Actor or behavior**: what kind of activity you expect to see
- **Environment**: where you expect to see it
- **Evidence**: what logs, telemetry, or events should confirm or disprove it
- **Outcome**: what you expect if the behavior is present

For example, if a report suggests lateral movement, your hypothesis should focus on whether the environment shows signs of that behavior rather than on a specific file or address.

**Why behavior-based hunting works**

Threat hunting is proactive. It looks for malicious or suspicious activity that may not yet have triggered an alert. That makes it useful when automated tools miss activity or when attackers use methods that evade signature-based detection.

Behavior-based hunting is stronger because:
- It is harder for attackers to hide the underlying method than a single artifact.
- It supports repeatable investigations across different incidents.
- It aligns with how ATT&CK describes adversary tradecraft.
- It helps you prioritize what to search for in large volumes of data.

**Practical hunt workflow**

A simple ATT&CK-driven hunt workflow keeps the process manageable and defensible.

1. Review the threat intelligence or incident context.
2. Extract the attacker behavior from the narrative.
3. Map the behavior to ATT&CK tactics and techniques.
4. Write a hypothesis that can be tested with available telemetry.
5. Search for evidence that supports or refutes the hypothesis.
6. Refine the hunt if the data suggests a different technique or sub-technique.

This workflow is useful when you are overwhelmed by data and need a structured way to begin. It also helps you avoid jumping straight to conclusions before you have evidence.

**Key takeaways**

- ATT&CK gives you a common language for adversary behavior.
- Tactics describe the goal; techniques describe the method.
- Hunt hypotheses should be specific, testable, and tied to observable evidence.
- Behavior-based hunting is more durable than chasing single indicators.
- Good technique selection narrows the search to what you can validate in your environment.
