## **Course:** Comptia Network Plus

**Switching fundamentals**

- A switch connects multiple devices in a LAN.
- A switch learns **MAC addresses** and forwards frames to the correct port.
- Switching reduces **collision domains** compared with hub-based networks.
- A switch filters traffic instead of sending every frame everywhere.
- A switch improves efficiency by sending frames only where they belong.

**Broadcast domains and segmentation**

- A LAN is a group of devices in the same **broadcast domain**.
- Devices on the same switch can share a broadcast domain.
- Separate switches can create separate broadcast domains.
- VLANs create separate broadcast domains on a single switch.
- VLANs provide logical segmentation without changing physical cabling.

**VLAN ports and trunking**

- An **access port** carries one VLAN.
- A **trunk port** carries multiple VLANs.
- Trunking supports inter-switch communication across VLANs.
- `802.1Q` is used for VLAN trunking.
- VLANs can support voice and video traffic on the same connection.

**Loop prevention and switch behavior**

- Redundant links between switches can create loops.
- Loops can cause broadcast storms.
- **Spanning Tree Protocol (STP)** prevents loops on switched networks.
- STP blocks paths as needed to keep the topology loop-free.
- Use STP when you add redundant switch links.

**Practical switching takeaways**

- Use switching to segment traffic and improve performance.
- Use VLANs to separate groups logically.
- Use trunks when you need multiple VLANs across a link.
- Use access ports for end devices that belong to one VLAN.
- Check for loops when you troubleshoot unstable switched networks.
