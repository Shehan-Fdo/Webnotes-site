## **Course:** Building Wealth Online with AI in 2026: A Complete Guide

**Privacy and disclosure basics**

If you collect personal information through a website, app, or AI-powered workflow, you need a clear privacy policy and a practical disclosure strategy. A privacy policy explains how you collect, use, share, and protect personal information. It also helps show users that you are transparent about your data practices.

A policy alone is not enough. Your site behavior must match what the policy says, and your disclosures must reflect how your tools actually work. Regulators can look at both the written policy and the way your website functions.

- A **privacy policy** is expected when you collect customer data.
- Generic templates are not a substitute for a policy tailored to your business activities.
- Your disclosures should match actual data collection, sharing, and user-rights workflows.
- If you use AI tools, consider how prompts, training data, and outputs affect privacy expectations.

**What a privacy policy should cover**

A useful privacy policy gives users a plain-language summary of your data practices. It should explain what personal information you collect, why you collect it, and how you use it in your business operations.

It should also identify where the policy appears on your site or app and how users can access it. The goal is to make the policy easy to find, easy to understand, and consistent with your real-world processing.

- What personal information you collect
- Why you collect it
- How you use and share it
- Where users can find the policy
- How you handle updates to the policy

**Data protection and security**

Data protection focuses on keeping sensitive personal information out of the wrong hands. The Federal Trade Commission notes that businesses often store names, Social Security numbers, credit card data, and other account information needed for normal operations. If that data is exposed, it can lead to fraud, identity theft, and loss of trust.

Protecting data is both a security issue and a business issue. You reduce risk when you limit access, safeguard stored information, and align your internal practices with your public disclosures.

- **Sensitive personal information** needs stronger handling than ordinary business data.
- Security failures can create legal, financial, and reputational harm.
- Your internal controls should support the promises made in your privacy policy.

**AI-specific privacy concerns**

AI systems create new privacy questions because they can process large amounts of personal information at scale. Current policy discussions highlight concerns about whether personal data is used for model training, how prompts are handled, and whether outputs can reveal information from a user’s broader online activity.

In practice, you should treat AI privacy as part of your broader data governance plan. If your tools collect prompts, store conversation history, or use personal information for model improvement, disclose that clearly and review whether those practices fit your legal obligations.

- AI tools may involve personal data in training or deployment.
- Prompts and chat history can create privacy exposure.
- User-facing disclosures should explain how AI-related data is handled.

**Compliance and website functionality**

A privacy policy must be supported by website functionality that lets users exercise their rights. Some U.S. privacy laws require more than a posted notice; they also require operational features that make rights requests possible.

That means you should check whether your site supports the actions your policy promises. If users are told they can request access, deletion, or opt-out choices, the site should provide a working path to do so.

- Match policy language to actual site behavior.
- Test rights-request flows regularly.
- Update disclosures when your data practices change.

**Practical compliance steps**

1. Identify every place you collect personal information, including forms, checkout pages, analytics tools, and AI features.
2. Write a privacy policy that reflects your actual data practices, not a generic template.
3. Make the policy easy to find on your website or app.
4. Review whether your site functions support the rights and choices you disclose.
5. Revisit the policy whenever your tools, vendors, or AI workflows change.

**Key takeaways**

Privacy, data protection, and disclosure work together. The policy tells users what you do, security protects the data you hold, and your website functionality proves that your disclosures are real. For AI-driven businesses, this alignment matters even more because data use can be broader, faster, and less visible to users.
