## **Course:** Building Wealth Online with Tech: A Complete Guide

**Competitor analysis overview**

- Competitor analysis helps you understand similar businesses.
- It shows how rivals price, market, and position their offers.
- It helps you find ways to stand out in the market.
- It supports a clearer **unique selling point** (USP).

**Why it matters**

- It helps you make smarter business decisions.
- It reveals competitor strengths and weaknesses.
- It can show where competitors fall short.
- It helps you identify niche markets and growth opportunities.
- It supports realistic goals through benchmarking.

**Use market research with competitor analysis**

- Market research helps you find customers.
- Competitive analysis helps you make your business unique.
- Use both together to find a competitive advantage.
- Market research blends consumer behavior and economic trends.
- It helps confirm and improve your business idea.
- It reduces risk early in planning.
- Gather demographic data to understand opportunities and limits.
- Useful data can include age, wealth, family, and interests.
- Check whether there is demand for your product or service.
- Check whether the market is large enough to support your offer.

**What to examine**

- Compare product features and add-ons.
- Compare pricing models, discounts, and trials.
- Review marketing channels and messaging.
- Look at brand identity and customer journey.
- Identify direct and indirect competitors.
- Note how competitors serve customer needs.
- Compare strengths, weaknesses, and likely future moves.

**How to approach the analysis**

1. Identify your competitors.
2. Review their products, pricing, and marketing.
3. Compare their strengths and weaknesses.
4. Study their value propositions and customer journeys.
5. Use the findings to refine your position.

**What to do with the results**

- Use the findings to sharpen your offer.
- Adjust your marketing strategy where needed.
- Set goals based on real market conditions.
- Improve your value proposition.
- Use the analysis to support go-to-market planning.
- Revisit the analysis as the market changes.

> Do not treat a quick spreadsheet or occasional social check as a full analysis.

**Practical focus**

- Keep the process research-driven.
- Use both quantitative and qualitative information.
- Quantitative data can include market size and price points.
- Qualitative data can include values, desires, and buying motives.
- Focus on what helps you win customers before competitors do.
