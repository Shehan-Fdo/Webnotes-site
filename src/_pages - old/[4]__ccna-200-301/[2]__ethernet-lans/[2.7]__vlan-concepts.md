## **Course:** 200-301 CCNA

**VLAN basics**

- VLAN means **Virtual Local Area Network**.
- A VLAN lets you divide one physical switch into multiple logical networks.
- Devices in the same VLAN communicate as if they are on the same LAN segment.
- VLANs work at **OSI Layer 2**.
- VLANs help you segment a network without adding new hardware.
- VLANs are widely used to separate traffic in modern networks.

**Broadcast domains and segmentation**

- A switch forwards **BUM** traffic.
- BUM stands for broadcast, unknown unicast, and multicast.
- A broadcast domain includes devices that receive the same broadcast, unknown unicast, or multicast frame.
- VLANs reduce the size of broadcast domains.
- Smaller broadcast domains can improve performance.
- Smaller broadcast domains can also improve security.
- A single LAN is often treated as one broadcast domain and one subnet.

**Why VLANs matter**

- VLANs let you separate departments on the same physical switch.
- You can place users in different logical networks even when they share infrastructure.
- VLANs reduce unnecessary broadcast traffic.
- VLANs can lower operational cost by avoiding extra physical segmentation.
- VLANs support cleaner network design in larger environments.

**Trunking overview**

- A **trunk link** carries traffic for multiple VLANs over one physical link.
- Trunking helps extend VLANs across multiple switches.
- Without trunking, spanning multiple VLANs between switches is harder.
- Broadcast domains can span more than one switch.
- Trunk links are a core part of VLAN design in switched networks.

**Practical takeaways**

- Use VLANs to group devices by function, department, or policy.
- Use separate VLANs when you want traffic isolation.
- Use trunk links when you need multiple VLANs between switches.
- Remember that VLANs create logical separation, not physical separation.
- Keep the main goal in mind: reduce broadcast scope and organize traffic.
