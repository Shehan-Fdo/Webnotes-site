## **Course:** 200-301 CCNA

**Collision domains**

- A **collision domain** is the part of a network where packet collisions can occur.
- A collision happens when two devices send at the same time on a shared segment.
- Collisions reduce network efficiency because devices must resend the packets.
- Hubs place all connected ports in the same collision domain.
- A bridge, switch, or router places each port in a separate collision domain.

**Broadcast domains**

- A **broadcast domain** is the set of devices that can receive a broadcast at Layer 2.
- Broadcasts stay within the broadcast domain unless a router forwards them.
- All ports on a hub or switch are in the same broadcast domain by default.
- Router ports belong to different broadcast domains.
- Routers do not forward broadcasts from one broadcast domain to another.

**Device behavior**

- A hub repeats incoming electrical signals out every other port.
- A hub creates one shared medium for all connected devices.
- A switch reduces collisions by separating collision domains per port.
- A router separates broadcast domains and limits broadcast propagation.

**Key comparisons**

- Collision domains describe where frames can collide.
- Broadcast domains describe where broadcasts can spread.
- Hubs increase collision risk because all ports share one collision domain.
- Switches separate collision domains but do not separate broadcast domains by default.
- Routers separate broadcast domains and stop broadcasts at the boundary.

**Practical takeaways**

- Use collision domain to reason about resend events on shared media.
- Use broadcast domain to reason about Layer 2 broadcast reach.
- Remember that a hub keeps all ports together in both domains.
- Remember that a switch separates collision domains per port.
- Remember that a router blocks broadcasts between broadcast domains.
